QUESTION 1

Stem: Which among the following is embryologically and physiologically unrelated to other neural cells?

Correct answer: Microglia

Explanation: Most neural cells originate from the **neuroectoderm**: neurones, **astrocytes** and **oligodendrocytes** arise from neural-tube ectoderm; **Schwann cells** of the peripheral nervous system arise from neural crest (also ectoderm) [Boron ch.2]. **Microglia**, by contrast, are **mesodermal in origin** — they descend from yolk-sac–derived myeloid progenitors that colonise the CNS during embryogenesis, making them the resident macrophages of the CNS. Functionally, microglia perform immune surveillance, phagocytose debris, and modulate inflammation, distinct from the support and myelinating roles of the other glia. Hence microglia are embryologically and functionally the outliers among the listed cells.

Hints:

1. Recall the embryological origin of each glial cell type.
2. Three of the listed cells are ectodermal in origin; one is mesodermal.
3. The exception is the resident immune cell of the CNS.

Distractors:

- "Astrocytes" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Schwann Cells" — Reveals the misconception that Schwann cells are unrelated to other glia. They share neural-crest origin with sympathetic neurones.
- "Oligodendrocyte" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 2

Stem: In which of the following areas is a pseudo-unipolar type of neuron present?

Correct answer: Dorsal root ganglia

Explanation: Neurones are classified by the number of processes emerging from the soma. **Pseudo-unipolar neurones** have a single short process that bifurcates into a peripheral and a central branch — both functionally axonal — characteristic of primary sensory neurones in the **dorsal root ganglia (DRG)** and the equivalent sensory ganglia of certain cranial nerves (trigeminal ganglion). The peripheral branch carries information from the skin, joints or viscera; the central branch enters the spinal cord. **Sympathetic** and **parasympathetic ganglion** neurones are typically **multipolar**. **Cranial nerve nuclei** within the brainstem contain motor or sensory relay neurones, mostly multipolar.

Hints:

1. Recall the four morphological neurone classes (multipolar, bipolar, pseudo-unipolar, unipolar) and where each is found.
2. Pseudo-unipolar morphology is characteristic of primary somatosensory afferents.
3. The somata of these afferents reside in dorsal root ganglia and certain cranial sensory ganglia.

Distractors:

- "Sympathetic ganglia" — Reveals the misconception that all peripheral ganglia share neuronal morphology. Autonomic ganglion cells are multipolar.
- "Parasympathetic ganglia" — Same misconception as (b).
- "Cranial nerve nuclei" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 3

Stem: Where does the initiation of nerve impulse occur in a spinal motor neuron?

Correct answer: Axon hillock+ initial segment

Explanation: The **axon hillock and the adjacent initial segment** of the axon have the lowest threshold for action-potential generation in a typical neurone because they contain the **highest density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels** [Guyton ch.5] [Boron ch.5]. Synaptic potentials (EPSPs and IPSPs) generated on the dendrites and soma are summed and conducted electrotonically toward the axon hillock; if the depolarisation reaches threshold there, the regenerative Na⁺ inflow ignites the propagated action potential, which then spreads down the axon. The cell body and dendrites have lower channel densities and consequently higher thresholds.

Hints:

1. Action potentials are initiated where the threshold is lowest.
2. The threshold for AP initiation depends on the local density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels.
3. Channel density peaks at the junction between soma and axon.

Distractors:

- "Initial node of Ranvier" — Reveals the misconception that the first node is the trigger zone. The initial segment, before the first node, is the trigger.
- "Cell body" — Reveals the misconception that the soma initiates the AP. The soma sums inputs but does not have the highest channel density.
- "Dendritic tree" — Reveals the same misconception. Dendrites generate EPSPs/IPSPs but not propagated APs in most neurones.

QUESTION 4

Stem: Which part of the neuron contains a maximum number of Na+ channels per square micrometer?

Correct answer: Node of Ranvier

Explanation: In a myelinated axon, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are densely concentrated at the **nodes of Ranvier** — small unmyelinated gaps between adjacent myelin internodes — with channel densities of ~1000–2000 per μm², compared with ~25 per μm² at the cell body and essentially zero beneath the myelin sheath [Guyton ch.5]. This clustering at nodes is the structural basis of **saltatory conduction**: action potentials regenerate at each node, "jumping" from node to node, dramatically increasing conduction velocity and reducing the metabolic cost of restoring ionic gradients. Demyelinating diseases (e.g. multiple sclerosis, Guillain-Barré) disrupt this organisation and slow or block conduction.

Hints:

1. In a myelinated nerve, channels are concentrated where the membrane is exposed to extracellular fluid.
2. Saltatory conduction requires very high channel density at the regenerative sites.
3. The unmyelinated gaps in myelin are the answer.

Distractors:

- "Cell body" — Reveals the misconception that the soma matches axon-segment channel density.
- "Axon terminal" — Reveals the misconception that voltage-gated Na⁺ channels dominate the terminal. The terminal has voltage-gated _Ca²⁺_ channels for transmitter release.
- "Surface of myelin" — Reveals the misconception that the myelinated internode has channels. The internode is essentially channel-free; this is the basis of saltatory conduction.

QUESTION 5

Stem: In a cutaneous sensory neuron, at which of the following places is the propagated action potential generated?

Correct answer: First node of Ranvier

Explanation: In a **cutaneous primary sensory neurone** (a pseudo-unipolar DRG cell), the peripheral nerve ending in the skin is a _receptor_ that generates a graded **receptor potential** in response to mechanical, thermal or chemical stimuli [Guyton ch.5]. This receptor potential is _not_ itself a propagated action potential — it spreads electrotonically along the unmyelinated fine terminal until it reaches the **first node of Ranvier**, where the high density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels allows the regenerative all-or-none action potential to be generated and then propagated saltatorily along the axon to the spinal cord. This contrasts with motor neurones (Q3), where the trigger zone is the axon hillock/initial segment.

Hints:

1. Distinguish receptor potentials (graded) from action potentials (all-or-none, propagated).
2. In sensory afferents the receptor potential travels electrotonically to the nearest excitable membrane patch.
3. The first excitable site downstream of the cutaneous receptor is the trigger zone.

Distractors:

- "Initial segment" — Reveals the misconception that all neurones initiate APs at the axon hillock/initial segment. In primary sensory neurones, the initial segment is anatomically distant from the receptor; the first node of Ranvier on the peripheral process is the trigger zone.
- "Dendrite" — Reveals the misconception that the cutaneous "receptive" structure functions as a dendrite generating APs.
- "Nerve ending" — Reveals the misconception that the receptor itself generates the propagated AP. The receptor generates a graded receptor potential.

QUESTION 6

Stem: What is the relationship between diameter of the nerve and conduction velocity?

Correct answer: Linear

Explanation: For myelinated mammalian axons, **conduction velocity is approximately linearly proportional to fibre diameter**: velocity (m/s) ≈ 6 × diameter (μm) [Guyton ch.5]. Larger axons have lower internal axial resistance to current flow and (for myelinated fibres) larger internodal distances, both of which speed up the spread of local circuit currents that depolarise the next active site. For _unmyelinated_ axons, velocity scales with the _square root_ of diameter (so that doubling the diameter increases velocity by only ~40%), because there is no internodal gain. The MCQ's intended answer of "linear" reflects the myelinated-fibre relation as taught at the first-year MBBS level.

Hints:

1. Recall the relationship between axon diameter and conduction velocity for myelinated fibres.
2. The simple formula gives velocity ≈ 6 × diameter (μm) — that is a linear relationship.
3. Identify which mathematical relationship a 6× multiplier represents.

Distractors:

- "Parabolic" — Reveals the misconception that velocity scales with diameter squared. It does not.
- "Hyperbolic" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "No relation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 7

Stem: What process is responsible for the change in membrane potential that occurs between point 1 and point 3?

Correct answer: Movement of Na+ into the cell

Explanation: The question references points 1 and 3 on a standard action-potential trace not present in the source `.txt` file. From the keyed answer, points 1–3 correspond to the **rising (depolarising) phase** of the action potential — from threshold up to the peak [Guyton ch.5]. This rising phase is driven by the regenerative inward flow of Na⁺ through voltage-gated Na⁺ channels: as the membrane depolarises, more Na⁺ channels open, which depolarises the membrane further, opening still more channels — classic positive feedback. The result is an inrush of Na⁺ that drives V_m from −70 mV up toward E_Na (~+60 mV).

Hints:

1. The phase between points 1 and 3 is the depolarising upstroke of the AP.
2. Recall the ion responsible for the rising phase of the action potential.
3. The cation flooding into the cell carries the positive charge that depolarises the membrane.

Distractors:

- "Movement of Na+ out of the cell" — Reveals the misconception that the AP is driven by Na⁺ extrusion. The Na⁺ gradient _into_ the cell drives depolarisation.
- "Movement of K+ into the cell" — Reveals the misconception that K⁺ moves into the cell during the AP. K⁺ moves _out_ during repolarisation.
- "Movement of K+ out of the cell" — Reveals the misconception that K⁺ efflux is responsible for depolarisation. K⁺ efflux drives _repolarisation_ (between points 3 and 4 typically).

QUESTION 8

Stem: The membrane conductance for potassium is maximum at which of the following points in a nerve action potential?

Correct answer: 4

Explanation: Working from the keyed answer (4), this point on a standard AP trace corresponds to the **late repolarising / early after-hyperpolarisation phase** [Guyton ch.5]. K⁺ conductance (g_K) rises in a delayed fashion after Na⁺ activation; voltage-gated K⁺ channels open more slowly than Na⁺ channels, peak as the membrane is repolarising, and stay open beyond the time when V_m has returned to baseline — producing the brief after-hyperpolarisation. QUESTION 9

Stem: Which of the following is true regarding the upstroke of a nerve action potential?

Correct answer: There is net inward current and the cell interior becomes less negative

Explanation: During the upstroke (rising phase) of the nerve action potential, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open and Na⁺ flows **into** the cell down its electrochemical gradient — a **net inward current** of positive charge [Guyton ch.5]. This positive charge entering the cytoplasm depolarises the membrane: the cell interior becomes less negative (less polarised), passing through 0 mV and overshooting to ~ +35 mV at the AP peak. Both clauses of option (d) are correct.

Hints:

1. Identify the direction of Na⁺ flux during the rising phase and the net charge it carries.
2. Inward positive current must depolarise (make the inside less negative).
3. "Inward" and "less negative" must both be present in the correct option.

Distractors:

- "There is net outward current and the cell interior becomes more negative" — Reveals the misconception that the upstroke is hyperpolarising.
- "There is net outward current and the cell interior becomes less negative" — Inconsistent: outward positive current would hyperpolarise, not depolarise.
- "There is net inward current and the cell interior becomes more negative" — Inconsistent: inward positive current depolarises, not hyperpolarises.

QUESTION 11

Stem: What is known as the time period in which a second stronger impulse can elicit action potential after the application of one stimulus?

Correct answer: Relative refractory period

Explanation: Two refractory periods follow each action potential [Guyton ch.5]. During the **absolute refractory period (ARP)** — encompassing the depolarising phase and most of repolarisation — the inactivation gates of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are closed, and _no stimulus, however strong_, can elicit a second AP. During the subsequent **relative refractory period (RRP)** — late repolarisation through after-hyperpolarisation — Na⁺ channels are progressively recovering from inactivation and a _stronger-than-normal stimulus_ can elicit an AP. This is precisely the definition in option (b). The ARP for a typical large myelinated nerve is ~1/2500 s, setting the upper limit of firing frequency [Guyton ch.5].

Hints:

1. Distinguish absolute from relative refractoriness by whether _any_ stimulus or only a _stronger_ stimulus can elicit an AP.
2. The relative refractory period covers late repolarisation and recovery from Na⁺ channel inactivation.
3. The defining feature is that excitability is reduced but not abolished.

Distractors:

- "Absolute refractory period" — Reveals the misconception that absolute and relative refractoriness are interchangeable. Absolute means _no_ stimulus can elicit an AP.
- "Latent refractory period" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming. (Not a recognised standard term.)
- "Local response" — Reveals the misconception that subthreshold electrotonic responses are a refractory phenomenon. Local responses occur at subthreshold stimulation and do not relate to refractoriness.

QUESTION 12

Stem: From where does the relative refractory period start?

Correct answer: One third of repolarization

Explanation: The transition from absolute to relative refractoriness occurs as voltage-gated Na⁺ channels begin to recover from inactivation, which proceeds gradually as the membrane repolarises [Guyton ch.5]. Conventional MBBS teaching places the start of the **relative refractory period (RRP) at approximately one-third of repolarisation** — i.e. when V_m has repolarised by about a third of the way back from peak to baseline. By this point a fraction of Na⁺ channels have recovered, and a stronger-than-normal stimulus can recruit enough channels to elicit a propagated AP. The RRP continues through after-hyperpolarisation, ending when normal excitability returns.

Hints:

1. Recall the textbook fraction of repolarisation at which the absolute-to-relative transition occurs.
2. The RRP begins when _some_ but not all Na⁺ channels have recovered.
3. The fractional value is one of the four listed options — the smallest fraction that still allows AP generation.

Distractors:

- "Half of repolarization" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Quarter of repolarization" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Two thirds of repolarization" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 13

Stem: Rheobase is an indicator of which of the following?

Correct answer: Magnitude of current

Explanation: **Rheobase** is the **minimum current intensity** required to elicit an action potential when applied for an infinitely long duration [Guyton ch.5]. It is the asymptote of the strength-duration curve. **Chronaxie** is the corresponding _time_: the duration required for a stimulus of twice rheobasic strength to elicit an AP. Rheobase therefore measures excitability in current units (typically mA), reflecting how _strong_ a stimulus must be to depolarise to threshold. Lower rheobase = higher excitability. It does _not_ measure conduction velocity, firing rate or specificity of transmission.

Hints:

1. Distinguish rheobase (current axis of the strength-duration curve) from chronaxie (time axis).
2. Rheobase is a measure of stimulus _strength_, not of stimulus duration or conduction.
3. The unit of rheobase is current (mA).

Distractors:

- "Specificity of impulse transmission" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Rate of discharge" — Reveals the misconception that rheobase relates to firing frequency. It does not.
- "Velocity of nerve conduction" — Reveals the misconception that rheobase relates to conduction velocity. They are independent.

QUESTION 14

Stem: A slowly rising stimulus fails to fire a nerve because of a process known as **\_\_\_\_**.

Correct answer: Accommodation

Explanation: **Accommodation** is the rise in firing threshold that occurs when a stimulus is applied very slowly: a gradual depolarisation allows Na⁺ channel **inactivation gates** to close progressively, removing channels from the available pool faster than the depolarisation can recruit them. As a result, the cell cannot reach threshold even though the stimulus may eventually exceed the normal threshold magnitude [Guyton ch.5]. This is why a slowly rising stimulus fails to fire a nerve, while a sharp step of equal final amplitude does fire it. **Adaptation** is a related but distinct phenomenon at sensory receptors — decreasing receptor potential to a maintained stimulus. **Refractoriness** follows an action potential. **Electrotonus** refers to passive (subthreshold) potential changes around an applied current.

Hints:

1. Distinguish accommodation (slowly rising stimulus failure) from adaptation (sensory-receptor desensitisation).
2. The molecular basis is progressive inactivation of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels.
3. The phenomenon is named for the nerve's "adjusting" to the slow rise.

Distractors:

- "Adaptation" — Reveals the misconception that adaptation and accommodation are interchangeable. Adaptation is a sensory-receptor phenomenon.
- "Refractoriness" — Reveals the misconception that any failure to fire is refractoriness. Refractoriness follows an AP, not a slow stimulus.
- "Electrotonus" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 15

Stem: Which of the following is true?

Correct answer: Type C fibre has the slowest conduction velocity

Explanation: The **Erlanger-Gasser classification** of peripheral nerve fibres ranks them by diameter and conduction velocity [Guyton ch.5]:

- **Aα**: largest, ~13–22 μm, ~70–120 m/s — α-motor neurones, primary muscle spindle (Ia), Golgi tendon organ (Ib).
- **Aβ**: ~8–13 μm, ~30–70 m/s — touch, pressure, secondary spindle (II).
- **Aγ**: ~4–8 μm, ~15–30 m/s — γ-motor (to muscle spindle).
- **Aδ**: ~1–4 μm, ~12–30 m/s — fast pain, cold, crude touch.
- **B**: ~1–3 μm, ~3–15 m/s — preganglionic autonomic.
- **C** (unmyelinated): ~0.4–1.2 μm, ~0.5–2 m/s — slow pain, postganglionic autonomic, temperature.

**Type C fibres are the smallest and unmyelinated, with the slowest conduction velocity** of all peripheral fibre classes. Aα — not Aβ — has the largest diameter; Aγ is myelinated; Aα (not Aδ as misspelt) carries proprioception/motor not pressure.

Hints:

1. Reconstruct the Erlanger-Gasser classification with diameters and velocities.
2. Velocity is roughly proportional to diameter for myelinated fibres; unmyelinated fibres are slowest.
3. The smallest, unmyelinated fibre class is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Type A° fibre carry information related to pressure" — The "°" is a Unicode-glyph artefact in the source; assuming intended Aα, this reveals the misconception that Aα carries pressure. Aα carries motor and proprioception; Aβ carries touch/pressure.
- "Type Aβ fibre has the largest diameter" — Reveals the misconception that Aβ is the largest. Aα is the largest.
- "Type AY fibre is unmyelinated" — Reveals the misconception that Aγ is unmyelinated. Aγ is myelinated; only C fibres are unmyelinated.

QUESTION 18

The clinical picture in this vignette — sustained mechanical compression of the arm during sleep ("Saturday night palsy" pattern) producing motor weakness (Aα) and tingling (Aβ) but preserved pain (carried by C fibres and partly Aδ) — reflects this differential susceptibility. 2. Pressure and hypoxia preferentially affect larger myelinated fibres; local anaesthetics preferentially affect smaller fibres.
3. The vignette shows preserved pain (small-fibre function) with lost motor (large-fibre function) — match this to the rule.

Distractors:

- "B fibers are more susceptible to pressure than A fibers" — Reveals the misconception that B fibres are most pressure-sensitive. A (large myelinated) fibres are most pressure-sensitive.
- "C fibers are more sensitive to pressure than A fibers" — Reverses the actual order — this would be true for _local anaesthetic_, not pressure.
- "Motor nerves are more affected by sleep than sensory nerves" — Misattributes the cause to "sleep" rather than to mechanical compression.

QUESTION 19

Stem: Spike duration is maximum in which of the following nerve fiber?

Correct answer: C

Explanation: Action potential (spike) duration varies inversely with fibre diameter and degree of myelination. Larger myelinated fibres have brief APs (~0.4 ms in Aα), while small unmyelinated **C fibres have the longest spikes** (~2 ms or more) [Guyton ch.5]. The slower kinetics of K⁺ channels and the higher input resistance of small fibres prolong repolarisation. C-fibre APs also typically have prominent after-hyperpolarisation. This is consistent with C fibres' general slow kinetics: slow conduction, slow repolarisation, slow recovery, prolonged refractoriness.

Hints:

3. Among the listed options, the smallest unmyelinated fibre is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Aδ" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "B" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Aβ" — Reveals the misconception that larger fibres have longer spikes. Larger means faster — both in conduction and in AP repolarisation.

QUESTION 22

Stem: All of the following are true regarding a biphasic action potential except **\_\_\_\_**.

Correct answer: 2 or more positive waves

Explanation: A **biphasic action potential** is what is recorded with two extracellular surface electrodes when a propagated action potential travels along a nerve: as the depolarisation passes the first electrode the trace deflects in one direction, and as it passes the second electrode it deflects in the opposite direction — yielding a **biphasic** waveform with one positive and one negative deflection (i.e. two phases, one of each sign, _not_ two positive waves). The recording is _extracellular_ (surface), reflects the same all-or-none phenomenon as the underlying intracellular AP, and shows the same refractoriness. Hence the false statement is (b) — biphasic does not mean two positive waves but two phases of opposite sign.

Hints:

1. Distinguish biphasic (two phases, opposite signs) from monophasic (one phase) recordings.
2. The recording method (extracellular surface electrodes) determines the shape.
3. "Two or more positive waves" misdescribes the canonical biphasic shape.

Distractors:

QUESTION 23

Stem: End plate potential follows all of the following except?

Correct answer: All or none law

Explanation: The **end plate potential (EPP)** is a graded depolarising potential generated at the postsynaptic neuromuscular junction by ACh-induced opening of nicotinic receptors (Na⁺/K⁺ permeable, with net depolarisation) [Guyton ch.5]. Unlike a propagated action potential, the EPP is **graded** — its magnitude depends on the amount of ACh released and the number of receptors activated — so it does _not_ obey the all-or-none law. It does undergo **summation** (multiple miniature EPPs add up, and tetanic stimulation summates EPPs). A normal EPP is depolarising; under conditions of local anaesthesia or competitive block (e.g. curare), the EPP can fail to reach threshold but is still depolarising. Hyperpolarisation is observed in some inhibitory junctions but not at the typical NMJ — although the question lists it as "follows", which the answer key permits as a feature in some contexts.

Hints:

1. Distinguish graded postsynaptic potentials (EPPs, EPSPs) from all-or-none action potentials.
2. EPPs depend on transmitter quantity — they are graded, not all-or-none.
3. The exception is the law that distinguishes propagated APs from local potentials.

Distractors:

- "Depolarization" — Correctly true; EPPs are depolarising.
- "Hyperpolarization" — Reveals the broader concept that postsynaptic potentials can be either depolarising (excitatory) or hyperpolarising (inhibitory), although at the NMJ they are exclusively depolarising. The answer key accepts (a) over (c).
- "Summation" — Correctly true; summation is a defining property of graded potentials.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Strictly, EPPs at the NMJ do not hyperpolarise — they are exclusively depolarising. Both (a) and (c) are technically true exceptions to "what an EPP follows," but (a) "all or none law" is the conventional textbook answer. Recommend reviewing whether (c) belongs as a distractor at all.]

QUESTION 24

Stem: Which of the following can be used to measure a synaptic potential?

Correct answer: Microelectrode

Explanation: A **microelectrode** (a fine glass pipette with a tip diameter <1 μm) inserted into a cell allows direct measurement of the cell's membrane potential, including synaptic potentials such as EPSPs and IPSPs [Guyton ch.5]. **Patch clamp** and **voltage clamp** both also measure transmembrane currents and potentials, but their characteristic use is for ion-channel current measurement (single-channel and macroscopic, respectively). The MCQ favours "microelectrode" as the canonical method for recording synaptic potentials _as voltages_. **ECG** records body-surface electrical activity of the heart and is unrelated to synaptic-potential measurement.

Hints:

1. Distinguish techniques that record voltage from those that primarily measure current.
2. The classical method for recording an intracellular synaptic potential as a voltage is a fine glass intracellular electrode.
3. The simplest technique listed is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Patch clamp" — Plausible — patch clamp can record synaptic currents and (in current-clamp mode) potentials. The answer key favours (b) microelectrode as the simpler/older method.
- "Voltage clamp" — Voltage clamp holds V*m fixed and measures current; it is used for synaptic \_currents* but not directly for recording the spontaneous synaptic potential.

QUESTION 25

Stem: A man who is fond of exotic cuisine develops perioral numbness after eating the Pufferfish. The given poisoning is due to which of the following toxins?

Correct answer: Tetrodotoxin

Explanation: **Tetrodotoxin (TTX)** is the famous neurotoxin concentrated in the liver, ovaries and skin of the **pufferfish** (Tetraodontidae family — the source of the Japanese delicacy _fugu_) [Guyton ch.5]. TTX is a high-affinity, selective blocker of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (binds the extracellular pore mouth, site 1), abolishing axonal action potentials. Clinical presentation begins ~30 min after ingestion with **perioral numbness and paraesthesiae**, progressing to peripheral paralysis, respiratory failure and death in severe cases. There is no specific antidote — treatment is supportive (mechanical ventilation). **Saxitoxin** is a structurally different but mechanistically similar Na⁺-channel blocker from dinoflagellates (paralytic shellfish poisoning). **Curare** blocks nicotinic AChR at the NMJ. **Ciguatoxin** _opens_ Na⁺ channels (from contaminated reef fish), causing temperature reversal and other neurological symptoms.

Hints:

1. Pufferfish poisoning is the classical clinical scenario for one specific Na⁺-channel toxin.
2. The toxin is named for the fish family it is associated with.
3. The mechanism is blockade of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels at the extracellular pore mouth.

Distractors:

- "Saxitoxin" — Reveals the misconception that all Na⁺-channel toxins are interchangeable. Saxitoxin is from dinoflagellates (paralytic shellfish poisoning), not pufferfish.
- "Curare" — Reveals the misconception that all paralytic poisons act at the same site. Curare blocks the nicotinic ACh receptor at the NMJ.
- "Ciguatoxin" — Reveals the misconception that all marine fish toxins are pufferfish toxins. Ciguatoxin is from reef-fish-associated dinoflagellates and _opens_ (not blocks) Na⁺ channels.

QUESTION 26

Stem: Conduction in the chemical synapses in CNS is

Correct answer: Always orthodromic

Explanation: A **chemical synapse** is intrinsically directional. The presynaptic terminal contains synaptic vesicles, voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels and the release machinery; the postsynaptic membrane contains transmitter receptors. Transmitter is released only from the presynaptic side and binds receptors only on the postsynaptic side. Hence chemical synapses conduct **only orthodromically** (from pre- to post-synaptic), regardless of which way the parent axon happens to be stimulated [Guyton ch.5]. **Antidromic** conduction is possible along the axon itself (if you stimulate mid-axon, the AP can spread in both directions), but it cannot cross a chemical synapse in reverse. By contrast, **electrical synapses (gap junctions)** can conduct bidirectionally.

Hints:

1. Recall the structural asymmetry of a chemical synapse — what is on each side?
2. Asymmetric placement of release machinery and receptors enforces directionality.
3. A propagated impulse cannot run "backward" across the synaptic cleft because the postsynaptic side has no transmitter to release.

Distractors:

- "Both antidromic and orthodromic" — Reveals the misconception that chemical synapses are bidirectional like gap junctions.
- "Always antidromic" — Reveals reversed direction of conduction.
- "Antidromic under lab conditions" — Reveals the misconception that experimental manipulation can drive antidromic transmission across a chemical synapse. Antidromic conduction occurs along axons but not across chemical synapses.

QUESTION 27

Stem: Fast axonal transport is by all except **\_**

Correct answer: Neurofilaments

Explanation: **Fast axonal transport** uses the microtubule "highway" with **kinesin** as the motor for **anterograde** transport (cell body → axon terminal, ~400 mm/day) and **cytoplasmic dynein** as the motor for **retrograde** transport (terminal → cell body, ~200 mm/day) [Boron ch.2]. Both motors hydrolyse ATP and walk along microtubules carrying vesicles, mitochondria and signalling endosomes. **Neurofilaments**, by contrast, are _cargoes_ of slow axonal transport: they themselves move very slowly down the axon (~0.1–3 mm/day), conveyed by the slow component. Hence neurofilaments are not part of fast axonal transport.

Hints:

1. List the molecular components of axonal transport: tracks, motors and cargoes.
2. Microtubules are the tracks; kinesin and dynein are the motors; everything else is cargo.
3. The exception is a cytoskeletal protein that itself travels as cargo by the slow component.

Distractors:

- "Dynein" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Kinesin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Microtubules" — Reveals the misconception that microtubules are not part of fast transport. They are the _track_ on which fast transport occurs.

QUESTION 28

QUESTION 29

Stem: When a nerve is cut, which of the following takes place first?

Correct answer: Mylein degeneration

Explanation: After a peripheral nerve is severed, the distal axonal segment undergoes **Wallerian degeneration**: the axon swells and breaks up within hours, and the surrounding **myelin sheath disintegrates within 1–2 days**. This **myelin degeneration** is the _first_ observable change. Subsequently, **Schwann cells** proliferate and form bands of Büngner that guide axonal regrowth (over days to weeks), the cell body undergoes chromatolysis, and **regenerative sprouting** from the proximal stump begins (typically by ~1 week, advancing ~1–4 mm/day). **Denervation supersensitivity** at the target organ develops over weeks (upregulation of receptors). The earliest of the listed events is myelin degeneration.

Hints:

1. Recall the temporal sequence of events after nerve transection — Wallerian degeneration first, regenerative changes later.
2. The first observable change in the distal stump is the breakdown of the myelin sheath.
3. Schwann cell proliferation, sprouting and supersensitivity all follow the initial degeneration.

Distractors:

- "Regenerative Sprouting" — Reveals the misconception that regeneration begins immediately. It begins after degeneration and Schwann cell preparation of the distal stump.
- "Denervation supersensitivity" — Reveals the misconception that supersensitivity is acute. It develops over weeks at the target organ.
- "Schwann cell proliferation" — Reveals the misconception that Schwann cell response precedes myelin breakdown. Schwann cell mobilisation follows myelin disintegration.

QUESTION 30

Stem: Which of the following is true regarding the resting membrane potential (RMP) in a nerve fibre?

Correct answer: It becomes more positive as extracellular K+ increases

Explanation: Hyperkalaemia (raised [K⁺]\_o) reduces the K⁺ concentration gradient → E_K becomes less negative → RMP **depolarises** (becomes less negative / more positive) [Guyton ch.5]. By Nernst, raising [K⁺]\_o from 4 to 8 mmol/L shifts E_K from ~ −94 to ~ −76 mV. Since RMP closely follows E_K, the membrane depolarises by a similar amount. Option (a) is wrong because cochlear inner hair cells have unusual RMPs around −40 to −60 mV with the endocochlear potential of +80 mV in scala media. QUESTION 32

Stem: Absolute refractory period is due to:

Correct answer: Closure of inactive gates of sodium channel

Explanation: Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels have **two gates**: an _activation_ gate that opens on depolarisation (allowing Na⁺ influx during the AP upstroke) and an **inactivation** gate that closes shortly after activation (terminating Na⁺ current within ~1 ms) [Guyton ch.5]. As long as the **inactivation gate is closed**, the channel cannot conduct, regardless of how strongly the membrane is stimulated — this is the molecular basis of the **absolute refractory period (ARP)**. The inactivation gate only opens again after the membrane has repolarised toward baseline. The terminology in option (d) is non-standard ("inactive gates of sodium channel" is the inactivation gate); option (c) ("active gates" = activation gates) refers to the gate that _opens_ on depolarisation, not closes during ARP.

Hints:

1. Recall that voltage-gated Na⁺ channels have two distinct gates.
2. The gate responsible for the absolute refractory period is the one that _closes_ shortly after the channel opens.
3. The closing of this gate prevents reopening until repolarisation.

Distractors:

- "Opening of calcium channels" — Reveals the misconception that Ca²⁺ channels mediate ARP. They mediate the cardiac plateau, not nerve ARP.
- "Closure of potassium channels" — Reveals the misconception that K⁺ channel closure causes ARP. K⁺ channels are _open_ during ARP, contributing to repolarisation.
- "Closure of active gates of sodium channel" — Reveals the misconception that activation gates close to produce ARP. Activation gates _opening_ generates the AP; ARP is due to _inactivation_ gate closure.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: The terminology "inactive gates" in option (d) is informal — the standard term is "inactivation gate". The intent and answer are clear.]
