QUESTION 1

Stem: Spatial distribution from which a stimulus produces a response is called **\_**

Correct answer: Receptive field

Explanation: The **receptive field** of a sensory neurone (or higher sensory cell) is the region of body surface (or sensory space) within which an appropriate stimulus elicits a response from that neurone [Guyton ch.47] [Guyton ch.48]. Receptive field size varies inversely with sensory acuity: small receptive fields (fingertips, lips) → high two-point discrimination; large receptive fields (back, thigh) → poor discrimination. **Motor unit** is one motor neurone plus its innervated muscle fibres; **dermatome** is the area of skin innervated by a single dorsal-root segment; **sensory neuron** is the cell itself rather than the spatial concept.

Hints:

1. Recall the term for the stimulus territory of a single sensory neurone.
2. The concept describes spatial distribution, not the cell itself.
3. Acuity is inversely related to the size of this field.

Distractors:

- "Motor unit" — Confuses sensory and motor concepts.
- "Dermatome" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming. (Dermatomes are spinal-segment territories, larger than receptive fields.)
- "Sensory neuron" — Confuses the cell with the spatial concept.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Fast vibration sense is mediated by **\_**

Correct answer: Pacinian corpuscles

Explanation: Cutaneous mechanoreceptors and their preferred stimuli [Guyton ch.47] [Guyton ch.48]: **Merkel's discs** — slowly adapting type 1 (SA1), sustained pressure and texture. **Meissner's corpuscles** — rapidly adapting type 1 (RA1), light touch and _low-frequency_ (~30–50 Hz) flutter/vibration. **Pacinian corpuscles** — rapidly adapting type 2 (RA2), _high-frequency_ (~100–300 Hz) vibration and deep pressure — the receptor for "fast vibration". **Ruffini's endings** — slowly adapting type 2 (SA2), sustained skin stretch. The **tuning-fork test (256 Hz)** in clinical neurology relies primarily on Pacinian corpuscle responses.

Hints:

1. Match each cutaneous mechanoreceptor to its preferred frequency range.
2. The receptor for high-frequency vibration is encapsulated and rapidly adapting.
3. The receptor activated by a 256 Hz tuning fork is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Merkel's disc" — Reveals the misconception that all cutaneous receptors transduce vibration. Merkel's discs encode sustained pressure.
- "Meissner's corpuscle" — Mediates _low-frequency_ flutter, not fast vibration.
- "Ruffini's end organ" — Mediates skin stretch, not vibration.

QUESTION 3

Stem: The phasic receptor is **\_**

Correct answer: Meissner's corpuscles

Explanation: **Phasic (rapidly adapting)** receptors fire briefly at the _onset_ and _offset_ of a stimulus and stop firing during sustained stimulation [Guyton ch.47]. They are best at detecting _changes_ and _velocity_ of stimuli. Examples: **Meissner's corpuscles**, **Pacinian corpuscles**, hair-follicle receptors. **Tonic (slowly adapting)** receptors continue firing throughout sustained stimulation, encoding stimulus _intensity_ and _duration_: **Merkel's discs**, **Ruffini's endings**, **muscle spindles** (group II), **Golgi tendon organs**, **nociceptors**, **carotid baroreceptors**. Hence Meissner's corpuscles are the listed phasic receptor.

Hints:

1. Distinguish phasic (rapidly adapting) from tonic (slowly adapting) receptors.
2. Phasic receptors detect changes; tonic receptors maintain a steady firing rate.
3. The encapsulated touch receptor in the dermal papillae of glabrous skin is phasic.

Distractors:

- "Merkel's disc" — Tonic, encodes sustained pressure.
- "Ruffini's end organs" — Tonic, encodes sustained skin stretch.
- "Muscle spindle" — Group II is tonic; group Ia has both phasic and tonic components but the spindle is generally classified as tonic for static length sensing.

QUESTION 4

Stem: You are doing a CNS examination of a patient with a spinal injury. At which of the following sites would the distance of two-point discrimination be greatest?

Correct answer: Back of scapula

Explanation: **Two-point discrimination** measures the smallest separation at which two simultaneously applied stimuli are perceived as separate rather than one [Guyton ch.48]. It varies inversely with **innervation density** and **receptive field size**: high discrimination (small distance) on regions with many receptors and small receptive fields; low discrimination (large distance) on regions with few receptors and large receptive fields. Approximate two-point thresholds: **lip ~2–3 mm**, **fingertip ~2–4 mm**, **palm ~10 mm**, **dorsum of hand ~20–30 mm**, **back ~50–70 mm**. Hence the back of the scapula has the **greatest** two-point discrimination distance — the lowest acuity. The cortical sensory homunculus reflects this: large representations for face, lips, hands, smaller representations for trunk and back.

Hints:

1. Recall the variation of two-point discrimination across body regions.
2. The greatest distance corresponds to the lowest acuity.
3. The sensory homunculus shows which regions have the smallest cortical representation.

Distractors:

- "Lip" — Highest acuity (~2–3 mm). Greatest is the opposite.
- "Palm of the hand" — Intermediate (~10 mm).
- "Dorsum of hand" — Less acute than palm but still better than back.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Which of the following is true about the nature of the sensory receptor given below: [image of Pacinian corpuscle]

Correct answer: Fast adapting and encapsulated

Explanation: **Pacinian corpuscles** are the largest cutaneous mechanoreceptors, located deep in the dermis and subcutaneous tissue. They consist of a central afferent nerve ending surrounded by **concentric lamellae** of connective tissue that form a multilayered **encapsulation** ("onion-like") [Guyton ch.47]. Their characteristic property is **rapid adaptation**: the lamellae mechanically filter sustained pressure (lamellae redistribute applied force away from the central axon over milliseconds), so the receptor responds only to _changes_ in pressure — the basis of high-frequency vibration sensing. Hence Pacinian corpuscles are both **fast-adapting and encapsulated**.

Hints:

1. Recall the structure and adaptation properties of Pacinian corpuscles.
2. The lamellar capsule is the structural basis of rapid adaptation.
3. The receptor is fast-adapting because its capsule mechanically filters sustained pressure.

Distractors:

- "Slow adapting and not encapsulated" — Reverses both properties.
- "Fast adapting and not encapsulated" — Correct adaptation but wrong on encapsulation.
- "Slow adapting and encapsulated" — Correct on encapsulation but wrong on adaptation.

QUESTION 6

Stem: Which of the following classification of receptors is the same?

Correct answer: Mechanoreceptors and exteroceptors

Explanation: This question pairs receptors classified by **modality** (what they transduce) and by **location** (where the stimulus comes from) [Guyton ch.47]. **By modality**: mechanoreceptors, thermoreceptors, photoreceptors, chemoreceptors, nociceptors. **By location**: exteroceptors (external surface), interoceptors (within body), proprioceptors (musculoskeletal), telereceptors (distant — vision, hearing, smell). The MCQ asks which pairing is the _same_: **mechanoreceptors and exteroceptors** is the closest match because most cutaneous mechanoreceptors _are_ exteroceptors (responding to external mechanical stimuli on body surface) — although strictly mechanoreceptors include interoceptors (e.g. baroreceptors) too. The other pairings cross categories incorrectly.

Hints:

1. Distinguish modality-based from location-based receptor classifications.
2. The same receptor can be classified in different ways depending on the framework.
3. Mechanoreceptors at the skin surface fall into the exteroceptor category.

Distractors:

- "Interoceptors and nociceptors" — Crosses categories: nociceptors are everywhere (skin, viscera), not all interoceptive.
- "Photoreceptors and proprioceptors" — Crosses categories: photoreceptors are telereceptors, not proprioceptors.
- "Telereceptors and interoceptors" — Telereceptors detect distant stimuli (light, sound, smell); interoceptors detect internal stimuli — opposite locations.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Strictly, mechanoreceptors include both exteroceptive (cutaneous) and interoceptive (baroreceptors, stretch in viscera) types, so the equivalence in (a) is approximate. The keyed answer accepts (a) as the closest match.]

QUESTION 7

Stem: Which of the following is true regarding sensory receptor properties?

Correct answer: The frequency of a train of action potentials in an afferent fibre reflects the intensity of the stimulus given to its receptor

Explanation: **Frequency coding** is the fundamental mechanism by which sensory afferents transmit stimulus intensity [Guyton ch.47]. A stronger stimulus generates a larger receptor (generator) potential, which produces a higher frequency of action potentials in the afferent axon. This is true (d). (a) is false: the magnitude of sensation is _not_ linearly proportional to stimulus intensity — it follows the **Weber-Fechner law (logarithmic)** or Stevens' power law; sensation is approximately proportional to log(stimulus). (b) is false: most touch receptors **adapt** — Meissner's and Pacinian corpuscles are rapidly adapting; only Merkel and Ruffini are slowly adapting. (c) is reversed: **Meissner's and Pacinian adapt rapidly; Merkel and Ruffini adapt slowly**.

Hints:

1. Recall how stimulus intensity is encoded in afferent firing.
2. Frequency coding (rate of APs) is the standard mechanism.
3. Magnitude of sensation follows a logarithmic, not linear, relation to intensity.

Distractors:

- "Magnitude of sensation felt is directly proportional to the intensity of the stimulus" — Reveals the misconception of linearity. The Weber-Fechner law is logarithmic.
- "A touch receptor will generate a constant rate of discharge of its axon for as long as the receptor is stimulated" — Reveals the misconception that all receptors are non-adapting.
- "Meissner's and Pacinian corpuscles adapt slowly, the others adapt rapidly" — Reverses the adaptation properties.

QUESTION 8

Stem: Aδ fibers do not transmit \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Slow pain

Explanation: Two qualitatively distinct kinds of pain are conducted by different fibre classes [Guyton ch.49]: **Aδ fibres** (small myelinated, ~12–30 m/s) carry **fast pain** — sharp, well-localised, and "first" felt — also called **epicritic** pain. **C fibres** (small unmyelinated, ~0.5–2 m/s) carry **slow pain** — dull, aching, poorly localised, and "second" felt — also called **protopathic** pain. Hence Aδ fibres do **not** transmit slow pain — that is the C-fibre domain. Note that "fast pain", "first pain" and "epicritic pain" are all synonyms for the same Aδ-mediated phenomenon.

Hints:

1. Match Aδ and C fibres to their respective qualities of pain.
2. Aδ → fast/first/sharp pain; C → slow/second/dull pain.
3. The exception is the C-fibre quality.

Distractors:

- "Epicritic pain" — Synonym for fast pain — Aδ does carry this.
- "Fast pain" — Aδ does carry fast pain.
- "First pain" — Synonym for fast pain — Aδ does carry this.

QUESTION 9

Stem: The most potent algogenic substance is \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Bradykinin

Explanation: **Algogens** are substances that elicit pain when applied to nociceptors. The list includes bradykinin, serotonin, histamine, prostaglandins, K⁺, ATP, H⁺ [Guyton ch.49]. Of these, **bradykinin is the most potent algogen** — even very low concentrations cause intense pain, and it is one of the principal mediators of inflammatory pain. Bradykinin acts on B1 and B2 receptors on nociceptor endings, sensitising them to other stimuli and directly activating them. Prostaglandins (PGE2 in particular) **sensitise** nociceptors to bradykinin and other stimuli but are themselves only weakly algogenic — this is why NSAIDs (which inhibit prostaglandin synthesis) reduce pain even though prostaglandins are not the most potent algogen.

Hints:

1. Rank the major algogens by potency.
2. The kinin-system peptide is the most potent of the listed substances.
3. Bradykinin is generated by tissue damage and is a major mediator of inflammatory pain.

Distractors:

- "Serotonin" — A modest algogen released from platelets and mast cells.
- "Histamine" — Causes itch primarily; produces only mild pain.
- "Prostaglandin" — Sensitises nociceptors but is itself weakly algogenic.

QUESTION 10

Stem: Vanilloid receptors are activated by all except \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Vibration

Explanation: **Vanilloid receptors (TRPV1 — transient receptor potential vanilloid type 1)** are nociceptor cation channels activated by [Guyton ch.49]: **noxious heat (>43°C)**, **chemical irritants** (capsaicin from chillies — the prototype agonist; resiniferatoxin from spurges; **acidic pH** from tissue damage), **endogenous lipids** (anandamide), and inflammatory mediators (bradykinin, prostaglandins, NGF — sensitisers). They are **not** activated by **vibration** (which is the domain of mechanoreceptors, especially Pacinian corpuscles). Hence (d) is the exception. Note: options (a) "heat" and (b) "thermal" are essentially the same modality — the question's listing is redundant.

Hints:

1. Recall the modalities that activate TRPV1 nociceptor channels.
2. Heat, capsaicin, acid and inflammation are agonists; vibration is not.
3. The exception is a mechanoreceptor stimulus, not a nociceptor stimulus.

Distractors:

- "Heat" — TRPV1 is activated by noxious heat (>43°C).
- "Thermal" — Synonymous with heat; TRPV1 is activated.
- "Chemicals" — TRPV1 is activated by capsaicin, acid, etc.

QUESTION 11

Stem: Heat-pain fibers are stimulated at temperatures around \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: 45° C

Explanation: **Heat-induced pain** begins at approximately **45°C** in human skin — the threshold at which TRPV1 channels begin opening and noxious heat is perceived [Guyton ch.49]. Temperatures up to ~43°C activate warm receptors (innocuous warmth); temperatures from 45°C upward activate **heat nociceptors** (TRPV1). At ~52°C, TRPV2 channels (very-noxious heat) begin to contribute. Tissue damage occurs at ~46°C with prolonged exposure. The **cold-pain threshold** is approximately 15°C (TRPM8 transitions to TRPA1 territory). Guyton ch.49 specifically notes that heat-pain receptors are stimulated at around 45°C.

Hints:

1. Recall the temperature thresholds for innocuous warmth and noxious heat.
2. TRPV1 begins to open around the threshold for heat pain.
3. The standard textbook number is 45°C.

Distractors:

- "30° C" — Innocuous warmth range.
- "49° C" — Above pain threshold (peak pain) but not the threshold itself.
- "35° C" — Innocuous warmth range; below thermoneutrality, no pain.

QUESTION 12

Stem: Weber-Fechner law relates magnitude of sensation to \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Log of Intensity of stimulus

Explanation: The **Weber-Fechner law** states that the perceived **magnitude of sensation is proportional to the logarithm of stimulus intensity**: Sensation = K × log(I/I₀), where I is stimulus intensity, I₀ is the threshold, and K is a constant [Guyton ch.47]. The law builds on Weber's earlier observation that the just-noticeable difference (JND) in stimulus intensity is a constant fraction of the baseline intensity (ΔI/I = constant). The logarithmic relationship explains why we can perceive a vast dynamic range of stimulus intensities (e.g. ~120 dB range in hearing). Stevens' power law (Sensation = K × Iⁿ) is a more modern refinement, but Weber-Fechner remains the textbook MBBS-level answer.

Hints:

1. Recall the law that relates perceived magnitude to physical stimulus intensity.
2. The relation is non-linear — specifically logarithmic.
3. The law explains why we tolerate huge ranges of sound and light intensity.

Distractors:

- "Strength of muscle contraction" — Confuses sensory with motor concepts.
- "Amplitude of action potential" — Reveals the misconception that AP amplitude codes intensity. APs are all-or-none; intensity is coded by frequency.
- "Duration of stimulus" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 13

Stem: A patient's right leg was amputated because it was unsalvageable after the motor vehicle accident. After 2 weeks of her surgery, she started feeling episodes of severe pain in the amputated area. This pain is most likely based on which of the following mechanisms?

Correct answer: Cortical plasticity

Explanation: **Phantom limb pain** is the perception of pain in an amputated limb. The major mechanism is **cortical reorganisation (plasticity)**: when the sensory cortical area for the amputated limb loses its peripheral input, neighbouring cortical regions (face, contralateral hand) gradually invade the deprived area. Stimulation of these neighbouring areas can be misperceived as coming from the missing limb, often as pain — particularly in amputees who experienced pain before amputation. **Muller's law** (specific nerve energies) states that each sensory modality is determined by the specific pathway carrying it (touch a touch nerve and you feel touch, regardless of the actual stimulus); **labeled line** is essentially the same principle; **Weber-Fechner** relates sensation magnitude to log of stimulus intensity. None of these directly explains phantom limb pain. Hence (c).

Hints:

1. Recall the mechanism of phantom limb pain.
2. After amputation, the cortical territory for the missing limb is reassigned.
3. The phenomenon is a form of cortical reorganisation.

Distractors:

- "Muller's specific nerve energy" — Reveals the misconception that any pain perception is by Muller's law. Muller's law explains modality specificity, not phantom limb.
- "Principle of labeled line" — Same misconception as (a).
- "Weber-Fechner law" — Reveals the misconception that any pain magnitude question invokes Weber-Fechner. Weber-Fechner relates intensity to perception, not anatomical phantom phenomena.

QUESTION 14

Stem: Perception of normal sensory stimuli as painful is \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Allodynia

Explanation: Pain terminology [Guyton ch.49]: **Allodynia** = pain in response to a normally non-painful stimulus (e.g. light touch perceived as painful). **Hyperalgesia** = increased pain response to a normally painful stimulus (e.g. mild pinprick perceived as severe pain). **Analgesia** = absence of pain in response to a stimulus that would normally be painful. **Causalgia** = a chronic regional pain syndrome characterised by burning pain, allodynia and autonomic dysfunction following partial peripheral nerve injury (now subsumed under "complex regional pain syndrome type II"). Hence (a) allodynia is the precise term for "normal stimuli felt as painful".

Hints:

1. Distinguish allodynia (normal stimulus → pain) from hyperalgesia (painful stimulus → exaggerated pain).
2. The defining feature of allodynia is that the stimulus is _not normally_ painful.
3. Light brushing of inflamed skin → pain is the textbook example.

Distractors:

- "Hyperalgesia" — Reveals the misconception that allodynia and hyperalgesia are interchangeable. Hyperalgesia requires a normally painful stimulus.
- "Analgesia" — Opposite phenomenon (pain absence).
- "Causalgia" — A specific clinical syndrome that may include allodynia but is not the term for the general phenomenon.

QUESTION 15

Stem: Which of the following is true about anandamide?

Correct answer: There are anandamide-containing neurons in the periaqueductal gray

Explanation: **Anandamide (N-arachidonoylethanolamine)** is an **endogenous cannabinoid** ("endocannabinoid") — produced _endogenously_ from arachidonic acid–containing phospholipids on demand [Guyton ch.49]. It binds **CB1 and CB2 cannabinoid receptors** (not opioid receptors). Anandamide is **antinociceptive** (anti-pain), not nociceptive — it is part of the descending pain-modulatory system, with anandamide-containing neurones distributed in the **periaqueductal gray (PAG)** matter and elsewhere in the descending pain-control pathway. Hence (c) is correct. (a) is false (it is produced endogenously); (b) is reversed (it is antinociceptive, not nociceptive); (d) is wrong (it binds CB receptors, not opioid).

Hints:

1. Recall what receptor system anandamide belongs to.
2. The endocannabinoid system is part of the descending pain-modulatory system.
3. Anandamide-containing neurones project from a specific midbrain structure.

Distractors:

- "It is not produced endogenously" — Reveals the misconception that anandamide is exogenous (like THC). It is endogenous.
- "It is a nociceptive agent" — Reverses the direction of effect.
- "It binds to opioid receptors" — Confuses cannabinoid with opioid systems.

QUESTION 16

Stem: Which of the following is true about visceral pain?

Correct answer: It is poorly localised

Explanation: **Visceral pain** has several distinguishing features [Guyton ch.49]: (a) it is **poorly localised** ("dull, deep, diffuse") because visceral afferents converge onto spinal neurones that also receive somatic input — leading to poor spatial discrimination and to **referred pain** patterns ✓. (b) Visceral nociceptors do **not** show rapid adaptation — sustained pathology produces sustained pain. (c) Visceral pain is mediated by **C fibres** (slow, unmyelinated) and to a lesser extent Aδ — not B fibres (which are autonomic preganglionic). (d) Visceral pain resembles **slow pain**, not fast pain — it is dull and aching, with delayed onset.

Hints:

1. Recall the qualities of visceral pain compared with somatic pain.
2. Visceral pain is poorly localised and may be referred to somatic territories.
3. The poor localisation is the most clinically relevant feature.

Distractors:

- "It shows rapid adaptation" — Reverses adaptation property of nociceptors.
- "It is mediated by B fibers" — Confuses pain afferents (Aδ, C) with autonomic preganglionic fibres (B).
- "It resembles fast pain" — Reverses the quality (visceral pain is slow-pain-like).

QUESTION 17

Stem: Pain insensitive structure is\_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Alveoli

Explanation: Pain sensitivity varies markedly across body structures [Guyton ch.49]. Structures with **little or no pain innervation** include the **lung alveoli and parenchyma**, the **brain parenchyma** (no nociceptors — though dura, vessels and meninges are pain-sensitive), and the **liver parenchyma** (without capsular distension). By contrast: **liver capsule** is highly pain-sensitive (liver pain in hepatitis is from capsular stretch); **parietal pleura, peritoneum and pericardium** are densely innervated by somatic afferents (sharp, well-localised pain); **arterial walls** contain nociceptors (vascular pain in angina, dissection, embolism). Hence the alveoli are the listed pain-insensitive structure.

Hints:

1. Recall which thoracic structures are pain-sensitive and which are not.
2. The visceral parenchyma may be insensitive; surrounding capsules and serous membranes are sensitive.
3. The lung alveolar tissue itself is pain-insensitive.

Distractors:

- "Liver capsule" — Pain-sensitive; capsular stretch causes pain.
- "Parietal pleura" — Highly pain-sensitive (somatic innervation).
- "Arterial wall" — Pain-sensitive (vascular pain in arterial pathology).

QUESTION 18

Stem: A patient with chronic pain is undergoing transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation for pain relief. What is the basis of this procedure?

Correct answer: Gate theory of pain

Explanation: The **gate control theory of pain** (Melzack & Wall, 1965) proposes that the perception of pain is modulated at the spinal cord (substantia gelatinosa, lamina II of the dorsal horn) by interactions between large-fibre (Aβ) and small-fibre (Aδ, C) afferents [Guyton ch.49]. Activity in **large myelinated Aβ fibres** (carrying touch/vibration) activates inhibitory interneurones in the substantia gelatinosa that **"close the gate"** to small-fibre pain transmission. **Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS)** activates large-diameter Aβ fibres preferentially with low-intensity electrical pulses, recruiting these inhibitory interneurones and reducing the transmission of pain signals to the brain — providing analgesia. The principle also explains why "rubbing" a painful area helps. Hence (b) gate theory is the basis.

Hints:

1. Recall the spinal-cord theory of how innocuous stimulation can reduce pain perception.
2. Activity in large-diameter fibres inhibits transmission of small-fibre pain at the dorsal horn.
3. TENS preferentially recruits the large-diameter fibres.

Distractors:

- "Referred pain" — A different phenomenon (visceral pain felt at a somatic site).
- "Allodynia" — A different phenomenon (normal stimulus → pain).
- "Pain relief by analgesics" — TENS does not work via pharmacological analgesics; it is an electrical (gate) mechanism.

QUESTION 19

Hints:

1. Distinguish primary hyperalgesia (peripheral, at injury site) from secondary (central, surrounding).
2. The mechanism of primary hyperalgesia is sensitisation of peripheral nociceptors.
3. Sensitisation = lowered threshold of pain receptors.

Distractors:

- "Develops in the normal skin region around the area of flare" — Describes secondary hyperalgesia.
- "Is an abnormal condition in the skin in which painful stimuli become less severe" — Reverses the direction.
- "Is associated with throbbing type of pain" — Plausible but not the defining feature of hyperalgesia mechanism.

QUESTION 20

Stem: The transient receptor potential (TRP) channel that is most active at normal skin temperature is **\_\_**.

Correct answer: TRPV3

Explanation: The TRP channel family has different thermal sensitivities [Guyton ch.49]: **TRPV1** activates at >43°C (noxious heat, capsaicin); **TRPV2** at >52°C (very-noxious heat); **TRPV3** at **30–39°C** — the warm range that includes **normal skin surface temperature (~32–34°C)**, making TRPV3 the most active TRP channel at normal skin temperature; **TRPV4** at moderate warmth (~25–34°C, overlapping); **TRPM8** at <25°C (innocuous cool, menthol); **TRPA1** at <17°C (noxious cold, mustard oil, allyl isothiocyanate). Hence TRPV3 is the answer.

Hints:

1. Map each TRP channel to its temperature range.
2. Normal skin surface temperature is around 32–34°C.
3. The warm (innocuous) channel active in this range is the answer.

Distractors:

- "TRPV4" — Overlapping range but TRPV3 is the canonical "normal skin temperature" channel.
- "TRPM8" — Cold-activated, not warm.
- "TRPA1" — Noxious cold-activated, not normal skin temperature.

QUESTION 21

Stem: A soldier posted in Siachen is brought to you in the ER. In order to rule out hypothermia, how do you best measure his temperature?

Correct answer: Rectal

Explanation: **Core body temperature** is the regulated variable, not surface temperature. In suspected **severe hypothermia**, accurate measurement of core temperature is essential because peripheral (oral, axillary, tympanic) measurements can underestimate or be unreliable when peripheral perfusion is compromised. **Rectal temperature** is one of the most reliable proxies for core temperature, with a **low-reading hypothermia thermometer** (capable of reading <35°C) used to detect severe hypothermia. **Oesophageal**, **bladder** and **pulmonary artery** temperatures are alternatives in critical care. **Infrared tympanic** is fast but unreliable when the ear is cold. **Oral** and **axillary** measurements are inaccurate in hypothermia. Hence (b) rectal is the best practical answer for the ER setting.

Hints:

1. Recall the most reliable site for measuring core temperature in hypothermia.
2. Peripheral measurements are unreliable when peripheral perfusion is compromised.
3. The site closest to core in a routine ER measurement is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Oral" — Inaccurate in hypothermia (peripheral, also affected by mouth breathing).
- "Infrared tympanic thermometer" — Unreliable when the external ear and surrounding tissue are cold.
- "Axillary" — Most peripheral and least reliable.
