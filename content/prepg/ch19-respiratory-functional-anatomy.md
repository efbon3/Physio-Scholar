QUESTION 1

Stem: What is the total number of airway divisions present in between trachea and alveolar sacs?

Correct answer: 23

Explanation: The **Weibel airway model** describes **23 generations** of dichotomous branching from the trachea (generation 0) to the alveolar sacs (generation 23) [Guyton ch.38] [West, *Respiratory Physiology*]. Generations 0–16 form the **conducting zone** (anatomical dead space, no gas exchange — trachea, main/lobar/segmental bronchi, terminal bronchioles). Generations 17–19 are **respiratory bronchioles** (transitional zone, with some gas exchange). Generations 20–22 are **alveolar ducts**, and generation 23 are **alveolar sacs** (respiratory zone, gas exchange). Hence (c) 23.

Hints:

1. Recall the Weibel model of airway generations.
2. Trachea is generation 0; alveolar sacs are the terminal generation.
3. The total number of generations is 23.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Plausible but incorrect generation counts.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Which among the following has the highest airway resistance?

Correct answer: Bronchi

Explanation: Airway resistance is _not_ highest in the smallest airways, despite their small individual diameter [Guyton ch.38] [West]. **Total cross-sectional area** increases massively with each generation due to the dichotomous branching: by generation 16 (terminal bronchioles), the total cross-sectional area is ~180 cm² (vs ~2.5 cm² for trachea). Therefore the **medium-sized bronchi (~generations 4–7)** have the **highest airway resistance** despite individual airways being larger than terminal bronchioles. The small bronchioles (despite small individual diameter) contribute little to total resistance because of their massive parallel summation. This is the **"silent zone"** of the lung — early small-airways disease (e.g. early COPD) can progress without detectable resistance changes.

Hints:

1. Recall how total cross-sectional area changes with airway generation.
2. Resistance is highest where summed cross-sectional area is smallest.
3. Medium-sized bronchi, not the smallest airways, contribute most resistance.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — All have larger total cross-sectional area and lower individual contribution to resistance.

QUESTION 3

Stem: A 14-year-old boy with shortness of breath was diagnosed with chronic inflammation of conducting airways. Which of the following is not a feature of the affected structure?

Correct answer: Membrane bound organelles namely lamellar bodies are present

Explanation: The **conducting zone** (generations 0–16) [Guyton ch.38]: (a) includes the first 16 generations from trachea — true. (b) cartilage in larger airways (trachea, main/lobar bronchi) and smooth muscle in bronchi/bronchioles — true. (c) goblet cells, club cells (Clara cells), mucus glands secrete defensive molecules — true. (d) **lamellar bodies are membrane-bound organelles in type II pneumocytes that store surfactant** — these are in the **alveolar (respiratory) zone**, not the conducting zone. Hence (d) is the false feature.

Hints:

1. Distinguish features of conducting zone from alveolar zone.
2. Lamellar bodies store surfactant in type II pneumocytes (alveolar).
3. The exception describes an alveolar feature.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Correctly true features of conducting airways.

QUESTION 4

Stem: Maximal smooth muscle relative to wall thickness is seen in **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Terminal bronchiole

Explanation: Smooth muscle proportion (relative to wall thickness) **increases** as airway diameter decreases through the conducting zone [Guyton ch.38] [West]. **Trachea and main bronchi** have predominant cartilage and a thinner smooth muscle layer relative to total wall thickness. **Smaller bronchi** have less cartilage but more smooth muscle. **Terminal bronchioles** have **no cartilage** and contain a relatively thick smooth muscle layer relative to their thin wall — making them most susceptible to bronchoconstriction (e.g. in asthma). Respiratory bronchioles have alveolar outpouchings interrupting the smooth muscle continuity. Hence terminal bronchioles have maximal smooth muscle relative to wall thickness.

Hints:

1. Recall the structural progression from trachea to alveoli.
2. Cartilage decreases and smooth muscle proportion increases distally — until alveoli interrupt it.
3. Terminal bronchioles have maximal smooth muscle proportion.

Distractors:

- (b), (c) — More cartilage; smaller smooth muscle proportion.
- "Respiratory bronchioles" — Smooth muscle interrupted by alveoli.

QUESTION 5

Stem: An elderly smoker who presented with an acute exacerbation of COPD is administered salbutamol. Which one of the following substances has a similar mechanism of action?

Correct answer: Vasoactive intestinal polypeptide

Explanation: **Salbutamol** (β₂ agonist) causes **bronchodilation** by activating airway smooth muscle β₂ receptors → Gαs → ↑cAMP → smooth muscle relaxation [Guyton ch.38]. Endogenous bronchodilator pathways include the **non-adrenergic non-cholinergic (NANC) inhibitory system**, which uses **vasoactive intestinal polypeptide (VIP)** and **NO** as transmitters — both produce bronchodilation. **Substance P** (a tachykinin) is a _bronchoconstrictor_. **Leukotrienes** (LTC4, LTD4) are potent _bronchoconstrictors_ (target of montelukast). **Cholinergic stimulation** (parasympathetic, M3) causes _bronchoconstriction_ (target of ipratropium). Hence (b) VIP shares the bronchodilator action of salbutamol.

Hints:

1. Identify the action of salbutamol.
2. Endogenous bronchodilators include VIP and NO.
3. The substance with similar (bronchodilator) action is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Substance P" — Bronchoconstrictor.
- "Leukotrienes" — Bronchoconstrictors.
- "Cholinergic stimulation" — Bronchoconstrictor.

QUESTION 6

Stem: Which of the following cells secrete surfactant in lungs?

Correct answer: Type 2 pneumocyte

Explanation: **Type II pneumocytes (type II alveolar epithelial cells)** synthesise and secrete **surfactant**, stored in cytoplasmic **lamellar bodies** [Guyton ch.38]. They constitute ~5% of alveolar surface area but are vital for surfactant production. **Type I pneumocytes** are flat squamous cells covering ~95% of alveolar surface, providing the gas-diffusion membrane. **Alveolar macrophages (= dust cells)** are phagocytic immune cells, not surfactant producers. Type II cells also serve as the alveolar stem cell, dividing and differentiating into type I cells in injury repair.

Hints:

1. Recall the alveolar cell type that produces surfactant.
2. Lamellar bodies storing surfactant are characteristic.
3. Type II pneumocytes secrete surfactant.

Distractors:

- "Type 1 pneumocyte" — Gas diffusion, not surfactant.
- "Alveolar macrophage", (c) "Dust cells" — Synonymous; phagocytic immune cells.

QUESTION 7

Stem: Surfactant is composed mainly of**\_**

Correct answer: Lecithin

Explanation: Pulmonary surfactant is ~90% phospholipid + ~10% protein [Guyton ch.38]. The dominant phospholipid is **dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC)** — also called **dipalmitoyl lecithin** (lecithin = phosphatidylcholine; the disaturated DPPC variant is the principal surface-active component). Other components: phosphatidylglycerol (~5–10%), cholesterol, surfactant proteins **SP-A, SP-B, SP-C, SP-D**. Hence (c) lecithin is the dominant phospholipid by mass. SP-B and SP-C accelerate surfactant function; SP-A and SP-D contribute to innate immunity.

Hints:

1. Recall the dominant phospholipid in surfactant.
2. Lecithin = phosphatidylcholine.
3. The disaturated dipalmitoyl form is the active component.

Distractors:

- "Phosphatidyl glycerol" — Minor component.
- "Cephalin" — Phosphatidylethanolamine; a minor membrane phospholipid, not surfactant's main component.
- "Protein" — Only ~10% of surfactant.

QUESTION 8

Stem: All are true of surfactant except**\_**

Correct answer: Helps prevents alveolar collapse according to Bernoulli's principle

Explanation: Surfactant functions [Guyton ch.38]: (a) reduces alveolar surface tension → reduces fluid filtration into alveoli → helps prevent **pulmonary oedema** — true. (b) **deficiency causes Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (NRDS)** in preterm infants — true (insufficient surfactant before ~32–34 weeks). (d) reduced surfactant → increased surface tension → increased work to expand the lungs → **increased work of breathing** — true. (c) is **false** — surfactant prevents alveolar collapse according to **Laplace's law (P = 2T/r)**, _not Bernoulli's principle_. Surfactant lowers surface tension preferentially in smaller alveoli, equalising pressure across alveoli of different sizes and preventing collapse of small alveoli into larger ones. Bernoulli's principle relates fluid pressure and velocity, irrelevant to surfactant function.

Hints:

1. Recall the physical principle governing alveolar surface tension.
2. Laplace's law relates pressure to surface tension and radius.
3. The exception attributes the wrong principle to surfactant.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Correctly true.

QUESTION 9

Stem: A young lady at 35 weeks of gestation is at high risk of preterm delivery in the next 7 days. She is started on antenatal corticosteroid therapy to increase surfactant production in the fetus. What is the mechanism of action of this substance?

Correct answer: Breaks the structure of water in alveoli

Explanation: The question asks about surfactant's mechanism. **Surfactant** (a surface-active agent) reduces the **surface tension** at the air-water interface in alveoli by inserting amphipathic phospholipid molecules between water molecules at the surface, **disrupting the hydrogen-bond network** of water and reducing the cohesive forces that produce surface tension [Guyton ch.38]. In the alveolus, this means less inward force tending to collapse the alveolus (per Laplace P = 2T/r). The wording "breaks the structure of water in alveoli" loosely captures this disruption of the water-water hydrogen bond network at the air-liquid interface. Options (b), (c), (d) misrepresent surfactant's mechanism.

Hints:

1. Recall the molecular mechanism of surface-tension reduction.
2. Surfactant disrupts the cohesive water-water bonds at the air-water interface.
3. The wording about "breaking the water structure" describes this.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Misrepresent surfactant's molecular action.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Option (a)'s wording "breaks the structure of water" is unusual phrasing for "reduces surface tension by disrupting the hydrogen-bond network at the air-water interface". The intent is clear but the language is non-standard.]

QUESTION 10

Stem: Stability of alveoli is maintained by**\_\_**

Correct answer: Increased alveolar surface area by surfactant

Explanation: Alveolar **stability** (resistance to collapse) is maintained by surfactant via its **surface-area dependence**: surfactant reduces surface tension _more_ in smaller alveoli (where its concentration per unit area is higher) and _less_ in larger alveoli, equalising the collapsing pressure (P = 2T/r) across alveoli of different sizes [Guyton ch.38]. This prevents small alveoli from emptying into larger ones (Laplace instability). The phrasing "increased alveolar surface area by surfactant" is awkward — the underlying mechanism is surfactant's surface-area-dependent surface-tension reduction. **Lung compliance** is a property influenced by surfactant but not the stability mechanism itself. **Negative intrapleural pressure** prevents lung collapse but is separate from alveolar-stability against collapse. **Residual air** is volume-related, not stability per se.

Hints:

1. Recall the principle by which surfactant prevents alveolar collapse.
2. Surfactant reduces surface tension preferentially in smaller alveoli.
3. The surface-area dependence stabilises alveoli of different sizes.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Different roles or properties.

QUESTION 11

Stem: A woman at risk for preterm delivery is planned for assessment of fetal lung maturity. At what gestational age will the surfactants be detectable in the amniotic fluid?

Correct answer: 28 weeks

Explanation: **Surfactant production** by fetal type II pneumocytes begins around **24–26 weeks gestation**, and surfactant is **detectable in amniotic fluid by ~28 weeks** [Guyton ch.38]. However, **adequate surfactant for postnatal lung function** is not achieved until ~34–35 weeks. The clinical **lecithin-to-sphingomyelin (L:S) ratio** is used to assess fetal lung maturity from amniotic fluid: ratio rises from baseline (~1:1) to ≥2:1 at ~35 weeks, marking lung maturity. Hence (a) 28 weeks is the earliest detectable presence.

Hints:

1. Recall the gestational timeline of surfactant production.
2. Surfactant first appears in amniotic fluid earlier than functional maturity.
3. Detection is at ~28 weeks; maturity at ~34-35 weeks.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Times of progressive maturity, not first detection.

QUESTION 12

Stem: A woman on multiple medications came for an antenatal check-up. Which among the following medications that she takes decreases surfactant production?

Correct answer: Inadequate insulin

Explanation: Factors affecting surfactant production [Guyton ch.38]: **Increase surfactant production**: glucocorticoids (basis of antenatal corticosteroid therapy), thyroid hormones, β-adrenergic agonists (terbutaline), oestrogens. **Decrease surfactant production**: **insulin** in high concentrations (i.e. fetal hyperinsulinaemia in maternal diabetes mellitus delays lung maturation — fetus of diabetic mother has higher risk of NRDS even at term), male sex (transient delay vs female), genetic surfactant deficiencies. The wording in option (c) "inadequate insulin" is unusual — strictly, it is **excess fetal insulin** (in maternal diabetes) that delays surfactant production. Reading "inadequate insulin" liberally (perhaps as "abnormal insulin levels"), the keyed answer (c) accepts this as the diabetes connection.

Hints:

1. Recall the factors that delay or accelerate surfactant production.
2. Maternal diabetes (with fetal hyperinsulinaemia) delays surfactant.
3. The insulin-related option is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Thyroid hormones" — Increase surfactant, not decrease.
- "Beta adrenergic agonists" — Increase surfactant.
- "Calcium supplements" — Not directly related.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Option (c) wording "inadequate insulin" is incorrect — the relevant clinical fact is *excess fetal insulin* (from maternal diabetes) decreases surfactant. The keyed answer (c) reflects the diabetes connection but the option text needs correction. Recommend rewording to "excess fetal insulin (maternal diabetes)" or similar.]

QUESTION 13

Stem: Which is the best metabolic function of the lung?

Correct answer: Conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II

Explanation: The pulmonary endothelium hosts several metabolic functions [Guyton ch.38]: it **inactivates bradykinin (~80%)**, **serotonin (~95%)**, prostaglandins (PGE, PGF), leukotrienes, noradrenaline (some), and metabolises drugs. It also **converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II** via the abundant **angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE)** on pulmonary endothelial cells — this is the largest source of circulating angiotensin II and is the **major metabolic / activation function** of the lung. ACE inhibitors block this step, lowering blood pressure. The MCQ asks for the _best_ metabolic function — the angiotensin conversion is canonically the highlighted lung metabolic role.

Hints:

1. Recall the metabolic functions of the pulmonary endothelium.
2. The lung is a major site of activation of the renin-angiotensin system.
3. Angiotensin I → angiotensin II via ACE is the key activation step.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Real lung metabolic functions but secondary to angiotensin activation.
