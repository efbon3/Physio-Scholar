QUESTION 1

Stem: All of the following are the connective tissue layers of the skeletal muscle except **\_\_**

Correct answer: Sarcolemma

Explanation: A skeletal muscle is wrapped in three concentric connective-tissue investments [Guyton ch.6]: **epimysium** (dense connective tissue around the entire muscle), **perimysium** (around bundles of muscle fibres called fascicles), and **endomysium** (around each individual muscle fibre). These layers carry blood vessels and nerves and converge with the tendon at the muscle-tendon junction. The **sarcolemma**, by contrast, is the _plasma membrane_ of an individual muscle fibre — a true cell membrane composed of a phospholipid bilayer with associated dystrophin-glycoprotein complex, _not_ a connective-tissue investment [Guyton ch.6]. Hence (b) is the exception.

Hints:

1. Distinguish connective-tissue investments from the cell membrane of the muscle fibre itself.
2. Three Greek-rooted "_-mysium_" terms denote concentric connective layers.
3. The exception ends in "-lemma" and denotes a cell membrane.

Distractors:

- "Endomysium" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Perimysium" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Epimysium" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Which of the following is a regulatory muscle protein?

Correct answer: Tropomyosin

Explanation: Skeletal muscle proteins are categorised as: **contractile** (actin, myosin); **regulatory** (tropomyosin, troponin C/I/T); and **structural / scaffolding** (titin, nebulin, α-actinin, desmin, dystrophin, tropomodulin) [Guyton ch.6]. **Tropomyosin** lies in the groove of the F-actin double helix and physically blocks the myosin-binding sites on actin at rest; on Ca²⁺ binding to **troponin C**, the troponin-tropomyosin complex shifts to expose the binding sites and allow cross-bridge formation [Guyton ch.6]. **Actin** is contractile; **dystrophin** anchors the cytoskeleton to the sarcolemma; **tropomodulin** caps the pointed end of thin filaments (length regulation). Hence the only regulatory protein on the list is tropomyosin.

Hints:

1. Categorise each listed protein as contractile, regulatory or structural.
2. Regulatory proteins gate the actin-myosin interaction in response to Ca²⁺.
3. The answer is the helical strand that lies along the actin filament.

Distractors:

- "Actin" — Reveals the misconception that actin is regulatory. Actin is contractile (the thin filament).
- "Tropomodulin" — Reveals the misconception that any "tropo-" prefix denotes regulation. Tropomodulin is a thin-filament _capping_ protein.
- "Dystrophin" — Reveals the misconception that dystrophin gates contraction. Dystrophin is a structural anchor between cytoskeleton and sarcolemma.

QUESTION 3

Stem: Which of the following does not belong to dystrophin-glycoprotein complex?

Correct answer: Perlecan

Explanation: The **dystrophin-glycoprotein complex (DGC)** mechanically links the intracellular F-actin cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix across the sarcolemma, distributing the forces of contraction and protecting the membrane from rupture [Guyton ch.6]. Its components include: **dystrophin** (intracellular rod-like protein binding F-actin); **dystroglycans (α and β)** (transmembrane adaptors); **sarcoglycans (α, β, γ, δ)** (transmembrane glycoproteins forming a sub-complex); syntrophins, dystrobrevins and sarcospan (intracellular). The extracellular matrix partner is **laminin-2 (merosin)**, not perlecan. **Perlecan** is a heparan sulphate proteoglycan of the basement membrane that is not part of the DGC. Mutations in dystrophin cause Duchenne and Becker muscular dystrophies; mutations in sarcoglycans cause limb-girdle dystrophies [Guyton ch.6].

Hints:

1. Recall the components of the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex and the extracellular partner.
2. The extracellular ligand of the DGC is laminin-2, not a basement-membrane proteoglycan.
3. The exception is a basement-membrane heparan sulphate proteoglycan.

Distractors:

- "Dystroglycan" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Sarcoglycan" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 4

Correct answer: Myosin

Explanation: The **thin filament** is composed of two strands of polymerised G-actin twisted into a helix, with **tropomyosin** lying in the helical groove and a **troponin** complex (TnT, TnI, TnC) at every seven actin monomers along the filament [Guyton ch.6]. **Myosin** is the protein of the **thick filament**, not the thin filament. Each thick filament contains ~200 myosin molecules with their heads (cross-bridges) projecting toward neighbouring thin filaments. Hence (b) is the exception.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Which of the following proteins is present in skeletal muscles, but absent in a smooth muscle?

Correct answer: Troponin

Explanation: All four listed proteins are present in _skeletal_ muscle. In **smooth muscle**, contraction is regulated through a different molecular machinery: there is no troponin complex on the thin filament; instead Ca²⁺ binds **calmodulin**, the Ca²⁺-calmodulin complex activates **myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK)**, which phosphorylates the myosin regulatory light chain — only then can myosin engage actin [Guyton ch.6]. Smooth muscle does still contain actin, tropomyosin and myosin (with caldesmon/calponin as additional thin-filament regulators). The protein **uniquely present in skeletal but absent in smooth** is **troponin**.

Hints:

1. Compare the regulatory machinery of skeletal/cardiac vs smooth muscle.
2. Smooth muscle uses a Ca²⁺-calmodulin/MLCK pathway rather than the troponin-tropomyosin gate.
3. The protein absent in smooth muscle is the Ca²⁺-binding subunit of the thin-filament regulatory complex.

Distractors:

- "Tropomyosin" — Reveals the misconception that smooth muscle lacks tropomyosin. It does contain tropomyosin (with caldesmon).
- "Actin" — Reveals the misconception that smooth muscle lacks actin. All muscle types contain actin.
- "Myosin" — Same misconception as (c).

QUESTION 6

Stem: Myosin filament has a fixed length of \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: 1.6 micrometers

Explanation: The thick (myosin) filament is **~1.6 µm long** in mammalian striated muscle, a length fixed by the bipolar self-assembly of ~200 myosin molecules whose tails interdigitate at the M line [Guyton ch.6]. The thin (actin) filament is ~1.0 µm long, capped at the pointed end by tropomodulin and anchored at the barbed end at the Z disc by α-actinin and CapZ. The fixed thick-filament length defines the A band; the thin filaments slide between thick filaments during contraction without changing their own length, generating the I-band shortening seen with sarcomere shortening. The 1.6 µm value is the standard textbook figure.

Hints:

1. Recall the standard textbook length of the thick filament in mammalian striated muscle.
2. Distinguish nanometre and micrometre scales — a sarcomere is in micrometres, a single protein domain is in nanometres.
3. The thick filament length is 1.6 µm.

Distractors:

- "16 nanometers" — Reveals scale confusion (a single myosin head is ~10 nm; the entire thick filament is 1000× longer).
- "1.6 nanometers" — Reveals scale confusion of three orders of magnitude.
- "16 micrometers" — Reveals scale confusion (16 µm would exceed the entire sarcomere).

QUESTION 7

Stem: The thick filament is made up of **\_\_**. 1. Heavy chain of myosin 2. Light chain of myosin 3. Dystrophin 4. Titin

Correct answer: 1 and 2

Explanation: The thick filament consists of myosin molecules — each a hexamer of **two heavy chains** (forming two intertwined α-helical tails plus two globular heads) and **four light chains** (two per head: an essential and a regulatory light chain) [Guyton ch.6]. About 200 myosin hexamers self-assemble in a bipolar arrangement to form one thick filament. **Titin**, although associated with the thick filament (anchoring it to the Z disc and acting as a molecular spring), is a _separate_ giant elastic protein, not a constituent of the thick filament _per se_. **Dystrophin** is a sarcolemmal cytoskeletal protein, unrelated to the thick filament. Hence options 1 and 2 (heavy and light chains of myosin) are correct.

Hints:

1. Recall the molecular composition of myosin: how many heavy and light chains per molecule?
2. Distinguish proteins that _form_ the thick filament from proteins that interact with it.
3. The thick filament is built entirely of myosin chains.

Distractors:

- "1, 3 and 4" — Reveals the misconception that titin and dystrophin are part of the thick filament. Titin tethers the thick filament; dystrophin is unrelated.
- "2, 3 and 4" — Compounds the misconception of (a) by also dropping the heavy chain, which is the largest myosin component.
- "3 and 4" — Reveals the misconception that titin and dystrophin alone constitute the thick filament. They do not.

QUESTION 8

Stem: Pseudo H zone in a sarcomere extends between the \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Two sides of M line

Explanation: In a stretched or relaxed sarcomere, the **H zone** is the central region of the A band where only thick filaments are present (no overlap with thin filaments). At the very middle of the H zone is the **M line**, where myosin tails are cross-linked. The **pseudo H zone** (also called the "bare zone") is the narrow region immediately on either side of the M line where thick filaments lack myosin heads — myosin molecules are arranged tail-to-tail at the centre, so heads project only outward [Guyton ch.6]. Hence the pseudo H zone extends between the two sides of the M line.

Hints:

1. Recall sarcomere band anatomy: A band, I band, H zone, M line, Z disc.
2. The "pseudo H zone" or bare zone is the narrow region immediately flanking the M line.
3. The bare zone exists because myosin molecules pack tail-to-tail at the centre of the thick filament.

Distractors:

- "Two Z lines" — Reveals the misconception that the pseudo H zone spans the entire sarcomere. It is a narrow central region.
- "Two I bands" — Reveals the misconception that the pseudo H zone overlaps the I bands. The I bands are at the periphery of the sarcomere.
- "A and I band" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 9

Stem: A cross-sectional view of a skeletal muscle fiber through the H zone would reveal the presence of **\_\_\_\_**

Correct answer: Myosin only

Explanation: In a sarcomere, the **H zone** is the central part of the A band where only **thick filaments** are present — thin filaments do not extend into this region in the relaxed state [Guyton ch.6]. A cross section through the H zone therefore intersects only thick filaments (myosin), arranged in a hexagonal lattice. By contrast, a cross section through the **outer A band** would show both thick and thin filaments (each thick surrounded by six thin), and a cross section through the **I band** would show only thin filaments (actin only). Hence (c) is correct.

Hints:

1. Recall which filaments are present in each band of the sarcomere.
2. The H zone is exclusively the thick-filament region.
3. The thick filament is myosin.

Distractors:

- "Actin and Titin" — Reveals the misconception that the H zone contains thin filaments. Thin filaments do not extend into the H zone.
- "Actin and Myosin" — Reveals the same misconception.
- "Actin only" — Reveals the misconception that the H zone is the I band. The I band contains actin only; the H zone contains myosin only.

QUESTION 10

Stem: The titin protein present in the muscle fiber binds **\_\_\_\_**

Correct answer: The head ends of myosin filaments to the Z disc

Explanation: **Titin** is the largest protein in the body (~3000–4000 kDa), spanning a half-sarcomere from the **Z disc to the M line**, and physically binding each thick (myosin) filament along the way [Guyton ch.6]. One end of titin anchors the _end of the myosin filament_ to the Z disc; the rest of titin is springy and attaches along the thick filament to the M line, holding it centrally between the two Z discs. Titin also serves as a passive elastic element generating restoring force when the sarcomere is stretched. Of the four options, (b) most closely captures the Z-disc end of titin's anchoring role for the thick filament.

Hints:

1. Titin spans from the Z disc to the M line of a sarcomere.
2. One end of titin tethers the thick filament's terminus to the Z disc; the other end runs along to the M line.
3. The answer describes the Z-disc anchorage of myosin via titin.

Distractors:

- "The tail ends of actin filaments to Z disc" — Reveals the misconception that titin tethers actin. Actin is anchored at the Z disc by α-actinin.
- "The tail ends of myosin filaments to M disc" — Partial truth (titin does extend to the M line) but the Z-disc tether is the canonical "anchor."
- "The myofibrils to each other, and also to the cell membrane" — Reveals the misconception that titin is desmin (intermyofibrillar) or dystrophin (membrane-anchoring).

QUESTION 12

Stem: Muscle myosin and actin filaments are kept in place by **\_**

Correct answer: Titin

Explanation: **Titin** acts as a molecular scaffold maintaining the precise alignment of thick (myosin) filaments centrally between the Z discs, keeping them in register with the surrounding thin (actin) filaments [Guyton ch.6]. Without titin, the thick filaments would drift off-centre when the sarcomere is stretched, and the regular interdigitation that produces force would be lost. Titin's springy I-band region also generates the passive elastic tension that resists overstretching. **α-actinin** crosslinks actin at the Z disc but does not maintain the thick-thin registration.

Hints:

1. The protein that maintains thick-thin filament alignment is the giant elastic scaffold spanning each half-sarcomere.
2. Without this protein, the myosin filaments would shift off-centre under stretch.
3. The protein is the largest in the human body.

Distractors:

- "Tropomyosin" — Reveals the misconception that thin-filament regulators also align filaments. Tropomyosin gates myosin-actin interaction; it does not maintain filament alignment.
- "Troponin" — Same misconception as (a).
- "Actinin" — Reveals the misconception that α-actinin alone keeps thick filaments in place. α-actinin anchors actin at the Z disc; titin keeps myosin centred.

QUESTION 14

Stem: Which of the following occurs during skeletal muscle contraction?

Correct answer: Both H and I band shorten

Explanation: During contraction the sliding-filament mechanism brings thin filaments deeper into the A band toward the M line [Guyton ch.6]. The **A band** length is fixed (= thick-filament length, ~1.6 µm). The **H zone** narrows because thin filaments now overlap the central region. The **I band** narrows because the Z discs move closer together as thin filaments penetrate further into the A band on each side. Hence both H and I bands shorten during contraction; the A band does not. The Z line moves in along with the I band shortening.

Hints:

3. The H zone and I band both shrink as filaments slide.

Distractors:

- "Both A and I band shorten" — Includes the same misconception.
- "Both A and H band shorten" — Same misconception about A band.

QUESTION 15

Stem: The cross-bridges of the sarcomere in skeletal muscle are made up of \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Myosin

Explanation: **Cross-bridges** are the projections from the thick filament that physically engage actin during the cross-bridge cycle. They consist of the **globular heads of myosin molecules** [Guyton ch.6]. Each myosin head contains the actin-binding site, the ATP-binding site and ATPase activity. During the power stroke, the head pivots, sliding the thin filament inward by ~10 nm. After ATP binds, the head detaches; ATP hydrolysis cocks it back; then it re-attaches further along the thin filament for the next cycle. The other listed proteins are regulatory or structural and do not form cross-bridges.

Hints:

1. The cross-bridge is the protein that physically contacts actin during contraction.
2. The cross-bridge is the head of the thick-filament protein.
3. The protein hydrolysing ATP at the head is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Titin" — Reveals the misconception that titin forms cross-bridges. Titin is structural.
- "Troponin" — Reveals the misconception that troponin engages actin. Troponin is on the thin filament and gates exposure of actin's myosin-binding sites.
- "Tropomyosin" — Same misconception as (c).

QUESTION 16

Stem: ATPase activity is present in \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Myosin Head

Explanation: The **globular myosin head** contains both the actin-binding site and the **ATPase active site** that hydrolyses ATP to ADP + Pi during the cross-bridge cycle [Guyton ch.6]. The energy released by ATP hydrolysis is captured as the conformational "cocked" state of the head; release of Pi during the power stroke produces the force-generating swing that slides the thin filament. The myosin **tail** has no enzymatic activity — it self-associates with other myosin tails to form the bipolar thick filament. **Actin** is a polymerised G-actin filament; it has its own ATPase activity related to polymerisation but not the contractile-cycle ATPase asked here. **Desmin** is an intermediate filament without ATPase activity.

Hints:

1. The site of ATP hydrolysis in the cross-bridge cycle is on a specific subdomain of myosin.
2. The catalytic and actin-binding sites are on the same globular subdomain.
3. The "head" of the myosin molecule is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Myosin Tail" — Reveals the misconception that the entire myosin molecule is enzymatically active. The tail is self-associative and structural.
- "Actin filaments" — Reveals the misconception that actin's ATPase activity drives contraction. Actin's intrinsic ATPase relates to polymerisation, not cross-bridge cycling.
- "Desmin filaments" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 17

Stem: Myosin head detaches from actin by which of the following mechanism?

Correct answer: Binding of ATP

Explanation: At the end of a power stroke, the myosin head is bound to actin in the rigor configuration with ADP and Pi already released [Guyton ch.6]. **Binding of a fresh ATP molecule** to the myosin head causes a conformational change that releases the myosin head from actin — _detachment_. ATP is then hydrolysed by myosin's ATPase, returning the head to its cocked, high-energy state ready for the next power stroke. This is why **rigor mortis** develops after death: ATP depletion prevents detachment, leaving the myosin heads stuck to actin in a rigid muscle. **Release of ADP and Pi** drives the _power stroke_, not detachment. **Troponin C configuration changes** (Ca²⁺ binding/dissociation) gate access to the cycle but do not detach a bound head.

Hints:

1. Walk through the cross-bridge cycle and identify the step at which the head leaves the thin filament.
2. Detachment requires fresh ATP — that is why ATP depletion produces rigor.
3. Binding of ATP precedes detachment, not the other way around.

Distractors:

- "Entry of calcium into sarcoplasmic reticulum" — Reveals the misconception that Ca²⁺ uptake terminates each cross-bridge cycle. Ca²⁺ uptake terminates _contraction_, not single-cycle detachment.
- "Change in troponin C configuration" — Reveals the misconception that troponin gates each detachment. Troponin gates whole-filament availability, not single-head dynamics.
- "Release of ADP and Pi" — Reveals the misconception that product release detaches the head. Product release drives the _power stroke_; detachment is by ATP rebinding.

QUESTION 18

Stem: In human skeletal muscles, calcium ions are stored in?

Correct answer: Terminal cistern

Explanation: Skeletal muscle Ca²⁺ stores reside in the **sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR)**, an internal membrane network analogous to the ER of other cells. The SR has a specialised dilation at each end of the sarcomere called the **terminal cistern**, which lies adjacent to a transverse (T) tubule, forming a triad (one T tubule flanked by two terminal cisterns) [Guyton ch.6] [Guyton ch.7]. The terminal cistern stores high concentrations of Ca²⁺ bound to **calsequestrin**. On excitation, depolarisation propagates down the T tubule, triggers the dihydropyridine receptor (DHPR), which in turn opens the ryanodine receptor (RyR1) on the terminal cistern membrane to release Ca²⁺ into the cytoplasm. **Sarcolemma** is the surface membrane (no significant Ca²⁺ store). **T tubules** are continuous with extracellular fluid (low Ca²⁺ store).

Hints:

1. Recall the structures of the muscle excitation-contraction coupling triad.
2. Ca²⁺ is stored in the SR, specifically at its dilated termini next to the T tubules.
3. The Ca²⁺-binding protein in this store is calsequestrin.

Distractors:

- "Sarcolemma" — Reveals the misconception that the surface membrane stores Ca²⁺. It does not.
- "Transverse tubules" — Reveals the misconception that T tubules are SR. T tubules are invaginations of the sarcolemma and are continuous with extracellular fluid.
- "All are correct" — Includes both wrong options.

QUESTION 21

Stem: A bodybuilder is regularly working out in a gym. Which of the following is true regarding total heat production during muscle contraction?

Correct answer: Activation heat is produced when a muscle is contracting

Explanation: Heat production by muscle is partitioned into [Guyton ch.6 — heat is the major output of muscle metabolism]:

- **Resting heat** — basal metabolism at rest.

- **Relaxation heat** — produced as the muscle returns to its resting length; present in _isotonic_ contractions (during shortening) but mostly absent in _isometric_ contractions (no shortening, no extra heat to dissipate during relaxation).
- **Recovery heat** — produced _after_ contraction during oxidative restoration of phosphocreatine and other intermediates.

Statement (a) "Activation heat is produced when a muscle is contracting" is correct. (b) is wrong because initial heat is _contraction-related_, not basal. (c) is wrong because relaxation heat is mainly produced in isotonic contractions. (d) is wrong because **recovery heat is approximately equal to initial heat**, not greater.

Hints:

1. Recall the four phases of muscle heat production and what each represents.
2. Activation heat is part of initial heat, released during the contraction.
3. Identify the option that correctly assigns activation heat to the contraction phase.

Distractors:

- "Initial heat produced is due to the basal metabolic processes." — Reveals confusion of resting heat with initial heat.
- "Relaxation heat is present in both isotonic and isometric contractions" — Reveals the misconception that relaxation heat is the same in both types. Relaxation heat is greater in isotonic.
- "Recovery heat of muscle is greater than the initial heat" — Reveals the misconception about magnitudes. They are roughly equal.

QUESTION 22

Stem: In a 100-m dash, most of the energy consumed by skeletal muscles to replenish ATP is derived from **\_\_\_\_**

Correct answer: Phosphocreatine

Explanation: Energy for muscle contraction is supplied in three stages [Guyton ch.6]: (1) **stored ATP** sustains contraction for ~1–2 seconds; (2) **phosphocreatine (PCr)** rapidly regenerates ATP by transferring a phosphate to ADP via creatine kinase, extending maximal contraction to ~5–8 seconds; (3) **anaerobic glycolysis** (pyruvate → lactate) extends maximal effort to ~1 minute; (4) **oxidative phosphorylation** sustains long-duration activity. A **100-metre dash** is over in ~10 seconds — within the time window where **phosphocreatine** is the dominant energy source for ATP regeneration [Guyton ch.6]. Aerobic glycolysis and fatty acid oxidation contribute negligibly during such a brief explosive effort.

Hints:

1. Recall the time scales over which each energy source dominates.
2. Stored ATP lasts seconds; phosphocreatine extends this; glycolysis extends to ~1 min; oxidative metabolism dominates beyond.
3. A 100-m dash takes ~10 seconds — within the phosphocreatine window.

Distractors:

- "Aerobic glycolysis" — Reveals the misconception that aerobic processes can supply energy fast enough for sprint efforts. They cannot — sprint relies on PCr and anaerobic glycolysis.
- "Oxidation of Fatty acids" — Reveals the misconception that fat oxidation is rapid enough for sprinting. Fat oxidation is the slowest energy system.

QUESTION 23

Stem: The length of the muscle fiber at the onset of contraction which is required to attain the maximum active tension is known as

Correct answer: Resting Length

Explanation: The **resting length** (or **optimum length**, L₀) is the muscle-fibre length at which active tension generated upon stimulation is maximal — corresponding to a sarcomere length of ~2.0–2.2 µm in mammalian striated muscle [Guyton ch.6]. At this length the overlap between thick and thin filaments is maximal, so the largest number of cross-bridges can engage simultaneously. At sarcomere lengths >2.2 µm, overlap diminishes and active tension falls; at lengths <2.0 µm, thin filaments collide at the M line and thick filaments butt against the Z disc, again reducing active tension. The **length-tension relationship** describes this inverted-U curve. _In vivo_ most skeletal muscles operate near their resting length.

QUESTION 25

Stem: Active tension in a muscle depends upon which of the following?

Correct answer: Number of motor units recruited

Explanation: Whole-muscle active tension depends primarily on **motor unit recruitment** and **firing frequency** (rate coding) — the two principal mechanisms by which the CNS grades force [Guyton ch.6]. As force demand rises, motor units are recruited progressively: small (slow, fatigue-resistant Type I) units first, large (fast, fatigable Type II) units last (the Henneman size principle). Within each unit, force is also graded by raising firing frequency (twitch summation → unfused tetanus → fused tetanus). The total number of muscle fibres is fixed for a given muscle. Aerobic capacity affects fatigue resistance but not peak tension. Action potential amplitude is essentially all-or-none; force does not scale with AP amplitude.

Hints:

1. Recall the two CNS-controlled variables that determine whole-muscle force.
2. The CNS grades force by recruiting more motor units and by firing them faster.
3. The number of recruited motor units is the answer of immediate relevance.

Distractors:

- "Number of muscle fibres" — Reveals the misconception that whole-fibre count varies with effort. Number of fibres in a muscle is fixed.
- "Aerobic capacity of muscle" — Reveals the misconception that aerobic capacity sets peak tension. It sets endurance, not peak force.
- "Action potential amplitude" — Reveals the misconception that AP amplitude codes force. APs are all-or-none; force is coded by recruitment and rate.

QUESTION 28

Stem: A motor unit is made up of **\_\_\_\_**.

QUESTION 29

Stem: Which of the following is correct about the recruitment of fast fatiguable motor unit ?

Correct answer: Recruited last

Explanation: The **Henneman size principle** governs motor-unit recruitment: motor units are recruited in order of _increasing size of the α-motor neurone_ — small motor neurones (innervating slow Type I fibres, low-force, fatigue-resistant) are recruited first, and large motor neurones (innervating **fast fatigable Type IIx/IIb fibres**, high-force, rapidly fatigable) are recruited **last**, only at high force demands [Guyton ch.6]. This produces an orderly recruitment from finely-graded low-force adjustments to powerful high-force outputs, and it pairs the right fibre type to the right task — postural muscles use slow units at rest, sprint or jump uses fast fatigable units. Fast fatigable units are recruited only briefly because of their rapid fatigue.

Hints:

1. Recall the Henneman size principle of motor unit recruitment.
2. Smallest motor neurones recruit first; largest recruit last.
3. Fast fatigable units are at the end of the recruitment sequence, used only when force demand is high.

Distractors:

- "Recruited first" — Reveals the misconception that all motor units fire simultaneously. Henneman ordering recruits small slow units first.
- "During relaxation" — Reveals the misconception that recruitment occurs during relaxation. Recruitment occurs during contraction.
- "For the less demanding tasks" — Reveals the misconception that fast fatigable units handle small forces. They are reserved for maximum-effort tasks.

QUESTION 30

Stem: Twitch of a single muscle fiber is called **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Fibrillation

Explanation: A **fibrillation** is the spontaneous twitch of a _single_ muscle fibre, recorded electromyographically as a small (~1 ms, ~50–500 µV) potential [Guyton ch.6]. Fibrillations are characteristic of **lower motor neurone (LMN) lesions** — denervated muscle fibres develop hypersensitivity to circulating ACh and discharge spontaneously. They are not visible at the surface but are detected by needle EMG. A **fasciculation** is the visible twitch of a _whole motor unit_ (all fibres innervated by one motor neurone) — visible through the skin and seen in motor-neurone disease and other LMN pathology. **Myoclonus** is a brief, involuntary contraction of a muscle or muscle group of central origin. **Jerk** typically refers to a tendon reflex response.

Hints:

1. Distinguish twitch of a single fibre from twitch of a whole motor unit.
2. The single-fibre phenomenon is detected only by EMG, not visible at the surface.
3. The term has a "fibre"-related root.

Distractors:

- "Fasciculation" — Reveals the misconception that fasciculation and fibrillation are interchangeable. Fasciculation is whole motor unit; fibrillation is single fibre.
- "Myoclonus" — Reveals the misconception that any muscle twitch is myoclonus. Myoclonus is a CNS-driven jerk of muscle groups.
- "Jerk" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 31

Stem: A scientist found that the latent period of muscle twitch is 10 ms and the contraction period is 40 ms. What is the tetanizing frequency (Hz)?

Correct answer: 25

Explanation: **Minimum tetanising frequency = 1 / contraction time** [Guyton ch.6]. The contraction time is the duration of the active shortening phase — given here as **40 ms = 0.04 s**. Therefore frequency = 1 / 0.04 = **25 Hz**. At this frequency, each successive stimulus arrives just as the previous twitch's contraction phase ends, producing fused (smooth) tetanus. The latent period (10 ms) is not added to the contraction time for this calculation — it is the conduction/coupling delay before contraction begins, and is irrelevant to the tetanising-frequency calculation. (Some textbooks prefer using the _total time including relaxation_ — that would give a lower minimum frequency for fully fused tetanus; the simpler "1/contraction time" formulation is standard at the MBBS level.)

Hints:

1. Recall the formula: minimum tetanising frequency = 1 / contraction time.
2. The latent period is conduction delay and is not part of the contraction phase.
3. 1 / 0.04 s = 25 Hz.

Distractors:

- "50" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "100" — Reveals the misconception that the latent period or other intervals are added wrongly to give an inverse of 10 ms.
- "75" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 32

Stem: Tetanic contraction is due to the accumulation of **\_\_** ions.

Correct answer: Ca2+

Explanation: **Tetanus** is the sustained contraction produced by repetitive, high-frequency motor neurone firing [Guyton ch.6]. With each AP, Ca²⁺ is released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum into the cytoplasm. If APs arrive faster than SERCA can pump Ca²⁺ back into the SR, **cytoplasmic [Ca²⁺] accumulates** and remains high enough throughout the inter-stimulus interval to maintain troponin C saturation. Cross-bridges therefore continue to cycle without interruption, producing fused tetanus. The accumulating ion is **Ca²⁺**.

Hints:

1. Recall the ion that triggers contraction at the troponin-tropomyosin level.
2. Tetanus persists because cytosolic levels of this ion remain high.
3. The same ion that triggers single twitches accumulates during tetanus.

Distractors:

- "Na+" — Reveals the misconception that Na⁺ accumulation drives tetanus. Na⁺ flux drives action potentials, not contraction.
- "K+" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Cl-" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 33

Stem: The function of the Golgi tendon organ is to **\_\_\_\_**

Correct answer: Sense changes in muscle tension

Explanation: The **Golgi tendon organ (GTO)** is a mechanoreceptor located at the _muscle-tendon junction_, in series with the muscle fibres [Guyton ch.6]. It senses **tension** generated in the muscle (force transmitted through the tendon), via Ib afferents (Aα). When the muscle contracts and develops tension, GTO afferents fire in proportion to the force, and at high tensions trigger inhibition of the homonymous α-motor neurone (the **inverse myotatic reflex** or autogenic inhibition) — protecting the muscle and tendon from injury. The **muscle spindle**, by contrast, is _parallel_ to the muscle fibres and senses _length_ and rate of length change. Hence the correct answer is (b) tension.

Hints:

1. Distinguish muscle spindle from Golgi tendon organ by their location relative to the muscle fibres.
2. Spindle: in parallel, senses length. GTO: in series, senses tension.
3. The receptor at the muscle-tendon junction senses force transmitted along the tendon.

Distractors:

- "Sense changes in muscle direction" — Reveals the misconception that the GTO is a directional sensor. It senses force, not direction.
- "Sense changes in position of the muscle" — Reveals the misconception that GTO is a position sensor. Position is signalled by spindles and joint receptors.
- "Sense changes in length of the muscle" — Reveals the misconception that GTO is a length sensor (the spindle is).
