QUESTION 1

Stem: Human cardiac muscle contains **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Both a and b

Explanation: Myosin heavy chain (MHC) in cardiac muscle exists as two isoforms: **α-MHC** (encoded by _MYH6_) — fast ATPase activity, predominant in atria and in small mammals — and **β-MHC** (encoded by _MYH7_) — slower ATPase, predominant in adult human ventricles [Guyton ch.9]. Both isoforms are expressed in human myocardium, with the proportion differing between chambers and shifting during disease (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy is associated with mutations in both genes; failing human ventricles show further shift toward β-MHC). Hence both α and β heavy chains are present in human cardiac muscle.

Hints:

1. Recall the two myosin heavy chain isoforms in cardiac muscle.
2. Both chambers of the human heart express both isoforms in different proportions.
3. The answer combines α-MHC (atrial-predominant) and β-MHC (ventricular-predominant).

Distractors:

- "Alpha heavy chain of Myosin" — Incomplete: α is present but so is β.
- "Beta heavy chain of Myosin" — Incomplete: β is present but so is α.
- "None" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Intercalated discs are seen in **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Cardiac muscle

Explanation: **Intercalated discs** are specialised junctional complexes found at the _end-to-end junctions_ between adjacent cardiac myocytes. They contain three components [Guyton ch.9]: (1) **fascia adherens** (anchoring actin filaments — homologue of the zonula adherens), (2) **desmosomes** (anchoring intermediate filaments — providing mechanical strength), and (3) **gap junctions** (connexin-43 channels — providing low-resistance electrical coupling, allowing the myocardium to function as a syncytium). Skeletal muscle cells are independent units (no cell-cell junctions); smooth muscle uses dense bodies and gap junctions but not the organised intercalated disc.

Hints:

1. Recall which muscle type behaves as a functional syncytium with low-resistance cell-cell coupling.
2. Intercalated discs combine adherens junctions, desmosomes and gap junctions at the end-to-end junctions of contractile cells.
3. The myocardium is the only muscle that contracts as a wave propagating cell-to-cell via this structure.

Distractors:

- "Skeletal muscle" — Reveals the misconception that skeletal muscle has cell-cell junctions. Skeletal muscle fibres are long syncytial cells but each is independently activated by its own NMJ.
- "Smooth muscle" — Reveals the misconception that smooth muscle organises into intercalated discs. Smooth muscle uses dense bodies and gap junctions.
- "All of the above" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 3

Stem: Activation of the sympathetic system increases the myocardial contractility of a patient. Which of the following is responsible for this positive inotropic effect?

Correct answer: Phosphorylation of Phospholamban

Explanation: Sympathetic activation releases noradrenaline at β₁-adrenergic receptors on cardiomyocytes [Guyton ch.9]. β₁ → Gαs → adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → activates protein kinase A (PKA). PKA phosphorylates several key targets: (1) **L-type Ca²⁺ channels** (greater Ca²⁺ entry per AP), (2) **ryanodine receptors** (more SR Ca²⁺ release), and crucially (3) **phospholamban (PLB)**. In its dephosphorylated state, PLB _inhibits_ SERCA2a (the SR Ca²⁺ ATPase). PKA-mediated **phosphorylation of phospholamban** _relieves_ this inhibition, accelerating Ca²⁺ uptake into the SR — increasing SR Ca²⁺ load (more Ca²⁺ available for the next contraction → larger contraction) and shortening relaxation (lusitropic effect). Hence sympathetic positive inotropy is mediated by PLB phosphorylation.

Hints:

1. Trace the β₁ adrenoceptor signalling cascade in the cardiomyocyte.
2. PKA phosphorylates several targets that increase Ca²⁺ available for contraction.
3. The inhibitor of SERCA in its dephosphorylated state is the SR-anchored protein modified by PKA.

Distractors:

- "Dephosphorylation of Phospholamban" — Reverses the modification. Dephosphorylated PLB _inhibits_ SERCA — slowing SR uptake.
- "Decreased levels of cAMP" — Reverses the second-messenger change. β₁ stimulation _raises_ cAMP.
- "Increased permeability of cell membrane" — Reveals the misconception that nonspecific permeability change drives inotropy. Specific channel and pump phosphorylation, not generalised permeability, mediate β-adrenergic effects.

QUESTION 4

Stem: Which among the following is the function of phospholamban?

Correct answer: Regulates calcium entry into sarcoplasmic reticulum

Explanation: **Phospholamban (PLB)** is a small (52-amino-acid) integral membrane protein of the cardiac SR membrane that functions as a regulatory inhibitor of **SERCA2a**, the Ca²⁺ ATPase that pumps Ca²⁺ from the cytosol back into the SR lumen during relaxation [Guyton ch.9]. In its dephosphorylated state, PLB binds and inhibits SERCA2a, slowing SR Ca²⁺ uptake. PKA-mediated phosphorylation of PLB at Ser16 (and CaMKII at Thr17) relieves this inhibition, accelerating Ca²⁺ re-uptake. Hence PLB regulates the _entry of Ca²⁺ back into the SR_ — option (c). It does not regulate the Na⁺-K⁺ pump, mitochondrial Ca²⁺ extrusion, or the actomyosin cross-bridge.

Hints:

1. Recall the molecular target of phospholamban.
2. PLB is a regulator of the SR Ca²⁺ pump, not the actomyosin machinery.
3. The function described is governance of Ca²⁺ flux into the SR.

Distractors:

- "Regulation of Na+-K+ pump" — Reveals the misconception that PLB regulates the surface Na⁺-K⁺ pump. PLB's target is SERCA on the SR.
- "Transport calcium out of mitochondria" — Reveals the misconception that PLB is mitochondrial. It is on the SR membrane.
- "Binds actin with myosin" — Reveals the misconception that PLB is part of the contractile machinery. It is a SR pump regulator.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Which of the following is not a feature of cardiac muscle?

Correct answer: Tetanization

Explanation: Cardiac muscle exhibits the **all-or-none phenomenon** at the level of the entire myocardium (because of low-resistance gap-junction coupling — every myocyte joins the syncytium); a **length-tension relationship** (the cellular basis of the **Frank-Starling law**, increased preload → increased stroke volume); and a distinctive **plateau potential** (~0.2 s) caused by L-type Ca²⁺ current balanced by delayed K⁺ rectifier closure [Guyton ch.9]. **Tetanization is not possible in cardiac muscle**, because the long absolute refractory period of the cardiac AP (~250 ms in ventricle) extends through nearly the entire mechanical contraction; a second stimulus cannot be applied early enough to generate summation [Guyton ch.9]. This is a critical safety feature — the heart must relax (and refill) between beats.

Hints:

1. Compare cardiac muscle properties with skeletal muscle properties.
2. The very long cardiac refractory period prevents one specific phenomenon that can occur in skeletal muscle.
3. The exception is the listed feature that requires summation of contractions.

Distractors:

- "All or none phenomenon" — Correctly true for the whole myocardium because of syncytial coupling.
- "Length-tension relationship" — Correctly true (Frank-Starling).
- "Plateau potential" — Correctly true; a defining feature of the cardiac AP.

QUESTION 6

Stem: The following graph displays which physiological law of the heart?

Correct answer: Starling Law

Explanation: The graph (not visible in the source `.txt` file) most likely depicts the **Frank-Starling relationship**: stroke volume (or stroke work) on the y-axis plotted against ventricular end-diastolic volume (or pressure / fibre length) on the x-axis [Guyton ch.9]. The curve rises with increasing preload up to an optimum, beyond which further stretching reduces output. The cellular basis is the length-tension relationship: at greater fibre length, troponin Ca²⁺ sensitivity rises and cross-bridge overlap is more favourable, so the heart pumps whatever blood returns to it. **Laplace's law** relates wall tension to pressure × radius / wall thickness (relevant to LVH and aneurysm). **Bainbridge** is the reflex tachycardia in response to atrial stretch. **Bell-Magendie** is the law of nervous-root segregation (dorsal sensory, ventral motor) — unrelated to the heart.

Hints:

1. Stroke volume vs end-diastolic volume defines a specific named cardiac law.
2. The law is named for two physiologists who established the cellular and whole-heart relationship.
3. The principle is "the heart pumps what it receives."

Distractors:

- "Laplace Law" — Reveals the misconception that the SV-EDV curve is Laplace. Laplace relates pressure-radius-tension, not preload to stroke volume.
- "Bainbridge Law" — Reveals the misconception that any cardiac law applies. Bainbridge is a heart-rate reflex, not a preload-stroke volume law.
- "Bell-Magendie law" — Reveals the misconception that any named law applies to the heart. Bell-Magendie governs dorsal/ventral nerve roots.

QUESTION 7

Stem: Muscles in hollow viscera behave as **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Single unit smooth muscle

Explanation: Smooth muscle is divided into two functional types [Guyton ch.8]: **single-unit (visceral, syncytial) smooth muscle** and **multi-unit smooth muscle**. **Single-unit smooth muscle** is the principal type in **hollow visceral organs** — gastrointestinal tract, ureter, urinary bladder, uterus, and most blood vessels. Its fibres are coupled by gap junctions, contracting as a coordinated mass with spontaneous slow waves and pacemaker activity. **Multi-unit smooth muscle** consists of fibres innervated individually with little or no cell-cell coupling — found in iris, ciliary muscle of the eye, piloerector muscles, and the larger arteries. The question's "hollow viscera" maps to single-unit smooth muscle.

Hints:

1. Distinguish single-unit (visceral, coupled) from multi-unit smooth muscle.
2. Hollow viscera depend on coordinated mass contraction — which type allows this?
3. The type with abundant gap junctions and pacemaker activity is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Multiunit smooth muscle" — Reveals the misconception that hollow viscera use individually innervated fibres. They use coupled single-unit muscle.
- "Both, depending on action" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "None" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 8

Stem: Which of the following is true regarding excitation-contraction coupling in smooth muscle?

Correct answer: Cellular calcium concentration is essential to cause contraction

Explanation: Smooth muscle EC coupling differs from skeletal/cardiac muscle in important ways [Guyton ch.8]: (1) **No troponin** — Ca²⁺ binds calmodulin instead (not troponin C). (2) Ca²⁺-calmodulin activates **myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK)**, which phosphorylates the **myosin regulatory light chain** (not actin) — exposing the cross-bridge cycle. (3) Ca²⁺ comes from both extracellular fluid (via L-type channels and receptor-operated channels) _and_ the SR (via IP₃ receptors); this dual sourcing means **cellular [Ca²⁺] (cytosolic) is essential** for contraction. (4) Sustained contraction is enabled by the **latch mechanism** (slow detachment of dephosphorylated cross-bridges), not simply by maintaining high [Ca²⁺]. Option (d) captures the universal truth.

Hints:

1. Compare the regulatory pathway of smooth muscle with skeletal muscle.
2. Troponin is absent in smooth muscle; calmodulin and MLCK take its place.
3. The ion essential for triggering smooth-muscle contraction is the same as in skeletal muscle.

Distractors:

- "Presence of troponin is essential" — Reveals the misconception that troponin is universal. Troponin is absent in smooth muscle.
- "Sustained contraction occurs with high calcium concentration" — Reveals the misconception that sustained smooth muscle contraction needs high Ca²⁺. The latch mechanism allows sustained tension at _low_ Ca²⁺ and low ATP cost.
- "Phosphorylation of actin is required for contraction" — Reveals the misconception that actin is phosphorylated. The myosin regulatory light chain is phosphorylated, not actin.

QUESTION 9

Stem: Calmodulin is mostly both structurally and functionally related to **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Troponin C

Explanation: **Calmodulin (CaM)** and **troponin C (TnC)** are members of the same superfamily of EF-hand Ca²⁺-binding proteins [Guyton ch.8]. Both have four EF-hand Ca²⁺-binding sites (CaM binds 4 Ca²⁺; TnC binds 4 in cardiac/skeletal isoforms but the Ca²⁺-regulated sites differ). Both undergo Ca²⁺-induced conformational changes that allow them to bind and activate downstream targets. Functionally: **TnC** in skeletal/cardiac muscle binds Ca²⁺ and shifts the troponin-tropomyosin complex to expose myosin-binding sites on actin; **CaM** in smooth muscle (and in many other Ca²⁺-signalled cells) binds Ca²⁺ and activates MLCK (and many other targets). They are functionally analogous Ca²⁺ sensors — CaM is essentially the smooth-muscle/general-cell version of TnC.

Hints:

1. Identify the structural protein family that both CaM and TnC belong to.
2. Both proteins are Ca²⁺ sensors with EF-hand domains.
3. CaM is the universal Ca²⁺ sensor; TnC is its specialised muscle-cell relative.

Distractors:

- "Actin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Myosin light chain kinase" — Reveals the misconception that calmodulin and MLCK are interchangeable. CaM is upstream of MLCK and _activates_ it; they are distinct proteins.
- "Tropomyosin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 10

Stem: A sustained contraction with little expenditure of energy is made possible by a latching mechanism in **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Smooth muscle

Explanation: The **latch mechanism** is unique to **smooth muscle** [Guyton ch.8]. Once Ca²⁺-calmodulin/MLCK has phosphorylated myosin and a contraction has been initiated, the cross-bridges enter a **dephosphorylated but still attached** ("latched") state. Latched cross-bridges have very slow detachment kinetics, so tension can be maintained for prolonged periods at a tiny fraction of the ATP cost of sustained skeletal-muscle contraction. This is the cellular basis for tonic vasoconstriction, sustained gut tone, and prolonged sphincteric closure — all without exhausting the muscle. Skeletal and cardiac muscle have rapid cross-bridge cycling and cannot latch.

Hints:

1. Recall the energy economics of sustained contraction across muscle types.
2. The latch mechanism is unique to one muscle type.
3. The muscle type capable of holding tension at low ATP cost is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Skeletal muscle" — Reveals the misconception that skeletal muscle latches. It cannot — sustained contraction requires continuous cross-bridge cycling and high ATP turnover.
- "Cardiac muscle" — Same misconception as (a).
- "All of the above" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 11

Stem: The most distinguishing feature between skeletal muscle and smooth muscle is **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Troponin

Explanation: Skeletal and smooth muscle both contain **actin**, **myosin** and **tropomyosin** in their thin and thick filaments [Guyton ch.6] [Guyton ch.8]. The defining molecular difference is that **smooth muscle lacks troponin** — its Ca²⁺-regulation pathway is fundamentally different, using calmodulin → MLCK → myosin light-chain phosphorylation. The presence or absence of troponin is therefore the most distinguishing molecular feature between the two muscle types. (See also Q5 of Chapter 7, which makes the same point with smooth muscle as the comparator.)

Hints:

1. Compare the molecular components of skeletal and smooth muscle thin filaments.
2. The Ca²⁺ regulatory protein of skeletal/cardiac muscle is absent in smooth muscle.
3. The protein not found in smooth muscle is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Tropomyosin" — Reveals the misconception that smooth muscle lacks tropomyosin. It contains tropomyosin (with caldesmon).
- "Myosin" — Reveals the misconception that the muscle types use different myosin types.
- "Actin" — Reveals the misconception that smooth muscle lacks actin.

QUESTION 12

Stem: Which of the following acts to increase the release of calcium ions from the endoplasmic reticulum of a smooth muscle?

Correct answer: Inositol Triphosphate (IP3)

Explanation: **Inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP₃)** is generated by phospholipase C (PLC)-mediated cleavage of membrane PIP₂ downstream of Gαq-coupled receptors (e.g. α₁-adrenergic, M₃ muscarinic, oxytocin) [Guyton ch.8]. IP₃ diffuses to the SR/ER membrane, binds **IP₃ receptors (IP₃R)** — Ca²⁺-release channels — and triggers Ca²⁺ release from the SR into the cytosol. This raises [Ca²⁺]ᵢ, activates calmodulin-MLCK and contraction. **Calsequestrin** is the Ca²⁺-binding protein _inside_ the SR lumen — a Ca²⁺ buffer/storage protein, not a release trigger. **Diacylglycerol (DAG)** is the membrane-bound co-product of PIP₂ hydrolysis that activates protein kinase C, not Ca²⁺ release. **1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol (calcitriol)** is the active vitamin D metabolite — relevant to Ca²⁺ absorption from gut and bone, not to acute SR Ca²⁺ release.

Hints:

1. Recall the second messenger that opens SR Ca²⁺-release channels in smooth muscle.
2. PLC generates two second messengers from PIP₂; identify which one binds the SR/ER receptor.
3. The water-soluble cleavage product is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Calsequestrin" — Reveals the misconception that calsequestrin is a release trigger. It is the intraluminal Ca²⁺ buffer.
- "Diacylglycerol" — Reveals the misconception that DAG triggers Ca²⁺ release. DAG activates PKC, not Ca²⁺ release.
- "1, 25 di hydroxy cholecalciferol" — Reveals the misconception that the vitamin D system controls acute smooth-muscle Ca²⁺. Calcitriol affects gut and bone Ca²⁺ over hours, not SR release.

QUESTION 13

Stem: Which of the following statements regarding smooth muscle is false?

Correct answer: Sympathetic stimulation produces excitatory membrane potentials

Explanation: (a) **Unitary (visceral) smooth muscle has an unstable membrane potential** with slow waves and pacemaker activity that gives rise to spontaneous contractions — true [Guyton ch.8]. (b) Smooth muscle has **dense bodies** (cytoplasmic α-actinin–containing structures and membrane-associated dense plaques) that anchor actin filaments instead of organised Z lines — true. (c) **Smooth muscle exhibits a more variable length-tension relation** than skeletal muscle, allowing it to develop tension over a wide range of lengths (important for hollow organs that vary in volume) — true. (d) **Sympathetic stimulation does NOT uniformly produce excitatory potentials in smooth muscle** — it depends on which adrenergic receptor predominates: α₁-adrenergic on vascular smooth muscle is excitatory (vasoconstriction), but β₂-adrenergic on bronchial and uterine smooth muscle is _inhibitory_ (relaxation). Hence the categorical claim in (d) is false.

Hints:

1. Test each claim against the variable autonomic responses of smooth muscle.
2. The autonomic effect on smooth muscle depends on the receptor subtype, not on the autonomic division alone.
3. The exception over-generalises that all sympathetic effects on smooth muscle are excitatory.

Distractors:

- "Unitary smooth muscle has an unstable membrane potential" — Correctly true.
- "Dense Bodies are present instead of Z lines" — Correctly true.
- "It can exert a variable tension at any given length" — Correctly true.
