QUESTION 1

Stem: Homeostasis is called as dynamic equilibrium because**\_**

Correct answer: Body maintains an internal balance within fluctuating limits

Explanation: Homeostasis refers to the maintenance of nearly constant conditions in the internal environment, but the regulated variables are not held at an unchanging fixed value. Instead they oscillate within a narrow physiological range around a reference set point, and the set point itself can shift transiently (e.g. during exercise, fever or sleep) [Guyton ch.1] [Berne & Levy ch.2]. Negative feedback loops continually correct deviations, so the steady state is _dynamic_ — the result of opposing influxes and effluxes that are continuously balanced rather than statically equal [Berne & Levy ch.2]. Pulmonary ventilation, arterial pressure, plasma osmolality and body temperature all illustrate this: each varies moment-to-moment yet remains within tight limits [Guyton ch.1]. Calling homeostasis a _dynamic_ equilibrium therefore captures both the constancy of the average value and the ongoing flux that produces it.

Hints:

1. Distinguish between values that are held perfectly fixed and values that oscillate around a reference.
2. Consider what "dynamic" adds to the word "equilibrium" — what is moving even though the overall state appears constant?
3. The body's regulated variables are kept within a permitted range, not at a single unchanging number.

Distractors:

- "All variables are given equal importance" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Can achieve a constancy in extreme environmental challenge" — Reveals the misconception that homeostatic systems can buffer any environmental challenge. In reality, homeostatic mechanisms have a finite operating range; in extreme environments (severe heat, prolonged hypoxia, massive blood loss) they decompensate [Guyton ch.1].
- "The set-point for all homeostatic variables is always fixed" — Reveals the misconception that set points are immutable. Set points are themselves regulated and shift physiologically (e.g. circadian temperature variation, fever raising the thermal set point, altitude raising the ventilatory set point) [Berne & Levy ch.2].

QUESTION 2

Stem: Which is a component of homeostatic control system?

Correct answer: All of the above

Explanation: Every homeostatic negative-feedback loop comprises a controlled variable, a sensor (receptor) that detects deviation from the set point, an integrating centre that compares the sensed value with the reference, and an effector that produces the response which restores the variable [Guyton ch.1] [Berne & Levy ch.2]. The triggering disturbance — the _stimulus_ — is what the receptor detects; without a stimulus, no feedback is engaged. In the baroreceptor reflex, for example, a fall in arterial pressure (stimulus) activates carotid and aortic baroreceptors (receptors), signals are integrated in the medulla, and autonomic outflow alters cardiac output and vascular tone (effectors) [Guyton ch.1]. Because stimulus, receptor and effector are each indispensable elements of the loop, all three are correct.

Hints:

1. Trace a feedback loop from beginning to end and list every distinct functional element it requires.
2. A control system needs something to detect, something to do the detecting, and something to act.
3. Examine each option individually — if every one is true, the combined option is the only complete answer.

Distractors:

- "Stimulus" — Incomplete; reveals the misconception that the disturbance alone defines the control system, ignoring the sensor and effector.
- "Receptor" — Incomplete; reveals the misconception that detection equals regulation. Detection without an effector limb produces no correction.
- "Effector" — Incomplete; an effector without a sensing arm is open-loop, not feedback-regulated.

QUESTION 3

Stem: Feedback regulation is seen in**\_**

Correct answer: The increased heart rate in response to a decrease in blood pressure

Explanation: Feedback regulation, by definition, requires that a change in a regulated variable has _already occurred_ and is _sensed_ before the corrective response is generated [Guyton ch.1] [Berne & Levy ch.2]. Option (d) describes the baroreceptor reflex: a fall in arterial pressure unloads carotid and aortic baroreceptors, the medullary cardiovascular centres reduce parasympathetic and increase sympathetic outflow, and heart rate rises to restore mean arterial pressure (MAP) [Guyton ch.1]. The other options are _feedforward_ (anticipatory) responses — they occur in advance of, rather than in response to, the disturbance. Anticipatory tachycardia at the start of exercise, the cephalic phase of digestion triggered by sight or smell, and pre-absorptive insulin release driven by incretins or vagal cues all initiate effector activity before the regulated variable has actually deviated [Berne & Levy ch.2] [Ganong ch.1].

Hints:

1. Differentiate responses that begin _after_ a sensed change from those that begin _before_ the change occurs.
2. Anticipatory responses are feedforward; corrective responses to a measured deviation are feedback.
3. Identify which option describes a sensor detecting an actual deviation in a regulated variable.

Distractors:

- "The increased heart rate that occurs in an athlete just before a sprint begins" — Reveals the misconception that any pre-emptive cardiovascular adjustment is a feedback response. Pre-sprint tachycardia is a _feedforward_ response driven by cortical/limbic input to autonomic centres before any haemodynamic deviation has occurred [Berne & Levy ch.2].
- "Smell of food triggers secretion of salivary and gastric juices" — Reveals the misconception that the cephalic phase of digestion is a feedback loop. It is a feedforward, vagally mediated anticipatory response to sensory cues, occurring before food enters the gut [Berne & Levy ch.2].
- "An increase in insulin before glucose absorption has raised blood glucose levels" — Reveals the misconception that pre-absorptive insulin release is a negative-feedback correction of glycaemia. It is feedforward — driven by gut-derived incretins (GLP-1, GIP) and vagal cues — and occurs before plasma glucose has risen [Ganong ch.1].

QUESTION 4

Stem: Positive feedback is seen in all except**\_**

Correct answer: Gastric secretion

Explanation: Positive feedback amplifies an initial change rather than opposing it. In the pre-ovulatory **luteinising hormone (LH) surge**, rising oestradiol from the dominant follicle reverses its usual inhibitory effect on the hypothalamic–pituitary axis and stimulates further LH release, culminating in ovulation [Ganong ch.1]. In the **action potential**, depolarisation opens voltage-gated Na⁺ channels; the resulting Na⁺ influx depolarises the membrane further, opening more channels — an explicit positive-feedback loop highlighted by Guyton as "another important use of positive feedback…the generation of nerve signals" [Guyton ch.1]. In **thrombus formation**, activated clotting factors (e.g. thrombin) catalyse the activation of more clotting factors until the breach is sealed [Guyton ch.1]. **Gastric secretion** as a whole is dominated by _negative feedback_: low antral pH inhibits gastrin release from G-cells, which in turn limits acid output, providing a self-limiting loop. It is therefore the exception.

Hints:

1. Recall the defining feature of positive feedback — a response that amplifies its own initiating stimulus.
2. Three of the listed processes are classical regenerative loops studied in physiology; one is regulated chiefly by self-limiting inhibition.
3. Consider which process _terminates itself_ through inhibition rather than escalating until a structural endpoint (ovulation, propagated action potential, clot) is reached.

Distractors:

- "LH surge" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Action potential" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Thrombus formation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Localised positive-feedback elements *do* occur within gastric secretion (e.g. pepsin autocatalytically activates more pepsinogen, and gastrin → ECL-cell histamine → parietal cell stimulation has self-reinforcing components). The question relies on the conventional teaching that "gastric secretion" as a global process is negative-feedback regulated. If a student answers (c) on this conventional reasoning the answer key is satisfied, but a strictly molecular reading could defend any of the options as containing positive-feedback steps.]

QUESTION 5

Stem: On infusion of a large volume of blood, the MAP rises from 100 mmHg to 175 mmHg. Due to the baroreceptor feedback mechanism, the MAP falls down to 125 mmHg. Calculate the gain of the feedback system.

Correct answer: -2

Explanation: The gain of a negative-feedback control system quantifies how effective the loop is at opposing a disturbance, and is defined as Gain = Correction / Error [Guyton ch.1]. The _uncontrolled_ rise in mean arterial pressure (MAP) is from 100 mmHg to 175 mmHg — a +75 mmHg disturbance. With the baroreceptor reflex active, MAP rises only to 125 mmHg, leaving an _error_ of +25 mmHg above normal. The _correction_ produced by the loop is the difference between the uncontrolled and the controlled outcome: 125 − 175 = −50 mmHg. Therefore Gain = −50 / +25 = **−2**. The negative sign reflects that the response opposes the disturbance, and the magnitude (2) means the disturbance is reduced to one-third of what it would otherwise be (uncontrolled rise 75 → controlled rise 25). Guyton uses this exact baroreceptor scenario to illustrate the gain calculation [Guyton ch.1].

Hints:

1. Write down the formula for gain of a negative-feedback control system before plugging in numbers.
2. Distinguish three quantities: the disturbance, the residual error, and the correction produced by the loop.
3. Correction is measured against the _uncontrolled_ outcome, not the original baseline.

Distractors:

- "-3" — Reveals the misconception that correction is calculated against the baseline (100 mmHg) rather than the uncontrolled outcome (175 mmHg). A student computing −75/+25 gets −3.
- "+2" — Reveals the misconception that gain magnitude alone is reported, ignoring the negative sign that distinguishes negative from positive feedback.
- "+3" — Combines both errors above (wrong reference and wrong sign).

QUESTION 6

Stem: Plasma membrane in eukaryotes is made up of all except \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Triglycerides

Explanation: The eukaryotic plasma membrane is a fluid lipid bilayer composed predominantly of three classes of lipids: glycerophospholipids (notably phosphatidylcholine, also called **lecithin**, plus phosphatidylethanolamine, phosphatidylserine and phosphatidylinositol), sphingolipids (chiefly **sphingomyelin** and glycosphingolipids), and **cholesterol** [Guyton ch.2] [Boron ch.2] [Berne & Levy ch.1]. By mass, an erythrocyte membrane is approximately 25% phospholipid, 13% cholesterol and 4% other lipids, plus 55% protein and 3% carbohydrate [Guyton ch.2]. **Triglycerides** are neutral storage lipids — three fatty acids esterified to glycerol with no polar head group — and are not amphipathic, so they cannot form bilayers. Cells store triglycerides as cytoplasmic lipid droplets (most abundantly in adipocytes), not in the plasma membrane [Guyton ch.2].

Hints:

1. Consider what lipid property is required to assemble a stable bilayer at an aqueous interface.
2. Three of the listed lipids are amphipathic; one is a neutral lipid lacking a polar head.
3. Identify the neutral lipid that is stored cytoplasmically rather than incorporated into membranes.

Distractors:

- "Cholesterol" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Lecithin" — Reveals the misconception that the trivial name "lecithin" denotes something other than phosphatidylcholine. Students unfamiliar with the synonym may exclude it.
- "Sphingomyelin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 7

Stem: Membrane fluidity is increased by \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Linoleic acid

Explanation: Membrane fluidity depends largely on how tightly the fatty-acyl chains of phospholipids pack against one another. _Saturated_ chains are straight and stack closely through extensive van der Waals interactions, raising the gel-to-fluid transition temperature and decreasing fluidity at body temperature [Boron ch.2]. _Cis-unsaturated_ chains contain one or more rigid kinks at each double bond; these kinks prevent close packing, lower the transition temperature, and increase fluidity [Boron ch.2]. **Linoleic acid** (18:2, ω-6) is polyunsaturated with two cis double bonds and is the only polyunsaturated option. **Stearic acid** (18:0), **palmitic acid** (16:0) and **myristic acid** (14:0) are all saturated; among saturated chains, longer length further increases packing and _reduces_ fluidity. Therefore linoleic acid is the option that raises fluidity. Cholesterol modulates fluidity bidirectionally: at body temperature it rigidifies, at lower temperatures it prevents crystallisation [Boron ch.2] [Guyton ch.2].

Hints:

1. Recall how the geometry of a fatty-acyl chain determines how tightly chains pack in the bilayer.
2. Cis double bonds introduce kinks; chain length and saturation determine packing density.
3. Three of the four fatty acids are saturated; identify the polyunsaturated one.

Distractors:

- "Stearic acid" — Reveals the misconception that long-chain fatty acids increase fluidity. Length increases hydrophobic interactions and _reduces_ fluidity when saturated.
- "Palmitic acid" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Myristic acid" — Reveals the misconception that shorter chain length alone is sufficient to raise fluidity. Shorter saturated chains do reduce transition temperature relative to longer saturated chains, but unsaturation has a far larger effect — and myristic acid is still saturated.

QUESTION 8

Stem: Which of the following is an example of peripheral protein?

Correct answer: Cell surface receptors

Explanation: Membrane proteins are classified as **integral** (embedded in or spanning the bilayer, removable only by detergents that disrupt the bilayer) or **peripheral** (loosely attached to the membrane via electrostatic or protein–protein interactions, removable by mild treatments such as high-salt washes) [Guyton ch.2] [Boron ch.2]. **Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase** is a multi-pass integral transporter, the **insulin receptor** is a single-pass integral receptor tyrosine kinase, and **G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs)** are seven-transmembrane integral proteins — none is peripheral [Boron ch.2]. The category "cell surface receptors" is heterogeneous and includes lipid-anchored species such as glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI)-linked receptors, which are tethered to the outer leaflet via a covalent lipid anchor and are conventionally classified as peripheral because they do not span the bilayer [Boron ch.2]. By elimination from the other three definitively integral options, (d) is the intended answer.

Hints:

1. Define peripheral vs integral proteins by their relationship to the lipid bilayer.
2. Three of the options are well-known transmembrane proteins; one is a generic class that can include non-spanning, lipid-anchored members.
3. The peripheral category includes lipid-anchored receptors that do not cross the bilayer.

Distractors:

- "Sodium potassium ATPase pump" — Reveals the misconception that ATPases lie on the cytoplasmic surface as peripheral proteins. Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase is a transmembrane pump with multiple membrane-spanning helices [Guyton ch.2] [Costanzo ch.1].
- "Insulin receptor" — Reveals the misconception that ligand-binding receptors are merely "stuck on" the membrane. Insulin receptor crosses the bilayer once with a tyrosine-kinase cytoplasmic domain.
- "G-Protein coupled receptors" — Reveals the misconception that GPCRs are peripheral because they couple to peripheral G-proteins. The receptor itself is a seven-transmembrane integral protein; only the heterotrimeric G-protein on its cytoplasmic side is peripheral.

[ANSWER REVIEW: This item is poorly constructed. None of the four options is unambiguously a peripheral protein in standard usage. The stated answer (d) is defensible only if "cell surface receptors" is interpreted as encompassing GPI-anchored or lipid-anchored receptors. Classical peripheral-protein examples (spectrin, ankyrin, G-protein α/βγ subunits, the Gα subunit) are not among the options. QUESTION 9

Stem: Which of the following membranes has the highest protein content per gram tissue?

Correct answer: Inner mitochondrial membrane

Explanation: Biological membranes vary widely in their protein-to-lipid mass ratio depending on function. The **inner mitochondrial membrane** is the most protein-rich membrane in the cell — its surface area is enormously expanded by the cristae, and it is densely studded with the four electron-transport-chain complexes and ATP synthase (complex V), as well as a large complement of metabolite carriers [Boron ch.2] [Guyton ch.2] [Ganong ch.2]. By mass it is approximately 75% protein. The **outer mitochondrial membrane** is comparatively protein-poor and freely permeable to small solutes via porins. The **sarcoplasmic reticulum** is enriched in Ca²⁺-ATPase but has a lower overall protein density than the inner mitochondrial membrane. The **myelin sheath** is the most lipid-rich membrane in the body (~80% lipid by mass), reflecting its role as an electrical insulator rather than a metabolic interface [Boron ch.2].

Hints:

1. Membrane protein content reflects functional density: insulating membranes have less protein, metabolically active membranes have more.
2. Where in the cell does oxidative phosphorylation occur? That membrane houses dozens of large protein complexes per square micrometre.
3. Compare insulators (lipid-dominated) with energy-transducing membranes (protein-dominated).

Distractors:

- "Myelin sheath" — Reveals the misconception that any specialised membrane is necessarily protein-rich. Myelin is the _most lipid-rich_ membrane in the body, structured for electrical insulation [Boron ch.2].
- "Outer mitochondrial membrane" — Reveals the misconception that "mitochondrial membrane" is a single entity. The two mitochondrial membranes have very different compositions; the outer is lipid-rich, the inner is protein-rich.
- "Sarcoplasmic reticulum" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

[NEEDS VERIFICATION: The specific quantitative claim "approximately 75% protein by mass" for the inner mitochondrial membrane is the standard textbook figure but I have not located the explicit number in the attached chapter extracts of Guyton, Boron or Ganong. The qualitative ranking (inner mitochondrial highest, myelin lowest) is firmly established in those sources.]

QUESTION 10

Stem: The sequence of vesicular transport in a cell is

Correct answer: ER - Cis - Trans - Cell membrane

Explanation: Newly synthesised proteins enter the secretory pathway co-translationally at the rough endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where N-linked glycosylation begins. Transport vesicles bud from the ER and fuse with the **cis face** of the Golgi apparatus, the entry compartment closest to the ER [Guyton ch.2] [Boron ch.2]. Cargo then passes sequentially through the medial cisterna and onward to the **trans face** and _trans_-Golgi network (TGN), where each cisterna catalyses distinct post-translational modifications (terminal glycosylation, proteolytic processing, sorting) [Boron ch.2]. From the TGN, secretory vesicles bud and traffic to the **plasma (cell) membrane** for constitutive or regulated exocytosis [Guyton ch.2]. Mitochondria are not part of the secretory pathway — mitochondrial proteins are imported post-translationally from the cytosol via dedicated translocases (TOM/TIM), so options ending at "mitochondria" describe a non-existent route. The directionality (cis → trans, never trans → cis) is fixed by the maturational organisation of the Golgi.

Hints:

1. Trace a newly synthesised secretory protein from its ribosome to its destination outside the cell.
2. Cargo enters the Golgi from the side facing the ER and exits from the opposite side.
3. Mitochondrial proteins are imported by a different mechanism altogether — they do not travel via the Golgi.

Distractors:

- "ER - Trans - Cis - Mitochondria" — Reveals two misconceptions simultaneously: that Golgi cisternae are entered from the _trans_ face and that mitochondria receive Golgi-derived vesicles.
- "ER - Cis - Trans - Mitochondria" — Correct Golgi polarity but reveals the misconception that mitochondria are the secretory-pathway endpoint. Mitochondrial proteins are imported via TOM/TIM, not Golgi vesicles [Boron ch.2].
- "ER - Trans - Cis - Cell membrane" — Correct overall destination but reverses Golgi polarity. Cargo always enters the cis face and exits the trans face.

QUESTION 11

Stem: All of the following are enzyme markers of mitochondria except \_\_\_\_

Correct answer: 5'-nucleotidase

Explanation: Subcellular fractionation studies use organelle-specific marker enzymes to identify which fractions contain which compartment. **ATP synthase** (complex V) is embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane and is one of the defining markers of mitochondria [Ganong ch.2]. **Glutamate dehydrogenase** is localised almost exclusively to the mitochondrial matrix, where it catalyses the reversible conversion of glutamate to α-ketoglutarate. The **mitochondrial isoform of creatine kinase (CK-Mt)** sits in the intermembrane space and couples oxidative phosphorylation to phosphocreatine synthesis. **5'-Nucleotidase**, by contrast, is a classical _plasma-membrane_ marker: it hydrolyses 5'-nucleoside monophosphates and is anchored to the outer leaflet of the cell membrane via a glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) tail, so it co-fractionates with the plasma membrane, not with mitochondria.

Hints:

1. Marker enzymes are tools for assigning a fraction to an organelle — each compartment has signature enzymes.
2. Three of the four options are mitochondrial; one is associated with the plasma membrane.
3. The exception is a GPI-anchored ectoenzyme that hydrolyses nucleotide monophosphates.

Distractors:

- "ATP synthase" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Glutamate dehydrogenase" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Creatine kinase" — Reveals the misconception that creatine kinase is purely cytosolic. The CK isozyme family includes a dedicated mitochondrial form (CK-Mt) localised to the intermembrane space.

QUESTION 12

Stem: Which of the following will test negative for all mitochondrial markers?

Correct answer: Mature red blood cells

Explanation: As an erythroid progenitor matures into a circulating red blood cell (RBC), it sequentially loses its nucleus and then its remaining cytoplasmic organelles. At the reticulocyte stage the cell still contains "remnants of the Golgi apparatus, mitochondria, and a few other cytoplasmic organelles", and these residual organelles are cleared during the final 1–2 days of maturation, after which the cell becomes a **mature erythrocyte** with no nucleus, no mitochondria and no ribosomes [Guyton ch.33]. Mature RBCs therefore generate ATP exclusively by anaerobic glycolysis and the pentose phosphate pathway and would test negative for every mitochondrial marker. **Spermatozoa** retain a tightly packed mitochondrial sheath in the midpiece that powers flagellar motility, **ova** are notably mitochondria-rich (the maternal contribution of mitochondrial DNA), and **white blood cells** retain a full complement of organelles including mitochondria.

Hints:

1. Identify which mature human cell undergoes programmed loss of organelles during differentiation.
2. Three of the listed cells require mitochondrial ATP for their function (motility, fertilisation, immune effector activity); one relies entirely on glycolysis.
3. Recall the maturation sequence from reticulocyte to circulating erythrocyte and what is lost at the final step.

Distractors:

- "Spermatozoa" — Reveals the misconception that small motile cells must have shed their organelles. Spermatozoa concentrate mitochondria into the midpiece sheath to power axonemal beating.
- "Ova" — Reveals the misconception that the female gamete is metabolically quiescent. Ova are exceptionally mitochondria-rich and are the source of all mitochondrial DNA inherited by the offspring.
- "White blood cells" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 13

Stem: Which is the characteristic enzyme present in peroxisome?

Correct answer: Catalase

Explanation: Peroxisomes are single-membrane organelles that house oxidative reactions producing hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂). They contain a battery of FAD-dependent oxidases that combine molecular oxygen with substrate-derived hydrogens to generate H₂O₂, which is then immediately destroyed by **catalase** — present in such abundance that it serves as the defining marker enzyme of peroxisomes [Guyton ch.2]. Catalase converts 2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂, preventing peroxide-mediated cytotoxicity. Other major peroxisomal functions include β-oxidation of very-long-chain fatty acids and the catabolism of about half of ingested ethanol to acetaldehyde in hepatocytes [Guyton ch.2]. **Arylsulfatases**, **ribonuclease** and **glycosidases** are characteristic _lysosomal_ hydrolases active at acid pH, not peroxisomal enzymes.

Hints:

1. Peroxisomes are named for the substance they generate as a by-product of oxidation.
2. The defining marker enzyme of an organelle handles its hallmark biochemical hazard.
3. Three of the listed enzymes are acid hydrolases of a different organelle; the fourth degrades H₂O₂.

Distractors:

- "Arylsulfatases" — Reveals the misconception that all hydrolytic/degradative organelles are equivalent. Arylsulfatases are lysosomal hydrolases (their deficiency causes metachromatic leukodystrophy).
- "Ribonuclease" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming. (Some isoforms are lysosomal; pancreatic RNase is secreted.)
- "Glycosidases" — Reveals the misconception that all sugar-cleaving enzymes are organelle-specific markers. Glycosidases are lysosomal hydrolases (their deficiencies cause lysosomal storage disorders such as Gaucher's, Pompe's and Tay–Sachs disease).

QUESTION 14

Stem: Gap junctions are found in all except **\_\_\_\_**.

Correct answer: Skeletal muscle

Explanation: Gap junctions are arrays of paired connexon hemichannels that connect the cytoplasm of adjacent cells, permitting passage of ions and small molecules (≤~1.2 kDa) and providing electrical coupling [Boron ch.2]. They are essential in tissues that require coordinated, syncytial activity. **Cardiac muscle** uses gap junctions in the intercalated discs to propagate the action potential cell-to-cell, allowing the myocardium to behave as a functional syncytium [Boron ch.2]. **Single-unit (visceral) smooth muscle** is similarly electrically coupled by gap junctions, enabling waves of contraction. **Neurons** can be coupled by gap junctions at electrical synapses, which mediate rapid, bidirectional signalling in the retina, certain brainstem nuclei and developing networks [Boron ch.2]. **Skeletal muscle fibres** are _not_ electrically coupled to one another — each fibre is independently activated by its own neuromuscular junction via a dedicated motor axon, and there are no gap junctions between adult mature fibres. Hence skeletal muscle is the exception.

Hints:

1. Identify which tissues need to behave as electrical syncytia and which are activated by individual nerve endings.
2. The myocardium and visceral smooth muscle propagate excitation cell-to-cell; what propagates excitation in skeletal muscle?
3. Skeletal muscle fibres receive one-to-one neural input; coupling between fibres would defeat motor unit recruitment.

Distractors:

- "Neurons" — Reveals the misconception that all neuronal communication is purely chemical (synaptic). Electrical synapses formed by gap junctions exist throughout the nervous system [Boron ch.2].
- "Cardiac muscle" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Smooth muscle" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 15

Stem: Tight junctions are not seen in **\_\_\_\_**.

Correct answer: Cardiac muscle

Explanation: Tight junctions (zonulae occludentes) are formed by claudins, occludin and junctional adhesion molecule (JAM) and create a paracellular permeability barrier that seals adjacent epithelial cells [Boron ch.2]. They are characteristic of epithelia that must establish distinct fluid compartments. **Renal tubules** rely on tight junctions to distinguish luminal urine from interstitial fluid and to determine paracellular permeability to ions such as Cl⁻ [Boron ch.2]. **Sertoli cells** form the blood-testis barrier through tight junctions that segregate developing germ cells from circulating immune cells. The **choroid plexus** epithelium uses tight junctions to form the blood–cerebrospinal-fluid barrier. **Cardiac muscle** cells, by contrast, are joined at intercalated discs comprising fascia adherens, desmosomes and gap junctions — but _not_ tight junctions, because the myocardium is not an epithelial barrier and does not need to seal one fluid compartment from another.

Hints:

1. Tight junctions are a defining feature of barrier-forming epithelia and certain blood–tissue barriers.
2. Three options are barrier epithelia (renal, blood–testis, blood–CSF); one is a contractile syncytium.
3. Identify the tissue whose intercellular junctions are organised for mechanical coupling and electrical conduction rather than for sealing fluid compartments.

Distractors:

- "Renal tubules" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Sertoli cells" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Choroid plexus" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 16

Stem: Which of the following is not a cell adhesion molecule (CAM)?

Correct answer: Spectrin

Explanation: Cell adhesion molecules are transmembrane (or lipid-anchored) glycoproteins that mediate binding between cells, or between cells and the extracellular matrix. The four major CAM superfamilies are **cadherins** (Ca²⁺-dependent cell-cell adhesion at adherens junctions and desmosomes), **integrins** (heterodimeric receptors that bind extracellular-matrix ligands such as fibronectin, laminin and collagen), **selectins** (carbohydrate-binding CAMs in leucocyte rolling) and the immunoglobulin superfamily (e.g. N-CAM, ICAM) [Boron ch.2] [Ganong ch.2]. **Spectrin** is _not_ a CAM. It is a heterotetrameric _cytoskeletal_ protein that, together with actin, ankyrin and band 4.1, forms the membrane-skeletal lattice on the cytoplasmic face of the erythrocyte plasma membrane and provides structural support for the discoid shape [Boron ch.2]. Mutations in spectrin cause hereditary spherocytosis.

Hints:

1. List the major cell adhesion molecule families and their characteristic binding partners.
2. Three of the options are membrane proteins that physically adhere cells to other cells or to matrix; one is a cytoskeletal scaffold.
3. The exception lies on the cytoplasmic side of the membrane, not in the extracellular space.

Distractors:

- "Integrin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Selectin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Cadherin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 17

Stem: Identify the incorrectly matched pair of intercellular connections and their structural units

Correct answer: Gap junctions - Cadherins

Explanation: Each intercellular junction has a defining transmembrane protein. **Desmosomes** are spot adhesions whose transmembrane core proteins are the cadherin-family members **desmoglein** and desmocollin, linked via plakoglobin and desmoplakin to intermediate filaments. **Hemidesmosomes** anchor epithelial cells to the underlying basement membrane and use **α6β4 integrin** as their transmembrane core, linking laminin in the basal lamina to keratin intermediate filaments. **Zona occludens** (tight junctions) use **claudins**, occludin and JAM as their sealing transmembrane proteins, with cytoplasmic ZO-1 anchoring them to the actin cytoskeleton [Boron ch.2]. **Gap junctions**, however, are formed by hexameric **connexins** that assemble into connexons, with paired connexons from adjacent cells forming the channel — they are _not_ built from cadherins [Boron ch.2]. Cadherins instead serve adherens junctions and desmosomes. Hence option (b) is the incorrectly matched pair.

Hints:

1. Each junctional class has a signature transmembrane component — recall the four named in the options.
2. Cadherins belong to a calcium-dependent adhesion family used at adherens junctions and desmosomes, not at communicating channels.
3. The protein that builds water-filled cell-to-cell channels for ions and small molecules is named for its function in _connecting_.

QUESTION 18

Stem: Integrin connects with:

Correct answer: Fibronectin

Explanation: **Integrins** are heterodimeric (αβ) transmembrane receptors that link the extracellular matrix to the intracellular actin cytoskeleton, transducing mechanical and biochemical signals bidirectionally [Boron ch.2]. Their canonical extracellular ligands are matrix glycoproteins, especially **fibronectin**, laminin and collagen, often via recognition of the RGD (Arg-Gly-Asp) tripeptide sequence [Boron ch.2] [Ganong ch.2]. Although integrins also bind vitronectin (vitronectin is recognised by αvβ3 and a few other integrin subtypes), the textbook-level association most strongly emphasised is with **fibronectin**, which is the prototype integrin ligand and the basis of focal adhesions. Cadherins mediate cadherin-cadherin homotypic binding at adherens junctions (not integrin-cadherin binding), and selectins bind sialylated carbohydrate ligands on leucocytes, not integrins directly.

Hints:

1. Integrins straddle the cell membrane and link the cytoskeleton to a partner protein outside the cell.
2. The classical extracellular partner is the prototype matrix glycoprotein bearing the RGD motif.
3. Identify the matrix protein most often paired with integrin in standard physiology and cell biology teaching.

- "Cadherin" — Reveals the misconception that all transmembrane adhesion proteins bind one another. Cadherins bind other cadherins on adjacent cells, not integrins.
- "Vitronectin" — Reveals the misconception that vitronectin is the _prototype_ integrin ligand. Some integrins do bind vitronectin (αvβ3 in particular), but fibronectin is the canonical RGD-containing ligand referenced in standard physiology texts.
- "Selectin" — Reveals the misconception that integrins and selectins are interchangeable adhesion partners. They function in sequence (selectin-mediated rolling precedes integrin-mediated firm adhesion in leucocyte transmigration), but they do not bind each other directly.

QUESTION 19

Stem: The force-generating proteins are

Correct answer: Dynein and kinesin

Explanation: Three superfamilies of molecular motor proteins generate mechanical force in eukaryotic cells: **myosins** (operating on actin filaments), **kinesins** (operating on microtubules, predominantly anterograde) and **dyneins** (operating on microtubules, retrograde and ciliary/flagellar) [Ganong ch.2] [Boron ch.2]. All three hydrolyse ATP to power directed movement. QUESTION 20

Stem: An absent dynein arm in cilia can lead to all of the following except:

Correct answer: Ciliocytophthoria

Explanation: Motile cilia possess a 9+2 axoneme in which axonemal **dynein arms** project from each outer microtubule doublet and hydrolyse ATP to slide adjacent doublets, producing the ciliary beat [Guyton ch.2] [Ganong ch.2]. Loss of dynein arms causes **primary ciliary dyskinesia (PCD)**: cilia are immotile or dyskinetic. Impaired mucociliary clearance of the upper and lower airways manifests as **chronic sinusitis**, recurrent otitis media and **bronchiectasis** [Ganong ch.2]. In approximately half of PCD patients, randomised left-right asymmetry during embryogenesis (because the embryonic node depends on motile primary cilia) results in **situs inversus**; this triad of chronic sinusitis, bronchiectasis and situs inversus is **Kartagener syndrome**. Male infertility is also typical because sperm flagellar motility shares the same dynein-driven mechanism. **Ciliocytophthoria**, by contrast, is a histopathological term for the detachment of ciliated apical fragments from columnar respiratory epithelial cells seen in viral upper-respiratory infections — it is unrelated to PCD and is not part of the Kartagener phenotype.

QUESTION 21

Stem: In dividing cells, mitotic spindles are formed by \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Tubulin

Explanation: The mitotic spindle is built from **microtubules**, hollow cylindrical polymers of α/β-tubulin heterodimers nucleated at the centrosomes (microtubule-organising centres, or MTOCs) that contain γ-tubulin [Ganong ch.2] [Guyton ch.2] [Boron ch.2]. During prophase the duplicated centrosomes migrate to opposite poles, and microtubules grow from γ-tubulin at the pericentriolar material to form astral, kinetochore and polar (interpolar) microtubules. Coordinated polymerisation/depolymerisation and motor activity of kinesins and dyneins drive chromosome capture, congression to the metaphase plate, and anaphase separation. **Ubiquitin** is a small protein that targets substrates for proteasomal degradation; it has no structural role in the spindle (although ubiquitin-mediated proteolysis of cyclin B and securin regulates mitotic transitions). **Vimentin** and **keratin** are intermediate filaments — vimentin in mesenchymal cells and keratin in epithelial cells. Intermediate filaments do not contribute to the mitotic spindle; the spindle is exclusively a microtubule structure.

Hints:

1. The mitotic spindle is composed of one of the three cytoskeletal filament systems — identify which.
2. Microtubules grow by polymerisation of a single class of heterodimeric subunit from organising centres at the spindle poles.
3. Name the heterodimeric protein subunit of the microtubule.

Distractors:

- "Ubiquitin" — Reveals the misconception that ubiquitin is structural. It is a regulatory tag for protein degradation, important in mitotic _control_ but not spindle architecture.
- "Vimentin" — Reveals the misconception that any cytoskeletal filament can build the spindle. Vimentin is an intermediate filament; spindles are microtubular.
- "Keratin" — Same misconception as (c). Keratin forms epithelial intermediate filaments and contributes to desmosome and hemidesmosome anchoring, not to the mitotic spindle.
