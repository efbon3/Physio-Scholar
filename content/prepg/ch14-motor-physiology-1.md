QUESTION 1

Stem: Each muscle spindle consists of **\_\_** intrafusal fibers.

Correct answer: 3 to 12

Explanation: A muscle spindle contains **3 to 12 specialised intrafusal muscle fibres** enclosed in a fluid-filled connective-tissue capsule, lying _parallel_ to the surrounding extrafusal fibres [Guyton ch.55]. The intrafusal fibres are subdivided into **nuclear bag fibres** (1–3 per spindle, with nuclei clustered centrally — dynamic and static varieties) and **nuclear chain fibres** (3–9 per spindle, with nuclei in a single row — static only). The middle (non-contractile) portion of each intrafusal fibre is wrapped by sensory afferents, while the polar contractile ends receive γ-motor innervation.

Hints:

1. Recall the cellular composition of the muscle spindle.
2. The number falls in the single-digit-to-low-teens range.
3. Spindles are made up of a few intrafusal fibres bundled in a capsule.

Distractors:

- "12 to 24" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "1 to 3" — Reveals the misconception that the spindle has very few fibres. The total is 3-12; only nuclear bag fibres number 1-3.
- "24 to 50" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Motor supply to muscle spindle is **\_\_**

Correct answer: γ

Explanation: The contractile (polar) ends of intrafusal fibres are innervated by **γ-motor neurones** (small motor neurones whose axons are Aγ fibres) [Guyton ch.55]. γ activation contracts the polar ends, stretching the central sensory zone and adjusting spindle sensitivity to muscle length — independently of α-motor neurone–driven extrafusal contraction. **Ia** and **II** are _afferent_ (sensory) fibres from the spindle, not motor. The α-γ co-activation principle means that both α and γ neurones fire together during voluntary movement, keeping spindle sensitivity tuned across changing muscle lengths.

Hints:

1. Distinguish motor from sensory innervation of the spindle.
2. The motor supply is from a small specialised motor neurone subtype.
3. The Greek letter denoting the motor neurone is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Iα" — Sensory afferent, not motor.
- "II" — Sensory afferent (from secondary endings), not motor.
- "None" — The spindle has motor innervation; this answer denies it.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Which of the following is sensed by the nuclear bag fibers?

Correct answer: Dynamic length of muscle

Explanation: **Nuclear bag fibres** (specifically the dynamic bag1 type) detect **rapid changes (rate) in muscle length** — the dynamic component of the stretch response [Guyton ch.55]. Their Ia primary afferent gives a transient burst of firing during stretch that is proportional to the rate of length change. **Nuclear chain fibres and static bag fibres** detect _static_ length (steady-state). **Muscle tension** is detected by the Golgi tendon organ; **joint position** is signalled by joint receptors and integrated proprioception (spindles + GTOs + joint receptors).

Hints:

1. Distinguish dynamic from static spindle responses.
2. Nuclear bag fibres provide the dynamic component of the stretch response.
3. The answer concerns rate of length change.

Distractors:

- "Muscle tension" — Detected by the GTO, not the spindle.
- "Joint position" — Joint receptors and integrated proprioception, not spindles alone.
- "None" — The spindle does sense length.

QUESTION 6

Stem: The afferents for deep tendon reflexes innervate **\_**

Correct answer: Nuclear bag and chain fibers

Explanation: The **deep tendon reflex** (e.g. knee jerk, ankle jerk) is the clinical name for the **stretch reflex** elicited by tapping a tendon. Tapping briefly stretches the muscle, activating spindle Ia afferents from **both nuclear bag and nuclear chain fibres** [Guyton ch.55]. Despite the name "deep tendon", the reflex is mediated by the _muscle spindle_, not the Golgi tendon organ — the tendon tap is a way to deliver the stretch stimulus. The Ia afferent makes a monosynaptic excitatory connection on the homonymous α-motor neurone, producing the brief twitch.

Hints:

1. Distinguish the receptor name from the clinical name of the reflex.
2. Tapping a tendon stretches the muscle and activates the muscle spindle.
3. Both nuclear bag and chain fibres of the spindle are involved.

Distractors:

- "Dynamic intrafusal fibers" — Partial truth (dynamic bag is involved) but incomplete; chain fibres are also activated.
- "Golgi tendon organ" — Reveals the misconception that the tendon hammer activates GTO. The reflex is spindle-mediated.
- "Golgi tendon and muscle spindle" — Includes the wrong receptor.

QUESTION 7

Stem: Stimulus for stretch reflex is **\_**

Correct answer: Passive stretch

Explanation: The **stretch reflex** is elicited by **passive stretch** of the muscle (externally imposed lengthening), which deforms the spindle and activates Ia afferents [Guyton ch.55]. _Active_ stretch (concentric or eccentric muscle activity) involves α-motor neurone activity and changes the loading on the spindle in a different way; the spindle is "unloaded" by α-motor neurone–driven shortening unless γ-coactivation maintains its sensitivity. Hence the classical stretch reflex stimulus is _passive_ lengthening — exactly what the tendon tap simulates.

Hints:

1. The stretch reflex tests the response to externally imposed lengthening.
2. Active muscle contraction would unload the spindle; the stimulus must be passive.
3. Passive stretching deforms the central spindle region and excites Ia afferents.

Distractors:

- "Active stretch" — Active stretch unloads spindles or shifts their loading; the classical stimulus is passive.
- "Both" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "None" — Stretch reflexes do have a stimulus.

QUESTION 8

The two sides therefore show _opposite_ responses (ipsilateral flexion + contralateral extension), not the same response.

QUESTION 9

Stem: The maintenance of normal posture depends on\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Integrity of reflex arc

Explanation: **Posture** is maintained primarily by **postural reflexes** — continuous, low-amplitude reflex adjustments of antigravity muscle tone in response to vestibular, visual, and proprioceptive input [Guyton ch.55] [Guyton ch.56]. The principal substrate is the **stretch reflex** (Ia spindle → α-motor neurone), which keeps antigravity muscles tonically active. Loss of any limb of the reflex arc (afferent, central, or efferent) disrupts posture. Muscle power and fibre type contribute to performance but not to the reflex-mediated maintenance per se.

Hints:

1. Posture relies on continuous low-level muscle activity adjusted by sensory feedback.
2. The substrate is reflex regulation of antigravity muscle tone.
3. Without an intact reflex arc, posture cannot be maintained.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Plausible but do not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 10

Stem: Receptor for inverse stretch reflex\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Golgi tendon organ

Explanation: The **inverse stretch reflex** (autogenic inhibition or "clasp-knife" reflex) is elicited by very strong contraction or stretch of a muscle: the **Golgi tendon organ (GTO)** at the muscle-tendon junction senses high tendon tension and signals via **Ib afferents** [Guyton ch.55]. The Ib afferent activates an inhibitory interneurone in the spinal cord that **inhibits the homonymous α-motor neurone** — opposite of the stretch reflex. Functionally, the inverse stretch reflex protects the muscle and tendon from injury at extreme tensions and contributes to the smooth release of joints in spasticity ("clasp-knife" phenomenon).

Hints:

1. Distinguish the muscle spindle (length) from the GTO (tension) sensor.
2. The inverse stretch reflex is the opposite of the stretch reflex — it inhibits the α-motor neurone.
3. The receptor at the muscle-tendon junction is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Muscle spindle" — Stretch reflex receptor, not inverse stretch.
- "Extrafusal fiber" — Not a receptor.
- "Intrafusal fiber" — Component of muscle spindle.

QUESTION 11

Stem: The number of muscle fibers per Golgi tendon organ is\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: 3-20

Explanation: A single **Golgi tendon organ** is encapsulated and arranged in series with **3 to 20 (~10 on average) extrafusal muscle fibres** at the muscle-tendon junction [Guyton ch.55]. This in-series arrangement makes the GTO sensitive to tension generated by active contraction of those fibres (in addition to passive stretch). Each Ib afferent therefore samples the activity of a small subset of motor units. The series arrangement contrasts with the parallel arrangement of muscle spindles relative to extrafusal fibres.

Hints:

2. Each GTO is small, encapsulating only a few extrafusal fibres in series.
3. The number is in the low double digits.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Plausible but do not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 12

Stem: The inverse stretch reflex\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Is a disynaptic reflex with a single interneuron inserted between the afferent and efferent limbs

Explanation: The inverse stretch reflex is **disynaptic**: Ib afferent from GTO → **inhibitory interneurone** in spinal cord → α-motor neurone (inhibition) [Guyton ch.55]. The single interposed interneurone provides the inhibitory sign of the reflex (the Ib afferent itself releases excitatory glutamate; the interneurone is glycinergic and inverts the sign). The stretch reflex (spindle), by contrast, is **monosynaptic** (Ia → α directly). A polysynaptic reflex (>2 synapses, many interneurones) describes withdrawal reflexes.

Hints:

1. Count the synapses in the inverse stretch reflex pathway.
2. One interneurone makes it disynaptic.
3. Disynaptic distinguishes inverse stretch from monosynaptic stretch reflex.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Misclassify the reflex's synaptic architecture.

QUESTION 14

Stem: Muscle tone is maintained by\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Muscle spindle

Explanation: **Muscle tone** is the resistance of a relaxed muscle to passive stretch — sustained by tonic, low-level α-motor neurone activity driven by ongoing **stretch reflex** activity [Guyton ch.55]. The receptor is the **muscle spindle**: spindle Ia afferents fire continuously at rest, providing tonic excitatory input to α-motor neurones. Loss of muscle spindle activity (e.g. dorsal-root section) abolishes muscle tone (flaccidity). The Golgi tendon organ contributes to the dynamic regulation of force but does not maintain tone. Renshaw cells provide recurrent inhibition, modulating but not generating tone.

Hints:

1. Recall the receptor underlying the tonic stretch reflex.
2. Continuous spindle Ia firing keeps α-motor neurones tonically active.
3. The receptor responsible for muscle tone is the spindle.

Distractors:

- "Golgi tendon organ" — Senses tension dynamically; does not maintain tone.
- "Renshaw cell" — Inhibitory interneurone; modulates not maintains.
- "None" — Tone is maintained reflexively; this denies it.

QUESTION 15

Stem: Which of the following is true during initiation of voluntary movements?

Correct answer: Both α and γ motor neurons are activated together

Explanation: The **α-γ co-activation principle** [Guyton ch.55]: during voluntary movement, descending motor commands activate **both α-motor neurones (extrafusal fibres) and γ-motor neurones (intrafusal fibres) simultaneously**. This keeps the spindle "loaded" — its sensitivity to length is maintained even as the muscle shortens (α-driven extrafusal contraction would otherwise unload the spindle). γ co-activation ensures that proprioceptive feedback continues throughout the movement, allowing the CNS to detect deviations from intended movement.

Hints:

1. Recall the α-γ coupling principle.
2. Without γ co-activation, spindles would go silent during shortening contractions.
3. The two are activated together to maintain spindle sensitivity.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Misrepresent the temporal relationship of α and γ activation.

QUESTION 16

Stem: Righting reflex is\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Postural reflex

Explanation: **Righting reflexes** are a family of **postural reflexes** that restore the body's normal orientation when displaced from upright [Guyton ch.56]. They include vestibular righting (otolith input), neck righting (cervical proprioception), body-on-body righting, optical righting (visual input). Integration occurs at the **midbrain level** (red nucleus, superior colliculus, vestibular nuclei). Decorticate animals retain righting reflexes; decerebrate animals at the midbrain level lose them. Hence righting reflexes are postural rather than purely spinal, and they involve more than just stretch or ocular reflex circuits.

Hints:

1. Recall the function of righting reflexes.
2. Restoration of upright posture involves multisensory integration.
3. The category of reflex is postural.

Distractors:

- "Stretch reflex" — Spinal monosynaptic reflex, not righting.
- "Spinal reflex" — Righting integrates at midbrain, not just spinal cord.
- "Ocular reflex" — Visual righting is one component, but righting reflexes include non-ocular forms.

QUESTION 18

Stem: Pyramids are formed by\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Corticospinal tract

Explanation: The **medullary pyramids** are paired bulges on the ventral surface of the medulla formed by the descending fibres of the **corticospinal tract** [Guyton ch.56]. At the lower medulla, ~80–90% of corticospinal fibres decussate (pyramidal decussation) to form the **lateral corticospinal tract**; the remaining ~10–20% continue ipsilaterally as the **anterior corticospinal tract**. The corticospinal tract is therefore synonymous with the "pyramidal tract" — the term derives from the pyramidal shape of these medullary structures and from the pyramid-shaped Betz cells in primary motor cortex.

Hints:

1. Recall the anatomical origin of the medullary pyramids.
2. The major descending motor tract passing through the pyramid gives them their name.
3. The corticospinal tract = pyramidal tract.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Plausible but do not reveal specific misconceptions worth naming.

QUESTION 27

Stem: Destruction of anterior cerebellum in a decerebrate animal leads to **\_\_\_\_**.

Correct answer: Increased rigidity via alpha motor neuron

Explanation: Decerebrate rigidity has two components [Guyton ch.56]: a **gamma loop component** (γ-motor neurone hyperactivity → spindle Ia → α-motor neurone excitation) — the dominant mechanism in standard decerebrate rigidity, and an **alpha component** (direct facilitation of α-motor neurones by descending reticulospinal/vestibulospinal pathways without γ involvement). The **anterior cerebellum normally inhibits α-motor neurone facilitation** by the lateral vestibular nucleus. Destruction of the anterior cerebellum **removes this inhibition**, allowing the α component of decerebrate rigidity to be expressed — increased rigidity via direct α-motor neurone hyperactivity (cerebellar rigidity).

Hints:

1. Recall the anterior cerebellum's inhibitory role on the lateral vestibular nucleus.
2. Loss of cerebellar inhibition releases vestibular drive to α-motor neurones.
3. The result is an increase in the alpha component of rigidity.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Misrepresent the cerebellar contribution.

QUESTION 28

Stem: Upper Motor Neurons (UMN) include **\_\_**

Correct answer: Pyramidal cells

Explanation: **Upper motor neurones (UMN)** are CNS neurones whose axons descend to influence lower motor neurones — including the **pyramidal cells (Betz cells in M1) of the corticospinal tract** and the brainstem motor neurones of the extrapyramidal pathways [Guyton ch.56]. **Lower motor neurones (LMN)** are the α-motor neurones in the **anterior horn of the spinal cord** and motor cranial nerve nuclei in the brainstem, whose axons leave the CNS to innervate muscle. UMN lesions cause spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign, no atrophy. LMN lesions cause flaccidity, hyporeflexia, atrophy, fasciculations.

Hints:

2. UMN axons descend within the CNS to LMN.
3. Pyramidal cells in motor cortex are the prototype UMN.

Distractors:

- "Peripheral nerves" — LMN axons in peripheral nerves.
- "Glial cells" — Not motor neurones.
- "Anterior horn cells" — LMN.

Explanation: **UMN lesions below the pyramidal decussation** (spinal cord lesion of the lateral corticospinal tract) produce classical UMN signs **on the same side as the lesion** [Guyton ch.56]: **Babinski sign** (extensor plantar response — upgoing big toe with fanning of others; **option (a) is correct**), spasticity, hyperreflexia, clonus, loss of superficial reflexes (e.g. abdominal). Initially after acute transection there may be **spinal shock** with flaccidity and areflexia for days to weeks, but the chronic state is UMN-pattern. **Loss of proprioception** would result from dorsal column damage, not corticospinal. **Flaccidity and loss of DTR** are LMN signs, not UMN.

2. Pyramidal injury produces UMN signs in the chronic state.
3. The Babinski sign is a hallmark UMN sign.

Hence (a) cog-wheel rigidity would _not_ be present in pure corticospinal injury.

QUESTION 32

Stem: A 50-year-old man with a history of medically controlled atrial fibrillation develops sudden onset weakness in the right side of his body. Which of the following Broadman's area is most likely affected?

Correct answer: 4

Explanation: Right-sided hemiplegia from sudden onset (suggesting embolic stroke from atrial fibrillation) most likely affects the **left primary motor cortex (Brodmann area 4)** in the precentral gyrus [Guyton ch.56]. M1 (area 4) is the principal output area of the corticospinal tract; lesion here causes contralateral motor weakness. **Areas 1, 2, 3** are primary somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus) — sensory deficits, not motor. **Areas 5, 7** are parietal association cortex (sensory integration, body schema). **Areas 16, 18** — area 16 doesn't exist in the standard Brodmann scheme; area 18 is secondary visual cortex. Hence (a) area 4.

Hints:

2. Primary motor cortex is in the precentral gyrus.
3. Sudden contralateral weakness → primary motor cortex stroke.

- "1,2,3" — Sensory cortex.
- "5,7" — Parietal association.
- "16,18" — Visual association.

QUESTION 33

Stem: Skilled voluntary movement is initiated by\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Cerebral cortex

Explanation: **Voluntary, skilled movement is initiated by the cerebral cortex** — specifically the prefrontal, premotor, supplementary motor and primary motor areas working together [Guyton ch.56]. The cortex generates motor commands transmitted via the corticospinal tract to spinal motor neurones. **Basal ganglia** participate in _selection_ and _inhibition_ of motor programmes (modulation, not initiation). **Cerebellum** participates in _coordination_, _timing_, and _learning_ of movement (modulation). **Association cortex** contributes to planning and decision making. The integrated initiation occurs at the cortical level overall.

Hints:

1. Recall which CNS region originates voluntary motor commands.
2. The corticospinal tract starts at the cortex.
3. Basal ganglia and cerebellum modulate but do not initiate.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Modulators of movement, not initiators.

QUESTION 34

Stem: In the homunculus, which of the following has maximum representation?

Correct answer: Increased preciseness of movements

Explanation: The **motor homunculus** depicts the somatotopic representation of body parts on the primary motor cortex with sizes proportional to the **degree of fine motor control** required, _not_ to muscle bulk [Guyton ch.56]. Hands (especially fingers and thumb), face (especially lips and tongue), and vocal apparatus have _huge_ cortical areas because they require precise, skilled movements. Trunk and proximal limbs have small representations despite having large muscle masses. The principle is: the more **precise and skilled** the movement, the **larger the cortical area** dedicated to it.

Hints:

1. Recall what the size of homuncular representation reflects.
2. Hands and lips have huge representation; trunk has small representation.
3. The driver is precision of movement, not muscle bulk.

Distractors:

- "Low control and skilled movements" — The opposite of what determines representation.
- "More muscle fibres are present" — Muscle bulk does not predict representation.
- "None of the above" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 36

Stem: While doing a neurosurgical procedure, the paracentral lobules of both hemispheres were damaged. It will lead to which of the following?

Correct answer: Paraplegia

Explanation: The **paracentral lobule** is the medial extension of the precentral and postcentral gyri on the medial surface of each hemisphere, and represents the **lower limb** (and genitalia and bladder) in the motor and sensory homunculi [Guyton ch.56]. **Bilateral damage** to paracentral lobules causes **bilateral lower limb paralysis** = **paraplegia**, often with associated bowel/bladder dysfunction. Causes include parasagittal meningioma compressing both medial frontal lobes. **Hemiplegia** would be unilateral, **monoplegia** one limb, **quadriplegia** all four.

3. Bilateral lower limb paralysis = paraplegia.

QUESTION 37

Stem: Setting posture before planned movement is associated with \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Premotor cortex

Explanation: The **premotor cortex (lateral area 6)** is responsible for planning and preparing externally-cued movements, including the **anticipatory postural adjustments** that stabilise the body before voluntary movement [Guyton ch.56]. Activity in premotor cortex precedes movement onset and contributes to readiness potentials. The **supplementary motor area (medial area 6, SMA)** is involved in internally-generated and complex sequence movements and bimanual coordination. **Primary motor cortex (M1, area 4)** executes the movement itself. The MCQ key prefers (a) premotor cortex for postural-set planning.

- "Frontal" — Too generic.
- "Supplementary" — More involved in internally-generated sequences.

QUESTION 38

Stem: A quadriplegic man with a high cervical lesion develops sudden onset of flushing, sweating, pounding headache and hypertension. He has a distended bladder due to a blocked urinary catheter. On immediate drainage of the urinary bladder and repositioning him from supine to reclining, his symptoms resolve. Which of the following is false regarding the most likely diagnosis?

Correct answer: Major portion of the body's skeletal muscle go into extensor spasm

Explanation: **Autonomic dysreflexia (autonomic hyperreflexia)** is a syndrome of **massive sympathetic discharge in patients with spinal cord lesions above T6**, typically triggered by a noxious stimulus below the lesion (distended bladder is the classical trigger; impacted bowel, pressure sores, etc.) [Guyton ch.55]. Features: severe paroxysmal **hypertension (SBP often >200 mmHg)**, pounding headache, **flushing and sweating above the lesion** (parasympathetic compensation via vagal-mediated vasodilation in the face/upper body), **piloerection and pallor below the lesion** (sympathetic vasoconstriction), **bradycardia** (baroreceptor-mediated reflex), and bowel/bladder evacuation may occur. Skeletal muscle spasms can occur but **massive extensor spasm is not a defining feature** — autonomic dysreflexia is primarily an autonomic, not somatic, phenomenon. Hence (b) is the false option.

Hints:

1. Recall the clinical features of autonomic dysreflexia.
2. The syndrome is dominated by sympathetic hypertensive response and autonomic effects.
3. The exception falsely emphasises massive somatic muscle spasm.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — Correctly true features.
