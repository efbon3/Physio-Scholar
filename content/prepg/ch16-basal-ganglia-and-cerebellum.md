QUESTION 1

Stem: Which of the following is a nucleus of basal ganglia?

Correct answer: Caudate

Explanation: The **basal ganglia** comprise: **caudate nucleus**, **putamen** (caudate + putamen = striatum), **globus pallidus** (interna + externa), **substantia nigra** (pars compacta + pars reticulata), and **subthalamic nucleus** [Guyton ch.57]. The **dentate** is a deep cerebellar nucleus. The **thalamus** is a separate diencephalic relay structure (a major basal ganglia output target but not part of the basal ganglia themselves). The **red nucleus** is a midbrain motor nucleus separate from the basal ganglia.

Hints:

1. List the components of the basal ganglia.
2. Three of the listed options belong to other systems (cerebellum, thalamus, midbrain).
3. The caudate is part of the striatum.

Distractors:

- "Dentate" — Cerebellar nucleus.
- "Thalamus" — Diencephalic relay; not basal ganglia.
- "Red nucleus" — Midbrain motor structure.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Which part of the basal ganglia receives the afferent input?

Correct answer: Striatum

Explanation: The **striatum (caudate + putamen)** is the principal **input nucleus** of the basal ganglia [Guyton ch.57]. It receives major afferents from the entire cerebral cortex (corticostriatal — glutamatergic), from the substantia nigra pars compacta (nigrostriatal — dopaminergic), and from intralaminar thalamic nuclei. **Globus pallidus internus and substantia nigra pars reticulata** are the principal **output nuclei** (GABAergic projections to thalamus). The **subthalamic nucleus** is an intermediate processing nucleus.

Hints:

1. Distinguish input from output nuclei of the basal ganglia.
2. The striatum receives the cortical and dopaminergic afferents.
3. The input gateway is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Globus pallidus" — Output nucleus.
- "Striatum nigra" — Misnomer; "substantia nigra" — pars compacta provides afferent dopamine but is not itself the receiving nucleus.
- "Subthalamic nuclei" — Intermediate processing.

QUESTION 3

Stem: A patient is found to have a hemorrhage in the basal ganglia region. Which of the following is least likely to be affected in this patient?

Correct answer: Maintenance of equilibrium

Explanation: Basal ganglia functions [Guyton ch.57]: motor planning, selection and initiation, learned motor sequences, suppression of unwanted movements, cognitive aspects of motor control (caudate-prefrontal loop). Lesions cause hypokinetic (parkinsonian) or hyperkinetic (chorea, athetosis, ballism) syndromes. **Equilibrium** (balance, postural stability), by contrast, is principally controlled by the **vestibulocerebellum (flocculonodular lobe)** and vestibular nuclei [Guyton ch.57]. Equilibrium is _not_ a primary basal ganglia function and is therefore _least likely_ affected by basal ganglia haemorrhage.

Hints:

1. Recall the functional repertoire of the basal ganglia.
2. Three of the listed are basal ganglia functions; one is cerebellar/vestibular.
3. The exception is equilibrium.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — All basal ganglia functions.

QUESTION 4

Stem: Glutamate is synthesized mainly in which part of basal ganglia?

Correct answer: Subthalamic nuclei

Explanation: The **subthalamic nucleus (STN)** is the only **intrinsic glutamatergic** nucleus of the basal ganglia, projecting glutamatergic excitation to the **GPi** (and to a lesser extent SNr) [Guyton ch.57]. The major glutamatergic input _to_ the basal ganglia comes from cortex (corticostriatal); but among basal ganglia _components themselves_, only STN releases glutamate. Striatum and globus pallidus medium-spiny/projection neurones are GABAergic; SNc releases dopamine; SNr releases GABA.

Hints:

1. Recall the neurotransmitters of the basal ganglia projection neurones.
2. Most basal ganglia nuclei are GABAergic; one is glutamatergic.
3. The exception is the subthalamic nucleus.

Distractors:

- "Striatum" — GABAergic medium spiny neurones.
- "Globus pallidus" — GABAergic.
- "Striatum nigra" — Likely meaning substantia nigra; SNc is dopaminergic, SNr is GABAergic.

QUESTION 5

Stem: A 56-year-old woman presents with abnormal movements of the limbs. A lesion of the globus pallidus is suspected. Which of the following did she most likely present with?

Correct answer: Athetosis

Explanation: Movement disorders associated with specific basal ganglia lesions [Guyton ch.57]: **Globus pallidus lesions** → **athetosis** (slow, writhing, sinuous distal-limb movements). **Caudate lesions** → **chorea** (rapid, irregular, jerky movements; classic in Huntington's). **Subthalamic nucleus lesions** → **hemiballismus** (violent, large-amplitude, proximal-limb flinging on contralateral side). **Substantia nigra (pars compacta) lesions** → **parkinsonism** (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, postural instability). Hence (b) athetosis.

Hints:

1. Recall the classical lesion-syndrome pairings in the basal ganglia.
2. Globus pallidus → athetosis.
3. The slow, writhing distal movements are athetosis.

Distractors:

- "Chorea" — Caudate lesion (Huntington's).
- "Hemiballismus" — Subthalamic nucleus lesion.
- "Parkinsonism" — Substantia nigra (SNc) degeneration.

QUESTION 6

Stem: Cells present in cerebellar cortex are all except

Correct answer: Bipolar

Explanation: The **cerebellar cortex** has three layers and contains **5 cell types** [Guyton ch.57]: **Purkinje cells** (single dendritic tree in molecular layer; sole output to deep cerebellar nuclei), **granule cells** (numerous; smallest cells in CNS; axons form parallel fibres), **Golgi cells** (large interneurones in granular layer), **stellate cells** (in molecular layer), and **basket cells** (in molecular layer; axons surround Purkinje somata in basket-like baskets). **Bipolar cells** are _not_ a recognised cerebellar cortical cell type — they are found in the **retina** (and olfactory epithelium). Hence (b) bipolar.

Hints:

1. Recall the 5 cell types of the cerebellar cortex.
2. Three layers: molecular, Purkinje, granular.
3. Bipolar cells belong to the retina, not the cerebellum.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — All cerebellar cortical cell types.

QUESTION 7

Stem: All are cerebellar nuclei except\_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Red nucleus

Explanation: The **deep cerebellar nuclei** (4, from medial to lateral) are: **fastigial**, **globose**, **emboliform** (the latter two together = interpositus in some texts), and **dentate** [Guyton ch.57]. The **red nucleus** is a **midbrain motor nucleus** that gives rise to the rubrospinal tract — it is _not_ a cerebellar nucleus, although it has reciprocal connections with the cerebellum (the red nucleus relays cerebellar output to the spinal cord and receives cerebellar afferents). Hence (d) is the exception.

Hints:

1. List the four deep cerebellar nuclei.
2. The red nucleus is in the midbrain, not the cerebellum.
3. The exception is in the midbrain.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Cerebellar nuclei.

QUESTION 8

Stem: Efferent from cerebellar cortex is through which cell:

Correct answer: Purkinje

Explanation: **Purkinje cells** are the **sole output neurones of the cerebellar cortex** [Guyton ch.57]. Their axons project from the molecular layer through the granular layer to terminate on the deep cerebellar nuclei (and the lateral vestibular nucleus from the flocculonodular lobe). Purkinje cells are **GABAergic** and inhibitory. All other cerebellar cortical cells are interneurones whose axons remain within the cortex. Mossy and climbing fibres are _afferent_ inputs.

Hints:

1. Recall the only output cell of the cerebellar cortex.
2. Purkinje cell axons are the sole exit from the cerebellar cortex.
3. The output is GABAergic (inhibitory).

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Interneurones; do not exit the cortex.

QUESTION 9

Stem: Flocculonodular lobe has direct connections with \***\*\_\*\***

Correct answer: Vestibular nucleus

Explanation: The **flocculonodular lobe (vestibulocerebellum)** receives direct vestibular afferents from the vestibular nuclei (and vestibular nerve), and its Purkinje cells project directly to the **vestibular nuclei** — bypassing the deep cerebellar nuclei [Guyton ch.57]. This is a unique feature: throughout the rest of the cerebellum, Purkinje cells project to deep cerebellar nuclei (which then project elsewhere). The flocculonodular lobe's direct connection with vestibular nuclei serves balance, posture, and the vestibulo-ocular reflex.

Hints:

1. Recall the unique outflow of the flocculonodular lobe.
2. The vestibulocerebellum bypasses deep cerebellar nuclei.
3. Direct connection is with vestibular nuclei.

Distractors:

- "Red nucleus" — Receives input from interpositus, not flocculonodular.
- "Inferior olivary nucleus" — Source of climbing fibres to all cerebellum, not specifically flocculonodular efferent target.
- "Dentate nucleus" — Receives Purkinje input from cerebrocerebellum (lateral hemispheres).

QUESTION 10

Stem: Which of the following are climbing fibers?

Correct answer: Olivocerebellar fibers

Explanation: The cerebellum receives two principal afferent fibre types [Guyton ch.57]: **Mossy fibres** — from spinal cord (spinocerebellar), pontine nuclei (pontocerebellar), reticular formation, vestibular nuclei — synapse on granule cells in the granular layer; granule cells then send parallel fibres to Purkinje cells. **Climbing fibres** — exclusively from the **inferior olivary nucleus (olivocerebellar tract)** — make a one-to-one, very strong synapse directly on Purkinje cell dendrites, producing complex spikes that mediate motor learning (long-term depression of parallel fibre synapses). Hence climbing fibres = olivocerebellar.

Hints:

1. Recall the two cerebellar afferent fibre types and their origins.
2. Climbing fibres come from a single brainstem nucleus.
3. The inferior olive sends climbing fibres.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — All mossy fibre afferents.

QUESTION 12

Stem: Which of the following is a function of the neocerebellum?

Correct answer: Planning and programming of voluntary movements

Explanation: **Neocerebellum (= cerebrocerebellum = lateral hemispheres)** receives mossy-fibre input from the contralateral cerebral cortex (via pontine nuclei) and outputs through dentate → VL of thalamus → motor cortex [Guyton ch.57]. Its function is **planning and programming of voluntary skilled movements** — particularly the timing and sequencing of complex distal-limb movements. **Equilibrium** = vestibulocerebellum. **Correction of ongoing voluntary movements** (online adjustment via spinocerebellar feedback) and **muscle tone maintenance** = spinocerebellum. Hence (c).

Hints:

1. Match each cerebellar division to its function.
2. Cerebrocerebellum = neocerebellum = planning of skilled movements.
3. The lateral hemispheres serve motor planning.

Distractors:

- "Maintenance of equilibrium" — Vestibulocerebellum.
- "Correction of voluntary movements" — Spinocerebellum.
- "Maintenance of muscle tone" — Spinocerebellum (anterior lobe).

QUESTION 13

Stem: A patient with a cerebellar lesion will show all of the following features except:

Correct answer: Resting tremor

Explanation: Cerebellar lesion features include **ataxia** (gait, truncal, limb), **dysmetria** (past pointing in finger-nose test), **dysdiadochokinesia**, **intention tremor** (oscillation appearing during voluntary movement and worsening near target), **dysarthria** (scanning speech), **hypotonia**, **nystagmus** (especially with vestibulocerebellar involvement) [Guyton ch.57]. **Resting tremor** is characteristic of **Parkinson's disease (basal ganglia)**, _not_ cerebellar disease. Cerebellar tremor is _intention_ tremor, distinguished by its absence at rest and worsening on goal-directed action. Hence (b) is the exception.

Hints:

1. Distinguish cerebellar from basal ganglia tremor.
2. Cerebellar tremor is _intention_ (action) tremor.
3. Resting tremor is parkinsonian.

Distractors:

- "Nystagmus" — Cerebellar (vestibulocerebellum).
- "Ataxia" — Cerebellar.
- "Past pointing" — Cerebellar (dysmetria).

QUESTION 14

Stem: The vestibulo-ocular reflex is being performed in a patient with vertigo. Which of the following is associated with this reflex?

Correct answer: Flocculonodular lobe

Explanation: The **vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR)** stabilises gaze during head movement by driving the eyes opposite to head rotation [Guyton ch.57]. The reflex arc: vestibular receptors → vestibular nuclei → contralateral abducens nucleus and oculomotor nuclei (via MLF) → eye muscles. The **flocculus (part of the flocculonodular lobe / vestibulocerebellum)** modulates the VOR, providing adaptive gain control — its lesions impair VOR adaptation. Hence (b). Spinocerebellum and neocerebellum are not primarily involved. Occipital lobe processes visual information but doesn't drive the VOR.

Hints:

1. Recall the cerebellar division involved in VOR.
2. The flocculus adjusts VOR gain.
3. Vestibular reflexes link to vestibulocerebellum.

Distractors:

- "Spinocerebellum" — Limb coordination, not VOR.
- "Neocerebellum" — Motor planning, not VOR.
- "Occipital lobe" — Visual processing.

QUESTION 15

Stem: Purkinje fibers are inhibitory for **\_\_\_\_**.

Correct answer: Deep cerebellar nuclei

Explanation: **Purkinje cells (note: 'Purkinje fibres' in cardiac physiology refers to a different structure; in the cerebellum, the term refers to Purkinje cell axons)** project from the cerebellar cortex to the **deep cerebellar nuclei** (and the lateral vestibular nucleus from the flocculonodular lobe), releasing **GABA** and _inhibiting_ the deep nuclei [Guyton ch.57]. The deep nuclei have spontaneous tonic firing that the Purkinje inhibition modulates. Climbing fibres synapse _on_ Purkinje cells (not vice versa). Granule cells project to Purkinje cells via parallel fibres. Spinocerebellar tracts are _afferent_, not Purkinje targets.

Hints:

1. Recall the targets of Purkinje cell output.
2. Purkinje GABAergic projection inhibits the deep cerebellar nuclei.
3. The output is from cortex to deep nuclei.

Distractors:

- "Climbing fibers" — Afferents to Purkinje, not targets.
- "Granule cells" — Inputs via parallel fibres.
- "Spino cerebellar tracts" — Afferents.

QUESTION 16

Stem: Which of the following fibres reach the Purkinje cell directly?

Correct answer: Inferior olivary nucleus

Explanation: **Climbing fibres from the inferior olivary nucleus** make direct one-to-one synapses on Purkinje cell dendrites — the only afferent that reaches Purkinje cells _directly_ (without intermediary granule cells) [Guyton ch.57]. Mossy fibres (from spinocerebellar, pontocerebellar, vestibular, reticular sources) synapse first on granule cells, which then project to Purkinje cells via parallel fibres. **Raphe nucleus** (serotonergic) and **locus coeruleus** (noradrenergic) modulatory projections to the cerebellum diffuse and synapse on multiple targets including Purkinje, but the textbook "direct" climbing fibre is the olive.

Hints:

1. Recall the only afferent that reaches Purkinje cells directly.
2. Climbing fibres come from the inferior olive.
3. All other afferents go via granule cells.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Either project through granule cells or are diffuse modulatory.

QUESTION 17

Stem: Which of the following is true?

Correct answer: Molecular layer contain stellate and basket cells

Explanation: Cerebellar cortex layers (outer to inner) [Guyton ch.57]: (1) **Molecular layer** (outermost) — contains **stellate cells** (superficial), **basket cells** (deeper, axons form baskets around Purkinje somata), parallel fibres of granule cells, and Purkinje cell dendritic trees. (2) **Purkinje cell layer** — single row of Purkinje cell somata (not Golgi cells). (3) **Granular layer** — granule cells, Golgi cells, and mossy-fibre rosettes. Hence (a) is true: molecular layer contains stellate and basket cells. (b) is false (molecular is _outermost_, not innermost). (c) is false (Golgi cells are in granular layer). (d) is false (Purkinje cells are GABAergic, not glutamatergic).

Hints:

1. Recall the three-layer structure of cerebellar cortex.
2. Molecular layer is outermost; granular layer is innermost.
3. Stellate and basket cells reside in the molecular layer.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Misassign location or transmitter.

QUESTION 20

Stem: Concerning the circuitry between mossy fibers, climbing fibers and cells of the cerebellar cortex, which of the following pairs provide excitatory input?

Correct answer: Granule cell parallel fibers and mossy fibers

Explanation: Excitatory inputs in the cerebellar cortex [Guyton ch.57]: **Mossy fibres** (excitatory; release glutamate onto granule cells in granular layer); **Climbing fibres** (excitatory; release glutamate directly onto Purkinje cells); **Granule cells** (excitatory; release glutamate via their axons = parallel fibres onto Purkinje cells, basket cells, stellate cells, and Golgi cells). Inhibitory cells: Purkinje, Golgi, basket, stellate (all GABAergic). Hence the _excitatory_ pair from the options is **(c) granule cell parallel fibres + mossy fibres** — both glutamatergic. Mossy fibres do not directly synapse on Purkinje cells (option a is wrong); Golgi cells are inhibitory (b is wrong); climbing fibres do not target basket cells directly (d is wrong).

Hints:

1. Recall which cerebellar cell types are excitatory.
2. Mossy fibres, climbing fibres, and granule cells are glutamatergic.
3. The pair providing excitatory input matches two of these three.

Distractors:

- "Mossy fibers and Purkinje cells" — Mossy fibres do not directly target Purkinje; Purkinje cells are inhibitory.
- "Golgi cells and climbing fibers" — Golgi cells are inhibitory.
- "Climbing fibers and basket cells" — Basket cells are inhibitory.
