QUESTION 1

Stem: A healthy 25-year-old software engineer participates in a 10-km charity run. Which of the following muscle does this person use during expiration?

Correct answer: Internal intercostals and abdominal recti

Explanation: Quiet expiration is **passive** — driven by elastic recoil of the lungs and chest wall, with no muscle contraction required [Guyton ch.38]. **Forced or active expiration** (during exercise, coughing, sneezing) requires muscles that compress the thoracic cavity: **internal intercostals** (pull ribs downward and inward), **abdominal muscles** (rectus abdominis, internal/external obliques, transversus abdominis — push diaphragm upward by raising intra-abdominal pressure). During a 10-km run, expiration becomes active. **Diaphragm and external intercostals** are inspiratory muscles. Hence (b).

Hints:

1. Distinguish quiet (passive) from active (forced) expiration.
2. Active expiration requires expiratory muscles.
3. Internal intercostals + abdominal muscles are expiratory.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — Include diaphragm and/or external intercostals (inspiratory muscles).

QUESTION 2

Stem: The intrapleural pressure is**\_\_**

Correct answer: Negative except in forced expiration

Explanation: **Intrapleural pressure (Ppl)** is the pressure in the thin pleural space between visceral and parietal pleurae, normally **subatmospheric (negative)** throughout the respiratory cycle in quiet breathing [Guyton ch.38]. It is approximately **−5 cmH₂O at end-expiration** (FRC) and **−7.5 cmH₂O at end-inspiration**. The negative pressure reflects the opposing elastic forces: the lungs tend to collapse inward while the chest wall tends to spring outward. During **forced expiration** (coughing, Valsalva, weight lifting), Ppl can become **positive**, transiently compressing airways. Hence Ppl is "negative except in forced expiration".

Hints:

1. Recall the normal range of intrapleural pressure.
2. Ppl is subatmospheric in quiet breathing.
3. Only forced expiration makes Ppl positive.

Distractors:

- (a), (b) — Misrepresent normal Ppl.
- "Negative at apex but positive at base" — Reveals partial truth (Ppl is more negative at apex than at base due to gravity), but not "positive at base" — base is still negative, just less so.

QUESTION 3

Stem: The intrapleural pressure is negative both during inspiration and expiration because**\_\_**

Correct answer: Thoracic cage and lung recoil in opposite direction

Explanation: Intrapleural pressure is negative because of **opposing elastic recoil**: the **lungs tend to collapse inward** (their elastic recoil pulls visceral pleura toward hilum), while the **chest wall tends to spring outward** (its elastic recoil pulls parietal pleura outward) [Guyton ch.38]. These opposing forces stretch the pleural fluid film between the two pleurae, creating a negative pressure (suction) in the pleural space. At FRC, the inward lung recoil exactly balances the outward chest wall recoil, and Ppl is approximately −5 cmH₂O. Surfactant (d) reduces (but does not eliminate) lung recoil — it modulates rather than directly determines Ppl negativity.

Hints:

1. Recall the elastic forces at the pleural interface.
2. Lung recoils inward; chest wall recoils outward.
3. The opposing recoil forces produce negative Ppl.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — Partial truths but not the primary determinant.

QUESTION 5

Stem: With reference to atmospheric pressure, what is the alveolar pressure at the end of normal inspiration (in cms of H2O)?

Correct answer: 0

Explanation: **Alveolar pressure (Palv)** changes during the respiratory cycle [Guyton ch.38]: at the **start of inspiration**, Palv = 0 (equal to atmosphere). During **inspiration**, lung expansion creates a slight negative Palv (~ **−1 cmH₂O**) — driving air inflow. At the **end of normal inspiration**, when air has equilibrated and flow has stopped, **Palv returns to 0** (atmospheric). During **expiration**, Palv becomes positive (~+1 cmH₂O), driving air outflow; at end-expiration, Palv returns to 0. Hence at end-inspiration: Palv = 0 (no flow). Option (b).

Hints:

1. Recall the alveolar pressure profile during the breath cycle.
2. Palv = 0 when no air is flowing (end-inspiration, end-expiration).
3. End-inspiration: no flow, Palv = 0.

Distractors:

- "+1.0" — Mid-expiration value.
- "-1.0" — Mid-inspiration value.
- "-6.0" — Approximates intrapleural, not alveolar.

QUESTION 6

Stem: The transpulmonary pressure is the pressure difference between **\_\_**

Correct answer: Intra alveolar and intrapleural pressure

Explanation: **Transpulmonary pressure (Ptp)** = **alveolar pressure − intrapleural pressure** [Guyton ch.38]. It is the _distending pressure_ that holds the lungs open against their elastic recoil. At FRC: Palv = 0, Ppl ≈ −5 cmH₂O, so Ptp = 0 − (−5) = +5 cmH₂O. The lungs are inflated to FRC by this distending pressure. Higher Ptp (e.g. at TLC) inflates the lungs further; lower Ptp (e.g. at RV) leaves them at minimum volume. Compliance = ΔV/ΔPtp. Pneumothorax abolishes Ptp, allowing the lung to collapse.

Hints:

1. Recall the formula for transpulmonary pressure.
2. Ptp = alveolar pressure − intrapleural pressure.
3. The distending pressure of the lung is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Intrapleural and atmospheric pressure" — This is the chest wall pressure.
- "Intrapulmonary and atmospheric pressure" — This is the pressure driving air flow.
- "None of the above" — (b) is correct.

QUESTION 7

Stem: A man inspires 1000ml from a spirometer. The intrapleural pressure was -4cm H2O before inspiration and -12cm H2O at the end of inspiration. What is the compliance of lungs?(ml/cm H2O)?

Correct answer: 125

Explanation: **Lung compliance C = ΔV / ΔP** [Guyton ch.38]. ΔV = 1000 mL. ΔP (intrapleural pressure change at end-inspiration vs start, when alveolar pressure equals atmospheric and equals 0 in both cases) = (−12) − (−4) = −8 cmH₂O. The change in transpulmonary pressure = ΔPalv − ΔPpl = 0 − (−8) = +8 cmH₂O (taking magnitudes for the calculation). Therefore C = 1000 / 8 = **125 mL/cmH₂O**. Hence (c). Normal lung compliance ≈ 200 mL/cmH₂O for two lungs in an adult (per side ≈ 100 mL/cmH₂O); the calculated 125 is in the lower-normal range.

Hints:

1. Recall the formula for lung compliance.
2. C = ΔV / ΔP, with ΔP being the change in transpulmonary pressure.
3. 1000 mL / 8 cmH₂O = 125 mL/cmH₂O.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Plausible but do not yield from the given data.

QUESTION 8

Stem: A 22-year-old woman has pulmonary compliance of 0.2 L/cm H2O and an alveolar pressure of 4 cmH2O and an intrapleural pressure of -5 cmH2O at the beginning of inspiration. What is the transpulmonary pressure (in cmH2O) when this woman inhales 600mL of air ?

Correct answer: 12

Explanation: Initial transpulmonary pressure: Ptp_initial = Palv − Ppl = 4 − (−5) = **9 cmH₂O**. After inhaling 600 mL: ΔV = 600 mL = 0.6 L. C = ΔV/ΔPtp → ΔPtp = ΔV/C = 0.6/0.2 = **3 cmH₂O** (increase in transpulmonary pressure required to accommodate the volume change). Final Ptp = 9 + 3 = **12 cmH₂O**. Hence (c).

Hints:

1. Calculate initial transpulmonary pressure first.
2. Use compliance to find the change in Ptp.
3. Final Ptp = initial Ptp + ΔPtp = 9 + 3 = 12.

Distractors:

- "5" — Plausible but does not yield from the given data.
- "1" — Misses the addition of initial Ptp.
- "10" — Off by 2 from the correct calculation.

QUESTION 9

Stem: Compared to a gas-ventilated lung, a liquid-ventilated lung **\_**

Correct answer: Is more compliant

Explanation: A **liquid-filled (saline-ventilated) lung** has **higher compliance** than a gas-filled lung — because **eliminating the air-water interface eliminates surface tension**, which is the major contributor to elastic recoil [Guyton ch.38] [West]. With surface tension abolished, only tissue elasticity remains, and compliance roughly doubles. Saline-filled lungs also show **markedly less hysteresis** between inflation and deflation curves (because hysteresis is largely due to surfactant rearrangement at the air-water interface). Hence (c). (a) airway resistance would _increase_ in saline (much higher viscosity than air); (b) residual volume is determined by chest wall mechanics and wouldn't increase; (d) hysteresis is _reduced_, not increased.

Hints:

1. Recall the contributions to lung elastic recoil: surface tension + tissue elasticity.
2. Saline ventilation eliminates surface tension.
3. Without surface tension, compliance increases and hysteresis decreases.

Distractors:

- "Has reduced airway resistance" — Wrong direction (liquid is more viscous than air).
- "Has increased residual volume" — Not a relevant consequence of saline filling.
- "Has more hysteresis" — Wrong direction; hysteresis is reduced.

QUESTION 10

Stem: The factor responsible for hysteresis is **\_**

Correct answer: Surfactant

Explanation: **Hysteresis** is the difference between inflation and deflation P-V curves of the lung [Guyton ch.38]. The principal cause is **surfactant**: during inflation, surfactant must be re-spread on newly formed air-liquid surface area (requiring more pressure to overcome the higher surface tension); during deflation, surfactant is more concentrated (lower surface tension, less recoil pressure required to maintain volume). This produces the characteristic hysteresis loop. Saline ventilation (eliminating the surfactant-air-liquid interface) abolishes hysteresis. Elastic recoil contributes to the overall P-V relation but is symmetric and does not produce hysteresis. Hence (b).

Hints:

1. Recall the cellular cause of lung P-V hysteresis.
2. Surfactant rearrangement at the air-liquid interface produces hysteresis.
3. Saline ventilation eliminates hysteresis because it eliminates the air-liquid interface.

Distractors:

- "Elastic recoil" — Symmetric force; doesn't cause hysteresis.
- "Negative intrapleural pressure" — Doesn't cause hysteresis.
- "All of the above" — Only surfactant is the principal cause.

QUESTION 13

Stem: What is specific compliance of lung?

Correct answer: Compliance/FRC

Explanation: **Specific compliance** is **compliance normalised to lung volume** — typically to **FRC (functional residual capacity)** [Guyton ch.38] [West]. The formula is **specific compliance = lung compliance / FRC**. This normalisation allows comparison of compliance between lungs of different sizes (e.g. child vs adult, healthy vs reduced-volume lungs). Although raw compliance differs greatly between an infant and an adult, _specific_ compliance is similar — reflecting comparable intrinsic elastic properties. Specific compliance is reduced in fibrosis and increased in emphysema even after volume normalisation.

Hints:

1. Recall the definition of specific compliance.
2. Compliance is normalised by lung volume (FRC).
3. The formula is C/FRC.

Distractors:

- "FRC/Compliance" — Inverted.
- "Compliance/TLC" — Some texts normalise by TLC; FRC is the more common reference.
- "Compliance/TV" — Tidal volume is too small for normalisation.

QUESTION 14

Stem: Work done in quiet breathing is (kg-m/min)**\_\_**

Correct answer: 0.5

Explanation: The **work of quiet breathing** is approximately **0.3–0.5 kg-m/min**, representing about 2–3% of resting energy expenditure [Guyton ch.38]. This work is divided into: **compliance work (~65%)** — to overcome elastic recoil of lung and chest wall, including surface tension; **tissue resistance work** — overcoming viscoelastic resistance of tissues; **airway resistance work** — overcoming friction of air movement. Total quiet-breathing work is dominated by compliance/elastic work in healthy lungs. Heavy exercise can raise work of breathing 5- to 10-fold.

Hints:

1. Recall the textbook value for work of quiet breathing.
2. The number is in the half kg-m/min range.
3. 0.5 kg-m/min is the standard value.

Distractors:

- "0.1" — Too low.
- "10" — Range for very heavy exercise.
- "2" — Too high for quiet breathing.

[NEEDS VERIFICATION: The exact figure 0.5 kg-m/min varies in different texts; some quote 0.3-0.5. The MBBS textbook convention is broadly in the half-unit range.]

QUESTION 15

Stem: Work of breathing is mostly due to**\_**

Correct answer: Surface tension elasticity

Explanation: The work of breathing in quiet breathing is dominated by **compliance (elastic) work**, which has two components: **surface tension elasticity** at the alveolar air-liquid interface (~65–70% of total elastic work) and **tissue elasticity** (~30–35%) [Guyton ch.38]. Hence **surface tension is the largest single component**. **Airway resistance** and **tissue viscous resistance** contribute the resistive work, which is smaller in quiet breathing but rises substantially with airflow obstruction or high ventilation rates. Surfactant reduces surface tension and dramatically lowers the work of breathing — its absence in NRDS markedly increases work and energy cost.

Hints:

1. Recall the components of work of breathing.
2. Compliance work dominates quiet breathing; within it, surface tension is dominant.
3. Surface tension elasticity is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Tissue elasticity" — Smaller than surface tension component.
- (c), (d) "Viscous resistance", "Airway resistance" — Smaller in quiet breathing.

QUESTION 16

Stem: If the lung is allowed to undergo elastic recoil without the chest wall, the resultant volume of the lung would be?

Correct answer: Minimum volume

Explanation: When the lung is removed from the chest cavity (or the chest wall is opened — pneumothorax), the lung's elastic recoil is no longer opposed by the outward-pulling chest wall, and the lung **collapses to its minimum volume** — but not to zero, because trapped air remains in alveoli held open by surface tension and structural support [Guyton ch.38]. **Minimum volume** (~10–15% of TLC) is greater than zero and less than residual volume. The clinical correlate: in tension pneumothorax, the affected lung collapses toward its minimum volume.

Hints:

1. Recall what happens when lung elastic recoil is unopposed.
2. The lung collapses but does not empty completely — surface tension traps some air.
3. The resulting volume is the minimum volume.

Distractors:

- "Zero" — Lung does not empty completely.
- "FRC" — Maintained by chest wall opposition.
- "Residual volume" — Set by chest wall, larger than minimum.

QUESTION 17

Stem: Difference in trajectory between inspiratory loop and the expiratory loop in the curve is due to:

Correct answer: Difference in conc. of surfactant during inspiration and expiration

Explanation: This is the **hysteresis question** in different language. The difference between inspiratory and expiratory P-V trajectories (hysteresis) is caused by **changes in surfactant surface concentration during the breath cycle** [Guyton ch.38]: during inflation, surfactant must spread over newly formed air-water interface (more dilute, higher surface tension, more pressure required for a given volume change); during deflation, surfactant is compressed (more concentrated, lower surface tension, lower pressure for the same volume). This concentration difference is the principal cause of hysteresis. Hence (b). Saline ventilation abolishes hysteresis by eliminating the air-liquid interface (Q9).

Hints:

1. Recall the cause of P-V hysteresis.
2. Surfactant surface concentration changes during inflation vs deflation.
3. The difference in surfactant behaviour drives hysteresis.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — Misrepresent the cause of hysteresis.

QUESTION 18

Stem: The following diagram shows the depth of respiration of a 45-year old man who suffered a head injury in an automobile accident. Identify the pattern of breathing.

Correct answer: Cheyne-Stokes breathing

Explanation: **Cheyne-Stokes breathing** is a periodic breathing pattern characterised by **alternating crescendo-decrescendo waxing and waning of tidal volume** with intervening apnoea — produced by oscillating loop in the respiratory control system, often due to **delayed feedback** between blood gases and chemoreceptors [Guyton ch.42]. Causes: **brainstem injury, congestive heart failure (delayed circulation), high altitude, opioid overdose**. The vignette (45-year-old, head injury) fits this pattern. **Biot breathing** is irregular gasping (medullary lesions). **Apnoea** is cessation of breathing. **Hyperpnoea** is increased depth without periodicity.

Hints:

1. Recall the named patterns of abnormal respiration.
2. Cheyne-Stokes is periodic crescendo-decrescendo with apnoea.
3. Brainstem injury is a classical cause.

Distractors:

- "Apnea" — Cessation, not periodic.
- "Biot breathing" — Irregular gasping, no crescendo-decrescendo.
- "Hyperpnea" — Increased depth, not periodic.
