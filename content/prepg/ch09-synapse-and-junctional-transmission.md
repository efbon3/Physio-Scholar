QUESTION 2

Stem: All of the following are true regarding chemical synapses except\_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: Protoplasmic continuity between pre- and post- synaptic membrane ensures faster conduction

Explanation: **Chemical synapses** are the dominant type in the mammalian nervous system [Guyton ch.46]. Their hallmarks: (a) most common type — true; (b) neurotransmitter is synthesised in the presynaptic neurone (somatic synthesis for peptides; local terminal synthesis for small-molecule transmitters) — true; (d) chemical synapses conduct only orthodromically (pre → post), because release machinery is presynaptic and receptors are postsynaptic — true. Hence (c) is false.

QUESTION 3

Stem: In a typical chemical synaptic cleft, the extracellular distance between the presynaptic plasma membrane and the postsynaptic plasma membrane is approximately\_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: 20-40 nm

Explanation: The synaptic cleft of a typical chemical synapse is **~20–30 nm** wide (Guyton specifies "200 to 300 angstroms" = 20–30 nm) [Guyton ch.46]. The cleft is filled with extracellular matrix containing neurotransmitter-degrading enzymes (e.g. acetylcholinesterase at the NMJ, with cleft of ~50 nm being slightly wider). Diffusion across this small distance takes <1 ms, allowing neurotransmitter to reach postsynaptic receptors quickly. By contrast, gap-junction electrical synapses have a separation of ~2–4 nm with connexon channels spanning the gap. The MCQ option (b) 20–40 nm is the textbook range that includes the typical chemical synapse.

Hints:

1. Recall the textbook width of the chemical synaptic cleft in nanometres.
2. The cleft must be small enough for transmitter diffusion in <1 ms but big enough to prevent direct cytoplasmic connection.
3. The standard figure is in tens of nanometres.

Distractors:

- "2-4 nm" — Reveals confusion with electrical synapse / gap junction separation, which is ~2–4 nm.
- "200-400 nm" — Reveals scale confusion of one order of magnitude.
- "2-4 µm" — Reveals scale confusion of three orders of magnitude.

QUESTION 6

Stem: Which of the following is true regarding metabotropic neurotransmitters?

Correct answer: They act through second messenger systems

Explanation: **Metabotropic neurotransmission** is mediated by **G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs)** that activate intracellular signalling cascades and **second messengers** (cAMP, IP₃, DAG, Ca²⁺) rather than by directly opening ion channels [Guyton ch.46]. Examples include muscarinic ACh receptors, GABAᴮ, dopamine D₁/D₂, β-adrenergic, opioid μ/δ/κ, metabotropic glutamate (mGluR) and serotonin 5-HT₂. The signalling is slower (tens of milliseconds to seconds), often modulatory rather than directly excitatory or inhibitory, and can affect gene transcription. By contrast, **ionotropic** receptors are themselves the ion channel and produce fast (millisecond) responses — these include nicotinic ACh, GABAᴬ (Cl⁻), glycine (Cl⁻), NMDA/AMPA/kainate (Na⁺/K⁺/Ca²⁺) receptors. Options (b) and (c) describe ionotropic receptors. Option (d) confuses metabotropic action with synaptic-vesicle inhibition.

Hints:

1. Distinguish ionotropic from metabotropic receptor pharmacology.
2. Ionotropic = ion channel itself; metabotropic = receptor + downstream messengers.
3. The defining feature of metabotropic action is intracellular signalling via second messengers.

Distractors:

- "They bind to cationic channels and increase influx of Na" — Reveals the misconception that metabotropic and ionotropic are interchangeable.
- "They bind to anionic channels and increase influx of Cl" — Same misconception.
- "They inhibit the fusion of synaptic vesicle with the synaptic cleft" — Reveals confusion with botulinum toxin or other vesicle-fusion blockers.

QUESTION 12

Stem: Excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP) is due to the influx of **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Na+

Explanation: A typical **EPSP** is generated when an excitatory neurotransmitter (e.g. glutamate at AMPA receptors, ACh at nicotinic) opens ligand-gated cation channels permeable to **Na⁺ and K⁺** [Guyton ch.46]. Although both ions can flow, the much larger inward Na⁺ driving force at resting membrane potential (V*m ≈ −70 mV; E_Na ≈ +60 mV → driving force ≈ −130 mV inward for Na⁺, while E_K ≈ −90 mV → driving force ≈ +20 mV for K⁺ outward) makes the \_net* flux strongly inward Na⁺-dominated. The result is depolarisation of the postsynaptic membrane — the EPSP. Cl⁻ influx produces inhibition (IPSP), Mg²⁺ blocks NMDARs at rest but does not flux during EPSP, K⁺ efflux hyperpolarises.

Hints:

1. Identify the cation that is most strongly driven into the cell at resting potential.
2. EPSP is depolarising — what cation entry depolarises?
3. Compare driving forces for Na⁺ and K⁺ at resting membrane potential.

Distractors:

- "K+" — Reveals the misconception that K⁺ influx mediates EPSP. K⁺ has only a small driving force at rest and tends to _exit_; K⁺ efflux causes IPSP.
- "Mg2+" — Reveals the misconception that Mg²⁺ flux contributes. Mg²⁺ blocks NMDA at rest; it doesn't flux during typical EPSP.
- "Cl-" — Reveals the misconception that Cl⁻ influx is excitatory. Cl⁻ influx is inhibitory (IPSP) in most adult neurones (because E_Cl is near or below RMP).

QUESTION 13

Stem: Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential (IPSP) is due to influx of **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Cl-

Explanation: The classical fast **IPSP** is mediated by **GABAᴬ receptors** (or **glycine receptors**) — ligand-gated **Cl⁻ channels** [Guyton ch.46]. Opening these channels allows Cl⁻ to flux down its electrochemical gradient. In adult neurones, E*Cl is typically slightly more negative than V_m (−70 to −80 mV vs −65 mV), so Cl⁻ influx **hyperpolarises** the cell or stabilises V_m at a level below threshold. Even when E_Cl ≈ V_m and Cl⁻ flux is small, increased Cl⁻ conductance **shunts** the depolarising effect of concurrent EPSPs, producing inhibition. Slow IPSPs via GABAᴮ open K⁺ channels (causing K⁺ \_efflux*) but the question's "influx" cue points specifically to Cl⁻ entry.

Hints:

1. Recall the ions involved in fast inhibitory transmission.
2. The two main fast inhibitory transmitters open the same ion channel.
3. Cl⁻ influx hyperpolarises the membrane in most adult neurones.

Distractors:

- "Na+" — Reveals the misconception that any influx is inhibitory. Na⁺ influx is excitatory (depolarising).
- "K+" — Reveals the misconception that K⁺ influx hyperpolarises. K⁺ _efflux_ hyperpolarises; K⁺ influx is rare physiologically and would depolarise.
- "Ca2+" — Reveals the misconception that Ca²⁺ influx is inhibitory. Ca²⁺ influx in postsynaptic neurones triggers various effects but does not produce IPSPs.

QUESTION 17

Stem: Renshaw cell inhibition is \***\*\_\_\*\***.

Correct answer: Feedback inhibition

Explanation: **Renshaw cells** are inhibitory interneurones in the ventral horn of the spinal cord [Guyton ch.46]. They receive **collateral branches** from α-motor neurone axons as those axons exit the ventral root, are activated when the motor neurone fires, and release **glycine** onto the _same_ and _neighbouring_ α-motor neurones, inhibiting them. This is the textbook example of **recurrent (feedback) inhibition**: the output of the motor neurone feeds back through the Renshaw cell to inhibit the same neurone (and adjacent ones). The function is to limit excessive firing rates and to sharpen the spatial pattern of motor unit activation. **Feedforward inhibition** describes excitatory input to a target neurone _together with_ parallel input to inhibitory interneurones that inhibit the same target — common in cerebellar circuits and hippocampus.

Hints:

1. Distinguish feedback (recurrent) from feedforward inhibition by the timing relative to the principal neurone firing.
2. Renshaw cells receive an axon collateral from the same motor neurone they inhibit.
3. The output activates an inhibitor of the source — that defines feedback.

Distractors:

- "Feedback facilitation" — Reveals the misconception that Renshaw cells facilitate motor neurones. They inhibit them.
- "Feed forward inhibition" — Reveals the misconception that Renshaw inhibition is feedforward. The collateral arrangement makes it feedback.
- "Indirect inhibition" — Plausible but the textbook term is "recurrent" or "feedback".

QUESTION 18

Stem: Feedforward inhibition is seen in \***\*\_\_\*\***.

Correct answer: Cerebellum

Explanation: **Feedforward inhibition** is a circuit motif in which an excitatory input to a target neurone is accompanied by a parallel excitatory input to an inhibitory interneurone that synapses onto the _same target_, briefly inhibiting it [Guyton ch.46]. The **cerebellum** is the classical site: mossy fibres excite granule cells; granule-cell axons (parallel fibres) excite Purkinje cells _and_ simultaneously excite basket and stellate (inhibitory) cells, which then inhibit the same Purkinje cells. The result is sharp temporal control of Purkinje firing. **Renshaw cells** mediate _feedback_ (recurrent) inhibition (Q17), not feedforward. Pituitary and medulla are not standard textbook examples of feedforward inhibition.

Hints:

1. Recall the cerebellar microcircuit.
2. Mossy fibre → granule cell → parallel fibre → Purkinje (excitatory) AND parallel fibre → inhibitory interneurone → Purkinje (feedforward inhibition).
3. The CNS region where this circuit is paradigmatic is the cerebellum.

- "Pituitary gland" — Reveals the misconception that endocrine glands have feedforward inhibition circuits. They have hormonal feedback, not neural feedforward inhibition.
- "Renshaw cell" — Reveals confusion of feedback and feedforward inhibition. Renshaw cells mediate feedback inhibition.

QUESTION 19

QUESTION 20

QUESTION 21

Explanation: When a postsynaptic effector loses its presynaptic input, the target cell **upregulates receptors** for the lost neurotransmitter — and may also redistribute them more widely along the membrane — over days to weeks [Guyton ch.7]. The resulting **denervation supersensitivity** means that the denervated effector responds _more strongly_ to the missing transmitter (or to exogenously applied agonist) than the normal innervated tissue. In this patient, the right pupillary dilator muscle has lost its sympathetic supply; its α-adrenergic receptors are upregulated; topical sympathomimetic produces an _exaggerated_ dilation in the right eye compared to the left. This is in fact a clinical test (cocaine and apraclonidine drops are used to demonstrate the same phenomenon in **Horner's syndrome**).

Hints:

1. Recall what happens to receptor density on a postsynaptic effector after denervation.
2. Loss of input → receptor upregulation → exaggerated response to exogenous agonist.
3. The phenomenon has a specific clinical name involving "supersensitivity".

Distractors:

- "The sympathetic nerve to the right eye had regenerated" — If regenerated, the responses should be similar bilaterally, not exaggerated on the right.
- "The parasympathetic nerve supply to the right eye remained intact and compensated for the loss of the sympathetic nerve" — Parasympathetic supply causes constriction (M3), not dilation. Cannot explain exaggerated dilation.
- "The left eye also had nerve damage and so was not responding as expected" — The left eye showed normal response; the abnormality is the _exaggerated_ right-eye response.

QUESTION 22

Stem: Which of the following statements is not true about neurotransmitters?

Correct answer: They are released on repolarization of the nerve

Explanation: The classical Eccles/Goodman criteria for a neurotransmitter [Guyton ch.46]:

- The substance must be present in the presynaptic terminal — true.
- The presynaptic neurone must be able to synthesise (or take up) the substance — true.
- Postsynaptic receptors specific to the substance must be present — true.
- Neurotransmitters are released **upon depolarisation** of the presynaptic terminal (action potential → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ entry → vesicle fusion), **not upon repolarisation** — _false_.

A fifth criterion (often cited): exogenous application should mimic the synaptic effect; and a sixth: a mechanism for terminating action (degradation, reuptake, diffusion).

Hints:

1. Recall the criteria for identifying a substance as a neurotransmitter.
2. Release is triggered by depolarisation, not repolarisation.
3. The exception reverses the polarity timing.

Distractors:

- "They must be present at the pre-synaptic membrane" — Correctly true (criterion 1).
- "Neurons must be able to synthesize the substance" — Correctly true (criterion 2).
- "Specific receptors must be present on the post-synaptic membrane" — Correctly true (criterion 3).

QUESTION 23

Stem: Neurotransmitter can be degraded by all except**\_**

Correct answer: Autoreceptor

Explanation: After release, neurotransmitter action is terminated by [Guyton ch.46]: **(a) diffusion** out of the cleft; **(b) enzymatic inactivation** in the cleft (e.g. acetylcholinesterase for ACh; MAO/COMT for catecholamines after reuptake); **(c) reuptake** into the presynaptic terminal or surrounding glia (transporters such as SERT for serotonin, NET for NA, DAT for dopamine, EAATs for glutamate, GAT for GABA). **Autoreceptors** are _presynaptic receptors_ that detect the released transmitter and feedback-regulate further release — they _modulate release_ but do **not degrade or terminate** the transmitter that has already been released. Hence (d) is the exception.

Hints:

1. List the canonical mechanisms of transmitter clearance from the cleft.
2. Diffusion, enzymatic breakdown and reuptake are the three modes of clearance.
3. The exception is a _receptor_, not a clearance mechanism.

Distractors:

- "Can diffuse out of synaptic cleft" — Correctly a clearance mechanism.
- "Enzymes inactivate it" — Correctly a clearance mechanism.
- "Reuptake into glial cell" — Correctly a clearance mechanism.

QUESTION 25

The other matches are correct: gap junctions mediate electrical transmission (a); Renshaw cells produce recurrent (feedback) inhibition (b); synaptic vesicles dock and fuse at the active zone of the presynaptic terminal (c). Hence (d) is the mismatched pair.

QUESTION 26

Stem: Which of the following electrophysiologic events is correctly paired with the change in ionic currents causing the event?

Correct answer: Endplate potential and an increase in Na+ conductance

Explanation: The **end-plate potential (EPP)** is produced by ACh binding to nicotinic AChR, opening a non-selective cation channel that conducts both Na⁺ and K⁺ — net inward Na⁺ flux dominates at resting V*m, depolarising the membrane [Guyton ch.7]. Hence (c) "EPP and increase in Na⁺ conductance" is correctly paired. (a) is wrong: fast IPSPs are produced by \_opening* (not closing) Cl⁻ channels (GABAᴬ, glycine). (b) is wrong: fast EPSPs are produced by _increased_ (not decreased) cation conductance. (d) is wrong: presynaptic inhibition involves _opening_ (not closing) of K⁺ channels (via GABAᴮ → GIRK) or _closing_ of voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels — option (d)'s "closure of voltage-gated K⁺ channels" is the wrong direction.

Hints:

1. For each option, check the directionality of the ionic-conductance change.
2. EPP and EPSP are excitatory: cation conductance _increases_. IPSP: anion conductance increases (Cl⁻ open) or K⁺ conductance increases.
3. The correct pairing has both the event and the conductance change matched.

Distractors:

- "Fast inhibitory postsynaptic potentials (IPSPs) and closing of Cl- channels" — Reverses the direction. Fast IPSPs _open_ Cl⁻ channels.
- "Fast excitatory postsynaptic potentials (EPSPs) and an decrease in Ca2+ conductance" — Wrong ion (Na⁺ not Ca²⁺) and wrong direction.
- "Presynaptic inhibition and closure of voltage-gated K+ channels" — Reverses direction; presynaptic inhibition _opens_ K⁺ channels (or closes Ca²⁺).

QUESTION 27

Stem: How is an electrical synapse different from a chemical synapse?

Correct answer: Connexons are involved in electrical synapse

Explanation: **Electrical synapses** are formed by **gap junctions**, which consist of paired hemichannels called **connexons** (each made of six connexin subunits) on the apposing cell membranes; the connexons join end-to-end to form a continuous aqueous channel ~2 nm in diameter linking the two cytoplasms [Guyton ch.46]. Through these channels, ions and small molecules (≤1 kDa) flow directly, transmitting electrical signals with essentially no synaptic delay. Hence (c) is correct. (a) is wrong (electrical synapses _do_ have cytoplasmic continuity through connexons). (b) is wrong (electrical synapses are _bidirectional_, not unidirectional). (d) is wrong (electrical synapses have _less_ latent period than chemical, because there is no transmitter release/diffusion step).

Hints:

1. Recall the structural protein that forms gap junctions.
2. Connexins assemble into hexamers (connexons) that form half-channels in the cell membrane.
3. The unique feature of electrical synapses among the listed options is the connexon channel.

Distractors:

- "No cytoplasmic continuity between presynaptic and postsynaptic membrane" — Reversed. Electrical synapses have cytoplasmic continuity; chemical synapses don't.
- "Direction of impulse is unidirectional" — Reversed. Electrical synapses are bidirectional; chemical synapses are unidirectional.
- "Electrical synapse has more latent period than chemical synapse" — Reversed. Electrical synapses have minimal latent period.
