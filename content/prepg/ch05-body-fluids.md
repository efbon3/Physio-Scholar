QUESTION 1

Stem: In an average young adult male, what percentage of the bodyweight is water?

Correct answer: 60

Explanation: In a young adult male, **total body water (TBW) is approximately 60% of body weight** [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]. The figure varies with age, sex and adiposity. Adipose tissue has a very low water content (~10%), so individuals with greater adiposity have a smaller proportion of TBW. Adult females, with a higher relative adipose mass, average about 50%; obese individuals can drop below 50%. Newborns are at about 75%, and the elderly fall to 45–55% as lean mass declines [Costanzo ch.6]. The textbook **"60-40-20 rule"** is the practical first-year mnemonic: 60% body weight is total body water, 40% is intracellular fluid (ICF), and 20% is extracellular fluid (ECF) [Costanzo ch.6].

Hints:

1. Recall the canonical first-year teaching figure for adult male TBW.
2. The 60-40-20 mnemonic captures TBW, ICF and ECF as percentages of body weight.
3. The figure for the young adult male is the upper anchor of the rule.

Distractors:

- "40" — Reveals the misconception that 40% is total body water. 40% is the _intracellular_ fraction within the 60-40-20 rule.
- "50" — Reveals the misconception that all adults have 50% body water. Adult _females_ approximate 50%; young adult males are higher.
- "70" — Reveals the misconception that adult body composition resembles the neonatal figure (~75%). Adults have less water as a percentage of body weight.

QUESTION 2

Stem: If the total body water is 60% of the body weight, what are the proportions of ECF and ICF?

Correct answer: 20, 40

Explanation: Of total body water (60% of body weight), approximately **two-thirds is intracellular** and **one-third is extracellular** [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]. As percentages of body weight: ICF ≈ 40%, ECF ≈ 20%. The ECF is further subdivided into interstitial fluid (about 75% of ECF, ~15% of body weight) and plasma (about 25% of ECF, ~5% of body weight), with very small additional volumes in transcellular fluids (CSF, ocular, joint, peritoneal, etc.) [Costanzo ch.6]. The order in the answer follows the question's order — ECF first (20), then ICF (40).

Hints:

1. Recall the 60-40-20 rule and split TBW into ICF and ECF.
2. ICF is about two-thirds of TBW; ECF is about one-third.
3. The question asks for ECF first, then ICF.

Distractors:

- "30, 30" — Reveals the misconception that ICF and ECF split TBW equally. ICF dominates at roughly 2:1 over ECF.
- "40, 20" — Reveals reversed reading of which is ECF and which is ICF. Order matters in the question.
- "10, 50" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 3

Stem: A 60 kg adult male has a haematocrit reading of 40% and a plasma volume of 3L. What is his total blood volume in L?

Explanation: Haematocrit is the _cellular_ fraction of blood by volume; plasma is the _non-cellular_ fraction [Costanzo ch.6]. If haematocrit is 40%, plasma is 60% of total blood volume. So plasma volume / total blood volume = 0.6 → total blood volume = plasma volume / 0.6 = 3 L / 0.6 = **5 L**. This matches the typical adult value (~70 mL/kg × 60 kg = 4.2 L if calculated by weight, but actual blood volume of ~5 L for a 60 kg male is consistent with haematocrit of 40%).

Correct answer: 5

Hints:

1. Haematocrit gives the cell fraction; the plasma fraction is its complement.
2. Plasma volume divided by plasma fraction yields total blood volume.
3. With haematocrit 0.40, plasma is 0.60 of total — divide.

Distractors:

- "4" — Reveals the misconception that haematocrit gives the _plasma_ fraction. Mistakenly using 0.4 as the plasma fraction yields 7.5 L; using 0.6 as the cell fraction and dividing 3/0.4 yields 7.5; the wrong direction with 3 × 0.4 + 3 = 4.2 ≈ 4.
- "6" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "7" — Reveals the misconception that 3 L plasma corresponds to a haematocrit-corrected calculation as 3/0.4 ≈ 7.5. The student here uses plasma fraction as 0.4 instead of 0.6.

QUESTION 4

Stem: What is the volume of blood in ml, in a child per kg body weight?

Correct answer: 70-80

Explanation: Estimated blood volume per body weight varies with age [Guyton ch.25]: neonates ~80–90 mL/kg, infants ~75–80 mL/kg, **children ~70–80 mL/kg**, adults ~65–75 mL/kg, and obese adults somewhat lower (because adipose tissue contributes negligibly to blood volume). The reduction with age and adiposity reflects the increasing proportion of low-perfusion adipose mass relative to lean tissue. The figure 70–80 mL/kg is the most commonly used clinical estimate for paediatric blood volume calculations (e.g. when calculating maximum allowable blood loss before transfusion).

Hints:

1. Blood volume per kg is highest in newborns and falls into adulthood.
2. The paediatric range overlaps the adult range — the typical estimate is in the 70s of mL/kg.
3. Recall the value used for paediatric maximum-allowable-blood-loss calculations.

Distractors:

- "100-150" — Reveals the misconception that paediatric blood volume per kg is much greater than adult. The neonate is ~80–90, not 100–150.
- "150-200" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "200-150" — Reversed range; not a meaningful physiological number.

QUESTION 5

Stem: What is the water content of lean body mass is (in mL/100g of tissue)?

Correct answer: 70

Explanation: **Lean body mass (LBM)** — total body mass minus adipose tissue — is approximately **70% water by weight** (≈70 mL water per 100 g lean tissue) [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]. Adipose tissue, by contrast, is only ~10% water. Because LBM has a fixed water content while body fat is dehydrated, the relative proportion of TBW to total body weight depends almost entirely on the ratio of lean to adipose tissue. This is why men (less adipose, more LBM) average 60% TBW and women average 50%, and why adiposity-corrected formulae (Watson, Hume) are used clinically when precise TBW estimates are required.

Hints:

1. Distinguish water content of lean tissue from water as a fraction of total body weight.
2. Lean tissue is much wetter than adipose tissue — the percentage is closer to 70 than to 60.
3. The 60% TBW figure for whole body weight reflects dilution of high-water lean tissue by very-low-water fat.

Distractors:

- "40" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "50" — Reveals the misconception that LBM water content equals female TBW percentage. Female TBW is 50% of _total_ body weight; LBM water content is 70% of _lean_ tissue mass.
- "60" — Reveals the misconception that water content of LBM equals TBW percentage of body weight in young adult male. The two are not the same — LBM is wetter.

QUESTION 6

Stem: How do we measure the volume of distribution of body fluid compartments?

Correct answer: Dye dilution method

Explanation: The volume of any body fluid compartment is measured using the **indicator dilution principle** (also called the dye dilution method): a known mass M of an indicator that distributes only within the compartment of interest is injected, allowed to equilibrate, and a sample is assayed for its concentration C; the volume of distribution is V = (M − amount excreted) / C [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]. The marker chosen depends on which compartment is being measured: heavy water (D₂O) or tritiated water (³H₂O) → total body water; mannitol, inulin or sodium thiosulfate → ECF; ¹²⁵I-albumin or Evans blue → plasma volume [Guyton ch.25]. ICF and interstitial fluid are not directly measurable — they are calculated by subtraction. The **Kety method** (or Kety-Schmidt method) measures cerebral blood flow using nitrous oxide. **Fick's principle** measures cardiac output. Neither is used for compartment volumes.

Hints:

1. The general principle for measuring a fluid compartment volume is mass-divided-by-concentration of an equilibrated marker.
2. Distinguish indicator dilution (compartment volume) from Fick's principle (organ blood flow) and Kety method (cerebral blood flow).
3. The technique is named after the act of injecting and tracking a tracer.

Distractors:

- "Kety method" — Reveals the misconception that Kety-Schmidt measures fluid compartments. It measures cerebral blood flow using N₂O.
- "Fick's principle" — Reveals the misconception that the Fick principle is for fluid compartment volumes. It is for organ blood flow / cardiac output.
- "None" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 7

Stem: 500 mg of mannitol was injected out of which 10% is excreted. After equilibrium, plasma concentration is 3.2 mg/100 ml. Calculate ECF Volume (L).

Correct answer: 14

Explanation: Volume of distribution V = (mass injected − mass excreted) / equilibrium concentration [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]. Mass remaining = 500 mg − 10% excreted = 500 − 50 = **450 mg**. Plasma concentration = 3.2 mg/100 mL = **0.032 mg/mL** = **32 mg/L**. Volume = 450 mg ÷ 32 mg/L ≈ **14.06 L** ≈ **14 L**. Mannitol distributes throughout the ECF (it does not cross cell membranes), so this volume of distribution corresponds to the ECF volume — a value consistent with the textbook ~14 L ECF for a 70-kg adult.

Hints:

1. Apply the indicator dilution formula, remembering to subtract the excreted fraction first.
2. Convert the concentration from mg/100 mL to mg/L before dividing.
3. (Mass in − mass excreted) ÷ concentration gives the answer in litres.

Distractors:

- "10" — Reveals an arithmetic error or omission of unit conversion.
- "42" — Reveals failure to subtract the excreted fraction (or a unit-conversion error).
- "52" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 8

Stem: 10 g of inulin is injected IV and after equilibrium the concentration of inulin in plasma is 50mg/100ml. If 10% of the inulin is excreted, what is the ECF volume?

Correct answer: 18 L

Explanation: Same indicator dilution formula [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]. Mass remaining = 10 g − 10% = **9 g** = **9000 mg**. Plasma concentration = 50 mg/100 mL = **500 mg/L**. Volume = 9000 ÷ 500 = **18 L**. Inulin distributes only in the ECF (it cannot cross cell membranes and is freely filtered at the glomerulus), so its volume of distribution equals the ECF volume. Note that this calculated value (18 L) is somewhat larger than the typical adult ECF (~14 L) because inulin distributes into the very-slow-equilibrating transcellular fluids, but the exam-level answer is the calculated 18 L.

Hints:

1. Same formula as Q7 — convert units carefully and subtract excreted mass.
2. Convert 50 mg/100 mL to mg/L (multiply by 10).
3. 9000 mg ÷ 500 mg/L = 18 L.

Distractors:

- "1.8 L" — Reveals a unit-conversion error of one order of magnitude.
- "2.4 L" — Reveals an arithmetic error.
- "24 L" — Reveals failure to subtract the excreted fraction (10/0.5 ≈ would give 20, not exactly 24, but reflects the _not-subtracting_ family of error).

QUESTION 9

Stem: Of the following compartment and indicator combinations which is true?

Correct answer: ECF volume-Sodium thiosulphate

Explanation: Marker–compartment matching [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]:

- **Total body water**: tritiated water (³H₂O), deuterium oxide (D₂O), antipyrine. _Can_ be measured directly.
- **Extracellular fluid**: mannitol, inulin, sodium thiosulphate, sucrose, sulfate (substances that cross capillary walls but not cell membranes). **Sodium thiosulphate is a classical ECF marker** [Guyton ch.25].
- **Plasma volume**: ¹²⁵I-labelled albumin, Evans blue (T-1824, which binds albumin and is restricted to plasma).
- **Interstitial fluid** and **ICF and RBC volume** are _not_ directly measurable; they are calculated by subtraction. Hence option (c) is the correct match.

Hints:

1. Match each indicator to the compartment by tracking which membranes the indicator can cross.
2. ECF markers cannot enter cells but cross capillaries; plasma markers (large protein-bound) cannot cross capillaries.
3. ICF and RBC volumes are derived by difference — they cannot be measured by tracer dilution directly.

Distractors:

- "Plasma volume-Tritium oxide" — Reveals the misconception that any water tracer measures plasma. Tritiated water enters every compartment — it measures TBW.
- "RBC volume-Evans' blue" — Reveals the misconception that Evans blue enters red cells. It binds albumin and stays in plasma.
- "Total body water-Cannot be measured directly" — Reveals the misconception that TBW is inaccessible. TBW _is_ measured directly using D₂O or ³H₂O.

QUESTION 10

Stem: A dye X which measures the plasma volume is replaced by dye Y which can diffuse out of the capillaries. Which of the following is true regarding the newly measured plasma volume?

Correct answer: It is falsely high

Explanation: Volume of distribution = (mass remaining) / (equilibrium plasma concentration) [Costanzo ch.6]. If a dye originally restricted to plasma is replaced by one that _also leaks into the interstitium_, the same injected mass distributes into a larger total volume → plasma equilibrium concentration falls → calculated _plasma volume_ (mass / concentration) is artefactually _higher_ than the true plasma volume. The technique only gives a true plasma volume if the indicator is fully retained in the plasma compartment (the rationale for using albumin-bound Evans blue or ¹²⁵I-albumin).

Hints:

1. The volume calculated from indicator dilution depends on whether the indicator is confined to the intended compartment.
2. If the indicator escapes into a larger volume, the measured concentration falls and the apparent volume rises.
3. Identify the direction of the error when the dye exceeds the intended boundary.

Distractors:

- "It is falsely low" — Reveals the misconception that more distribution gives smaller volume estimate. The opposite is true — wider distribution dilutes the indicator and inflates the apparent volume.
- "It remains the same" — Reveals the misconception that compartment boundaries are irrelevant. The boundary defines the volume measured.
- "Insufficient information given" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 11

Stem: Interstitial fluid volume is best measured using a combination of which two dyes?

Correct answer: Inulin and Evans blue

Explanation: Interstitial fluid (ISF) volume cannot be measured directly because no indicator distributes uniquely in the interstitium [Costanzo ch.6]. It is calculated as: **ISF volume = ECF volume − Plasma volume**. ECF volume is measured with an ECF-selective marker that crosses capillaries but not cell membranes — **inulin** (or mannitol or sodium thiosulphate). Plasma volume is measured with a plasma-restricted marker — **Evans blue** (which binds albumin) or ¹²⁵I-albumin. The combination **inulin (for ECF) and Evans blue (for plasma)** is the classical pairing to obtain ISF by subtraction.

Hints:

1. Interstitial fluid volume cannot be measured directly — what two compartments give it by subtraction?
2. ECF − Plasma = Interstitial fluid; pair the indicator for each compartment.
3. The first dye is an ECF marker; the second is a plasma marker.

Distractors:

- "Evans blue and D₂O" — Reveals the misconception that TBW − Plasma gives ISF. That difference is ICF + ISF, not ISF alone.
- "Tritium oxide and Inulin" — Reveals the misconception that TBW − ECF gives ISF. That difference is ICF, not ISF.
- "D₂O and Mannitol" — Same misconception as (c).

QUESTION 12

Stem: Which of the following is higher in the ECF compared to ICF?

Correct answer: pH

Explanation: Standard ICF/ECF composition [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]:

- **K⁺**: ICF ≈ 140 mmol/L vs ECF ≈ 4 mmol/L (ICF >> ECF).
- **Proteins**: ICF ≈ 4 mmol/L (high), ECF ≈ 1 mmol/L; proteins are _higher_ in ICF.
- **Phosphate**: ICF ≈ 75 mmol/L (mostly organic phosphates) vs ECF ≈ 2 mmol/L (ICF >> ECF).
- **pH**: ICF ≈ 7.0–7.2 vs ECF (arterial blood) ≈ 7.40 (ECF _higher pH_, i.e. less acidic).

Of the listed quantities, only **pH** is higher in ECF than in ICF [Costanzo ch.6]. The intracellular environment is slightly more acidic because of metabolic CO₂ production, lactate, and other organic acids generated within cells.

Hints:

1. Tabulate ICF vs ECF concentrations for each listed species.
2. Three of the listed quantities are higher in ICF; one is higher in ECF.
3. The intracellular environment is more acidic than the extracellular — what does that imply for pH?

Distractors:

- "K+" — Reveals the misconception that the dominant cation outside the cell is K⁺. Na⁺ dominates ECF; K⁺ dominates ICF.
- "Proteins" — Reveals the misconception that plasma is the protein-rich compartment. Plasma proteins (~7 g/dL) are real, but on a _molar_ basis ICF protein is greater.
- "Phosphate" — Reveals the misconception that organic phosphate stays out of cells. Most cellular phosphate is intracellular as ATP, ADP, organic phosphates and inorganic phosphate buffer.

QUESTION 13

Stem: Which of the following is the most abundant intracellular anion?

Correct answer: Phosphate

Explanation: The major _intracellular_ anions, in approximate order of abundance, are **phosphate** (mostly as organic phosphates in ATP, ADP, phosphocreatine, nucleic acids, phospholipid headgroups) and **proteins** (as anionic side chains at physiological pH) [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]. **Phosphate** is the most abundant intracellular anion (~75 mmol/L). **Proteins** are abundant by mass but less so on a per-mole-of-charge basis. **Chloride** is the dominant _extracellular_ anion (~108 mmol/L in ECF) but is low in ICF (~4–10 mmol/L). **Bicarbonate** is moderate in both compartments (~10 mmol/L ICF; ~24 mmol/L ECF) but is not the dominant intracellular anion.

Hints:

1. List intracellular anions and rank by concentration.
2. Cells are rich in compounds with phosphate groups — ATP, nucleic acids, phospholipids.
3. The dominant intracellular anion is the one carried by the energy currency of the cell.

Distractors:

- "Protein" — Reveals the misconception that intracellular proteins are the major anion. Proteins contribute substantial negative charge but on a molar basis phosphate dominates.
- "Chloride" — Reveals the misconception that chloride is the dominant anion in every compartment. Cl⁻ is the dominant ECF anion, not ICF.
- "Bicarbonate" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 14

Stem: How many grams of NaCl make 1 mole of NaCl?

Correct answer: 58.5

Explanation: One mole equals the molecular mass of the compound expressed in grams [Costanzo ch.1]. Atomic masses: **Na = 23.0**; **Cl = 35.5**. Therefore the molecular mass of NaCl = 23.0 + 35.5 = **58.5 g/mol**. Hence 1 mole of NaCl = **58.5 g**. This number underlies practical preparations: 0.9% (w/v) saline contains 9 g/L NaCl ≈ 154 mmol/L NaCl ≈ 308 mOsm/L (Na⁺ + Cl⁻ each contribute 154 mOsm/L) — the basis for "isotonic" saline being approximately iso-osmotic with plasma (~285–295 mOsm/kg).

Hints:

1. Recall the atomic masses of sodium and chlorine.
2. Add them to get the molecular mass of NaCl.
3. The value should be just under 60 g/mol.

Distractors:

- "56.5" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "57.5" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "50.5" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 15

Stem: How much is one equivalent of calcium (molecular weight = 40)?

Correct answer: 20g

Explanation: One **equivalent (Eq)** of an ion is the mass that contains one mole of unit charge: equivalent mass = atomic mass / valence [Costanzo ch.1]. Calcium has a valence of **+2**, so 1 Eq Ca²⁺ = 40 / 2 = **20 g**. Equivalently, 1 mole of Ca²⁺ contains 2 equivalents of charge. The unit "equivalent" (and milliequivalent, mEq) is used in clinical chemistry to express ion concentration on a charge basis, because it directly reflects electroneutrality and ionic interaction. Plasma Ca²⁺ ≈ 2.5 mmol/L = 5 mEq/L.

Hints:

1. Recall the definition of an equivalent: mass per unit charge.
2. Divide molecular mass by valence to obtain equivalent mass.
3. For divalent calcium, divide 40 by 2.

Distractors:

- "30g" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "40g" — Reveals the misconception that 1 equivalent equals 1 mole. They are equal only for monovalent ions.
- "50g" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 16

Stem: The unit expressing the concentration of osmotically active particles is **\_**

Correct answer: Osmole

Explanation: One **osmole** is the number of osmotically active particles into which one mole of solute dissociates in solution [Costanzo ch.1]. For a non-dissociating solute (e.g. glucose), 1 mole = 1 osmole. For a fully dissociating uni-univalent salt (e.g. NaCl), 1 mole → 2 osmoles (one Na⁺ + one Cl⁻). For CaCl₂, 1 mole → 3 osmoles. **Osmolarity** = osmoles per litre of solution; **osmolality** = osmoles per kilogram of solvent (water). The osmole is the appropriate unit because osmotic pressure depends on the _number_ of dissolved particles per unit volume (or mass) of solvent, regardless of their identity.

Hints:

1. The unit must count _particles_ in solution, not mass or charge.
2. Mole counts molecules; equivalent counts charge; the unit needed counts osmotically active particles.
3. The unit is named for the phenomenon of osmosis.

Distractors:

- "Mole" — Reveals the misconception that mole and osmole are interchangeable. They are equal only for non-dissociating solutes.
- "Equivalent" — Reveals the misconception that osmotic and electric units are equivalent. Equivalent counts charge, not particles.
- "Valency" — Reveals the misconception that valency is a concentration unit. Valency is a property of an ion, not a unit.

QUESTION 17

Stem: A female patient was infused with a solution which leads to rapid lysis of the RBCs. Which solution was most likely infused?

Correct answer: 300 mMol/L urea

Explanation: Cell lysis depends on **effective (tonicity) osmolarity**, which counts only solutes that _cannot_ freely cross the cell membrane [Costanzo ch.1] [Guyton ch.25]. Solutes that can freely cross — such as **urea** — distribute equally on both sides of the membrane and contribute to total osmolarity but not to effective osmolarity. **300 mmol/L urea** is iso-osmotic with plasma (~290 mOsm/L) but **hypotonic** in effective tonicity terms — water enters the cell down the gradient set by impermeant intracellular solutes, and red blood cells swell and lyse [Costanzo ch.1]. **150 mmol/L NaCl** dissociates fully into 300 mOsm/L of effectively impermeant ions — isotonic, no lysis. **300 mmol/L mannitol** is impermeant — isotonic, no lysis. **350 mmol/L mannitol** is impermeant and hypertonic — RBCs would shrink, not lyse.

Hints:

1. Distinguish total osmolarity from effective osmolarity (tonicity).
2. Solutes that cross the cell membrane freely contribute to osmolarity but not tonicity.
3. Identify the solution with permeant solute that creates effective hypotonicity for the RBC.

Distractors:

- "150 mMol/L NaCl" — Reveals the misconception that any solution other than normal saline causes lysis. 150 mmol/L NaCl = ~300 mOsm/L effective osmolarity = isotonic.
- "300 mMol/L mannitol" — Reveals the misconception that any non-saline solution lyses cells. Mannitol is impermeant; this is isotonic.
- "350 mMol/L mannitol" — Reveals the misconception that any hypertonic solution lyses RBCs. Hypertonic causes shrinking, not lysis.

QUESTION 18

Stem: Normal osmolarity of plasma is mainly due to **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Na+

Explanation: Normal plasma osmolality is ~285–295 mOsm/kg [Costanzo ch.6] [Guyton ch.25]. **Na⁺ (along with its accompanying anions Cl⁻ and HCO₃⁻) accounts for the majority** of plasma osmolarity. Plasma [Na⁺] ≈ 140 mmol/L; doubling for accompanying anions gives ≈ 280 mOsm/kg, contributed by Na⁺ and its salts. The simplified clinical formula reflects this: calculated plasma osmolality ≈ 2 × [Na⁺] + [glucose]/18 + [BUN]/2.8 (in mg/dL units), with the 2 × [Na⁺] term dominating. Glucose contributes ~5 mOsm/kg at normal levels; urea ~5 mOsm/kg; plasma proteins contribute only ~1–2 mOsm/kg despite being abundant by mass (because their molecular weights are very large and molar concentrations are correspondingly small).

Hints:

1. Recall the calculated-plasma-osmolality formula and identify the dominant term.
2. Plasma proteins are abundant by weight but contribute little to osmolarity because of their molecular weight.
3. The dominant cation in plasma, with its accompanying anions, accounts for most of the osmolarity.

Distractors:

- "Glucose" — Reveals the misconception that glucose dominates plasma osmolarity. At 5 mmol/L it contributes only ~5 mOsm/kg out of ~290.
- "Cl-" — Reveals the misconception that Cl⁻ is independent of Na⁺ in osmotic terms. Cl⁻ is the major accompanying anion; its osmotic contribution is part of the Na⁺ pair.
- "Plasma Proteins" — Reveals the misconception that mass equals molar concentration. Plasma proteins contribute ~1 mOsm/kg, important for _colloid osmotic pressure_ but not bulk osmolarity.

QUESTION 19

Stem: Plasma proteins contribute only slightly to the total number of osmotically active particles because**\_**

Correct answer: Low molar concentration, high molecular weight

Explanation: Osmotic activity is determined by the **number of particles per litre**, i.e. the _molar_ concentration [Costanzo ch.1]. Although plasma proteins constitute ~7 g/dL of plasma (a substantial mass), albumin's molecular weight (~66 kDa) and the globulins' (~150 kDa) translate this mass into a small molar concentration: ~0.9 mmol/L for albumin, much lower for globulins — total ~1 mmol/L for all plasma proteins. Compared with Na⁺ at 140 mmol/L, the molar protein concentration is over 100-fold lower, so plasma proteins contribute only ~1–2 mOsm/kg of the ~290 mOsm/kg total. They are physiologically critical for **colloid (oncotic) osmotic pressure** at the capillary wall (because the wall is permeable to small ions but not to proteins), but on a bulk-osmolarity basis they are minor.

Hints:

1. Osmotic activity depends on number of particles, not on mass concentration.
2. Plasma proteins are massive molecules — high mass, low number per litre.
3. Identify which combination of low molar concentration and high molecular weight makes the per-particle contribution small.

Distractors:

- "High molar concentration, high molecular weight" — Reveals the misconception that plasma proteins are abundant in molar terms. They are abundant by mass but not by molar concentration.
- "Low molar concentration, low molecular weight" — Reveals the misconception that plasma proteins are small molecules.
- "High molar concentration, low molecular weight" — Reveals reversed reasoning.

QUESTION 20

Stem: Daily water loss in sweat during normal activities is (ml)**\_**

Correct answer: 300-400

Explanation: Daily water output under normal sedentary conditions in a temperate environment is approximately [Guyton ch.25]: insensible loss through skin and respiration ~700 mL (350 + 350 mL); urine ~1500 mL; **sweat ~100 mL** at rest, or ~300–400 mL with light activity; faecal water ~100 mL. The **300–400 mL/day** figure used in many MBBS texts represents normal but not sedentary activity. Heavy exercise, hot environments and fever can increase sweat losses dramatically — to 1.5–4 L/h in extreme heat — which is why fluid replacement during exertion is essential. Note that sweat is hypotonic (Na⁺ ~30–60 mmol/L) so prolonged sweating produces hypertonic dehydration unless replaced with hypotonic fluid.

Hints:

1. Recall the daily fluid balance — intake and output components.
2. Distinguish basal sweat (very low) from sweat during normal activity.
3. The figure for ordinary daily activity is in the low hundreds of mL.

Distractors:

- "50-100" — Reveals the misconception that sweat output is the same as basal sweating. Normal activity slightly elevates this above the basal rate.
- "500-700" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "1000-1200" — Reveals the misconception that 1 L/day is the normal sweat. That magnitude of loss is seen in moderate-to-heavy activity or warm conditions, not at "normal" daily activity.

QUESTION 21

Stem: Which type of disturbance of body fluids is observed in sweating?

Correct answer: Hyperosmotic volume contraction

Explanation: Sweat is **hypotonic** relative to plasma — sweat [Na⁺] ranges from ~10 to 60 mmol/L (much lower than plasma 140 mmol/L) [Guyton ch.25]. Therefore, a person who loses a large volume of sweat loses _proportionally more water than solute_. The result is a **decrease in ECF volume** (volume contraction) accompanied by an **increase in plasma osmolality** (hypertonic / hyperosmotic) — i.e. **hyperosmotic volume contraction**. ADH secretion rises, thirst is stimulated, and renal water conservation is enhanced. By Darrow-Yannet representation: ECF shrinks while its osmolality rises, drawing water out of cells; ICF also shrinks. This pattern is the same as that produced by other forms of pure-water loss (diabetes insipidus, fever, hyperventilation in hot environments).

Hints:

1. Compare the tonicity of sweat with that of plasma.
2. Loss of hypotonic fluid changes both the volume and the osmolality of the remaining ECF.
3. Identify the direction of change in volume (down) and in osmolality (up).

Distractors:

- "Isosmotic volume contraction" — Reveals the misconception that sweat is iso-osmotic. Sweat is hypotonic; loss raises plasma osmolality.
- "Hyposmotic volume contraction" — Reveals the misconception that volume contraction must be hypotonic. Hypotonic fluid loss produces _hypertonic_ plasma, not hypotonic.
- "Isosmotic volume expansion" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

The student should note **glucose is divided by 18, BUN by 2.8** — option (b). Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state typically produces calculated osmolalities >320 mOsm/kg.

QUESTION 26

Stem: Various safety factors prevent excessive fluid accumulation in the interstitial spaces. Total safety factor against edema is (mmHg)**\_**

Correct answer: 17

Explanation: Three safety factors normally prevent oedema [Guyton ch.25]: (1) **low tissue compliance** in the negative-pressure range (about **3 mmHg**); (2) **increased lymphatic flow** (lymph flow can rise 10- to 50-fold when interstitial pressure rises, contributing about **7 mmHg**); (3) **washdown of interstitial proteins** by increased lymph flow, which reduces interstitial colloid osmotic pressure and reduces filtration (about **7 mmHg**). The **total safety factor is therefore 3 + 7 + 7 = 17 mmHg** [Guyton ch.25]. This means that capillary hydrostatic pressure must rise by approximately 17 mmHg above its normal value (~17 mmHg) — i.e. roughly double — before clinically detectable oedema develops.

QUESTION 28

Stem: I-125 labelled albumin is used for measurement of:

Correct answer: Plasma volume

Explanation: **¹²⁵I-labelled albumin** is too large to cross the capillary wall (in normal tissues) and remains confined to the **plasma compartment** after intravenous injection [Guyton ch.25]. After equilibration, plasma volume is calculated by the indicator dilution principle (mass injected − mass excreted) divided by the equilibrium plasma concentration. **Evans blue (T-1824)** binds endogenous albumin and is used identically — both methods rely on albumin's restriction to plasma to define the volume of distribution. **Interstitial fluid** and **ICF** cannot be measured directly; they are calculated by subtraction. **ECF** is measured with smaller, capillary-permeable but cell-impermeable markers (mannitol, inulin, sodium thiosulphate).

Hints:

1. Albumin is a plasma protein restricted to the plasma compartment by capillary endothelium.
2. The volume of distribution of albumin equals the plasma volume.
3. Markers for plasma volume must not cross capillary walls.

Distractors:

- "Interstitial fluid volume" — Reveals the misconception that albumin escapes into the interstitium. Under normal capillary integrity it does not (only ~5–10% leaks per hour, which is negligible for the rapid measurement).
- "ICF" — Reveals the misconception that an albumin-bound marker enters cells. It cannot.
- "ECF" — Reveals the misconception that albumin distributes throughout the ECF. ECF includes interstitium, which albumin cannot freely enter via capillary walls.
