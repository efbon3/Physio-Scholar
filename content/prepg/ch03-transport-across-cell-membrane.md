QUESTION 1

Stem: Transport of lipid-soluble substances across the cell membrane occurs via which of the following?

Correct answer: Diffusion

Explanation: Lipid-soluble (lipophilic, non-polar) substances — including O₂, CO₂, N₂, fatty acids, steroid hormones, and lipid-soluble drugs — cross the plasma membrane by **simple diffusion** directly through the lipid bilayer, without need for any transport protein. Their solubility in the hydrophobic core of the membrane allows them to dissolve into the bilayer, traverse it down their concentration gradient, and emerge on the other side [Guyton ch.4] [Costanzo ch.1] [Boron ch.5]. Permeability is proportional to the lipid:water partition coefficient. **Porins** are large β-barrel channels found in the outer mitochondrial membrane and bacterial outer membranes — they pass small _water-soluble_ solutes, not lipophiles. **Ionophores** are mobile or channel-forming molecules (valinomycin, gramicidin) that shuttle _ions_, not lipid-soluble substances. **Lipopolysaccharides** are bacterial outer-membrane components, not transport machinery.

Hints:

1. Recall how a substance's lipid solubility determines whether it needs a protein channel.
2. The hydrophobic core of the bilayer is itself a solvent for lipophilic molecules.
3. Lipid-soluble compounds dissolve in and traverse the bilayer without machinery.

Distractors:

- "Porins" — Reveals the misconception that all transmembrane channels handle hydrophobic solutes. Porins move small _water-soluble_ molecules across the outer mitochondrial/bacterial membrane.
- "Ionophore" — Reveals the misconception that lipid-soluble carriers are required for lipid-soluble substrates. Ionophores carry _ions_ across membranes; lipophiles need no carrier.
- "Lipopolysaccharides" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Which of the following will diffuse more rapidly through phospholipid membranes?

Correct answer: Oxygen

Explanation: The intrinsic permeability of a phospholipid bilayer to an uncharged, lipid-soluble solute is several orders of magnitude greater than to charged ions, and somewhat greater than to water [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. **Oxygen** is a small, uncharged, non-polar gas with a high lipid:water partition coefficient and crosses the bilayer freely by simple diffusion at one of the highest permeability coefficients of any physiologically relevant species [Guyton ch.4]. **Sodium** and **chloride** are charged ions that cannot dissolve in the hydrophobic core; their permeability through pure bilayer is negligible (≈10⁹ times lower than O₂) and they require dedicated channels or transporters. **Water** is small and weakly polar; it does cross the bilayer slowly but most rapid water flux through cell membranes occurs via **aquaporin channels**, not direct lipid permeation [Guyton ch.4].

Hints:

1. Rank the listed species by lipid solubility and electrical charge.
2. The most rapid diffuser through a pure bilayer is uncharged, small and non-polar.
3. Water and ions both face barriers (water polarity and ionic charge); oxygen faces neither.

Distractors:

- "Sodium" — Reveals the misconception that small ions readily cross lipid membranes. Charged species cannot dissolve in the hydrophobic core; they require channels.
- "Chloride" — Same misconception as (b).
- "Water" — Reveals the misconception that water freely crosses any membrane equally fast. Water permeability is dominated by aquaporins; through pure lipid the rate is much lower than for O₂ or CO₂ [Guyton ch.4].

QUESTION 3

Stem: Which of the following is explained by the fick's law of diffusion?

Correct answer: Passive diffusion along concentration gradient

Explanation: **Fick's law of diffusion** describes the rate of _passive_ movement of an uncharged solute _down_ its concentration gradient across a permeable barrier: J = (D × A × ΔC) / Δx, where J is net flux, D is the diffusion coefficient, A is the membrane surface area, ΔC is the concentration gradient, and Δx is the membrane thickness [Costanzo ch.1] [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. The term _passive_ indicates that no metabolic energy is required — the driving force is the concentration gradient itself, and net flux ceases at equilibrium. _Active_ transport, by contrast, moves solute _against_ its electrochemical gradient and requires an energy input (ATP for primary; an ion gradient for secondary), and is governed by carrier kinetics rather than Fick's relation. The phrase "active diffusion" is internally contradictory in standard physiology: diffusion is by definition passive.

Hints:

1. Recall the four parameters in Fick's equation and what each describes physically.
2. Identify whether Fick's law assumes an energy input or a downhill driving force.
3. Distinguish "diffusion" (always passive) from "transport" (which can be active or passive).

Distractors:

- "Active diffusion along concentration gradient" — Reveals the misconception that diffusion can be active. Diffusion is by definition passive — the term "active diffusion" is a contradiction.
- "Active diffusion against concentration gradient" — Same misconception as (a), compounded by the wrong direction.
- "Passive diffusion against concentration gradient" — Reveals the misconception that passive transport can run uphill. Passive flux always decays toward equilibrium and proceeds _down_ the gradient.

QUESTION 4

Stem: For a membrane permeable substance, the intracellular concentration (IC) is 10 units and extracellular concentration (EC) is 5 units. If the intracellular concentration, surface area of the membrane and thickness of the membrane are doubled, and the EC remains the same, What will be the net effect on the diffusion?

Correct answer: Diffusion becomes 3x

Explanation: Net flux follows Fick's law: J ∝ (A × ΔC) / Δx, where ΔC is the concentration _difference_ across the membrane [Costanzo ch.1] [Guyton ch.4]. **Initial state:** ΔC = 10 − 5 = 5 units; A = A₀; Δx = Δx₀; therefore J₀ ∝ (A₀ × 5) / Δx₀. **New state:** intracellular concentration doubles to 20, so ΔC' = 20 − 5 = 15 units; surface area doubles to 2A₀; thickness doubles to 2Δx₀; therefore J' ∝ (2A₀ × 15) / (2Δx₀) = (A₀ × 15) / Δx₀. The ratio J'/J₀ = 15/5 = **3**. The doubling of A and Δx cancel exactly, so the net change reduces to the ratio of the new to old concentration _differences_, which is 3-fold.

Hints:

1. Write down Fick's law with all four parameters before substituting numbers.
2. The driving force is the concentration _difference_, not the intracellular concentration alone.
3. Pay attention to which factors cancel — doubling area and doubling thickness are competing influences.

Distractors:

- "Diffusion becomes 2x" — Reveals the misconception that doubling intracellular concentration doubles the gradient. The gradient is IC − EC = 20 − 5 = 15, not 2 × 10 = 20. Students who treat "concentration" as the driving force (rather than concentration _difference_) will fall into this trap.
- "Diffusion becomes 1/2x" — Reveals the misconception that doubling thickness halves diffusion regardless of other changes — the student fails to track competing changes (A and ΔC also change).
- "Diffusion becomes 1/3x" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Which of the following does not follow transport saturation kinetics?

Correct answer: Simple diffusion

Explanation: Saturation kinetics arise whenever transport requires a _limited number of binding sites_ on a carrier protein. As substrate concentration rises, sites become progressively occupied; once all carriers are saturated, transport reaches a maximum velocity (V*max) — a hallmark of carrier-mediated transport [Guyton ch.4] [Costanzo ch.1]. **Facilitated diffusion** uses uniporter carriers (e.g. GLUT1–GLUT4 for glucose) and shows V_max behaviour. The **Na⁺-Ca²⁺ exchanger** is an antiporter and saturates as its binding sites for Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ become fully occupied. **Coupled transport** (symport or antiport) likewise saturates because it depends on carrier binding sites. **Simple diffusion**, by contrast, has \_no carrier* — flux is directly proportional to the concentration gradient (Fick's law) and therefore rises linearly with concentration without an upper limit, as illustrated explicitly in Guyton's figure comparing simple and facilitated diffusion [Guyton ch.4].

Hints:

1. Saturation kinetics requires a finite pool of binding sites; identify which mode of transport involves no protein binding sites.
2. Plot rate versus concentration for each option mentally — which one is linear with no plateau?
3. The exception is the only listed mode that does not use a carrier protein.

Distractors:

- "Facilitated diffusion" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Na+-Ca2+ exchanger" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Coupled transport" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 6

Stem: Which statement about the facilitated diffusion is true?

Correct answer: Requires carrier protein

Explanation: **Facilitated diffusion** is _passive_ (down the electrochemical gradient, no metabolic energy required) but is mediated by a **carrier protein** that binds the solute on one face of the membrane, undergoes a conformational change, and releases it on the other face [Guyton ch.4] [Costanzo ch.1]. The classical example is glucose uptake into most cells via GLUT transporters. Because the carrier has a finite number of binding sites, facilitated diffusion exhibits **Michaelis-Menten saturation kinetics** with a defined V*max — distinguishing it from simple diffusion, which is linear in concentration [Guyton ch.4]. It is therefore \_not* a form of active transport (no energy input), it _does_ follow saturation kinetics, and it does not require creatine phosphate (which is a phosphagen used to regenerate ATP in muscle, not a transport substrate).

Hints:

1. Distinguish facilitated diffusion from simple diffusion by what mediates the transport.
2. Distinguish facilitated diffusion from active transport by whether energy is required.
3. The defining feature of facilitated diffusion is that it is carrier-mediated yet still passive.

Distractors:

- "Form of active transport" — Reveals the misconception that any carrier-mediated transport is active. Facilitated diffusion is passive — it cannot pump against a gradient.
- "Doesn't follow saturation kinetics" — Reveals the misconception that all "diffusion" types are linear. Facilitated diffusion saturates because the carrier is rate-limiting [Guyton ch.4].
- "Requires creatine phosphate" — Reveals the misconception that all energy-requiring biology uses creatine phosphate, or confuses facilitated diffusion with primary active transport.

QUESTION 7

Stem: The movement of which of the following substance across the membrane is most affected by osmotic pressure?

Correct answer: Water

Explanation: **Osmotic pressure** is, by definition, the hydrostatic pressure required to prevent the net movement of _water_ across a semi-permeable membrane separating two solutions of different solute concentration [Costanzo ch.1] [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. Osmosis is the passive movement of water from a region of low solute concentration (high water activity) to one of high solute concentration (low water activity), driven by the chemical potential gradient of water itself. Therefore the molecular movement _most directly_ affected by osmotic pressure is that of **water**. Solutes such as Na⁺, K⁺ and glucose can generate osmotic pressure by being unable (or slow) to cross the membrane, but their _own_ movement is governed by their own electrochemical gradients and transporters, not by osmotic pressure.

Hints:

1. The concept of osmotic pressure is named for the species that actually moves — identify it.
2. Recall the formal definition: the pressure needed to halt the net movement of which species?
3. Solutes generate osmotic pressure; the movement that osmotic pressure controls is the solvent.

Distractors:

- "Sodium" — Reveals the misconception that osmotic pressure pushes solutes. Solutes cause the osmotic gradient; the species that moves is water.
- "Potassium" — Same misconception as (a).
- "Glucose" — Same misconception as (a).

QUESTION 8

Stem: Osmolarity is defined as **\_\_**

Correct answer: Number of osmole per litre

Explanation: One **osmole** is the number of osmotically active particles (in moles) into which one mole of solute dissociates in solution [Costanzo ch.1]. **Osmolarity** is the concentration of osmoles per _litre of solution_ (Osm/L; physiologically expressed as mOsm/L) [Costanzo ch.1]. **Osmolality** is the concentration of osmoles per _kilogram of solvent_ (Osm/kg). For dilute aqueous solutions at body temperature, the numerical difference between osmolarity and osmolality is small (~1%), so the terms are sometimes used loosely; strictly, osmolality is the more thermodynamically rigorous measurement because it is independent of temperature (kg of solvent does not change with temperature, whereas litre of solution does). Plasma osmolality is normally about 290 mOsm/kg [Costanzo ch.1] and is regulated within ~±1% by ADH-mediated control of renal water handling.

Hints:

1. Distinguish osmol*arity* (per litre of solution) from osmol*ality* (per kg of solvent).
2. The two suffixes refer to volume vs mass denominators.
3. The term in the question ends in _-arity_ — what denominator does that imply?

Distractors:

- "Number of osmole per kg" — Reveals the misconception that osmolarity and osmolality are interchangeable. They have different denominators.
- "Weight of solute per litre" — Reveals the misconception that osmolarity is a simple weight/volume concentration. It is a _molar_ concentration of osmotically active particles.
- "Weight of solvent per litre" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 9

Stem: Passive transfer of only solvent occurs in **\_\_**

Correct answer: Osmosis

Explanation: **Osmosis** is the passive movement of _solvent_ (water, in biological systems) across a semi-permeable membrane down its chemical-potential gradient — from a region of low solute concentration to one of high solute concentration — driven by the difference in water activity, not by external energy [Costanzo ch.1] [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. The membrane is semi-permeable: water passes freely (often via aquaporins) while the solute is retained. **Diffusion** as a general term refers to the movement of any species (solute or solvent) down its concentration gradient — not solvent-specific. **Active transport** is energy-requiring and moves _solutes_ against their electrochemical gradient. **Pinocytosis** is bulk-fluid endocytosis (vesicle internalisation of extracellular fluid + dissolved solute) — not selectively a solvent-only transfer.

Hints:

1. The transfer mode in question is selective for the solvent rather than the solute.
2. A semi-permeable membrane allows the solvent through but retains the solute.
3. The word for water moving across a membrane down its activity gradient is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that osmosis and diffusion are interchangeable. Diffusion is a general term; osmosis specifically describes solvent movement across a semi-permeable membrane.
- "Active transport" — Reveals the misconception that water can be actively pumped. There is no primary active transporter for water; water always moves passively down osmotic or hydrostatic gradients.
- "Pinocytosis" — Reveals the misconception that bulk fluid uptake is osmotic. Pinocytosis is vesicle-mediated and brings in solutes along with solvent.

QUESTION 10

Stem: A 47-year-old diabetic man takes a low-dose aspirin daily due to cardiovascular risk. The absorption of aspirin in the stomach is an example of which type of transport?

Correct answer: Nonionic diffusion

Explanation: **Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid)** is a weak acid with a pKa ≈ 3.5. In the gastric lumen, where pH is ~1.5–3.5, the ratio of unionised (HA) to ionised (A⁻) aspirin is high — most of the drug is **non-ionised and lipid-soluble**, allowing it to cross the gastric mucosal membrane by **non-ionic (or non-ionised) simple diffusion** [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. Once inside the more alkaline cytoplasm and interstitial fluid (pH ~7.4), the drug ionises to A⁻, becomes lipid-insoluble, and is "trapped" on the systemic side — a classical illustration of pH-partition theory governing weak-acid/base absorption. **Facilitated diffusion** would require a carrier; aspirin uses no carrier. **Secondary active transport** is irrelevant because there is no co-substrate gradient driving aspirin uptake. The category "non-ionic diffusion" specifically distinguishes this mechanism from generic simple diffusion of permanently uncharged species.

Hints:

1. Aspirin is a weak acid with a low pKa — recall how its ionisation state depends on local pH.
2. In the acidic stomach, the drug exists predominantly in one of its two forms — the form that is lipid-soluble.
3. The transport label given to passive movement of the unionised fraction of a weak acid is a specific term.

Distractors:

- "Facilitated diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that drug absorption requires a carrier. Most weak acids and bases cross by non-ionic diffusion, not via transporters.
- "Simple diffusion" — Defensible but less precise. The pH-dependent ionisation of aspirin makes "non-ionic diffusion" the more specific and conventional answer in pharmacology teaching.
- "Secondary active transport" — Reveals the misconception that any saturable membrane phenomenon must be coupled. Aspirin absorption is passive.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Options (a) and (c) overlap — non-ionic diffusion *is* a special case of simple diffusion specific to weak electrolytes. The textbook convention treats them as distinct labels in pharmacology, where (a) is the more precise answer for weak acids/bases. The answer key (a) reflects this convention.]

QUESTION 11

Stem: Energy is required in which of the following mode of transport.

Correct answer: Active transport

Explanation: **Active transport** is the _only_ listed mode that requires direct energy input to drive solute movement against its electrochemical gradient [Guyton ch.4]. **Primary active transport** uses ATP hydrolysis directly (Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase, Ca²⁺ ATPase, H⁺-K⁺ ATPase, H⁺ ATPase). **Secondary active transport** uses an ion-gradient (typically Na⁺) established by primary active transport to power the uphill movement of a co-transported solute (e.g. SGLT for glucose, NCX for Ca²⁺ extrusion) — energy is therefore still required, just delivered indirectly via ATP-maintained gradients [Guyton ch.4]. **Osmosis, diffusion and facilitated diffusion** are all passive: their driving force is a pre-existing chemical-potential gradient, and they cease at equilibrium without energy input.

Hints:

1. Identify which mode can move a solute _against_ its electrochemical gradient.
2. Three of the listed modes equilibrate with no energy required; one requires ATP.
3. Pumps that maintain ion gradients across cell membranes belong to this category.

Distractors:

- "Osmosis" — Reveals the misconception that osmosis requires energy because water moves "uphill." Water moves down its own chemical-potential gradient — no energy is needed.
- "Diffusion" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Facilitated diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that the involvement of a carrier protein implies energy use. The carrier is passive — it accelerates equilibration but does not pump against the gradient [Guyton ch.4].

QUESTION 12

Stem: Movement of calcium from the cytosol to the extracellular space takes place by

Correct answer: Primary active transport

Explanation: Cells maintain cytosolic [Ca²⁺] at ~100 nmol/L against an extracellular [Ca²⁺] of ~1.2 mmol/L — a >10,000-fold gradient — by extruding Ca²⁺ from the cytosol [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. The principal export pathways are: (1) the **plasma-membrane Ca²⁺ ATPase (PMCA)**, a primary active transporter that uses ATP hydrolysis to pump one Ca²⁺ out per ATP; and (2) the **Na⁺-Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX)**, a _secondary_ active transport that exchanges 3 Na⁺ in for 1 Ca²⁺ out using the inward Na⁺ gradient. Because the question stem specifies movement of Ca²⁺ from cytosol to extracellular space (i.e. against its electrochemical gradient), and offers "primary active transport" as an option without distinguishing primary from secondary, the answer (a) reflects the PMCA. (Sarcoplasmic reticulum Ca²⁺ ATPase, SERCA, similarly pumps Ca²⁺ from cytosol into the SR/ER lumen.) Calcium movement against its gradient cannot occur by passive mechanisms.

Hints:

1. Cytosolic Ca²⁺ is held many orders of magnitude below extracellular Ca²⁺ — what kind of transport sustains such a gradient?
2. Two membrane systems extrude Ca²⁺ from cytosol; both run uphill and so are active.
3. The ATP-dependent pump at the plasma membrane is the prototype primary active mode.

Distractors:

- "Facilitated diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that any carrier-mediated transport is passive. Facilitated diffusion cannot run against a gradient and so cannot maintain a 10,000-fold cytosolic-extracellular ratio.
- "Simple diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that an ion can freely diffuse across the lipid bilayer. Charged species need protein-mediated transport.
- "None of the above" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: The Na⁺-Ca²⁺ exchanger (a *secondary* active transporter) handles a substantial fraction of Ca²⁺ extrusion in many cell types. The answer key (a) "Primary active transport" reflects the PMCA contribution and the typical exam framing. A strict reading would acknowledge both primary (PMCA) and secondary (NCX) mechanisms.]

QUESTION 13

Stem: Na+-K+ ATP pump uses **\_\_\_\_**

Correct answer: ATPase

Explanation: The **Na⁺-K⁺ pump** is itself an **ATPase** — its catalytic subunit hydrolyses ATP to ADP + P_i and uses the released energy to translocate **3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in** per cycle, against both ions' electrochemical gradients [Guyton ch.4] [Costanzo ch.1] [Boron ch.5]. Hydrolysis proceeds via a phosphorylated enzyme intermediate (E1-P → E2-P), with the conformational changes coupled to ion translocation. Because it directly hydrolyses ATP, it is the prototype of **P-type ATPases** and of **primary active transport**. It does not use GTP (heterotrimeric G-proteins and small GTPases use GTP, but that is for signalling, not pumping), nor acetyl-CoA (a metabolic intermediate), nor NADPH (a reducing equivalent for biosynthesis). Approximately 25–30% of basal cellular ATP consumption is devoted to maintaining Na⁺/K⁺ gradients via this pump.

Hints:

1. Recall the suffix in the pump's name — what enzymatic activity does it imply?
2. The pump's energy currency is the same as the cell's general energy currency.
3. The catalytic activity is hydrolysis of ATP.

Distractors:

- "GTPase" — Reveals the misconception that ATPases and GTPases are interchangeable. GTP-hydrolysing proteins exist (G-proteins, Ras, tubulin) but they signal or polymerise; pumps generally use ATP.
- "Acetyl CoA" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "NADPH" — Reveals the misconception that NADPH is a general energy source. NADPH provides reducing equivalents for biosynthesis and antioxidant defence, not membrane pumping.

QUESTION 14

Stem: Primary active transport is mediated by which one of the following enzymes?

Correct answer: Na+-K+ ATPase

Explanation: **Primary active transport** is defined by direct ATP hydrolysis driving solute movement against its electrochemical gradient [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. The major mammalian primary active transporters are the **P-type ATPases** — including the **Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase** (the prototype, in every plasma membrane), the sarco/endoplasmic reticulum Ca²⁺ ATPase (SERCA), the plasma-membrane Ca²⁺ ATPase (PMCA), and the H⁺-K⁺ ATPase (gastric parietal cells, distal nephron) — and the **V-type H⁺ ATPase** of lysosomes/secretory vesicles, plus the **F-type ATP synthase** of mitochondria. **G-proteins** are signal transducers, not pumps. **Carrier protein** is a general term that includes both primary active transporters and passive uniporters; it is not specific. **Channel proteins** are passive — they allow flux down the electrochemical gradient and do not perform active transport. Of the listed options, only the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase is unambiguously a primary active transporter.

Hints:

1. Primary active transport requires direct ATP hydrolysis; recall which option has built-in ATPase activity.
2. Two of the listed options are non-pumps; one is a generic term; one is the named pump.
3. The named option is the prototype P-type ATPase.

Distractors:

- "G-Protein" — Reveals the misconception that any protein with bound nucleotide is a pump. G-proteins use GTP for signal transduction, not for solute pumping.
- "Carrier protein" — Reveals the misconception that "carrier" automatically means active. Carriers include uniporters (passive), symporters (often secondary active) and primary pumps.
- "Channel protein" — Reveals the misconception that channels and pumps are equivalent. Channels are passive — they cannot pump against a gradient.

QUESTION 15

Stem: For Na+-K+ ATPase coupling ratio is **\_\_**

Correct answer: 3:2

Explanation: Per cycle, the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase translocates **3 Na⁺ out** and **2 K⁺ in**, hydrolysing one molecule of ATP — a stoichiometry of **Na⁺:K⁺ = 3:2** [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. This asymmetric stoichiometry has two physiologically critical consequences: (1) the pump is **electrogenic** — every cycle exports a net positive charge, contributing approximately −4 mV to the resting membrane potential of typical neurones; and (2) the resulting steep Na⁺ gradient stores potential energy that powers a wide array of secondary active transporters (SGLT for glucose, NCX for Ca²⁺ extrusion, NHE for pH regulation, etc.). The pump is also responsible for cell-volume regulation by counteracting the osmotic effect of intracellular impermeant anions (proteins).

Hints:

1. Recall the exact ion stoichiometry of the pump — three of one direction, two of the other.
2. The pump exports more positive charges than it imports — that determines the sign of its contribution to membrane potential.
3. Per ATP, three Na⁺ leave and two K⁺ enter.

Distractors:

- "1:1" — Reveals the misconception that ion pumps are simple one-for-one exchangers. The Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase is not 1:1.
- "1:2" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "2:3" — Reveals the misconception that the Na⁺ and K⁺ directions are reversed. Three Na⁺ out and two K⁺ in is the correct order.

QUESTION 16

Stem: A 40-year-old diabetic man has been prescribed empagliflozin. Which of the following processes will most likely be affected by this drug?

Correct answer: Secondary active transport

Explanation: **Empagliflozin** is a **sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitor** prescribed to lower blood glucose in type 2 diabetes (and increasingly used for cardiovascular and renal protection) [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. SGLT2 is expressed on the apical membrane of the renal proximal tubule (S1/S2 segments), where it reabsorbs the bulk of filtered glucose by **secondary active transport**: it co-transports 1 Na⁺ and 1 glucose into the cell, using the inward Na⁺ gradient (maintained by the basolateral Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase) as the energy source to move glucose against its concentration gradient [Boron ch.5]. Empagliflozin blocks this cotransport, causing **glycosuria** and a modest osmotic diuresis. The drug therefore directly affects **secondary active transport**. Primary active transport, facilitated diffusion (GLUT2 at the basolateral side handles the exit step) and simple diffusion are not the proximate targets.

Hints:

1. Recall the mechanism of action of empagliflozin and the location of its target in the nephron.
2. SGLT2 couples the movement of glucose to the gradient of which ion?
3. The transport mode that uses an ion gradient (rather than ATP directly) to power uphill solute movement is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Primary active transport" — Reveals the misconception that SGLT2 itself hydrolyses ATP. SGLT2 is a secondary active transporter; the primary active transport (Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase) maintains the gradient that powers it.
- "Facilitated diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that SGLT2 is passive. Facilitated diffusion (GLUT2) handles glucose exit at the basolateral side, not apical reabsorption.
- "Simple diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that glucose can cross membranes without a transporter. Glucose is hydrophilic and requires a carrier.

QUESTION 17

Stem: Glucose is absorbed in intestine by?

Correct answer: Secondary active transport

Explanation: Glucose absorption across the intestinal epithelium is a two-step process [Guyton ch.4] [Boron ch.5]. At the **apical (luminal)** brush-border membrane, glucose enters the enterocyte via **SGLT1**, a Na⁺-glucose cotransporter that imports 2 Na⁺ for every 1 glucose, using the inward Na⁺ gradient (maintained by the basolateral Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase) to drive glucose against its gradient — this is **secondary active transport**. At the **basolateral** membrane, glucose exits down its now-elevated intracellular concentration into the interstitium via **GLUT2**, a uniporter — facilitated diffusion. The rate-limiting and characteristic step of intestinal glucose absorption is the apical SGLT1 step, hence "secondary active transport" is the canonical answer. The same mechanism applies in the renal proximal tubule for glucose reabsorption (via SGLT2, then GLUT2).

Hints:

1. Glucose absorption is a coupled, two-step transcellular process — recall the ion to which it is coupled.
2. The apical step uses a Na⁺-glucose cotransporter; the basolateral step is a uniporter.
3. The defining and rate-limiting step at the brush border is powered by the Na⁺ gradient.

Distractors:

- "Primary active transport" — Reveals the misconception that SGLT1 directly hydrolyses ATP. The Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase performs primary active transport indirectly powering SGLT1.
- "Facilitated diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that the entire absorption is passive. The basolateral exit step is facilitated diffusion (GLUT2), but the apical entry step against gradient is not.
- "Simple diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that glucose can cross epithelial membranes without a transporter. Glucose is polar and requires carriers.

QUESTION 18

Stem: A 40-year-old lady is diagnosed with Lambart- Eaton syndrome. Radioimmunoassay reveals the presence of serum antibodies against voltage-gated calcium channels. Which of the following process will be affected as a result of this pathology?

Correct answer: Exocytosis

Explanation: **Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome (LEMS)** is an autoimmune disorder in which IgG antibodies target **voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCCs)** on the _presynaptic_ motor nerve terminal. At the neuromuscular junction, action-potential–driven opening of presynaptic VGCCs causes Ca²⁺ influx into the terminal, which is the trigger for **synaptic vesicle exocytosis** of acetylcholine (ACh) into the synaptic cleft — Ca²⁺ binds synaptotagmin, promoting SNARE-mediated vesicle fusion with the presynaptic membrane [Boron ch.5]. With reduced VGCC function, presynaptic Ca²⁺ entry is impaired, ACh release (exocytosis) is reduced, and patients develop proximal muscle weakness with characteristic _post-tetanic facilitation_ (repeated stimulation builds up presynaptic Ca²⁺, transiently improving release). LEMS is paraneoplastically associated with small-cell lung cancer in approximately 50% of cases. The cellular process directly affected is **exocytosis** of neurotransmitter.

Hints:

1. The autoantibody targets the calcium channel at a specific location — recall whether it is presynaptic or postsynaptic.
2. Calcium influx through presynaptic VGCCs is the trigger for which membrane event?
3. The resulting deficit is in the release of neurotransmitter from synaptic vesicles.

Distractors:

- "Facilitated Diffusion" — Reveals the misconception that VGCCs perform facilitated diffusion of glucose-like solutes. VGCCs gate Ca²⁺ flux, not glucose, and the downstream consequence in LEMS is impaired transmitter release.
- "Endocytosis" — Reveals the misconception that vesicle membrane retrieval is the primary defect. Endocytosis recycles vesicle membrane _after_ exocytosis; the primary defect in LEMS is exocytosis itself.
- "Simple Diffusion" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 19

Stem: Which pathway is called regulated pathway?

Correct answer: Non Constitutive exocytosis

Explanation: Cells release their secretory contents by two pathways [Boron ch.2]. The **constitutive (continuous) exocytic pathway** delivers proteins to the plasma membrane and to the extracellular space at a steady rate without an external trigger — examples include continuous release of plasma proteins by hepatocytes and ongoing membrane lipid renewal. The **regulated (non-constitutive) exocytic pathway** stores secretory products in dense-core or zymogen vesicles and releases them only on demand, in response to a stimulus — typically a rise in cytosolic Ca²⁺ that triggers SNARE-mediated vesicle fusion. Examples include hormone secretion (insulin from β-cells, catecholamines from chromaffin cells), neurotransmitter release at synapses, and pancreatic zymogen secretion. Hence "non-constitutive exocytosis" is synonymous with the regulated pathway.

Hints:

1. Distinguish exocytosis that proceeds continuously from exocytosis triggered by a stimulus.
2. The regulated pathway requires a signal (typically a rise in Ca²⁺) before vesicles fuse.
3. The opposite of _constitutive_ is the term used in the answer.

Distractors:

- "Constitutive exocytosis" — Reveals the misconception that _constitutive_ and _regulated_ are equivalent. They are antonyms in the exocytic-pathway taxonomy.
- "Constitutive endocytosis" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Receptor mediated endocytosis" — Reveals the misconception that receptor-mediated implies regulated secretion. RME is uptake (not release) and is itself often constitutive.

QUESTION 20

Stem: A 30-year-old gentleman is diagnosed with elevated blood cholesterol levels. A defect in a process that involves the protein 'clathrin' is being suspected. Which of the following process will be most likely affected by this defect?

Correct answer: Receptor mediated endocytosis

Explanation: **Clathrin** is a triskelion-shaped scaffold protein that polymerises into lattices of pentagons and hexagons over the cytoplasmic face of the plasma membrane to form **clathrin-coated pits** — the structural basis of **receptor-mediated endocytosis (RME)** [Boron ch.2]. Cargo-receptor complexes (e.g. **LDL bound to the LDL receptor**, transferrin–transferrin receptor) cluster in coated pits, the pit invaginates, dynamin pinches off the neck, and the resulting clathrin-coated vesicle releases its coat and fuses with early endosomes. Mutations affecting LDL-receptor function or clathrin-mediated uptake lead to **familial hypercholesterolaemia**, characterised by markedly elevated plasma LDL-cholesterol from impaired hepatic LDL clearance [Boron ch.2]. The clinical vignette of hypercholesterolaemia plus a clathrin-related defect points squarely to this pathway. Exocytosis, cell adhesion and the plasma membrane as a structural entity are not clathrin-dependent functions.

Hints:

1. Clathrin is the defining coat protein of one type of vesicle traffic.
2. Recall the disease in which a defect in LDL-receptor uptake elevates plasma cholesterol — what cellular process is impaired?
3. The pathway that brings receptor–ligand complexes into the cell at coated pits is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Exocytosis" — Reveals the misconception that clathrin coats secretory vesicles. Clathrin coats _endocytic_ and _trans-Golgi_ vesicles, not secretory release vesicles.
- "Cell to cell adhesion" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Plasma membrane" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 21

Stem: Which of the following is true about secretory vesicle transport?

Correct answer: No relation with concentration gradient

Explanation: Secretory vesicle transport is **vesicular** (bulk membrane-enclosed) trafficking, not transport of individual solute molecules across the membrane [Boron ch.2] [Guyton ch.2]. Vesicles bud from the trans-Golgi network, are conveyed along microtubule tracks by motor proteins (kinesin, dynein), dock at the plasma membrane via SNARE-protein recognition, and fuse to release their contents by exocytosis — typically triggered by a rise in cytosolic Ca²⁺. Because the cargo is sequestered inside the vesicle lumen and the entire vesicle (membrane + contents) is moved as a unit, _no concentration gradient of the cargo across a membrane drives the transport_. The process is energy-dependent (motor-protein ATPase activity, SNARE assembly/disassembly via NSF ATPase), but it is unrelated to the concentration of the secretory product itself. Hence option (b).

Hints:

1. Distinguish between transmembrane solute transport and bulk vesicular trafficking.
2. Vesicle movement is driven by motor proteins and signalling, not by solute concentration gradients.
3. The cargo is enclosed in a membrane — so its own concentration on either side of the plasma membrane is irrelevant to the transport step.

Distractors:

- "Along concentration gradient" — Reveals the misconception that vesicular transport is governed by transmembrane gradients. Vesicles carry their cargo as a closed packet — gradients are irrelevant.
- "Against the concentration gradient" — Same misconception as (a), with reversed direction.
- "Passive method of membrane transport" — Reveals the misconception that vesicle transport is passive. Motor proteins, SNARE machinery and Ca²⁺-triggered fusion all consume energy.

QUESTION 22

Stem: Transcytosis occurs in which of the following cell types?

Correct answer: Epithelial cells of intestine

Explanation: **Transcytosis** is the vectorial transport of intact macromolecules across a polarised epithelial or endothelial cell — endocytosis at one membrane domain (e.g. apical), traffic of the resulting vesicle through the cytoplasm, and exocytosis at the opposite domain (e.g. basolateral) [Boron ch.2]. It is a key mechanism for moving substances that would otherwise be degraded or excluded by paracellular tight junctions. Important biological examples include: maternal IgG transfer across placental syncytiotrophoblast and across neonatal **intestinal epithelium** (FcRn-mediated, the basis for passive immunity in breastfed infants); insulin and other macromolecule transit across capillary endothelium; and LDL transport across endothelium in atherogenesis. Transcytosis specifically requires _polarised_ cells with distinct apical/basolateral compartments — neurones, neuroglia and the axolemma do not exhibit transcytosis as a defining function.

Hints:

1. Transcytosis requires a cell with two distinct membrane domains for vectorial vesicle traffic.
2. Three of the listed cell types are non-polarised neural elements; one is a polarised epithelium.
3. Transfer of maternal IgG across the neonatal intestine is the classical example of this process.

Distractors:

- "Neuroglia" — Reveals the misconception that any cell can transcytose. Glia are not polarised epithelia; they perform glymphatic and metabolic functions but not vectorial transcytosis as a defining role.
- "Neuron" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Axolemma" — Reveals the misconception that the axonal membrane is itself a "cell type." The axolemma is a membrane domain, not a cell.

QUESTION 23

Stem: Which of the following ion is used in exocytosis?

Correct answer: Ca2+

Explanation: **Ca²⁺ is the universal trigger for regulated exocytosis** [Guyton ch.2] [Boron ch.2]. A rise in cytosolic [Ca²⁺] — produced by Ca²⁺ entry through voltage-gated channels (presynaptic terminals), receptor-operated channels (chromaffin cells), or release from intracellular stores via IP₃ or ryanodine receptors (acinar cells, β-cells in part) — is detected by **synaptotagmin**, a Ca²⁺-binding protein on the synaptic-vesicle membrane. Synaptotagmin's Ca²⁺-bound conformation engages the assembled **SNARE complex** (vesicle v-SNAREs synaptobrevin/VAMP paired with target t-SNAREs syntaxin and SNAP-25) and triggers vesicle fusion and contents release [Boron ch.2]. The same mechanism underlies neurotransmitter release at synapses, hormone release from endocrine cells, zymogen release from acinar cells, and degranulation of mast cells. K⁺, Na⁺ and Mg²⁺ do not trigger exocytosis directly.

Hints:

1. Identify the universal intracellular signal for regulated exocytosis.
2. Recall the protein on the vesicle that senses this ion to initiate fusion.
3. The trigger ion is the same one that triggers muscle contraction.

Distractors:

- "K+" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Na+" — Reveals the misconception that the depolarising ion is itself the trigger. Na⁺ entry causes the depolarisation that opens VGCCs; Ca²⁺ entry through those channels is the trigger for exocytosis.
- "Mg" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 24

Stem: A 35-year-old diabetic man is started on an insulin secretagogue. Which of the following ions is required for emeiocytosis or reverse pinocytosis during the insulin secretion process?

Correct answer: Ca2+

Explanation: Insulin secretion from pancreatic β-cells is the canonical example of **stimulus-secretion coupling** mediated by Ca²⁺ [Boron ch.5] [Guyton ch.75]. A rise in plasma glucose enters the β-cell via GLUT2, is phosphorylated by glucokinase and metabolised to raise intracellular ATP/ADP. The increased ATP closes ATP-sensitive K⁺ channels (K_ATP), depolarising the β-cell membrane. Depolarisation opens **voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels (VGCCs)**, allowing Ca²⁺ influx; the rise in cytosolic [Ca²⁺] triggers SNARE-mediated **exocytosis (emeiocytosis / reverse pinocytosis)** of insulin-containing dense-core granules. **Sulfonylurea insulin secretagogues** (glibenclamide, glimepiride) close the K_ATP channel directly, mimicking this depolarisation. The required ion for the exocytotic step itself is **Ca²⁺** — the same as for all regulated exocytosis. Na⁺, K⁺ and Mg²⁺ play modulatory roles but are not the triggering ion.

Hints:

1. Insulin secretion is a textbook example of stimulus-secretion coupling — recall the chain of events.
2. The exocytotic trigger is the same ion that triggers all regulated secretion across cell types.
3. Sulfonylureas close K_ATP channels; the resulting depolarisation opens channels for which ion?

Distractors:

- "Na+" — Reveals the misconception that depolarisation requires Na⁺ entry. β-cell depolarisation is driven by K_ATP closure (not Na⁺ entry); Ca²⁺ entry through VGCCs is the trigger.
- "K+" — Reveals the misconception that K⁺ flux is the trigger. K⁺ permeability decrease (K_ATP closure) sets the stage, but Ca²⁺ entry triggers the exocytotic step.
- "Mg2+" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
