QUESTION 1

Stem: Which of the following is true about thalamus?

Correct answer: Thalamus is the sensory gateway to cerebral cortex

Explanation: The **thalamus** is the principal **sensory relay** of the brain — virtually all ascending sensory pathways (except olfaction) synapse in thalamic relay nuclei before reaching the cortex [Guyton ch.46] [Guyton ch.48]. **VPL** relays body somatosensation, **VPM** relays facial somatosensation, **MGN** auditory, **LGN** visual. Thus the thalamus is the **sensory gateway**. (b) false: thalamus is part of the **diencephalon**, not telencephalon. (c) the Y-shaped **internal medullary lamina** divides thalamus into **3** main subdivisions (anterior, medial, lateral), not 4. (d) the thalamus also relays motor signals (VL relays cerebellar/basal-ganglia output to motor cortex), but the cardinal characterisation is sensory.

Hints:

1. Recall the thalamus's principal role in CNS information flow.
2. Almost all sensory pathways relay through it before reaching cortex.
3. The cardinal description is "sensory gateway".

Distractors:

- "largest part of telencephalon" — Thalamus is in diencephalon, not telencephalon.
- "Y-shaped band... 4 subdivisions" — The internal medullary lamina divides thalamus into 3 (anterior, medial, lateral), not 4.
- "motor gateway from cortex" — Plausible but the textbook framing emphasises sensory.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Which thalamic nuclei transmit pain?

Correct answer: Ventral posterior

Explanation: **Pain transmission** to the cortex via the spinothalamic pathway relays in the **ventral posterolateral nucleus (VPL)** of the thalamus (body) and **VPM** (face), which then project to primary somatosensory cortex (S1) for discriminative pain perception [Guyton ch.49]. The **intralaminar nuclei** (centromedian, parafascicular) also receive spinothalamic input and project diffusely to cortex and limbic system, mediating the _affective/arousal_ component of pain — but the textbook MCQ-level answer for "thalamic nuclei that transmit pain" is the **ventral posterior nucleus**. **Lateral dorsal** is associated with limbic functions; **pulvinar** is association cortex projections.

Hints:

1. Recall the thalamic relay for pain in the dorsal column-spinothalamic system.
2. The ventral posterior nuclei relay pain from spinothalamic afferents.
3. The discriminative-pain relay is VPL/VPM.

Distractors:

- "Lateral dorsal" — Limbic-associated.
- "Intralaminar" — Mediates affective/arousal aspects of pain (true secondary contributor); the keyed answer favours (c).
- "Pulvinar" — Association cortex projections.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Both VPL/VPM (option c) and intralaminar nuclei (option b) transmit pain — VPL/VPM for discriminative localisation, intralaminar for the affective/arousal component. The keyed answer (c) reflects the textbook emphasis on the discriminative pathway.]

QUESTION 3

Stem: Which of the following is called "the window of limbic system"?

Correct answer: Amygdala

Explanation: The **amygdala** is sometimes referred to as the **"window of the limbic system"** because it integrates sensory inputs from cortex (especially temporal cortex) with emotional, autonomic and endocrine responses, providing a clear "view" of the limbic system's role in linking sensation to emotion [Guyton ch.58]. The amygdala mediates fear, threat detection, emotional learning, and the autonomic correlates of emotion. The **hypothalamus** is sometimes called the "head ganglion" of the autonomic nervous system. The **hippocampus** is the gateway to memory consolidation. The thalamus is the sensory gateway.

Hints:

1. Recall which limbic structure receives broad sensory input and outputs to autonomic/emotional centres.
2. The amygdala couples sensation to emotion.
3. The "window" metaphor refers to the amygdala's integrative role.

Distractors:

- "Hypothalamus" — "Head ganglion" of ANS, not "window".
- "Hippocampus" — Memory consolidation.
- "Thalamus" — Sensory gateway, not limbic specifically.

[NEEDS VERIFICATION: The phrase "window of the limbic system" is found in some Indian MBBS texts but is not standard terminology in Guyton or other major references. The amygdala's integrative role is well-established but the specific eponym "window" requires text-specific verification.]

QUESTION 4

Stem: Which of the following is a component of the Papez circuit?

Correct answer: Anterior nucleus of thalamus

Explanation: The **Papez circuit** is the classical limbic loop for emotion (and contributing to memory): **hippocampus → fornix → mammillary bodies → mammillothalamic tract → anterior nucleus of thalamus → cingulate cortex → cingulum → entorhinal cortex → hippocampus** [Guyton ch.58]. Hence the **anterior nucleus of the thalamus** is a key component. The other options (lateral dorsal, ventral anterior, pulvinar) are thalamic nuclei serving different functions and are not part of Papez circuit.

Hints:

1. Recall the named structures in the Papez circuit.
2. The thalamic relay in Papez is the anterior nucleus.
3. Mammillothalamic tract terminates in the anterior thalamus.

Distractors:

- "Lateral dorsal nucleus" — Limbic-related but not Papez.
- "Ventral anterior nucleus" — Motor relay (basal ganglia output).
- "Pulvinar" — Sensory association.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Stimulation of which of the following regions promotes a 'sense of reward' in human beings?

Correct answer: Medial forebrain bundle

Explanation: The **medial forebrain bundle (MFB)** is the **principal reward pathway**, carrying ascending fibres from VTA dopaminergic neurones through the lateral hypothalamus to the nucleus accumbens, prefrontal cortex, and other limbic targets [Guyton ch.58]. Olds & Milner's classic experiments showed that rats with electrodes in the MFB self-stimulate compulsively. In humans, MFB stimulation produces intense pleasure/reward sensations. The MFB is therefore the canonical "reward pathway". **Insula** is involved in interoception and visceral sensation. **Putamen** is motor (basal ganglia). **Aqueduct of Sylvius** is a CSF channel, not a reward structure.

Hints:

1. Recall the principal anatomical substrate of the brain's reward system.
2. The medial forebrain bundle carries reward-related dopaminergic projections.
3. Stimulation of MFB is intensely rewarding.

Distractors:

- "Insula" — Interoception, not reward per se.
- "Putamen" — Motor.
- "Aqueduct of sylvius" — Anatomical channel, not a functional structure.

QUESTION 6

Stem: In response to an increase in plasma osmolality, ADH is released in blood. Which of the following sites contains the receptor to detect a change in plasma osmolality?

Correct answer: Anterior hypothalamus

Explanation: **Osmoreceptors** that detect plasma osmolality reside in the **anterior hypothalamus**, particularly in the **organum vasculosum of the lamina terminalis (OVLT)** and **subfornical organ (SFO)** — circumventricular organs that lack a blood-brain barrier and can directly sample plasma osmolality [Guyton ch.58]. Increased osmolality triggers neural signals to the **supraoptic** and **paraventricular nuclei**, which secrete **ADH (vasopressin)** from their posterior pituitary terminals. The **preoptic area** contains thermoregulatory neurones (heat-loss centre); **arcuate** is involved in feeding and reproductive endocrinology; **mammillary bodies** are part of the Papez memory circuit.

Hints:

1. Recall the location of osmoreceptors in the brain.
2. Osmoreceptors lie in circumventricular organs of the anterior hypothalamus.
3. The anterior hypothalamus contains the osmosensitive cells.

Distractors:

- "Preoptic" — Thermoregulation.
- "Arcuate" — Feeding/reproductive endocrinology.
- "Mammillary" — Papez memory circuit.

QUESTION 7

Stem: The main organ for biological clock is **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Hypothalamus

Explanation: The brain's **master biological clock** is the **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus** [Guyton ch.58]. SCN neurones have an autonomous ~24-hour molecular oscillator (CLOCK/BMAL1 / PER/CRY transcription-translation feedback loop), are entrained by light input from intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs containing melanopsin) via the retinohypothalamic tract, and synchronise downstream rhythms (cortisol, body temperature, sleep-wake, melatonin via the pineal gland). Hence the hypothalamus is the principal organ of the biological clock.

Hints:

1. Recall the location of the master circadian pacemaker in the brain.
2. The suprachiasmatic nucleus is in the hypothalamus.
3. The hypothalamus houses the master clock.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — Not the master circadian pacemaker.

QUESTION 9

Stem: Diurnal variation of ACTH is controlled by which of the following regions?

Correct answer: Supra chiasmatic nucleus

Explanation: The **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** is the master circadian pacemaker that imposes diurnal rhythms on hormone secretion, including the well-known **diurnal cortisol/ACTH rhythm** (peak around dawn, trough at midnight) [Guyton ch.58]. The SCN entrains the paraventricular nucleus's CRH-secreting neurones, which drive the pituitary ACTH rhythm, which drives the adrenal cortisol rhythm. The PVN is the _executor_ of the rhythm, but the **clock** that imposes the diurnal pattern is the SCN. Hence (d).

Hints:

1. Recall the master circadian pacemaker.
2. SCN imposes diurnal rhythms on the HPA axis (CRH → ACTH → cortisol).
3. The clock is the SCN, not the PVN.

Distractors:

- "Paraventricular hypothalamus" — Executes the rhythm via CRH but does not generate it.
- (b), (c) — Other hypothalamic regions, not circadian.

QUESTION 10

Stem: Which of the following is not a function of the categorical hemisphere?

Correct answer: Music ability

Explanation: The **categorical hemisphere** is the language-dominant hemisphere (left in ~95% of right-handers and ~70% of left-handers) — also called the dominant or analytical hemisphere [Guyton ch.58]. Its specialised functions: **language** (Broca's, Wernicke's areas), **writing**, **mathematical/calculation ability**, sequential and logical analysis. The **representational (non-dominant) hemisphere** specialises in **music**, spatial relationships, face recognition, and emotional/prosodic aspects of speech. Hence music ability is _not_ a function of the categorical hemisphere — it is the representational hemisphere's domain.

Hints:

1. Recall the functional specialisation of categorical (dominant) vs representational (non-dominant) hemispheres.
2. Language, writing, and calculation are categorical-hemisphere functions.
3. Music is a representational-hemisphere function.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — All categorical hemisphere functions.

QUESTION 11

Stem: Lesions in which nucleus of the hypothalamus produce a voracious appetite and rage?

Correct answer: Ventromedial nucleus of the hypothalamus

Explanation: The **ventromedial nucleus (VMN)** of the hypothalamus is the **satiety centre** [Guyton ch.58] [Guyton ch.74]: stimulation suppresses feeding, and **bilateral lesions cause hyperphagia, obesity, and rage** (the classical "VMN syndrome" first described in cats). The **lateral hypothalamus** is the **feeding centre**: stimulation triggers eating, lesions cause aphagia and emaciation (the opposite). **Mammillary** is part of Papez circuit. **Anterior hypothalamus** is thermoregulation/osmoregulation. Hence VMN lesion → voracious appetite + rage.

Hints:

1. Recall the satiety vs feeding centres of the hypothalamus.
2. VMN is the satiety centre; its lesion releases appetite.
3. The "voracious appetite + rage" combination is classical for VMN lesion.

Distractors:

- "Upper region of the lateral hypothalamus" — Lateral hypothalamus = feeding centre; lesion causes aphagia, not hyperphagia.
- "Mammillary region" — Memory circuit.
- "Anterior region of the hypothalamus" — Thermoregulation/osmoregulation.

QUESTION 13

Stem: The factors that can cause a person to drink water due to stimulation of thirst mechanism is/are **\_\_\_\_**.

Correct answer: All of the above

Explanation: **Thirst** is regulated by the hypothalamic thirst centre (lateral preoptic area / OVLT/SFO circumventricular organs) responding to multiple inputs [Guyton ch.74]: (a) **dehydration** raises plasma osmolality, activating osmoreceptors in OVLT/SFO → thirst (true). (b) **haemorrhage** reduces blood volume, activating volume-sensitive baroreceptors and stimulating angiotensin II (which acts on SFO) → thirst (true). (c) **prandial drinking** is associative — eating triggers thirst via cephalic-phase oropharyngeal/learned mechanisms (anticipating osmotic load) (true). All three can stimulate thirst, hence (d).

Hints:

1. Recall the multiple stimuli that activate thirst.
2. Osmolality, volume, and feeding-associated cues all stimulate thirst.
3. All listed factors apply.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Each stimulates thirst; (d) is the inclusive answer.
