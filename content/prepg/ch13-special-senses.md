QUESTION 1

Stem: Light entering the eye first passes through **\_\_** retinal layer.

Correct answer: Ganglion cell layer

Explanation: The vertebrate retina is **inverted**: the photoreceptors are at the _back_ (outer surface, adjacent to the pigment epithelium and choroid), and light has to pass _through_ all the inner retinal layers — ganglion cells, nerve fibre layer, inner plexiform, inner nuclear, outer plexiform, outer nuclear — before reaching the photoreceptor outer segments [Guyton ch.51]. Because the **ganglion cell layer** lies on the vitreal (inner) side of the retina, light **first** passes through it. The fovea is an exception: the inner layers are displaced laterally, allowing light to reach the foveal cones with minimal interference, which is the structural basis of high foveal acuity [Guyton ch.51].

Hints:

1. Recall the orientation of the vertebrate retina: photoreceptors are at the back, ganglion cells at the front.
2. Light must traverse all the inner layers before reaching photoreceptors.
3. The first layer encountered by light is on the vitreal (inner) side.

Distractors:

- "Inner nuclear layer" — Inner-third of the retina but not the most vitreal.
- "Outer nuclear layer" — Far back; light reaches it last among nuclear layers.
- "Inner plexiform layer" — Reached after the ganglion cell layer.

QUESTION 2

Stem: The fovea centralis is characterized by all of the following except:

Correct answer: Presence of all layers of retina

Explanation: The **fovea centralis** is a small central depression in the retina (~0.5 mm) specialised for high-acuity vision [Guyton ch.51]. Its features: (a) **highest visual acuity** in the retina because of dense cone packing and minimal interposed neural layers — true; (b) **rods are absent** in the foveola (central foveal pit) — true; (c) **only cones** in the foveola — true; (d) is **false** — the fovea is characterised by **lateral displacement of the inner retinal layers**, so that the inner nuclear, ganglion cell and other layers are pushed _aside_, exposing the cones directly to incoming light. The fovea therefore does _not_ contain "all layers of the retina" — it specifically lacks the inner layers in the central foveolar region.

Hints:

1. Recall the structural specialisation of the foveola.
2. The displacement of inner layers is the basis of the foveal acuity advantage.
3. The fovea has fewer, not more, layers.

Distractors:

- "Highest visual acuity" — Correctly true.
- "Absence of rods" — Correctly true.
- "Presence of only cones" — Correctly true.

QUESTION 3

Stem: The blind spot in the visual field corresponds to:

Correct answer: Optic disc

Explanation: The **optic disc** is where retinal ganglion cell axons converge to leave the eye as the optic nerve, and where retinal blood vessels enter and exit [Guyton ch.51]. There are **no photoreceptors** at the optic disc; light falling on this region is not detected, producing a **physiological blind spot** ~15° lateral to the fixation point in each eye. We are normally unaware of the blind spot because the brain fills in the missing visual field from the surrounding image and from the other eye's input.

Hints:

1. Recall the location on the retina that lacks photoreceptors.
2. The exit of the optic nerve from the eye creates a region with no rods or cones.
3. This region is the optic disc.

Distractors:

- "Fovea centralis" — Highest visual acuity, opposite of blind spot.
- "Macula lutea" — Surrounding the fovea, dense cone region; not blind.
- "Ora serrata" — The peripheral edge of the retina where it ends; not the blind spot.

QUESTION 5

Stem: The refractive power of the cornea is about:

Correct answer: 43 diopters

Explanation: The total refractive power of the eye at rest is **~59 diopters (D)**, of which the **cornea contributes ~43 D** (about two-thirds) and the **lens ~17 D** (about one-third), with smaller contributions from the aqueous and vitreous [Guyton ch.50]. The cornea is the dominant refractive element because of its strong curvature and the large refractive-index step from air (n = 1) to corneal stroma (n ≈ 1.376) at its anterior surface; this air-cornea interface accounts for almost all of the cornea's power. The lens has a smaller index step (immersed in aqueous and vitreous on either side, both with n ≈ 1.34), so despite being a biconvex lens it contributes much less power than the cornea — but it is the only element whose power can be _adjusted_ (accommodation, ~13 D extra in young adults).

Hints:

1. Recall the standard textbook breakdown of the eye's total refractive power.
2. The cornea contributes the majority of the eye's fixed refractive power.
3. The cornea's value is approximately two-thirds of the eye's total ~60 D.

Distractors:

- "20 diopters" — Approximately the lens contribution at maximum accommodation (~17 D), not the cornea.
- "60 diopters" — Total refractive power of the whole eye, not cornea alone.
- "80 diopters" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 6

Stem: The greatest refractive power of the eye is provided by:

Correct answer: Cornea

Explanation: As detailed in Q5, the **cornea** provides ~43 D of the eye's ~59 D total — the greatest single contribution [Guyton ch.50]. The lens contributes ~17 D (variable with accommodation). The aqueous and vitreous have minimal refractive power individually because they are bounded on both sides by media of similar refractive index. Hence the cornea is the dominant refractive element. Counter-intuitively, even though the lens is what people most commonly associate with refraction, its power is less than the cornea's because of the smaller index step at its surfaces.

Hints:

1. Recall the contributions of each refractive element of the eye.
2. The element with the largest refractive index step against air is the answer.
3. The corneal-air interface is the largest single source of refraction.

Distractors:

- "Lens" — Reveals the misconception that the lens is the major refractive element. Lens contributes ~17 D vs cornea's ~43 D.
- "Aqueous humor" — Negligible refractive contribution.
- "Vitreous humor" — Negligible refractive contribution.

QUESTION 7

Stem: Which of the following is the correct order of the visual pathway?

Correct answer: Retina → Optic nerve → Optic chiasm → Optic tract → Lateral geniculate nucleus → Optic radiation → Visual cortex

Explanation: Standard visual pathway sequence [Guyton ch.52]: photoreceptors → bipolar cells → ganglion cells (retina) → **optic nerve** (anterior to the chiasm) → **optic chiasm** (where nasal fibres decussate) → **optic tract** (posterior to chiasm, carrying contralateral hemifield from both eyes) → **lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of thalamus** (synapse) → **optic radiation (geniculocalcarine tract)** → **primary visual cortex (V1)** in the calcarine sulcus of the occipital lobe. Option (a) is the only correct sequence.

Hints:

1. Trace the visual signal from retina to cortex.
2. The thalamic relay (LGN) precedes the optic radiation.
3. The sequence is: retina → nerve → chiasm → tract → LGN → radiation → cortex.

Distractors:

- "Retina → Optic nerve → Optic chiasm → Optic tract → Optic radiation → Lateral geniculate nucleus → Visual cortex" — Reverses LGN and optic radiation (LGN precedes radiation).
- "Retina → Optic nerve → Lateral geniculate nucleus → Optic chiasm → Optic tract → Optic radiation → Visual cortex" — Misplaces LGN before chiasm.
- "Retina → Optic chiasm → Optic nerve → Optic tract → Lateral geniculate nucleus → Optic radiation → Visual cortex" — Reverses optic nerve and chiasm.

QUESTION 8

Stem: The primary visual cortex is located in:

Correct answer: Occipital lobe

Explanation: The **primary visual cortex (V1, Brodmann area 17, striate cortex)** lies in the **occipital lobe**, on either side of the **calcarine sulcus** on the medial surface of the hemisphere [Guyton ch.52]. From V1, signals project to extrastriate visual areas (V2–V5) for higher-order processing along two streams: a **dorsal "where/how" stream** to parietal cortex (motion, location, action), and a **ventral "what" stream** to temporal cortex (object recognition, faces).

Hints:

1. Recall the cortical lobe that houses V1.
2. The visual cortex sits on either side of the calcarine sulcus.
3. V1 is in the occipital lobe.

Distractors:

- "Parietal lobe" — Houses the dorsal extrastriate stream and the somatosensory cortex, not V1.
- "Temporal lobe" — Houses the ventral extrastriate stream and the auditory cortex, not V1.
- "Frontal lobe" — Motor and executive areas, not visual.

QUESTION 9

Stem: Which of the following is true regarding the visual pathway?

Correct answer: Nasal fibers cross at the optic chiasm

Explanation: The **optic chiasm** is the partial decussation site of the visual pathway [Guyton ch.52]. **Nasal retinal fibres** (carrying the temporal hemifield) **cross** to the contralateral optic tract; **temporal retinal fibres** (carrying the nasal hemifield) **stay ipsilateral**. This arrangement ensures that each optic tract carries information from the _contralateral hemifield_ of vision (right hemifield → left optic tract → left LGN → left V1, etc.) — the rule that allows lesions to produce the characteristic visual field defects.

Hints:

1. Recall which retinal fibres cross at the chiasm and which do not.
2. Nasal fibres cross; temporal fibres stay ipsilateral.
3. The crossing arrangement maps each hemifield to the contralateral cortex.

Distractors:

- "Temporal fibers cross at the optic chiasm" — Reverses crossing arrangement.
- "All fibers cross at the optic chiasm" — Reveals the misconception that the chiasm is a complete decussation. It is partial.
- "No fibers cross at the optic chiasm" — Reveals the misconception that the chiasm is just an anatomical landmark with no crossing.

QUESTION 10

Stem: Lesion of the optic chiasm causes:

Correct answer: Bitemporal hemianopia

Explanation: A **midline lesion of the optic chiasm** (classically a **pituitary adenoma** compressing the chiasm from below) damages the **decussating nasal fibres of both eyes** [Guyton ch.52]. Since nasal fibres carry the temporal visual hemifield from each eye, the patient loses **bilateral temporal visual field defects** — **bitemporal hemianopia**. Pituitary tumours, craniopharyngiomas and aneurysms of the internal carotid are typical causes. Bilateral lateral compression of the chiasm (rare; e.g. internal carotid artery aneurysms compressing both lateral chiasm edges) would damage the temporal/uncrossed fibres and produce the very rare **binasal hemianopia**.

Hints:

1. Recall what fibres run through the central chiasm.
2. Crossing nasal fibres carry the temporal visual hemifield.
3. Damaging both crossing groups produces bilateral temporal hemianopia.

Distractors:

- "Homonymous hemianopia" — Caused by lesions _posterior_ to the chiasm (optic tract, LGN, optic radiation, visual cortex on one side).
- "Binasal hemianopia" — Caused by _bilateral_ compression of the lateral chiasm — very rare.
- "Central scotoma" — Caused by macular lesions or optic neuritis, not chiasmal lesions.

QUESTION 11

Stem: The aqueous humor is produced by:

Correct answer: Ciliary body

Explanation: **Aqueous humour** is secreted by the **non-pigmented epithelium of the ciliary processes (ciliary body)** at a rate of ~2.5 µL/min [Guyton ch.50]. It flows from the posterior chamber through the pupil into the anterior chamber and exits via the **trabecular meshwork** at the iridocorneal angle into the **canal of Schlemm**, then into episcleral veins. Imbalance between production and outflow raises intraocular pressure and causes glaucoma. **Choroid plexus** produces cerebrospinal fluid, not aqueous humour.

Hints:

1. Recall the source of aqueous humour.
2. The ciliary processes contain the secreting epithelium.
3. The trabecular meshwork is the outflow site.

Distractors:

- "Choroid plexus" — Produces CSF, not aqueous humour.
- "Iris" — Forms the diaphragm, does not secrete aqueous.
- "Lens" — Avascular, does not secrete aqueous.

QUESTION 13

Stem: Rods are responsible for:

Correct answer: Vision in dim light

Explanation: Two photoreceptor types serve different roles [Guyton ch.51]: **Rods** — high sensitivity, single rhodopsin photopigment, **scotopic (dim-light, night) vision**, no colour discrimination, lower acuity, predominate outside the macula. **Cones** — lower sensitivity, three photopigments (S, M, L for blue, green, red — iodopsins), **photopic (bright-light, day) vision**, colour discrimination, high acuity, concentrated at the fovea. Hence rods → dim light. The transition from rod to cone function at increasing light levels is the **Purkinje shift** (Q17).

Hints:

1. Recall the division of labour between rods and cones.
2. Rods are the dim-light, high-sensitivity, monochromatic photoreceptors.
3. Cones serve bright-light colour vision.

Distractors:

- "Color vision" — Cones, not rods.
- "Vision in bright light" — Cones (rods saturate in bright light).
- "High visual acuity" — Cones (foveal cones).

QUESTION 14

Stem: Cones are maximally concentrated in:

Correct answer: Fovea centralis

Explanation: **Cones are most densely packed in the fovea centralis** (~199,000/mm² in the foveola) [Guyton ch.51]. Cone density falls off rapidly outside the foveola, while rod density rises from zero at the foveola to a peak at ~20° eccentricity, then falls again toward the periphery. **Optic disc** has no photoreceptors. **Peripheral retina** is rod-dominated. **Ora serrata** is the anterior boundary of the retina — minimal photoreceptors.

Hints:

1. Recall where cones are densest.
2. Cone density peaks at the central retinal pit.
3. The fovea is the high-acuity, cone-rich region.

Distractors:

- "Optic disc" — No photoreceptors.
- "Peripheral retina" — Rod-dominated.
- "Ora serrata" — Anterior retinal boundary, minimal photoreceptors.

QUESTION 15

Stem: The photopigment in rods is:

Correct answer: Rhodopsin

Explanation: **Rods contain rhodopsin** — a single photopigment composed of **opsin (scotopsin)** combined with **11-cis retinal** (a vitamin A1 derivative) [Guyton ch.51]. Light isomerises 11-cis retinal to all-trans retinal, triggering the phototransduction cascade. **Cones** contain three different opsins ("photopsins" or "iodopsins") tuned to short (S, blue), medium (M, green) and long (L, red) wavelengths. **Melanopsin** is a separate non-image-forming photopigment in **intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs)**, mediating circadian entrainment and the pupillary light reflex.

Hints:

1. Recall the photopigment of rods.
2. Rhodopsin = opsin + 11-cis retinal.
3. Cones use the related but distinct iodopsins.

Distractors:

- "Iodopsin" — Cone photopigment.
- "Photopsin" — Cone opsin.
- "Melanopsin" — Non-image-forming ipRGC pigment for circadian rhythm.

QUESTION 16

Stem: Which of the following is true about dark adaptation?

Correct answer: It is due to regeneration of rhodopsin

Explanation: **Dark adaptation** is the gradual increase in retinal sensitivity when transitioning from bright light to darkness [Guyton ch.51]. The dark adaptation curve has two phases: a fast initial **cone phase** (~5 minutes) and a slower **rod phase** that begins after the rod-cone break (Kohlrausch break) at ~7–10 minutes and continues to maximum sensitivity at ~30 minutes. The mechanism is the **regeneration of rhodopsin** (and cone photopigments) — bleached pigment must regenerate from opsin and 11-cis retinal, restoring the sensitivity of the photoreceptors. Vitamin A deficiency impairs rhodopsin regeneration, causing **night blindness (nyctalopia)**.

Hints:

1. Recall the molecular basis of dark adaptation.
2. Bleached photopigment must regenerate to restore sensitivity.
3. The principal photopigment regenerated for night vision is rhodopsin.

Distractors:

- "It is faster in cones than rods" — Defensible (cones do adapt faster initially) but the keyed answer favours the molecular mechanism (d).
- "It is faster in rods than cones" — Wrong: cones adapt faster, but reach lower maximum sensitivity than rods.
- "It takes about 5 minutes" — Partial truth (cone phase is ~5 min) but full dark adaptation takes ~30 min.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Both (a) and (d) are true statements about dark adaptation. Cones do adapt faster (within ~5 min) than rods (~30 min). The keyed answer (d) reflects the more fundamental mechanistic explanation. A student answering (a) is not factually wrong but the question key prefers (d).]

QUESTION 17

Stem: The Purkinje shift refers to:

Correct answer: Shift from cone to rod vision in dim light

Explanation: The **Purkinje shift** describes the change in spectral sensitivity of vision as light levels fall from photopic to scotopic [Guyton ch.51]. In bright light (photopic), peak sensitivity is at ~555 nm (yellow-green; cone-mediated). In dim light (scotopic), peak sensitivity shifts to ~507 nm (blue-green; rod-mediated, governed by the absorption spectrum of rhodopsin). The visible consequence: red flowers appear bright in daylight but very dim at twilight, while blue flowers retain their relative brightness — the so-called Purkinje effect. The shift represents the transition from **cone-dominated to rod-dominated vision** as illumination decreases. Hence (b).

Hints:

1. Recall the spectral sensitivity peaks of rods and cones.
2. The shift in peak sensitivity to shorter wavelengths in dim light is the Purkinje shift.
3. The shift reflects the transition from cone-dominated to rod-dominated vision.

Distractors:

- "Shift from rod to cone vision" — Reverses the direction.
- "Change in color perception" — Plausible but the Purkinje shift specifically refers to the rod-cone transition, not colour perception per se.
- "Change in visual acuity" — Acuity does change with light levels but is not the Purkinje shift.

QUESTION 19

Stem: The optic radiation is also known as:

Correct answer: Both a and b

Explanation: The **optic radiation** (axons projecting from the LGN to the primary visual cortex) is also called the **geniculocalcarine tract** because it runs from the (lateral) **geniculate** body to the **calcarine** cortex [Guyton ch.52]. Within the optic radiation, the lower fibres take a detour anteriorly into the temporal lobe before turning posteriorly to reach the inferior bank of the calcarine sulcus — this temporal-lobe loop is **Meyer's loop**, carrying the upper visual field representation. Damage to Meyer's loop (e.g. temporal lobectomy) produces a contralateral **superior quadrantanopia** ("pie in the sky"). Hence both (a) and (b) are valid names — option (c).

Hints:

1. Recall the anatomical names for the LGN-to-cortex projection.
2. The geniculocalcarine name reflects the trajectory; the Meyer's loop name refers to the temporal-lobe portion.
3. Both terms are valid for the optic radiation as a whole or its components.

Distractors:

- "Meyer's loop" — Correct part of the answer (the temporal-lobe portion of the optic radiation).
- "Geniculocalcarine tract" — Correct part of the answer (the entire pathway from LGN to V1).
- "Neither a nor b" — Wrong: both terms are valid.

QUESTION 20

Stem: Lesion in the right optic tract causes:

Correct answer: Left homonymous hemianopia

Explanation: After the optic chiasm, fibres in each optic tract carry information from the **contralateral visual hemifield** of _both_ eyes [Guyton ch.52]: the right optic tract carries the **left visual hemifield**. A lesion of the **right optic tract** therefore causes loss of the **left visual hemifield** in both eyes — i.e. **left homonymous hemianopia**. The same defect can arise from any post-chiasmal lesion on the right side (right LGN, right optic radiation, right occipital cortex), differing only in completeness, macular sparing and other secondary features.

Hints:

1. Recall the side-of-hemifield rule for post-chiasmal lesions.
2. Each post-chiasmal pathway carries the contralateral visual hemifield of both eyes.
3. A right-side lesion produces a left-sided field defect.

Distractors:

- "Right homonymous hemianopia" — Wrong side; would require a left-sided post-chiasmal lesion.
- "Bitemporal hemianopia" — Caused by chiasmal lesion, not post-chiasmal.
- "Binasal hemianopia" — Caused by bilateral lateral chiasm compression, very rare.
