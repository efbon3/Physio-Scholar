QUESTION 1

Stem: Which of the following neurotransmitter is not a biogenic amine?

Correct answer: Glycine

Explanation: **Biogenic amines** are neurotransmitters synthesised from a single amino acid by decarboxylation [Guyton ch.46]. They include the **catecholamines** (dopamine, **norepinephrine**, epinephrine — all derived from tyrosine via DOPA), the **indoleamines** (**serotonin** and melatonin — from tryptophan), and **histamine** (from histidine). **Glycine** is itself an **amino acid neurotransmitter** (used directly without modification, primarily in the spinal cord and brainstem as an inhibitory transmitter at glycine receptors). Hence (d) is the exception — glycine is an amino acid transmitter, not a biogenic amine.

Hints:

1. Recall the chemical classes of neurotransmitters: amino acids, biogenic amines, peptides, gaseous.
2. Three of the listed transmitters are amine derivatives; one is itself an amino acid.
3. The exception is the smallest amino acid, used as an inhibitory transmitter in the spinal cord.

Distractors:

- "Norepinephrine" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Dopamine" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Serotonin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 2

Stem: The main excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters in the CNS are **\_\_**

Correct answer: Glutamate and glycine respectively

Explanation: **Glutamate** is the principal excitatory neurotransmitter throughout the CNS (~80% of excitatory synapses) [Guyton ch.46]. **Glycine and GABA** are both inhibitory transmitters; **GABA dominates in the brain**, while **glycine dominates in the spinal cord and brainstem**. The MCQ's pairing of glutamate (excitatory) and glycine (inhibitory) is a defensible answer because it covers both major regions of the CNS and pairs the dominant excitatory transmitter with one of the two dominant inhibitory transmitters. (Some texts would prefer "glutamate and GABA" as the brain-focused answer.)

Hints:

1. Recall the two dominant neurotransmitters of the CNS — one excitatory, one inhibitory.
2. The excitatory transmitter is also the immediate precursor of GABA via glutamate decarboxylase.
3. The pairing in this question takes glycine as the inhibitory partner; some texts pair glutamate with GABA instead.

Distractors:

- "Aspartate and glycine respectively" — Reveals the misconception that aspartate is the dominant excitatory transmitter. Aspartate is excitatory but quantitatively minor compared with glutamate.
- "Norepinephrine and GABA respectively" — Reveals the misconception that NA is the dominant excitatory transmitter in the CNS. NA modulates arousal and attention but is not the major excitatory transmitter.
- "Dopamine and GABA respectively" — Same misconception as (c) with dopamine.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: The pairing of glutamate with glycine (rather than GABA) is unusual; some textbooks would deem "glutamate and GABA" the canonical answer. Students answering (a) here are correct per the keyed answer; those who would prefer "glutamate and GABA" are not factually wrong but the question key prioritises (a).]

QUESTION 3

Stem: 'A' delta fibers and 'C' fibers carry pain sensation. The neurotransmitter released by 'A' delta fibers is **\_\_**

Correct answer: Glutamate

Explanation: First-order pain afferents synapse on second-order neurones in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord. **Aδ fibres** (carrying _fast_ "first" pain — sharp, well-localised) release **glutamate** as their primary transmitter, producing fast EPSPs at AMPA/NMDA receptors on the second-order neurone [Guyton ch.46]. **C fibres** (carrying _slow_ "second" pain — dull, poorly-localised) co-release **substance P** (a peptide) along with glutamate; substance P binds neurokinin-1 (NK1) receptors and produces slow, prolonged depolarisation contributing to the chronic, aching quality of C-fibre pain. The MCQ correctly distinguishes Aδ (glutamate) from C (substance P + glutamate).

Hints:

1. Recall the neurotransmitters used by Aδ and C pain afferents.
2. Aδ uses fast excitatory transmission with the major CNS excitatory transmitter.
3. C fibres add a peptide (substance P) for the slow component.

Distractors:

- "Substance P" — Reveals the misconception that all pain afferents use substance P. Substance P is the slow-pain transmitter of C fibres; Aδ uses glutamate.
- "Histamine" — Reveals the misconception that histamine is a pain-pathway transmitter. Histamine modulates arousal from the tuberomammillary nucleus.
- "Norepinephrine" — Reveals the misconception that pain afferents use NA. NA descends from locus coeruleus and modulates pain at the dorsal horn but is not released _by_ pain afferents themselves.

QUESTION 4

Stem: The most important neurotransmitter of reward pathway is **\_\_**

Correct answer: Dopamine

Explanation: The brain's **reward pathway** is the **mesolimbic dopaminergic system**, projecting from the **ventral tegmental area (VTA)** of the midbrain to the **nucleus accumbens** and prefrontal cortex [Guyton ch.46]. **Dopamine** released into the nucleus accumbens encodes reward prediction and motivation; it is the common final pathway of all addictive drugs (cocaine, amphetamine, opioids, nicotine, alcohol — all increase mesolimbic dopamine) and of natural rewards (food, sex, social bonding). Other dopaminergic pathways serve different functions: nigrostriatal (motor), tuberoinfundibular (prolactin inhibition), mesocortical (executive function).

Hints:

1. Recall the transmitter of the mesolimbic system.
2. All addictive drugs converge on this single transmitter.
3. The transmitter is also implicated in Parkinson's (different pathway) and schizophrenia.

Distractors:

- "Serotonin" — Reveals the misconception that serotonin is the reward transmitter. Serotonin modulates mood, anxiety and impulsivity but is not the primary reward signal.
- "Noradrenaline" — Reveals the misconception that NA is reward-related. NA mediates arousal and attention from the locus coeruleus.
- "Acetylcholine" — Reveals the misconception that ACh is reward-related. ACh from the basal forebrain modulates attention and learning.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Dopamine beta hydroxylase converts dopamine to **\_\_**

Correct answer: Norepinephrine

Explanation: The catecholamine biosynthetic pathway [Guyton ch.46]: **Tyrosine** → DOPA (by **tyrosine hydroxylase** — rate-limiting step) → **Dopamine** (by aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase, a.k.a. DOPA decarboxylase) → **Norepinephrine** (by **dopamine β-hydroxylase**) → **Epinephrine** (by **PNMT — phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase**, expressed only in adrenal medulla and a few brainstem cells). Hence dopamine β-hydroxylase converts dopamine into norepinephrine. The enzyme is unusual in that it is localised inside the secretory vesicles rather than the cytoplasm, so dopamine must be pumped into vesicles before being β-hydroxylated.

Hints:

1. Recall the catecholamine biosynthesis pathway from tyrosine to epinephrine.
2. The enzyme that adds a β-hydroxyl group to dopamine yields the next catecholamine in the pathway.
3. The product is the synaptic transmitter of postganglionic sympathetic neurones.

Distractors:

- "DOPA" — Reveals the misconception that the enzyme acts in the reverse direction. DOPA is upstream of dopamine.
- "Epinephrine" — Reveals the misconception that DBH performs N-methylation. Epinephrine is produced from NA by PNMT.
- "Tyrosine" — Reveals the misconception that DBH degrades dopamine back to tyrosine. The pathway is unidirectional and DBH adds OH, not degrades.

QUESTION 6

Stem: Which of these is not a dopaminergic pathway?

Correct answer: Corticostriatal pathway

Explanation: The four major dopaminergic pathways in the CNS [Guyton ch.46]: (1) **Nigrostriatal** — substantia nigra pars compacta → striatum (caudate/putamen); degenerates in Parkinson's disease. (2) **Mesolimbic** — VTA → nucleus accumbens, amygdala, hippocampus; reward/motivation. (3) **Mesocortical** — VTA → prefrontal cortex; executive function and cognition. (4) **Tuberoinfundibular** — arcuate nucleus → median eminence; tonically inhibits prolactin secretion. The **corticostriatal pathway**, by contrast, is a **glutamatergic** projection from cortex → striatum, providing the major excitatory drive to basal ganglia circuits — not dopaminergic. Hence (d) is the exception.

Hints:

1. Recall the four major dopaminergic pathways and their projections.
2. Corticostriatal projections are excitatory and use glutamate.
3. The exception describes cortex-to-striatum projection, which is glutamatergic.

Distractors:

- "Mesocortical pathway" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Nigrostriatal pathway" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Tuberoinfundibular pathway" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 7

Stem: Find the incorrect match.

Correct answer: Basal forebrain complex - GABA

Explanation: Brainstem and forebrain neuromodulatory nuclei [Guyton ch.46]: **Raphe nuclei** (midline brainstem) → serotonergic projections throughout CNS — (a) correct. **Locus coeruleus** (dorsal pons) → noradrenergic projections throughout CNS — (b) correct. **Tuberomammillary nucleus** (posterior hypothalamus) → histaminergic projections — (d) correct. The **basal forebrain complex** (medial septal nucleus, diagonal band of Broca, nucleus basalis of Meynert) is the major **cholinergic** source for the cortex and hippocampus, not GABAergic. (Some basal forebrain neurones do release GABA, but the _signature_ transmitter is ACh; loss of basal forebrain cholinergic neurones is a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease.) Hence (c) is the incorrect match.

Hints:

1. Recall the signature transmitter of each neuromodulatory nucleus.
2. The basal forebrain is famous for its role in Alzheimer's disease, which involves a specific transmitter.
3. The exception assigns GABA to a famously cholinergic complex.

Distractors:

- "Raphe nucleus - Serotonin" — Correctly matched.
- "Locus coeruleus - Norepinephrine" — Correctly matched.
- "Histamine - hypothalamus" — Correctly matched (tuberomammillary nucleus).

QUESTION 8

Stem: A patient with involuntary jerking and writhing movements is diagnosed with Huntington's disease. Loss of which of the following is responsible for hyperkinetic symptoms in this condition?

Correct answer: Intrastriatal GABA-ergic and cholinergic system

Explanation: **Huntington's disease** is an autosomal dominant trinucleotide-repeat disorder (CAG expansion in _HTT_) characterised by **chorea** (involuntary jerking) and **athetosis** (writhing) — hyperkinetic movements [Guyton ch.46]. The pathology is degeneration of **medium spiny neurones** in the striatum, particularly those projecting to the external globus pallidus (the "indirect pathway" GABAergic neurones) and the **intrastriatal cholinergic interneurones**. Loss of these inhibitory and modulatory neurones disinhibits the thalamus and motor cortex, releasing excess movement. Loss of the nigrostriatal dopaminergic system (option a) characterises Parkinson's disease (hypokinesia), the _opposite_ clinical picture. Hence (d) is correct.

Hints:

1. Recall the basal ganglia neurones lost in Huntington's vs Parkinson's.
2. Huntington's loses inhibitory striatal projections, producing hyperkinesia.
3. The losses include both GABAergic medium spiny neurones and cholinergic interneurones.

Distractors:

- "Nigrostriatal dopaminergic system" — Reveals the misconception that nigrostriatal loss produces hyperkinesia. It produces hypokinesia (Parkinson's).
- "Intrastriatal cholinergic pathway" — Partial truth (cholinergic is lost) but incomplete — GABAergic loss is also key.
- "Glutamate and cholinergic system" — Reveals the misconception that glutamate loss is involved. Glutamatergic input to the striatum is preserved.

QUESTION 9

Stem: An infant develops features of botulism following honey intake. This toxin blocks neuromuscular transmission by which of the following mechanisms?

Correct answer: Prevents release of Ach at presynaptic membrane

Explanation: **Botulinum toxin (BoNT)** from _Clostridium botulinum_ is a zinc-metalloprotease that **cleaves SNARE proteins** in the presynaptic terminal — specifically synaptobrevin (VAMP), syntaxin or SNAP-25 depending on the serotype [Guyton ch.46]. Without functional SNAREs, synaptic vesicles cannot dock and fuse with the presynaptic membrane, and **ACh release is blocked**. Clinically: descending flaccid paralysis (cranial → bulbar → respiratory), no fever, preserved sensation, dilated pupils. Infant botulism follows ingestion of _C. botulinum_ spores in honey (gut colonisation; mature gut flora outcompete the organism in older children/adults). Antitoxin and supportive ventilation are mainstays of treatment.

Hints:

1. Recall the molecular target of botulinum toxin.
2. The toxin acts presynaptically to block transmitter release.
3. The mechanism involves cleavage of vesicle-fusion proteins.

Distractors:

- "Opening of calcium channels at the presynaptic membrane" — Reveals confusion with α-latrotoxin (black widow), which opens Ca²⁺ channels and causes excessive ACh release.
- "Prevents the binding of Ach at the postsynaptic membrane" — Reveals confusion with curare (nicotinic AChR antagonist).
- "Prevents the action of Ach at the postsynaptic membrane" — Same misconception as (c).

QUESTION 10

Stem: A farmer presents with difficulty swallowing and jaw tightening. He gives a history of getting his finger jammed in the rustic gate a few days ago. He gives no history of previous tetanus immunization. After evaluation, he was diagnosed with tetanus. The release of which of the following neurotransmitters is affected by the toxin responsible for this condition?

Correct answer: GABA

Explanation: **Tetanus toxin (tetanospasmin)** from _Clostridium tetani_ — like botulinum — cleaves SNARE proteins (synaptobrevin), blocking transmitter release [Guyton ch.46]. The crucial difference is that tetanospasmin **selectively targets inhibitory interneurones in the spinal cord** (Renshaw cells and other glycinergic and **GABAergic** interneurones), via retrograde axonal transport from peripheral motor nerves into the spinal cord. Loss of inhibitory transmitter release from these interneurones disinhibits α-motor neurones, producing **spastic paralysis** with characteristic features: trismus (lockjaw), risus sardonicus, opisthotonus, and reflex spasms. The vignette's farmer with a tetanus-prone wound and no immunisation fits the classical picture. Although glycine is also affected, the question's listed options include GABA but not glycine, so (c) is the answer.

Hints:

1. Recall the molecular target of tetanus toxin and which neurones it preferentially affects.
2. Tetanus blocks inhibitory transmitter release in the spinal cord.
3. Of the listed options, the inhibitory transmitter is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Dopamine" — Reveals the misconception that tetanus affects dopamine. It affects spinal inhibitory transmitters.
- "Acetylcholine" — Reveals confusion with botulism, which blocks ACh release.
- "Adrenaline" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Tetanus toxin's *primary* target is glycine release at Renshaw cells and other glycinergic interneurones in the spinal cord; GABAergic transmission is also affected. The listed options include GABA but not glycine, so the question keys (c) GABA. A more rigorous question would include both inhibitory transmitters or specifically glycine.]

QUESTION 11

Stem: The neurotransmitter released by Renshaw cell interneuron is **\_\_**.

Correct answer: Glycine

Explanation: **Renshaw cells** are inhibitory interneurones in the ventral horn of the spinal cord that receive collateral input from α-motor neurones and send recurrent (feedback) inhibition back to those same neurones [Guyton ch.46]. Their transmitter is **glycine**, the dominant inhibitory transmitter of the spinal cord. Glycine binds glycine receptors (ligand-gated Cl⁻ channels), producing fast IPSPs that hyperpolarise the motor neurone and limit excessive firing. **Strychnine** (a glycine receptor antagonist) blocks Renshaw inhibition and produces hyperexcitable motor responses similar in some respects to tetanus.

Hints:

1. Recall the dominant inhibitory transmitter of the spinal cord.
2. Renshaw cells produce fast inhibitory postsynaptic potentials at glycine receptors.
3. Strychnine, a glycine antagonist, mimics tetanus by blocking Renshaw output.

Distractors:

- "Glutamate" — Reveals the misconception that Renshaw cells excite. They inhibit.
- "Norepinephrine" — Reveals the misconception that Renshaw inhibition is autonomic.
- "Acetyl choline" — Reveals the misconception that Renshaw uses ACh. Renshaw cells _receive_ cholinergic input from motor-neurone collaterals but _release_ glycine.

QUESTION 12

Stem: Which of the following neurotransmitters is increased during REM sleep?

Correct answer: Acetylcholine

Explanation: The neurochemistry of sleep stages [Guyton ch.46]: **NREM sleep** is associated with reduced activity of all major modulatory systems. **REM sleep** is characterised by **high cholinergic activity** (from pontine cholinergic nuclei, particularly laterodorsal and pedunculopontine tegmental nuclei) — driving cortical activation, rapid eye movements and muscle atonia. Conversely, **monoaminergic systems are silent during REM**: locus coeruleus (NA) and dorsal raphe (5-HT) neurones almost cease firing, and dopamine activity is reduced. This **reciprocal interaction** model (cholinergic ON, monoaminergic OFF) is the basis of REM/NREM cycling. Hence ACh is the listed transmitter that _increases_ during REM.

Hints:

1. Recall the neurochemistry of REM sleep.
2. One transmitter system is _active_ during REM; the others are silent.
3. The transmitter active during REM also drives the cortical desynchronisation seen in REM EEG.

Distractors:

- "Dopamine" — Reveals the misconception that DA increases during REM. DA activity is reduced.
- "Norepinephrine" — Reveals the misconception that NA increases during REM. NA neurones are nearly silent during REM.
- "Serotonin" — Same misconception as (c).

QUESTION 13

Stem: Which of the following is the wrong match?

Correct answer: Sweat glands - innervated by sympathetic adrenergic fibres

Explanation: Autonomic neurochemistry rules [Guyton ch.46]: (a) sympathetic preganglionic → ACh on nicotinic receptors at sympathetic ganglia ✓; (b) parasympathetic preganglionic → ACh on nicotinic receptors at parasympathetic ganglia ✓; (c) sympathetic postganglionic → NA on adrenergic receptors at most effector organs ✓. The classical exception to (c) is the **eccrine sweat glands**, which are innervated by **sympathetic postganglionic _cholinergic_** fibres (releasing ACh onto muscarinic M3 receptors), not adrenergic. This is why **anticholinergics** (atropine) inhibit sweating and **β-blockers** do not. Hence (d) is the wrong match.

Hints:

1. Recall the postganglionic transmitter at sweat glands — the famous exception in autonomic pharmacology.
2. Most sympathetic postganglionic fibres release NA; sweat glands are an exception.
3. The exception is sympathetic but cholinergic.

Distractors:

- "Sympathetic preganglionic fibres - release acetylcholine" — Correctly matched.
- "Parasympathetic preganglionic fibres - release acetylcholine" — Correctly matched.
- "Sympathetic post ganglionic fibres - release noradrenaline" — Correctly matched (with the noted exception).

QUESTION 14

Stem: Which part of the basal ganglia is the neurotransmitter glutamate secreted from?

Correct answer: Subthalamic nucleus

Explanation: Basal ganglia neurochemistry [Guyton ch.46]: most projection neurones of the basal ganglia are **GABAergic** (medium spiny neurones of the striatum to GPe and GPi/SNr, GPe → STN, GPi → thalamus). The major **glutamatergic** projections within and into the basal ganglia are: (1) **cortex → striatum (corticostriatal)** glutamatergic, providing the principal excitatory drive; and (2) **subthalamic nucleus (STN) → globus pallidus interna (GPi)** — the _only intrinsic_ glutamatergic projection within the basal ganglia. The STN is therefore the basal ganglia structure that _secretes glutamate_ in the indirect pathway, driving the GPi to inhibit the thalamus. **GPi, GPe and putamen** are GABAergic. Hence (c).

Hints:

1. Recall the major neurotransmitters of basal ganglia projections.
2. Most basal ganglia projections are GABAergic; identify the glutamatergic one.
3. The glutamatergic intrinsic nucleus is the deep brain stimulation target in Parkinson's disease.

Distractors:

- "Globus pallidus interna" — GABAergic.
- "Globus pallidus externa" — GABAergic.
- "Putamen" — GABAergic (medium spiny neurones).

QUESTION 15

Stem: All of the following are excitatory neurotransmitters except:

Correct answer: Serotonin

Explanation: This question hinges on the textbook framing — strict classification is challenging because most "modulatory" transmitters can be either excitatory or inhibitory depending on receptor subtype [Guyton ch.46]. **Glutamate** is the major excitatory transmitter (a). **Acetylcholine** is excitatory at nicotinic receptors and at muscarinic M1, M3, M5 (Gq → IP3/DAG); inhibitory at M2, M4 (Gi). **Norepinephrine** is excitatory at α₁ (Gq) and β₁ (Gs); inhibitory at α₂ (Gi). **Serotonin (5-HT)** has an even broader spectrum — most 5-HT receptors are GPCRs, and many are _inhibitory_ (5-HT1A → Gi → opens K⁺ channels — hyperpolarising; 5-HT1B/1D similar); only 5-HT3 (ionotropic) and 5-HT2 (Gq) are excitatory. The textbook classification at the MBBS level treats **serotonin as predominantly inhibitory / modulatory** — hence the keyed answer (c). Note that this is a simplification; serotonin's effects depend on the receptor subtype.

Hints:

1. Recall the dominant action of each transmitter at typical CNS receptors.
2. Three of the listed transmitters are predominantly excitatory; one is predominantly inhibitory at most receptor subtypes.
3. The exception's main receptors are inhibitory (Gi-coupled, opening K⁺ channels).

Distractors:

- "Acetylcholine" — At nicotinic and most muscarinic receptors, ACh is excitatory.
- "Glutamate" — The major excitatory transmitter.
- "Norepinephrine" — Excitatory at most postsynaptic receptors.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: All four listed transmitters can be excitatory or inhibitory depending on the receptor subtype. The keyed answer (c) reflects the common textbook simplification that serotonin is predominantly inhibitory. A rigorous reader could mount cases for several of the options as exceptions.]

QUESTION 16

Stem: Which of the following is not true about serotonin receptor?

Correct answer: 5-HT2C receptor agonists increase appetite

Explanation: Serotonin receptor families [Guyton ch.46]: (a) **5-HT2A** receptors on platelets mediate **platelet aggregation** in response to released serotonin — true. (c) **5-HT3** is the only ionotropic serotonin receptor; activation in the area postrema triggers **vomiting** (5-HT3 antagonists ondansetron/granisetron are anti-emetics) — true. (d) **5-HT4** receptors on enteric neurones facilitate **secretion and peristalsis** in the gut (5-HT4 agonists such as prucalopride are prokinetic agents) — true. (b) is **false**: **5-HT2C agonists _decrease_ appetite**, not increase. The 5-HT2C agonist **lorcaserin** is a weight-loss drug that suppresses appetite by activating hypothalamic POMC neurones. Hence (b) reverses the direction of effect.

Hints:

1. Recall the function of each major serotonin receptor subtype.
2. Lorcaserin (a 5-HT2C agonist) is a weight-loss drug — what does that imply for appetite direction?
3. The exception reverses the direction of the appetite effect.

Distractors:

- "5HT2A receptors mediate platelet aggregation" — Correctly true.
- "5HT3 receptors are related to vomiting" — Correctly true.
- "5HT4 receptors facilitate secretion and peristalsis" — Correctly true.

QUESTION 17

Stem: Serotonin is present in highest concentration in **\_\_\_\_**

Correct answer: Platelets

Explanation: Although serotonin is synthesised in **enterochromaffin cells of the gut** (which contain ~90% of body serotonin) and in serotonergic neurones of the **raphe nuclei**, **platelets** are the major _carrier_ of circulating serotonin — they express the high-affinity SERT (serotonin transporter) on their surface and actively take up serotonin from plasma, storing it in dense granules at high concentrations [Guyton ch.46]. Platelets do not synthesise serotonin themselves. Released serotonin during platelet activation contributes to vasoconstriction and further platelet aggregation (5-HT2A). **Mast cells** and **basophils** contain histamine, not predominantly serotonin (rodent mast cells contain serotonin but human mast cells contain histamine). The **posterior hypothalamus** has histaminergic neurones (tuberomammillary nucleus), not the highest concentration of serotonin.

Hints:

1. Distinguish sites of serotonin synthesis from sites of serotonin storage.
2. Although enterochromaffin cells make most serotonin, blood-borne serotonin is concentrated in one specific cell type.
3. The blood cell with high SERT activity and dense granules carries the bulk of circulating serotonin.

Distractors:

- "Basophil" — Reveals the misconception that basophils contain high serotonin. Basophils contain histamine (and heparin in human cells).
- "Mast cell" — In humans, mast cells contain histamine; rodent mast cells contain serotonin (a common confusion).
- "Posterior hypothalamus" — Reveals the misconception that the hypothalamus is the highest-serotonin region. It has histaminergic neurones, not serotonergic.

QUESTION 18

Stem: Which statement regarding the NMDA receptor is incorrect?

Correct answer: It is a chloride ion channel

Explanation: The **NMDA (N-methyl-D-aspartate) receptor** is an **ionotropic glutamate receptor** — a non-selective **cation channel** permeable to **Na⁺, K⁺ and (importantly) Ca²⁺** [Guyton ch.46]. Ca²⁺ entry through the NMDAR triggers downstream signalling cascades essential for synaptic plasticity (long-term potentiation, long-term depression). The receptor has unusual properties: (a) **glutamate** is its endogenous agonist — true; (b) **glycine** (or D-serine) is required as a co-agonist at a separate binding site — true; (d) **NMDARs are abundant in hippocampal neurones**, where they mediate LTP underlying learning and memory — true. (c) is **false** — NMDA is a **cation channel**, not a chloride channel. Chloride channels are characteristic of inhibitory ionotropic receptors (GABAᴬ, glycine).

Hints:

1. Recall the ions that flow through the NMDA receptor channel.
2. NMDARs are _excitatory_ — their channel must conduct depolarising cations.
3. The exception assigns an inhibitory-type ion conductance to an excitatory receptor.

Distractors:

- "Glutamate is its endogenous agonist" — Correctly true.
- "Glycine is essential for this receptor's response to its endogenous agonist" — Correctly true.
- "It is present in hippocampal neurons" — Correctly true.
