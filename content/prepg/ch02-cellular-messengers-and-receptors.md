QUESTION 1

Stem: Which of the following is an example of a polypeptide hormone?

Correct answer: Parathormone

Explanation: Hormones fall into three principal chemical classes: (1) **proteins and polypeptides** (the largest group, including pituitary hormones, insulin, glucagon, calcitonin and parathyroid hormone); (2) **steroids** synthesised from cholesterol (cortisol, aldosterone, sex steroids); and (3) **amine** derivatives of tyrosine — the catecholamines (adrenaline, noradrenaline, dopamine) and the iodinated thyronines (thyroxine, triiodothyronine) [Guyton ch.75] [Boron ch.3]. **Parathormone** (parathyroid hormone, PTH) is an 84-amino-acid single-chain polypeptide secreted by chief cells of the parathyroid glands [Guyton ch.75]. **Adrenaline** is a catecholamine, **cortisol** a glucocorticoid steroid, and **thyroxine** an iodinated tyrosine amine — none is a polypeptide.

Hints:

1. Recall the three principal chemical classes of hormones and which biosynthetic precursors define each.
2. Two of the listed hormones derive from tyrosine; one from cholesterol; one is a chain of amino acids.
3. The polypeptide is secreted by chief cells of the parathyroid gland.

Distractors:

- "Adrenaline" — Reveals the misconception that any small water-soluble hormone is a peptide. Adrenaline is a catecholamine derived from tyrosine.
- "Cortisol" — Reveals the misconception that all hormones are peptides. Cortisol is a steroid synthesised from cholesterol in the adrenal cortex.
- "Thyroxine" — Reveals the misconception that thyroid hormone is a polypeptide because it is stored in thyroglobulin. The released hormone is an iodinated amine, not a peptide [Guyton ch.75].

QUESTION 2

Stem: Which hormone crosses the plasma membrane for its action?

Correct answer: Thyroxine

Explanation: Lipid solubility determines whether a hormone signals via cell-surface receptors or by crossing the plasma membrane to reach intracellular receptors. **Steroid hormones and thyroid hormones are highly lipid-soluble** and diffuse readily through the membrane to bind nuclear or cytoplasmic receptors that act as ligand-activated transcription factors [Guyton ch.75] [Boron ch.3] [Berne & Levy ch.3]. Despite being amine-derived rather than steroid, **thyroxine (T₄)** is sufficiently lipophilic — and uses specialised monocarboxylate transporters (MCT8, MCT10) — to enter target cells and bind the nuclear thyroid hormone receptor [Guyton ch.75]. **Epinephrine, glucagon and insulin** are water-soluble and act through plasma-membrane receptors: epinephrine via adrenergic GPCRs, glucagon via the glucagon GPCR, and insulin via a transmembrane receptor tyrosine kinase [Boron ch.3].

Hints:

1. Hormones split into two pharmacological camps based on whether they cross the lipid bilayer.
2. The lipid-soluble class includes steroids and one group of amine hormones; the water-soluble class uses cell-surface receptors.
3. The lipid-soluble amine here acts via a nuclear receptor and an intracellular transporter family.

Distractors:

- "Epinephrine" — Reveals the misconception that small molecular size implies membrane permeability. Catecholamines are charged at physiological pH and act through cell-surface adrenergic GPCRs.
- "Glucagon" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Insulin" — Reveals the misconception that all hormones with intracellular effects must cross the membrane. Insulin acts at a surface RTK; downstream cascades produce its intracellular actions.

QUESTION 3

Stem: Which of the following hormone is not stored before release?

Correct answer: Cortisol

Explanation: Storage of hormones depends on chemical class. **Polypeptide hormones** (insulin) are packaged into secretory granules and released by exocytosis [Guyton ch.75]. **Thyroid hormones** are uniquely stored extracellularly within thyroglobulin in colloid-filled follicles, with reserves sufficient for several weeks [Guyton ch.75]. **Renin** is stored as inactive prorenin in granules of juxtaglomerular cells and released on stimulation. **Steroid hormones — including cortisol — are not stored**: because they are lipid-soluble they would diffuse out of any storage compartment, so they are synthesised on demand from cholesterol esters in cytoplasmic vacuoles and diffuse out of the cell as soon as they are produced [Guyton ch.75]. The rate-limiting step is conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone by the cholesterol side-chain cleavage enzyme.

Hints:

1. Storage is feasible only when the hormone can be sequestered in a compartment from which it cannot leak.
2. Three classes of hormones can be packaged in vesicles or follicles; one class diffuses out of the cell as soon as it is made.
3. The unstored hormone is synthesised from cholesterol on demand.

Distractors:

- "Insulin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Thyroxine" — Reveals the misconception that thyroid hormone behaves like other lipid-soluble hormones. It is exceptional: stored extracellularly in thyroglobulin within colloid [Guyton ch.75].
- "Renin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 4

Stem: Downregulation of receptors occurs due to all except**\_**

Correct answer: Decreased level of stimulating hormone

Explanation: Downregulation describes a _reduction_ in target-cell receptor number or responsiveness, typically as an adaptive response to **prolonged exposure to high concentrations of the agonist** [Guyton ch.75] [Boron ch.3]. Mechanisms include receptor phosphorylation by GPCR kinases (causing **desensitisation** and uncoupling from G-protein), β-arrestin–mediated **internalisation** by clathrin-coated pits, and lysosomal degradation or sequestration of the receptors after endocytosis [Boron ch.3]. Receptor **inactivation** by covalent modification or conformational change also reduces the active receptor pool. By contrast, **falling agonist concentration** drives the _opposite_ adaptation — _upregulation_, in which receptor synthesis is increased to maintain target-cell sensitivity (denervation supersensitivity is a classical example).

Hints:

1. Downregulation is an adaptation to _too much_ signalling, not too little.
2. List the cellular mechanisms by which a cell reduces functional receptor number when an agonist is persistently elevated.
3. The exception is the stimulus that triggers the _opposite_ adaptive change.

Distractors:

- "Inactivation of receptor" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Internalisation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Desensitisation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Receptors on cell membrane that activate ion channel after binding with agonists are**\_**

Correct answer: Nicotinic cholinergic

Explanation: Membrane receptors are grouped by mechanism into ligand-gated ion channels (ionotropic), G-protein-coupled receptors (metabotropic), catalytic/enzyme-linked receptors and intracellular receptors [Boron ch.3]. **Ligand-gated ion channels** are themselves the channel — agonist binding produces an immediate conformational change that opens the pore [Boron ch.3]. The **nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (nAChR)** is the prototype: a pentameric cation channel of stoichiometry 2α:β:γ:δ at the neuromuscular junction, opening in milliseconds on ACh binding to allow Na⁺ and K⁺ flux [Boron ch.3]. **Muscarinic** ACh receptors, **GABAᴮ** receptors and **opioid μ** receptors are all GPCRs that act via second messengers and modulate ion channels indirectly via G-protein subunits — they are _not_ ligand-gated channels themselves [Boron ch.3]. (Note: GABAᴬ — distinct from GABAᴮ — _is_ a ligand-gated Cl⁻ channel.)

Hints:

1. Distinguish ionotropic (channel-is-receptor) from metabotropic (receptor-then-second-messenger) signalling.
2. Three of the listed receptors couple through G-proteins; one is itself the ion channel.
3. The ionotropic option is the receptor of the neuromuscular junction.

Distractors:

- "Muscarinic cholinergic" — Reveals the misconception that all ACh receptors are ionotropic. Muscarinic AChRs are seven-transmembrane GPCRs (M1, M3, M5 → Gq; M2, M4 → Gi).
- "Opioid μ receptors" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming. (μ opioid receptors are Gi-coupled GPCRs.)
- "GABAB receptors" — Reveals the misconception that all GABA receptors are ionotropic. **GABAᴬ** is a ligand-gated Cl⁻ channel; **GABAᴮ** is a Gi-coupled GPCR that activates inwardly rectifying K⁺ channels indirectly via βγ subunits [Boron ch.3].

QUESTION 6

Stem: Adrenaline, noradrenaline and dopamine act through **\_**

Correct answer: 7 pass receptor

Explanation: Catecholamines — **adrenaline, noradrenaline and dopamine** — bind to adrenergic and dopaminergic receptors that all belong to the **G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) superfamily**, the largest receptor superfamily in the genome [Boron ch.3]. The defining structural feature of all GPCRs is **seven α-helical transmembrane domains** (hence "7-pass" or "heptahelical" receptors), with an extracellular N-terminus that contributes to ligand binding and a cytoplasmic C-terminus that couples to a heterotrimeric G-protein [Boron ch.3]. Adrenergic α₁ (Gq), α₂ (Gi), β₁/β₂/β₃ (Gs) and dopaminergic D₁-like (Gs) and D₂-like (Gi) all share this 7-TM architecture. **1-pass** (single transmembrane) receptors include RTKs and many cytokine receptors; **4-pass** describes ligand-gated ion channels (per subunit). No major receptor family is canonically described as "2-pass."

Hints:

1. Catecholamines act through the largest receptor superfamily — recall its membrane topology.
2. The signature architecture of metabotropic catecholamine receptors involves a fixed number of transmembrane helices.
3. Count the helices in a heptahelical receptor.

Distractors:

- "1 pass receptor" — Reveals the misconception that all hormone receptors are single-pass. Single-pass topology characterises insulin receptor, GH receptor and similar single-TM receptors; not catecholamine receptors.
- "2 pass receptor" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "4 pass receptor" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 7

Stem: Which of the following statements is true about the G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR)?

Correct answer: G proteins act as inhibitory or excitatory because of differences in ° subunit.

Explanation: Heterotrimeric G-proteins consist of α, β and γ subunits. The **α subunit** is the GTPase that determines the functional class of the G-protein and the downstream effector pathway: **Gαs** stimulates adenylyl cyclase (raising cAMP); **Gαi** inhibits adenylyl cyclase; **Gαq** activates phospholipase Cβ (raising IP₃ and DAG); **Gαt** (transducin) activates retinal cGMP phosphodiesterase [Boron ch.3]. Hence the _excitatory or inhibitory_ nature of a G-protein arises from differences in the α subunit. In the _resting state_, the α subunit is bound to GDP and stably associated with βγ; agonist-bound receptor catalyses GDP-for-GTP exchange on α, dissociating α-GTP from βγ to engage effectors [Boron ch.3]. G-proteins act on cell-surface effectors, not on the nuclear surface. Option (c) is therefore correct.

Hints:

1. Map each Gα isoform (s, i, q, t) to its preferred effector and decide what the directionality of those effectors implies.
2. The same receptor architecture can produce opposite cellular effects — what dictates the sign?
3. Identify which subunit determines the choice between activating and inhibiting adenylyl cyclase.

Distractors:

- "G proteins bind to hormones on the surface of the nucleus" — Reveals the misconception that G-proteins act on the nuclear envelope. Heterotrimeric G-proteins act at the cytoplasmic face of the plasma membrane.
- "All the three subunits °, β, and ■ should bind to each other for G proteins to act" — Reveals the misconception that the heterotrimer is the active form. The trimer is the _inactive_ state; activation requires α-GTP to dissociate from βγ [Boron ch.3].
- "G protein is bound to GTP in resting state" — Reveals the misconception that GTP-binding marks the resting state. The resting state is GDP-bound; GTP-binding is the activated state, terminated by intrinsic GTPase hydrolysis to GDP [Boron ch.3].

QUESTION 8

Stem: Action of ° subunit of G protein is**\_**

Correct answer: Breakdown of GTP to GDP

Explanation: The α subunit of a heterotrimeric G-protein is itself a **GTPase**: after activation by agonist-bound receptor, α dissociates as α-GTP and engages an effector enzyme (adenylyl cyclase, PLCβ, etc.); its intrinsic GTPase activity then hydrolyses the bound GTP to GDP, returning α to its inactive form and allowing it to re-associate with βγ [Boron ch.3]. This timed self-inactivation is the molecular basis of signal termination at the G-protein level. **GDP→GTP exchange** is _not_ catalysed by α itself — it is the role of the activated receptor (a guanine-nucleotide exchange factor, GEF) [Boron ch.3]. **Receptor internalisation** is mediated by GPCR kinases and β-arrestin, not by α. **Agonist binding** is performed by the receptor's transmembrane core, not by the G-protein.

Hints:

1. The α subunit acts as a built-in molecular timer for the active state of the G-protein.
2. Identify the enzymatic activity of the α subunit and which direction it operates.
3. The α subunit terminates its own activation by hydrolysing the nucleotide it is bound to.

Distractors:

- "Conversion of GDP to GTP" — Reveals the misconception that the α subunit catalyses its own activation. Nucleotide exchange is catalysed by the agonist-occupied receptor.
- "Internalization of receptors" — Reveals the misconception that the G-protein α subunit drives receptor trafficking. β-arrestin and GRKs perform that role.
- "Binding of agonist" — Reveals the misconception that G-proteins bind extracellular ligands. Agonists bind the receptor; G-proteins bind the receptor's cytoplasmic face.

QUESTION 9

Stem: PDGF acts through which of the following signaling pathways?

Correct answer: Tyrosine kinase

Explanation: **Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF)** is one of a family of growth-factor peptides — alongside epidermal growth factor (EGF), nerve growth factor (NGF), fibroblast growth factor (FGF), insulin and IGF-1 — that act through **receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs)** [Boron ch.3]. RTKs are single-pass transmembrane proteins that **dimerise on ligand binding**, undergo trans-autophosphorylation on cytoplasmic tyrosine residues, and recruit SH2-domain–containing adaptor and effector proteins (Grb2, PI3K, PLCγ) to activate downstream cascades — most importantly the Ras/Raf/MEK/ERK MAPK pathway driving proliferation, and PI3K/Akt promoting survival [Boron ch.3]. PDGF therefore signals via tyrosine phosphorylation, not via cAMP, cGMP or IP₃/DAG (although PLCγ activation downstream of PDGF can produce IP₃ as a _secondary_ pathway).

Hints:

1. Growth factors and proliferative peptide ligands cluster into one specific receptor superfamily.
2. RTK ligands typically share dimerisation-induced kinase activation as their initiating step.
3. Identify the catalytic activity built into the cytoplasmic domain of the PDGF receptor.

Distractors:

- "cAMP" — Reveals the misconception that all hormone signalling defaults to cAMP. cAMP is generated downstream of Gαs-coupled GPCRs, not RTKs.
- "IP3-DAG" — Reveals the misconception that any membrane-bound receptor uses PLC. IP₃/DAG generation is the canonical Gαq pathway; PLCγ is recruited by some RTKs as a _secondary_ arm but is not the defining mechanism for PDGF signalling [Boron ch.3].
- "cGMP" — Reveals the misconception that growth factors use cGMP. cGMP is generated by guanylyl cyclase activated by ANP (membrane GC) or NO (soluble GC).

QUESTION 10

Stem: Which hormone acts on JAK-STAT kinase receptor?

Correct answer: GH

Explanation: The **JAK-STAT** pathway is associated with **tyrosine kinase–associated receptors** — single-pass cytokine-receptor-superfamily proteins that _lack intrinsic kinase activity_ and instead recruit cytoplasmic Janus kinases (JAK1, JAK2, JAK3, TYK2) on ligand binding [Boron ch.3]. JAKs trans-phosphorylate the receptor and the bound STAT (Signal Transducer and Activator of Transcription) proteins; phosphorylated STATs dimerise, translocate to the nucleus, and drive transcription. Ligands of this family include **growth hormone (GH)**, prolactin, leptin, erythropoietin, thrombopoietin, the interferons (IFN-α, β, γ), and the interleukins IL-3, IL-5, IL-6 and GM-CSF [Boron ch.3]. **TSH** and **FSH** are GPCR ligands (Gαs → cAMP). **Thyroxine** acts on intracellular nuclear thyroid hormone receptors. Of the listed options, only GH uses the JAK-STAT pathway.

Hints:

1. JAK-STAT is the canonical cytokine-receptor signalling mechanism — recall the families of ligands that use it.
2. Two of the listed hormones are anterior-pituitary glycoprotein GPCR ligands; one is a polypeptide that uses cytokine-style signalling.
3. The hormone most strongly associated with linear growth in children uses JAK2 and STAT5.

Distractors:

- "TSH" — Reveals the misconception that all anterior-pituitary hormones share a signalling pathway. TSH binds a Gαs-coupled GPCR on thyroid follicular cells [Guyton ch.75].
- "Thyroxine" — Reveals the misconception that thyroid hormones use surface receptors. Thyroxine is lipid-soluble and binds intracellular nuclear receptors.
- "FSH" — Reveals the same misconception as (a). FSH acts via a Gαs-coupled GPCR on granulosa and Sertoli cells.

QUESTION 11

Stem: All of the following act through cell-surface receptors except\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Cortisol

Explanation: Whether a hormone uses a surface or intracellular receptor follows directly from its lipid solubility. **Insulin, FSH and TSH** are water-soluble peptides that cannot cross the lipid bilayer and signal via plasma-membrane receptors: insulin via a transmembrane RTK; FSH and TSH via Gαs-coupled GPCRs that raise cAMP in their target cells [Boron ch.3] [Guyton ch.75]. **Cortisol** is a lipid-soluble glucocorticoid steroid that diffuses freely across the plasma membrane and binds the **cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptor (GR)**, a member of the nuclear-receptor superfamily; the ligand–receptor complex translocates to the nucleus, dimerises, binds glucocorticoid-response elements (GREs) in target gene promoters, and modulates transcription [Guyton ch.75] [Boron ch.3]. Because cortisol's receptor is intracellular, not on the surface, it is the exception.

Hints:

1. The lipid solubility of a hormone predicts where its receptor lives.
2. Three of the listed hormones must use cell-surface receptors because they cannot cross the bilayer; one diffuses straight through it.
3. The exception is a steroid hormone synthesised from cholesterol.

Distractors:

- "Insulin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "FSH" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "TSH" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 12

Stem: Steroid hormone receptors have an attachment site for all except\_\_\_\_

Correct answer: Transcription repressors

Explanation: Steroid hormone receptors are modular ligand-activated transcription factors, each containing a **ligand-binding domain (LBD)** that binds the steroid hormone, a **DNA-binding domain (DBD)** with two zinc fingers that recognise the **hormone-response element (HRE)** in target gene promoters, and an **activation function (AF) domain** that recruits coactivators (transcription activators) such as steroid receptor coactivator (SRC) family proteins, p300/CBP and components of the Mediator complex [Boron ch.3] [Guyton ch.75]. The textbook account of the steroid receptor emphasises these three docking surfaces (hormone, HRE, coactivator). It does _not_ describe a dedicated transcription-_repressor_ attachment site as a defining feature of the receptor itself.

Hints:

1. Recall the modular domain structure of a steroid receptor and what each domain binds.
2. Three of the listed partners are required for ligand-driven gene activation; one is conceptually opposed to that role.
3. The exception is a regulatory partner that would _suppress_ transcription rather than enhance it.

Distractors:

- "Steroid hormone" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Hormone-responsive element" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Transcription activators" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Some nuclear receptors *do* recruit corepressors in their unliganded state — most notably the thyroid hormone receptor and retinoic acid receptor recruit NCoR/SMRT corepressor complexes when not bound to their ligand. The textbook treatment for *steroid* receptors typically emphasises coactivator recruitment, but a strict molecular reading would acknowledge that some steroid receptors (e.g. unliganded glucocorticoid receptor in certain contexts) can interact with corepressor proteins. The question relies on the simplified textbook framing of steroid-receptor structure.]

QUESTION 13

Stem: True statement about second messengers is **\_**

Correct answer: Mediate the intracellular responses to many different hormones and neurotransmitters

Explanation: A **second messenger** is an intracellular molecule whose concentration changes in response to first-messenger (hormone, neurotransmitter, growth factor) binding at a surface receptor, and which transduces and amplifies the signal to downstream effectors inside the cell [Boron ch.3] [Guyton ch.75]. Canonical examples include cAMP, cGMP, IP₃, DAG, Ca²⁺ and the eicosanoid lipids. A few key features capture their biology: they are _intracellular_ (not extracellular), they _converge_ signals from many different first messengers onto common downstream pathways (e.g. cAMP is generated by ACTH, glucagon, vasopressin V₂, β-adrenergic agonists), and their concentration is tightly regulated by opposing synthesis and degradation enzymes (e.g. adenylyl cyclase vs phosphodiesterase) [Boron ch.3]. Option (b) is the only statement consistent with this definition.

Hints:

1. Recall the operational definition of a second messenger and what distinguishes it from a hormone.
2. Where in the cell does a second messenger act, and how many different upstream stimuli can converge on it?
3. The defining feature is intracellular relay of signals originating from diverse extracellular ligands.

Distractors:

- "Are substances that interact with first messengers outside cells" — Reveals the misconception that second messengers are extracellular partners of hormones. They are strictly intracellular.
- "Are hormones secreted by cells in response to stimulation by another hormone" — Reveals the misconception that second messengers are secondary _hormones_ (such as cortisol secreted in response to ACTH). Tropic-target hormone cascades exist but are unrelated to the second-messenger concept.
- "Are substances that bind to first messengers in the cell membrane" — Reveals the misconception that second messengers are receptors. Receptors bind first messengers; second messengers are downstream products of receptor activation.

QUESTION 14

Stem: Which one of the following acts as a second messenger?

Correct answer: Ca2+

Explanation: **Calcium ion (Ca²⁺)** is the principal _ionic_ second messenger in the cell [Boron ch.3]. Resting cytosolic [Ca²⁺] is held very low (≈100 nmol/L) against extracellular concentrations of ≈1.2 mmol/L and against the much higher concentrations sequestered in the endoplasmic/sarcoplasmic reticulum [Boron ch.3]. On stimulation, Ca²⁺ enters the cytosol either through plasma-membrane channels (voltage-gated, ROC, store-operated) or via release from the ER through IP₃ receptors (downstream of Gαq/PLCβ) and ryanodine receptors (in muscle excitation-contraction coupling) [Boron ch.3]. Cytosolic Ca²⁺ binds calmodulin, troponin C, synaptotagmin and a wide range of effector proteins to trigger contraction, secretion, gene transcription and apoptosis. **Mg²⁺, Cl⁻ and PO₄³⁻** do not function as canonical second messengers — their cytosolic concentrations are not transiently and sharply modulated as a signalling step.

Hints:

1. Among inorganic ions in the cell, only one undergoes large, rapid, regulated transients in the cytosol that drive downstream effectors.
2. The principal ionic second messenger is sequestered in the ER/SR and released through IP₃ and ryanodine receptors.
3. The ion in question is essential for muscle contraction, synaptic transmission and many secretion events.

Distractors:

- "Mg2+" — Reveals the misconception that any divalent cation is a second messenger. Mg²⁺ is a cofactor (e.g. for ATPases) but its cytosolic concentration is not used as a signalling transient.
- "Cl-" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "PO43-" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 15

Stem: A primigravida with 38 completed weeks of gestation has gone into labor. Oxytocin was started to augment labor. The second messenger system through which oxytocin acts is **\_**

Correct answer: Phospholipase c

Explanation: **Oxytocin** binds the oxytocin receptor (OXTR), a Gαq-coupled GPCR expressed at high density in late-pregnancy myometrium [Boron ch.3]. Activation of Gαq stimulates **phospholipase Cβ (PLCβ)**, which hydrolyses membrane PIP₂ to **IP₃ and DAG**: IP₃ binds its receptor on the sarcoplasmic reticulum and releases stored Ca²⁺ into the cytosol, raising [Ca²⁺]ᵢ; the rise in [Ca²⁺]ᵢ activates myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK) via calmodulin, driving uterine smooth-muscle contraction [Boron ch.3] [Guyton ch.75]. Oxytocin therefore acts via the PLC/IP₃/DAG arm, not via cAMP or cGMP. (DAG additionally activates protein kinase C, contributing to sustained contraction.) The same Gαq/PLC pathway is shared by vasopressin V₁ receptors, angiotensin II AT₁, α₁-adrenergic and several other vasoconstrictor signals.

Hints:

1. Oxytocin receptors couple to one of the four major Gα families — identify which one drives smooth-muscle contraction.
2. Uterine contraction requires a rise in cytosolic Ca²⁺ from intracellular stores; recall the second-messenger arm that releases SR/ER calcium.
3. The pathway begins with hydrolysis of a membrane phospholipid to two second messengers (one soluble, one membrane-bound).

Distractors:

- "cAMP" — Reveals the misconception that all hormones use cAMP. cAMP-elevating agents (β₂-adrenergic agonists, magnesium sulphate via different mechanism) actually _relax_ uterine smooth muscle and are used as tocolytics — the opposite of oxytocin's effect.
- "cGMP" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Tyrosine kinase" — Reveals the misconception that all peptide hormones use RTKs. Oxytocin is a peptide but its receptor is a GPCR, not an RTK.

QUESTION 16

Stem: cAMP is the exclusive second messenger for all the following except **\_\_\_\_**

Correct answer: Vasopressin

Explanation: **ACTH (corticotropin), glucagon and dopamine** signal _exclusively_ through cAMP-dependent pathways at their canonical receptors: ACTH binds the melanocortin-2 receptor (MC2R, Gαs); glucagon binds the glucagon receptor (Gαs); dopamine D₁-like receptors are Gαs-coupled (with D₂-like receptors being Gαi-coupled — both still using cAMP, just bidirectionally) [Boron ch.3] [Guyton ch.75]. **Vasopressin (ADH)**, by contrast, is the exception in this list because it has _two_ major receptor subtypes that use _different_ second messengers: **V₂ receptors** in the renal collecting duct couple to Gαs → cAMP (driving aquaporin-2 insertion), while **V₁ receptors** on vascular smooth muscle and hepatocytes couple to Gαq → IP₃/DAG (driving vasoconstriction and glycogenolysis) [Boron ch.3] [Guyton ch.75]. Vasopressin is therefore not an _exclusive_ cAMP signal.

Hints:

1. cAMP signalling is the canonical Gαs pathway — list the listed hormones and recall whether each is purely cAMP-driven.
2. One of the listed hormones has two receptor subtypes coupling to different G-proteins.
3. The exception is a posterior-pituitary peptide hormone with both Gαs and Gαq receptor subtypes.

Distractors:

- "Corticotropin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Dopamine" — Reveals a subtler misconception: students sometimes assume a single hormone implies a single G-protein. Dopamine has D₁-like (Gαs) and D₂-like (Gαi) receptor subtypes, but both still use cAMP (in opposing directions); the question's wording "exclusive second messenger" is generally accepted for dopamine.
- "Glucagon" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 17

Stem: cGMP is the second messenger of

Correct answer: NO (nitric oxide)

Explanation: **Cyclic GMP (cGMP)** is generated from GTP by guanylyl cyclase. There are two isoforms: a _membrane-bound_ receptor guanylyl cyclase (GC-A, GC-B, GC-C — activated by atrial natriuretic peptide, brain natriuretic peptide, guanylin and uroguanylin); and a _soluble cytosolic_ guanylyl cyclase (sGC) — directly activated by **nitric oxide (NO)** [Boron ch.3]. NO, synthesised from L-arginine by nitric oxide synthase (NOS), is a freely diffusible gas that crosses cell membranes and binds the haem group of sGC, raising cytosolic cGMP. cGMP activates protein kinase G (PKG), which phosphorylates targets including myosin light-chain phosphatase, leading to vascular and other smooth-muscle relaxation. **Somatostatin** acts via Gαi (inhibits cAMP). **Angiotensin II** acts via the Gαq-coupled AT₁ receptor (PLC/IP₃/DAG). **ADH** uses Gαs/cAMP (V₂) or Gαq/IP₃ (V₁) but not cGMP.

Hints:

1. cGMP is the second messenger of two distinct pathways — natriuretic peptides through membrane GC, and another endogenous mediator through soluble GC.
2. The listed candidate that activates _soluble_ guanylyl cyclase is a freely diffusible gas synthesised from L-arginine.
3. The agent in question mediates penile erection and endothelium-dependent vasodilation.

Distractors:

- "Somatostatin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Angiotensin II" — Reveals the misconception that all vasoactive peptides use the same second messenger. Angiotensin II is a vasoconstrictor that uses Gαq/PLC; NO is a vasodilator that uses cGMP.
- "ADH" — Reveals the misconception that any hormone affecting fluid balance uses cGMP. ADH uses cAMP (V₂) or IP₃/DAG (V₁), not cGMP.

QUESTION 18

Stem: Which of the following is not a second messenger?

Correct answer: Guanylyl cyclase

Explanation: A second messenger is a _small intracellular signalling molecule_ whose concentration is rapidly raised or lowered downstream of receptor activation. **cAMP, IP₃ and DAG** are textbook second messengers: cAMP is generated by adenylyl cyclase from ATP; **IP₃ and DAG** are produced by PLC-mediated cleavage of PIP₂ [Boron ch.3]. **Guanylyl cyclase**, by contrast, is the **enzyme** that synthesises cGMP from GTP — it is _not itself_ a messenger; the messenger it generates is cGMP [Boron ch.3]. Confusing the enzyme with its product is a classical source of error in this category.

QUESTION 19

Stem: Activation of protein kinase C causes

Correct answer: Phosphorylation of proteins

Explanation: **Protein kinase C (PKC)** is a family of serine/threonine protein kinases activated downstream of Gαq → PLCβ → DAG and Ca²⁺ [Boron ch.3]. PKC does not itself raise cAMP, alter adenylyl cyclase activity directly, or specifically increase ion-channel conductance (although phosphorylated channels may behave differently as a downstream consequence).

QUESTION 20

Stem: A 7-year-old girl presents with skin lesions as shown in the image. The parents also complain of early breast development, growth acceleration, and vaginal bleeding. On genetic studies, it was found that there is a gain of function mutation of G Protein. What is the most probable diagnosis?

Correct answer: McCune-Albright syndrome

Explanation: **McCune-Albright syndrome (MAS)** is caused by a _somatic, post-zygotic, activating (gain-of-function) mutation in GNAS1_, encoding the α subunit of the stimulatory G-protein (Gαs) [Boron ch.3]. The mutation locks Gαs in its GTP-bound state, leading to constitutive cAMP elevation in tissues that carry the mutant allele in mosaic distribution. The clinical triad is: (1) **polyostotic fibrous dysplasia** of bone; (2) **café-au-lait macules** with characteristic irregular ("coast of Maine") borders; and (3) **autonomous endocrine hyperfunction**, most commonly **gonadotropin-independent precocious puberty** (early breast development, growth acceleration and vaginal bleeding) but also thyrotoxicosis, growth hormone excess and Cushing syndrome [Boron ch.3]. The mosaic nature reflects the post-zygotic timing of the mutation — only some cells carry the mutant Gαs. Other listed conditions are unrelated to G-protein gain-of-function.

Hints:

1. Recall the named syndromes caused by gain-of-function and loss-of-function G-protein mutations.
2. The clinical triad described — bone lesions, skin lesions and precocious puberty — defines a single eponymous syndrome.
3. The constitutive activation is in the α subunit of the stimulatory G-protein, raising cAMP in a mosaic distribution.

Distractors:

- "Color blindness" — Reveals the misconception that colour-vision defects are G-protein disorders. They are X-linked photopigment (opsin) gene mutations.
- "Hirschsprung disease" — Reveals the misconception that any inherited multisystem disorder is a candidate. Hirschsprung is congenital absence of enteric ganglia from RET or EDN3/EDNRB mutations.
