QUESTION 1

Stem: When dynamic γ-motor neurons are activated at the same time as α-motor neurons to muscle, **\_\_\_\_**

Correct answer: The number of impulses in spindle Ia afferents is greater than when α discharge alone is increased.

Explanation: **α-motor neurone activation alone** causes extrafusal contraction, which _unloads_ the spindle (since the spindle is in parallel with extrafusal fibres) — Ia firing decreases as the muscle shortens [Guyton ch.55]. **α-γ co-activation** simultaneously contracts the spindle's intrafusal polar regions via γ-motor neurones, keeping the spindle's central sensory zone stretched and Ia firing maintained or increased. Hence Ia firing is **greater** during α-γ co-activation than during α-discharge alone — this is why γ co-activation preserves spindle sensitivity throughout voluntary movement.

Hints:

1. Trace what happens to the spindle when α alone vs α + γ co-activation occurs.
2. γ activation keeps the spindle "loaded" during muscle shortening.
3. The result is preserved or increased Ia firing during co-activation.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Misrepresent the α-γ co-activation principle.

QUESTION 3

Stem: The bulboreticular facilitatory area of the brain stem excites the gamma efferent system. This bulboreticular area receives impulses from which of the following areas?

Correct answer: All of the above

Explanation: The **bulboreticular facilitatory area** in the dorsolateral pons and rostral medulla provides tonic excitatory drive to γ-motor neurones, maintaining baseline muscle tone [Guyton ch.55] [Guyton ch.56]. It receives convergent input from **cerebral cortex** (motor and premotor areas), **basal ganglia**, and **cerebellum**, integrating these signals to set γ-tone appropriately for ongoing movement and posture. Damage to or imbalance of any of these inputs alters muscle tone — the basis of clinical signs in motor system disease.

QUESTION 4

Stem: Which of the following describes components of the central pathway responsible for control of posture?

Correct answer: The medullary reticulospinal pathway terminates on neurons in the ventromedial area of the spinal ventral horn that innervate axial and proximal muscles.

Explanation: Descending motor pathways are organised somatotopically [Guyton ch.56]: **Medial pathways** (ventromedial spinal cord; reticulospinal, vestibulospinal, tectospinal) terminate on motor neurones innervating **axial and proximal muscles** for posture and gross movements. **Lateral pathways** (dorsolateral spinal cord; lateral corticospinal, rubrospinal) terminate on motor neurones innervating **distal limb muscles** for fine skilled movements. The pontine reticulospinal pathway terminates ventromedially (option a is wrong about flexor specification); tectospinal innervates head/neck muscles, not limbs (option b is wrong); medullary reticulospinal terminates ventromedially on axial/proximal motor neurones — **option (c) is correct**.

Hints:

2. Medial pathways control axial and proximal posture; lateral pathways control distal limb skill.
3. Reticulospinal is medial → axial/proximal.

Distractors:

- "Pontine reticulospinal terminates dorsomedial → flexors" — Wrong location (ventromedial) and wrong target (axial extensors, not flexors).
- "Tectospinal → dorsolateral → limb muscles" — Wrong (tectospinal is medial, controls head/neck).
- "None of the above" — (c) is correct.

QUESTION 6

Stem: Increased neural activity before a skilled voluntary movement is first seen in \_\_\_\_.

Correct answer: cortical association areas

Explanation: The temporal sequence of neural activation before a voluntary movement [Guyton ch.56]: (1) **Cortical association areas** (prefrontal, posterior parietal) — activity precedes movement onset by ~800 ms, reflecting movement _intention_ and _planning_; (2) **basal ganglia and cerebellum** receive these signals and contribute to selection and refinement; (3) **premotor and supplementary motor cortex** prepare the motor programme; (4) **primary motor cortex (precentral, area 4)** — final execution command via corticospinal tract. The earliest activity is in the **cortical association areas** — option (d).

Hints:

1. Trace the temporal hierarchy of voluntary movement initiation.
2. Intention and planning precede execution; recall which cortical areas serve each.
3. Association cortex fires first; M1 fires last in the cortical hierarchy.

Distractors:

- "precentral motor cortex" — Late-stage execution.
- "midbrain" — Modulatory, not the initiation site.
- "cerebellum" — Refinement, not initiation.

QUESTION 8

Stem: Which of the following is true about experimental sham rage?

Correct answer: Occurs in decorticate animals

Explanation: **Sham rage** is the term for the rage-like behaviour that emerges in **decorticate animals** (cerebral cortex removed but hypothalamus and brainstem intact) — the lower limbic and hypothalamic circuits, no longer modulated by cortex, produce stereotyped autonomic and motor rage components (piloerection, tachycardia, hissing, biting) without the usual cortical regulation [Guyton ch.58]. Key features: (a) **occurs in decorticate animals** — true; (b) it is _not goal-directed_ (no integrated cortex direction) — false; (c) it is _not_ caused by removing the hypothalamus — removing the hypothalamus _abolishes_ rage; (d) it appears and goes **rapidly** in response to minor stimuli — false. Hence (a).

Hints:

1. Recall the lesion that produces sham rage.
2. Removal of cortex with intact hypothalamus produces stereotyped rage.
3. The phenomenon is named for its lack of cortical direction.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Misrepresent sham rage features.

QUESTION 10

Stem: Which of the following statements regarding a clasp knife effect is false?

Correct answer: Pacinian Corpuscle is involved

Explanation: The **clasp-knife effect (lengthening reaction)** is the sudden release of resistance during passive stretch of a spastic muscle [Guyton ch.55]. Mechanism: in spasticity, hypertonia (a — true) creates strong resistance initially; passive stretch activates **Golgi tendon organs (b — true)** at high tendon tensions; Ib afferents trigger autogenic inhibition (inverse stretch reflex), abruptly relaxing the muscle. The phenomenon is called the **lengthening reaction (c — true)**. (d) is **false** — **Pacinian corpuscles** are vibration-sensitive cutaneous mechanoreceptors and are not involved in clasp-knife.

Hints:

1. Recall the mechanism and named features of clasp-knife.
2. The reflex involves muscle spindle and GTO, not Pacinian corpuscles.
3. The exception attributes a non-musculotendinous receptor to the reflex.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Correctly true.

QUESTION 11

Stem: Fill in the blanks: Efferent axons from the dentate nucleus terminate in \***\*\_\_\_\*\*** thalamic nucleus and travel through \***\*\_\_\_\*\*** peduncle?

Correct answer: Ventral lateral---superior cerebellar

Explanation: The **dentate nucleus** (largest deep cerebellar nucleus) is the major output of the **cerebrocerebellum** (lateral hemispheres) [Guyton ch.57]. Its efferent axons exit the cerebellum via the **superior cerebellar peduncle (brachium conjunctivum)**, decussate in the midbrain, and synapse in the **ventral lateral (VL)** and ventral anterior (VA) nuclei of the thalamus, which then project to the motor cortex. This dentato-thalamo-cortical loop is essential for the planning and execution of skilled voluntary movements. The **inferior cerebellar peduncle** carries climbing fibres from the inferior olive and afferents from the spinal cord; the **middle cerebellar peduncle** is dominated by mossy fibre afferents from pontine nuclei.

Hints:

1. Recall the dentato-thalamo-cortical loop anatomy.
2. The dentate nucleus exits via the superior cerebellar peduncle.
3. The thalamic relay is the VL nucleus.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — Misidentify either thalamic nucleus or peduncle.

QUESTION 13

Stem: A patient has developed an inability to control the distance, speed, and range of motion necessary to perform smoothly coordinated movements. His finger-to-nose test and heel-to-shin tests are abnormal. A lesion in which of the following sites is most likely to be found?

Correct answer: Spinocerebellar tract

Explanation: The clinical picture — **dysmetria** (inability to judge distance/speed/range), abnormal finger-nose and heel-shin tests — is the **cerebellar ataxia syndrome** [Guyton ch.57]. Cerebellar damage produces dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesia, intention tremor, ataxic gait, dysarthria, hypotonia. **Spinocerebellar tract** lesions disrupt proprioceptive input to the cerebellum, producing similar ataxic features. Hence (b). **Basal ganglia** lesions cause hypokinetic (parkinsonian) or hyperkinetic (chorea) features, not dysmetria. **Midbrain or pons** lesions can affect cerebellar inputs/outputs but are less direct.

Hints:

1. Recall the clinical signs of cerebellar dysfunction.
2. Dysmetria, intention tremor and dysdiadochokinesia are cerebellar.
3. Spinocerebellar tract carries the proprioceptive input critical for cerebellar coordination.

Distractors:

- "Basal Ganglia" — Hypo- or hyperkinetic syndromes, not dysmetria.
- (c), (d) — Less specific than spinocerebellar.

It is a sign of cerebellar dysfunction reflecting impaired timing and coordination of antagonist muscle groups. **Tandem-gait inability** is gait ataxia (cerebellar but a different specific sign). **Swaying with eyes open** suggests truncal ataxia or vestibular disorder. **VOR control** is brainstem/cerebellar (flocculus).

Hints:

2. The clinical test is rapid pronation-supination.
3. The deficit is impaired alternating coordination.

Distractors:

- "Tandem-Gait test" — Different cerebellar sign (gait ataxia).

QUESTION 15

Stem: The patient exhibited an unsteady "drunk like" gait and the physician noted that he swayed while entering the examination room. Which region of the cerebellum was most likely affected by the bleed?

Correct answer: Flocculonodular Lobe

Explanation: Functional divisions of the cerebellum [Guyton ch.57]: **Vestibulocerebellum (flocculonodular lobe)** — receives vestibular input, controls **balance, posture and gait**; lesions cause truncal ataxia, swaying, and a **"drunk-like" gait** (option a). **Spinocerebellum (vermis + paravermal)** — receives spinocerebellar input, controls limb coordination during walking; lesions cause gait ataxia with limb dysmetria. **Cerebrocerebellum (lateral hemispheres)** — coordinates planning of skilled voluntary movements; lesions cause delayed initiation, dysmetria of distal limb movements, intention tremor. The "drunk-like" swaying gait without prominent limb signs points to flocculonodular lesion. (Note: alcohol intoxication preferentially affects the vermis/anterior lobe; chronic alcohol cerebellar degeneration causes gait ataxia with truncal sway.)

Hints:

1. Recall the functional divisions of the cerebellum.
2. The vestibulocerebellum controls balance and gait.
3. Drunk-like swaying suggests vestibulocerebellar (flocculonodular) damage.

Distractors:

- "Spinocerebellum" — Limb coordination dominant, less truncal sway.
- "Cerebrocerebellum" — Distal-limb skilled movement, not gait.
- "Superior Vermis" — Affected in chronic alcoholics; could cause gait ataxia but classically with anterior-vermis/spinocerebellar features.

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: Both flocculonodular and superior vermis lesions can produce truncal ataxia and gait sway. Chronic alcohol cerebellar degeneration affects the superior anterior vermis. QUESTION 19

Stem: In a patient suffering from parkinson's disease, what is the most likely reason behind the gradual decline in the therapeutic effect of L-dopa?

Correct answer: The dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra continue to degenerate.

Explanation: **L-dopa** crosses the blood-brain barrier and is converted to dopamine by aromatic amino acid decarboxylase (AADC) in the surviving SNc dopaminergic neurones, which then store and release the dopamine [Guyton ch.57]. In Parkinson's disease, **SNc neurones progressively degenerate** — fewer surviving neurones means less L-dopa-to-dopamine conversion capacity and less storage, leading to **gradually declining therapeutic effect, motor fluctuations, and on-off phenomena**. Patients also develop dopaminergic dyskinesias as the surviving terminals release dopamine in pulsatile rather than tonic patterns. Antibody-mediated mechanisms (a) are not characteristic; pathway sprouting (b) is not the cause; α-synuclein (c) is the pathology but not the direct cause of L-dopa decline.

Hints:

1. Recall how L-dopa works at the cellular level.
2. L-dopa requires surviving SNc neurones to convert and store dopamine.
3. Progressive neurone loss reduces this capacity.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Misrepresent the mechanism of L-dopa decline.

QUESTION 20

Stem: A 50-year-old gentleman who is a known patient of parkinson's disease, develops refractoriness to L-dopa therapy. An alternative treatment modality is being planned, which employs ablation of certain thalamic regions by neurosurgery. Which thalamic nucleus is the stereotaxic target for the neurosurgeons?

Correct answer: Ventrolateral

Explanation: **Stereotactic ablation (or deep brain stimulation, DBS) of the ventrolateral nucleus of the thalamus** (specifically the ventral intermediate or VIM nucleus, part of the VL complex) is an effective treatment for parkinsonian and essential tremor [Guyton ch.57]. The VIM/VL receives cerebellar and pallidal input and projects to motor cortex; its disruption interrupts the abnormal oscillatory loop that produces tremor. Modern practice favours **DBS of the subthalamic nucleus (STN)** for advanced PD, but historically VL thalamotomy was a key surgical option and remains a textbook answer. **VPL** is somatosensory; **centromedian** is intralaminar (arousal/attention); **ventromedial** is not a standard PD target.

1. Recall the thalamic targets for movement-disorder surgery.
2. VL/VIM is the classical thalamotomy target for tremor.
3. The motor relay nucleus of the thalamus is the answer.

Distractors:

- "Centromedian" — Intralaminar, not motor relay.
- "Ventroposterolateral" — Sensory relay.
- "Ventromedial" — Not a standard target.
