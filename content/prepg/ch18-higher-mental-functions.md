QUESTION 1

Stem: Which of the following statements about the reticular activating system is false?

Correct answer: Does not produce EEG alerting response

Explanation: The **reticular activating system (RAS)** is the brainstem reticular formation pathway projecting via the intralaminar thalamic nuclei to widespread cortex, mediating **arousal and wakefulness** [Guyton ch.59]. (a) involved in arousal — true. (b) polysynaptic — true (multiple synapses through reticular formation). (c) receives collaterals from all ascending sensory pathways — true (the basis of arousal in response to sensory input). (d) is **false** — RAS stimulation **produces** the **EEG alerting (desynchronisation) response**, characterised by replacement of slow synchronous waves with low-voltage fast activity (beta).

Hints:

1. Recall the function and EEG correlates of RAS activation.
2. RAS activation causes EEG desynchronisation (alerting).
3. The exception falsely denies the EEG alerting response.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (c) — Correctly true.

QUESTION 2

Stem: Which is the EEG wave recorded in a subject who is awake but eyes closed?

Correct answer: Alpha waves

Explanation: **EEG frequency bands** [Guyton ch.59]: **Beta (>13 Hz)** — alert, eyes open, focused mental activity. **Alpha (8–13 Hz)** — **awake but relaxed with eyes closed**, posterior dominant. **Theta (4–7 Hz)** — drowsy/light sleep. **Delta (<4 Hz)** — deep sleep (NREM stage 3, "slow wave sleep"). Hence (a) alpha is the canonical "awake, eyes closed" rhythm. Opening the eyes blocks the alpha rhythm (alpha-blocking response).

Hints:

1. Recall the four major EEG frequency bands and the states they represent.
2. The "relaxed wakefulness with eyes closed" rhythm is named for the first letter of the Greek alphabet.
3. Alpha rhythm is characteristic.

Distractors:

- "Beta waves" — Eyes open, alert.
- "Theta waves" — Drowsy/light sleep.
- "Delta waves" — Deep sleep.

QUESTION 3

Stem: Which of the following structures regulates the sleep-wake cycle?

Correct answer: Hypothalamus

Explanation: The **hypothalamus** is the master regulator of the sleep-wake cycle through several specialised nuclei [Guyton ch.59]: **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** — the master circadian clock; **ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (VLPO)** — sleep-promoting GABAergic neurones that inhibit the arousal-promoting nuclei; **tuberomammillary nucleus (TMN)** — wake-promoting histaminergic neurones; **lateral hypothalamic orexin/hypocretin neurones** — stabilise wakefulness (loss → narcolepsy with cataplexy). The hypothalamic "flip-flop switch" between VLPO and TMN/orexin systems generates the bistable wake/sleep state.

Hints:

1. Recall the brain region housing the master circadian clock and sleep-promoting nuclei.
2. The hypothalamus contains both wake-promoting and sleep-promoting populations.
3. The flip-flop switch is hypothalamic.

Distractors:

- (a), (c), (d) — Not the master sleep-wake regulator.

QUESTION 4

Stem: In which stage of sleep are K complex and sleep spindles seen?

Correct answer: Stage 2 NREM

Explanation: **EEG hallmarks of sleep stages** [Guyton ch.59]: **Stage 1 NREM** — drowsiness with low-amplitude theta. **Stage 2 NREM** — sleep spindles (12–14 Hz brief bursts) and K complexes (large biphasic waves) on a background of theta — **defining features**. **Stage 3 NREM** — slow-wave (delta) sleep, >20% delta. **REM** — low-voltage mixed-frequency, sawtooth waves, EOG eye movements. Hence sleep spindles + K complexes = stage 2 NREM.

Hints:

1. Recall the EEG signatures of each NREM stage.
2. Sleep spindles and K complexes are stage-specific markers.
3. They define NREM stage 2.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Different EEG signatures.

QUESTION 5

Stem: Which stage constitutes the largest amount of sleep time of a young adult?

Correct answer: NREM 2

Explanation: Sleep architecture in young adults [Guyton ch.59]: **NREM 1** ~5%, **NREM 2** ~50% (largest), **NREM 3** (slow-wave sleep) ~20%, **REM** ~20–25%. Hence stage 2 NREM occupies the largest fraction of total sleep time. With age, NREM 3 (slow-wave sleep) declines markedly while stage 2 remains dominant. Sleep architecture changes (less SWS, fragmented sleep) are characteristic of ageing.

Hints:

1. Recall the proportions of total sleep time in each stage in young adults.
2. Stage 2 NREM is the dominant stage by duration.
3. The single largest fraction is stage 2 NREM.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Smaller proportions.

QUESTION 6

Stem: Which of the following is observed during sleep induction?

Correct answer: ↑ Acetyl choline

Explanation: Sleep neurochemistry [Guyton ch.59]: during the wake-to-sleep transition, monoaminergic systems (NA from locus coeruleus, 5-HT from raphe nuclei, histamine from tuberomammillary nucleus) **decrease** their firing — eliminating arousal drive. **GABAergic activity** from the **VLPO increases**, promoting sleep. **Cholinergic activity** declines into NREM but **rises sharply during REM** sleep (REM is sometimes called "paradoxical sleep" because the cortex is highly activated cholinergically while behavioural sleep persists). The keyed answer (b) ↑ ACh fits the _REM_ phase of sleep more than the NREM induction phase.

Hints:

1. Recall the neurochemistry of NREM vs REM sleep.
2. Monoamines fall, GABA rises, ACh varies by stage.
3. ACh increases during REM phase.

Distractors:

- "↑ Norepinephrine and serotonin" — Wrong direction (these decrease).
- "↑ Histamine" — Wrong direction (histamine decreases).
- "↓ GABA" — Wrong direction (GABA increases).

[AMBIGUITY NOTE: The keyed answer (b) ↑ ACh applies primarily to REM sleep, not to "sleep induction" (the wake-to-NREM transition) where ACh actually declines from waking levels. If "sleep induction" is interpreted broadly to include REM, (b) is defensible. The other three options (a, c, d) are definitively wrong by their direction of change. By elimination, (b) is the only option that could be correct in any sleep context.]

QUESTION 8

Stem: Delta waves are seen in**\_**

Correct answer: Deep sleep

Explanation: **Delta waves (<4 Hz, high-amplitude)** dominate the EEG of **NREM stage 3 — slow-wave sleep — also known as "deep sleep"** [Guyton ch.59]. Delta sleep is the most restorative NREM stage and is associated with growth hormone release, immune function, and memory consolidation (declarative). Delta waves _also_ appear in pathological states (encephalopathy) and in infancy as physiological. **REM sleep** has low-voltage mixed-frequency EEG resembling waking. **Awake** state has alpha/beta. **NREM 1** has theta.

Hints:

2. Delta is the slowest waveband.
3. Slow waves dominate the deepest NREM stage.

Distractors:

- (b), (c), (d) — Different EEG dominance.

QUESTION 10

Stem: Which of the following is the source of melatonin?

Explanation: **Melatonin (N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine)** is synthesised from serotonin in the **pineal gland** by N-acetyltransferase and HIOMT [Guyton ch.59]. Melatonin secretion follows a strong circadian rhythm — **rising at night and falling during the day** — driven by the SCN via sympathetic input to the pineal. It functions in circadian rhythm entrainment and (weakly) in sleep promotion. Pineal calcification with age reduces but does not abolish melatonin secretion.

QUESTION 12

Stem: In which type of memory are basal ganglia involved?

Correct answer: Procedural

Explanation: Memory systems and their substrates [Guyton ch.59]: **Declarative (explicit) memory** — facts and events; depends on **medial temporal lobe (hippocampus, entorhinal cortex)**. **Non-declarative (implicit) memory** — includes **procedural memory** (skills, habits), classical conditioning, priming, non-associative learning. **Procedural memory** depends on the **basal ganglia** (especially the striatum) and **cerebellum** — supporting motor skill learning, habit formation, sequence learning. Hence basal ganglia → procedural memory.

Hints:

1. Match the basal ganglia to its memory function.
2. Skills, habits, and sequences are procedural memory.
3. The basal ganglia (with cerebellum) support procedural memory.

Distractors:

- "Conditioning" — Classical conditioning is amygdala/cerebellum-dependent (depending on type).
- "Explicit" — Hippocampus-dependent.
- "None" — Basal ganglia do support memory.

QUESTION 13

Stem: A 50-year-old lady develops severe anterograde amnesia. On further evaluation, a suprasellar craniopharyngioma invading adjacent structures is seen on T1-weighted MRI. Compression of which of the following structures can explain her symptoms?

Correct answer: Mammillary body

Explanation: **Anterograde amnesia** (inability to form new memories) reflects damage to the **medial temporal lobe memory circuit**, especially **hippocampus and mammillary bodies** [Guyton ch.59]. **Mammillary bodies** are adjacent to the suprasellar region and can be compressed by suprasellar masses (craniopharyngiomas, large pituitary adenomas). They are part of the **Papez circuit**: hippocampus → fornix → mammillary bodies → mammillothalamic tract → anterior thalamus → cingulate. Mammillary body damage causes severe anterograde amnesia (also seen in Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome). **Prefrontal cortex** governs working memory and executive function (different deficit). **Amygdala** governs emotional memory (different deficit).

Hints:

1. Recall the anatomical structures of the Papez memory circuit.
2. Mammillary bodies are crucial for memory consolidation and lie in the suprasellar region.
3. Suprasellar mass compression of mammillary bodies → anterograde amnesia.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Different memory roles.

QUESTION 14

Stem: Which of the following neural mechanism is not involved in synaptic plasticity and learning?

Correct answer: Desensitization

Explanation: Forms of synaptic plasticity in learning [Guyton ch.59]: **Habituation** — decreased response to repeated stimulus (non-associative learning). **Sensitisation** — enhanced response to noxious stimulus. **Post-tetanic potentiation (PTP)** — short-term increase in synaptic strength after high-frequency stimulation. **Long-term potentiation (LTP)** — sustained increase in synaptic strength (hippocampus, NMDA-dependent — basis of memory). **Long-term depression (LTD)** — sustained decrease in synaptic strength (cerebellum, motor learning). **Desensitisation** (option c), by contrast, is a **receptor-level phenomenon** of reduced response to continuous agonist exposure (e.g. nicotinic AChR desensitisation) — a _millisecond-to-second_ time-scale process, not synaptic plasticity in the learning sense. Hence (c).

Hints:

1. Distinguish synaptic plasticity (changes in synaptic strength) from receptor desensitisation (millisecond-scale agonist tolerance).
2. Habituation, PTP, LTP, and LTD are forms of plasticity.
3. Desensitisation is a receptor property, not a plasticity mechanism.

Distractors:

- (a), (b), (d) — Plasticity mechanisms.

QUESTION 15

Stem: The type of potentiation in hippocampus is **\_\_**

Correct answer: Long-term potentiation

Explanation: **Long-term potentiation (LTP)** in the hippocampus is the prototype of synaptic plasticity underlying declarative memory [Guyton ch.59]. Mechanism: high-frequency stimulation of glutamatergic afferents activates **NMDA receptors** (which are voltage-dependent and Ca²⁺-permeable). The Ca²⁺ influx triggers signalling cascades (CaMKII, PKC) that phosphorylate AMPA receptors, increase AMPA insertion into the postsynaptic membrane, and (over time) cause structural changes. The result: increased synaptic strength lasting hours to weeks — the cellular basis of memory.

Hints:

1. Recall the form of synaptic plasticity that underlies hippocampal memory.
2. NMDA-dependent synaptic strengthening is LTP.
3. LTP is the canonical hippocampal plasticity.

Distractors:

- "Post tetanic potentiation" — Brief (seconds-to-minutes) NA-related effect, not LTP.
- "Long-term depression" — Cerebellar form of plasticity.
- "Habituation" — Non-associative learning, different mechanism.

QUESTION 20

Stem: Which of the following is the test for immediate memory?

Correct answer: Digit span forward up to 7 digits with 2 skips allowed

Explanation: **Memory** is divided clinically into **immediate (working/short-term)**, **recent**, and **remote** memory [Guyton ch.59]. **Immediate memory** is tested with **digit span forward** (typical normal: 7 ± 2 digits), reflecting the verbal working-memory loop. **Digit span backward** tests both immediate and a degree of executive manipulation. **Serial subtraction tests** assess working memory and concentration but are also influenced by attention/executive function. Hence (a) digit span forward is the canonical immediate-memory test.

Hints:

1. Recall the neuropsychological tests for immediate vs working memory.
2. Digit span forward tests the verbal short-term store.
3. The classical test is digit span forward, ~7 digits.

Distractors:

- (b), (d) — Working memory + executive function.
- "Digit span backwards" — Tests working memory with manipulation, harder than forward span.

QUESTION 22

Correct answer: NREM 2

Explanation: **Bruxism** (teeth grinding/clenching during sleep) typically occurs during **NREM stage 2 sleep** (and sometimes during arousals from sleep) [Guyton ch.59].
