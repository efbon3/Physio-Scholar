QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the integrated process of haemostasis from vessel injury to clot formation.

Model answer: Haemostasis - balance of procoagulant and anticoagulant forces; sequential cascade. (1) _Vasoconstriction_ - immediate, reduces blood loss. (2) _Primary haemostasis_ (platelets - seconds-minutes): vWF (Weibel-Palade bodies) bridges exposed collagen to platelet GPIb-IX-V → adhesion; ADP, TXA2, thrombin activate; granule release; GPIIb/IIIa binds fibrinogen → aggregation → platelet plug. Drugs: aspirin (irreversible COX-1), P2Y12 (clopidogrel/prasugrel/ticagrelor), GPIIb/IIIa (abciximab). (3) _Secondary haemostasis_ (cascade - minutes): tissue factor + VIIa → activates IX, X; intrinsic amplification (XII, XI, IX, VIII); common pathway Xa+Va → thrombin → fibrinogen→fibrin → factor XIII crosslinks → stable clot. Vitamin K-dependent: II, VII, IX, X, protein C/S. Tests: PT/INR (warfarin), aPTT (heparin, factor deficiency), fibrinogen, factor assays. (4) _Fibrinolysis_ (hours-days): plasminogen→plasmin (tPA) → cleaves fibrin → D-dimers. Anticoagulant systems: antithrombin III (heparin enhances), protein C/S, TFPI maintain balance. Localised clot at injury, contained systemically [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: Describe haemophilia A and B. How are they distinguished and managed?

Model answer: _Haemophilia A_ - X-linked recessive; factor VIII deficiency; ~ 1/5000 males. _Haemophilia B (Christmas)_ - X-linked recessive; factor IX deficiency; ~ 1/30,000 males. Severity by factor level: severe < 1%, moderate 1-5%, mild > 5%. Clinical: severe presents in infancy with haemarthroses (knees, elbows, ankles - chronic arthropathy if untreated); intramuscular bleeding; intracranial (rare but devastating); prolonged bleeding after circumcision/procedures. Diagnosis: prolonged aPTT (corrects on mixing); normal PT; factor assay; family history. Management: (1) Factor replacement - recombinant FVIII or FIX for bleeds; prophylaxis 2-3x weekly to prevent arthropathy. (2) Long-acting concentrates - efmoroctocog (FVIII), nonacog beta pegol (FIX); less frequent dosing. (3) _Emicizumab_ - bispecific antibody mimicking FVIII function (binds FIXa and X); subcutaneous; effective in HA + inhibitors; revolutionary for severe HA. (4) _Gene therapy_ - valoctocogene roxaparvovec (HA, FDA 2022), etranacogene dezaparvovec (HB, FDA 2022) - AAV-based; durability and safety being established. (5) _Inhibitors_ (alloantibodies, ~ 30% severe HA): bypassing agents (FEIBA, recombinant FVIIa); immune tolerance induction. (6) Comprehensive haemophilia centre care. Modern outcomes - normal life expectancy and minimal joint damage with prophylaxis + emicizumab [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: Describe von Willebrand disease - the most common inherited bleeding disorder.

Model answer: _von Willebrand disease (vWD)_ - inherited deficiency or dysfunction of vWF; ~ 1% prevalence; clinically significant ~ 0.1%. vWF normally: (a) bridges platelets to subendothelial collagen via GPIb-IX-V (primary haemostasis); (b) carries FVIII (extends half-life). Types: (1) Type 1 (~ 75%) - partial quantitative; AD; usually mild; (2) Type 2 (qualitative, AD): 2A (loss of HMW multimers), 2B (gain-of-function - thrombocytopenia from clearance), 2M (decreased binding, normal multimers), 2N (decreased FVIII binding); (3) Type 3 (severe; AR; rare; absent vWF; profound bleeding). Clinical: mucocutaneous bleeding - heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB; very common; often missed for years), epistaxis, GI, surgical, dental; rarely deep bleeding (Type 3). Diagnosis: vWF antigen, ristocetin cofactor activity (vWF:RCo - functional), FVIII activity, vWF multimer analysis (subtype). Family history important. Management: (1) DDAVP - releases vWF from Weibel-Palade bodies; useful in Type 1, 2A; trial dose first. (2) vWF/FVIII concentrate - severe cases, types unresponsive, surgery. (3) Antifibrinolytics (tranexamic acid) - mucosal bleeding. (4) Hormonal - HMB (OCP, levonorgestrel-IUD). (5) Avoid antiplatelets/NSAIDs. Newer: rVWF (vonicog alfa). vWD is underdiagnosed - HMB in young women warrants vWD workup [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Type: recall

Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: Describe immune thrombocytopenia (ITP) - mechanism and management.

Model answer: _Immune thrombocytopenia (ITP)_ - autoimmune destruction of platelets + impaired production. Anti-platelet IgG (often against GPIIb/IIIa, GPIb-IX) → splenic phagocytosis + complement-mediated destruction; megakaryocyte impairment. Primary (idiopathic) or secondary (viral - HIV, HCV; H. pylori; CTD - SLE; lymphoproliferative - CLL; drugs; vaccines rarely). Bimodal age distribution. Clinical: isolated thrombocytopenia; mucocutaneous bleeding (petechiae, purpura, gum, nose, GI, GU, menorrhagia); intracranial rare but feared; many asymptomatic and incidentally discovered. Severity by platelet count: mild > 50, moderate 30-50, severe < 30, very severe < 10. Diagnosis: (1) Isolated thrombocytopenia, otherwise normal FBC; blood film no abnormal cells (smudge cells in pseudothrombocytopenia from EDTA - retest in citrate); (2) Bone marrow normal/increased megakaryocytes (not always done); (3) Exclude secondary causes (viral, drugs, autoimmune, lymphoproliferative); (4) Anti-platelet antibodies not routine (low sensitivity/specificity). Management: (1) Watch + wait if asymptomatic and platelets > 30 (no significant bleeding risk); (2) _Corticosteroids_ - prednisolone 1 mg/kg or dexamethasone pulse; first-line; many respond but most relapse. (3) _IVIG_ - rapid effect (24-48 h); useful for emergencies, surgery, pregnancy; transient. (4) _Anti-D_ - in RhD+ non-splenectomised; transient. (5) _TPO-receptor agonists_ - eltrombopag (oral), romiplostim (SC weekly), avatrombopag (oral); high response rates; long-term option; bone marrow fibrosis monitor. (6) _Rituximab_ (anti-CD20); 60% response. (7) _Splenectomy_ in refractory; cure ~ 60%; vaccinations + antibiotic prophylaxis. (8) _Fostamatinib_ (SYK inhibitor) - newer for refractory. Acute severe bleeding: IV methylprednisolone + IVIG + platelet transfusion (despite ongoing destruction, useful for emergencies). Pregnancy considerations: monitor; treat as needed; no fetal effect typically [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

1. Anti-platelet IgG → splenic destruction + impaired production.

3. Steroids, IVIG, TPO agonists, rituximab, splenectomy.

Stem: Describe deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism. How are they diagnosed and managed?

Model answer: _Venous thromboembolism (VTE)_ - includes DVT and PE; spectrum of same disease. _DVT_: usually lower limb (calf - distal; popliteal/femoral/iliac - proximal); presents with leg swelling, pain, warmth, erythema; can extend or embolise. Distal alone less likely to embolise. _PE_: sudden dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, tachycardia; massive PE - shock, syncope, RV strain; sub-massive - RV strain on echo or troponin/BNP elevated; non-massive - smaller without compromise. _Virchow triad_: stasis, hypercoagulability, endothelial injury. Risk factors: stasis (immobilisation, long flights, paralysis, surgery), hypercoagulability (cancer - especially adenocarcinoma; pregnancy/postpartum; OCP/HRT; obesity; smoking; nephrotic; PNH; thrombophilia inherited - factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, antithrombin/protein C/S deficiency, antiphospholipid syndrome; severe inflammation; COVID-19), endothelial injury (surgery, trauma, central catheters, IBD, vasculitis). _Diagnosis_: Wells score (DVT, PE) → low-pretest probability + negative D-dimer rules out; otherwise imaging - DVT compression ultrasound; PE CT pulmonary angiography (gold standard) or VQ scan (contrast contraindicated, pregnancy); MR/CT venography for iliac/IVC. Investigate cause - cancer screening (especially unprovoked, age-appropriate); thrombophilia workup in selected. _Management_: (1) Anticoagulation - DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban) first-line for most VTE; convenient, no monitoring, specific reversal; LMWH for cancer (DOACs now equivalent); UFH for haemodynamic compromise / massive PE / renal impairment; pregnancy LMWH only. Duration 3-6 months provoked; longer or indefinite for unprovoked, recurrent, ongoing risk. (2) Thrombolysis - massive PE with shock or sub-massive with RV dysfunction; bleeding risk significant. (3) Surgical/catheter thrombectomy in selected. (4) IVC filter when anticoagulation contraindicated; retrievable preferred. (5) Compression stockings symptomatic. (6) Treat underlying (cancer; APLS - lifelong anticoagulation, warfarin preferred for triple-positive arterial). DOAC reversal: andexanet alfa (Xa inhibitors), idarucizumab (dabigatran). Modern anticoagulation transformed VTE management [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

1. Virchow triad: stasis, hypercoagulability, endothelial injury.
2. Diagnose with Wells + D-dimer + imaging (US for DVT, CTPA for PE).
3. DOACs first-line; LMWH cancer/pregnancy.

Stem: Describe heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) - paradoxical thrombosis in patients on heparin.

Model answer: _HIT_ - immune-mediated paradoxical _thrombotic_ complication of heparin; thrombocytopenia and thrombosis (not bleeding). Mechanism: IgG antibodies against PF4-heparin complex (more common with UFH > LMWH > fondaparinux) → cross-link FcγRIIa on platelets → activation, aggregation, microthrombi formation; consumption causes thrombocytopenia. ~ 0.2-3% risk on UFH; lower for LMWH; rare for fondaparinux. Typically 5-10 days after heparin start (or within hours if recent prior heparin). Clinical: 4Ts score - Thrombocytopenia (≥ 50% drop or absolute), Timing (5-10 days typical), Thrombosis (new venous or arterial), oTher causes excluded. Thrombosis - venous (DVT, PE more common), arterial (limb ischaemia, MI, stroke), bizarre sites (skin necrosis at injection site, adrenal vein - adrenal infarction, mesenteric, skin). Diagnosis: 4Ts score + immunoassay (anti-PF4-heparin ELISA - sensitive, less specific) → confirmatory functional assay (serotonin release assay - SRA, gold standard). Management: (1) STOP all heparin (including line flushes); (2) Alternative anticoagulation - argatroban (direct thrombin, hepatic clearance, monitor aPTT), bivalirudin (DTI, short half-life), fondaparinux (off-label, indirect Xa - very low cross-reactivity); not warfarin alone (risk of microvascular thrombosis with protein C drop); (3) Once platelets recover (> 150) - bridge to warfarin or DOAC; (4) Document HIT - lifelong avoidance of heparin (re-exposure can be fatal); (5) Continue anticoagulation 4 weeks (just HIT) or 3 months (HIT + thrombosis); (6) Do _not_ give platelets unless bleeding (worsens thrombosis). HIT is a critical clinical entity to recognise [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) - a true emergency.

Model answer: _TTP_ - microangiopathic thrombotic disorder from severe ADAMTS13 deficiency. ADAMTS13 normally cleaves ultra-large vWF multimers (ULVWF) released from endothelium; deficiency → ULVWF → spontaneous platelet aggregation in microvasculature → microthrombi → MAHA + thrombocytopenia + organ ischaemia. Pentad (classical, often incomplete - any 2 + of MAHA + thrombocytopenia warrants suspicion): (1) MAHA (haemolysis with schistocytes - LDH up, indirect bilirubin up, haptoglobin down, retic up); (2) Thrombocytopenia (often profound, < 30); (3) AKI (variable); (4) Neurological (any - confusion, focal deficits, seizures, coma); (5) Fever. Pentad complete in only ~ 40%. _Acquired TTP_ (most cases): autoimmune anti-ADAMTS13 IgG; women > men; precipitants - pregnancy, infection (HIV), drugs (clopidogrel, ticlopidine, quinine), autoimmune (SLE), malignancy. _Congenital (Upshaw-Schulman)_: ADAMTS13 mutations; presents childhood to adulthood; recurrent. Diagnosis: clinical + MAHA + thrombocytopenia + ADAMTS13 activity < 10% (definitive); inhibitor assay; exclude HUS (typical with bloody diarrhoea + Shiga toxin), DIC (different lab pattern), HELLP, pregnancy-associated. Management - emergency: (1) _Urgent plasma exchange_ (TPE) - removes anti-ADAMTS13 + replaces enzyme; daily until platelets recover and LDH normalises; cornerstone. (2) Corticosteroids (high-dose). (3) _Caplacizumab_ - anti-vWF nanobody; reduces TTP-related deaths and refractory disease (FDA 2019). (4) Rituximab - reduces relapse, often added during/after exchange. (5) Avoid platelet transfusion (worsens thrombosis) unless life-threatening bleeding. (6) Long-term: monitor ADAMTS13 activity + inhibitor; relapse common; rituximab maintenance in selected. Mortality untreated ~ 90%, with treatment ~ 10-20%. Distinguish from HUS - typical (E. coli O157, Shiga toxin, children, bloody diarrhoea) and atypical (complement dysregulation, eculizumab). True medical emergency [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: Describe DIC - disseminated intravascular coagulation. How does it cause both bleeding and thrombosis?

Model answer: _DIC_ - acquired widespread coagulation activation → intravascular fibrin formation + microvascular thrombosis + consumption of platelets/factors → bleeding. Always _secondary_; treat both. Causes: (1) Sepsis (most common - LPS, cytokines induce TF expression); (2) Trauma (massive TF release; head injury - brain rich in TF); (3) Malignancy (adenocarcinoma, APL - profound DIC; mucin-secreting); (4) Obstetric (placental abruption, amniotic fluid embolism, HELLP, retained dead fetus); (5) Severe burns; (6) Severe transfusion reactions; (7) Snake envenomation; (8) Pancreatitis severe; (9) Liver failure (mimics DIC). Pathophysiology: tissue factor exposure → thrombin generation → microvascular fibrin → MOF (kidney, liver, lung, brain); consumption of platelets, fibrinogen, V, VIII, antithrombin, protein C; compensatory fibrinolysis - plasmin → further depletion; raised D-dimers, FDPs. Net: bleeding + thrombosis simultaneously. Clinical: (1) _Bleeding_ - oozing from venepunctures, IV sites; ecchymoses, petechiae; mucosal bleeding; haematuria; surgical wound; ICH; (2) _Thrombotic_ - microvascular dysfunction across organs (AKI, ARDS, hepatic, encephalopathy); purpura fulminans (severe); digital gangrene; (3) Underlying cause features. Diagnosis: ISTH score - low platelets (declining), prolonged PT and aPTT, low fibrinogen (often < 1 g/L), raised D-dimer (cardinal); blood film - schistocytes. Differential: TTP/HUS, HELLP, hepatic failure, hypothermia. Management: (1) _Treat underlying cause_ - antibiotics/source control for sepsis, delivery for abruption, ATRA for APL, surgery for trauma; (2) _Supportive_: platelets if thrombocytopenia + bleeding; FFP for clotting factors; cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate for fibrinogen < 1.5 g/L; PCC for severe coagulopathy; RBC; (3) _Anticoagulation_ controversial - LMWH may help chronic compensated DIC (Trousseau syndrome, malignancy); not standard in acute DIC; (4) _Antifibrinolytics_ (TXA) selectively in hyperfibrinolytic DIC (e.g., APL); (5) Minimise iatrogenic bleeding. Mortality high, especially sepsis-induced [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: A 25-year-old woman with three miscarriages, recurrent DVT, and lupus anticoagulant. Decompose antiphospholipid syndrome.

Model answer: _Antiphospholipid syndrome (APLS)_ - autoimmune thrombophilia. Diagnosis (revised Sapporo/Sydney criteria): laboratory - persistent positive (≥ 12 weeks apart): lupus anticoagulant (functional clotting test), anti-cardiolipin IgG/IgM, anti-β2-glycoprotein I IgG/IgM; PLUS clinical - venous or arterial thrombosis OR pregnancy morbidity (≥ 3 unexplained consecutive miscarriages < 10 weeks; ≥ 1 fetal death > 10 weeks; ≥ 1 preterm < 34 weeks for severe pre-eclampsia/IUGR/placental insufficiency). Triple-positive (LA + ACA + anti-β2GPI all positive) - highest thrombosis risk. Pathogenesis: antibodies against phospholipid-binding proteins disrupt anticoagulant systems (annexin V), activate complement, endothelial dysfunction, platelet activation. Clinical: venous thrombosis (DVT, PE - most common); arterial thrombosis (stroke - young patients; MI; limb ischaemia; mesenteric); pregnancy morbidity; thrombocytopenia (~ 30%); haemolytic anaemia; livedo reticularis; neurological (chorea, transverse myelitis); valvular disease (Libman-Sacks); skin (digital ulcers, gangrene). Catastrophic APLS (CAPS) - rare, severe; multiple thrombosis + organ failure; high mortality. Primary or secondary (most often SLE). Paradoxical aPTT prolongation that _does not correct on mixing_ (Lupus anticoagulant). Management: (1) _Anticoagulation_: indefinite for any thrombosis; _warfarin preferred for triple-positive arterial APLS_ (DOACs less effective in trials - TRAPS, RAPS); INR target 2-3 for venous, 3-4 for arterial (controversial). DOACs may be acceptable for venous in non-triple-positive but warfarin remains default. (2) Pregnancy: aspirin 75 mg + LMWH (prophylactic or therapeutic); reduces miscarriage. (3) Treat underlying autoimmune (e.g., SLE). (4) CAPS - immediate immunosuppression + plasma exchange + steroids + IVIG. (5) Thrombocytopenia management as needed. Vitamin K for bleeding on warfarin; specific reversal as needed. APLS is a critical diagnosis with specific management implications [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Type: clinical

Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: Describe inherited thrombophilias - factor V Leiden, prothrombin gene mutation, protein C/S/antithrombin deficiency.

Model answer: Inherited thrombophilias - genetic predisposition to venous thrombosis; varying penetrance. (1) _Factor V Leiden (FVL)_ - most common; G1691A mutation in factor V gene → APC resistance (FVa not inactivated by activated protein C); ~ 5% of Caucasians; heterozygous 5-10x VTE risk, homozygous 50-100x. (2) _Prothrombin gene mutation (G20210A)_ - 2nd most common; ~ 2% of Caucasians; ~ 3x VTE risk heterozygous. (3) _Antithrombin deficiency_ - rare (~ 1/2000); 25-50x VTE risk; severe; AD; resistant to UFH initially. (4) _Protein C deficiency_ - ~ 1/300; 7x VTE risk; warfarin-induced skin necrosis (transient procoagulant when warfarin starts due to short PC half-life - bridge with heparin until INR therapeutic); homozygous deficiency in newborns - purpura fulminans, requires PC concentrate. (5) _Protein S deficiency_ - ~ 1/3000; similar to PC; pregnancy lowers PS levels (interpretation challenging). Combinations multiply risk. _When to test_: not routine. Consider in: (a) Idiopathic VTE in young patient (< 50 years); (b) Recurrent VTE; (c) VTE in unusual sites (cerebral vein, mesenteric, hepatic); (d) Strong family history (multiple first-degree relatives); (e) Skin necrosis on warfarin (PC); (f) Recurrent miscarriage. Test off anticoagulation when possible (warfarin lowers PC, PS; heparin lowers AT). Acute thrombosis affects levels - retest in remission. _Management_: (1) Anticoagulation for acute VTE - same as without thrombophilia; (2) Duration - same as for VTE indication; thrombophilia _may_ extend duration in some scenarios; (3) Asymptomatic carriers - no anticoagulation; risk-modify (smoking cessation, weight, OCP avoidance, prophylaxis around surgery/pregnancy). (4) Family screening - controversial; counsel and let individuals decide. (5) Pregnancy - LMWH prophylaxis in selected (severe thrombophilia, prior VTE, family history, comorbidities). Inherited thrombophilias matter clinically when they alter management (asymptomatic vs anticoagulation duration vs family screening) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: Describe warfarin and DOACs - mechanisms, monitoring, reversal, and clinical use.

Model answer: _Warfarin_ - vitamin K antagonist; inhibits VKORC1 → cannot regenerate reduced vitamin K → cannot γ-carboxylate factors II, VII, IX, X, protein C, S; depletes vitamin K-dependent factors. Stem: Synthesise this chapter: how do haemostasis mechanisms integrate, and what design principles emerge?

Model answer: Haemostasis is paradigmatic dynamic balance in physiology. Design principles: (1) _Localised activation_ - tissue factor exposure at injury site initiates; spread limited by anticoagulant systems (antithrombin, protein C/S, TFPI). (2) _Sequential cascade_ - amplification (one Xa makes ~ 1000 thrombin) allows rapid clot at injury without systemic activation. (3) _Multiple parallel pathways_ - intrinsic + extrinsic + common provide redundancy + cross-checks. (4) _Cofactor requirement_ - calcium + phospholipid + vitamin K + others enable specificity to injury site (cells exposing tissue factor). (5) _Built-in brakes_ - anticoagulant systems prevent runaway clot; fibrinolysis dissolves once healed. (6) _Cellular and humoral integration_ - platelets + endothelium + plasma proteins coordinate. (7) _Time scales_ - vasoconstriction (seconds) → platelet plug (minutes) → fibrin clot (minutes) → fibrinolysis (hours-days) - each appropriate to its role. (8) _Feedback_ - thrombin activates platelets, factors V/VIII; protein C activated by thrombin-thrombomodulin (negative feedback). (9) _Specificity through compartmentalisation_ - subendothelial collagen exposure activates platelets; tissue factor activates factor VII; localised by injury location. Disease patterns reveal design failures: (1) _Bleeding_ - too little procoagulant or too much anticoagulant/fibrinolytic. (2) _Thrombosis_ - too much procoagulant or too little anticoagulant; localised dysfunction (atherosclerosis) or systemic (thrombophilia). (3) _DIC_ - simultaneous excessive activation + consumption + bleeding + thrombosis. (4) _HIT_ - paradoxical thrombosis from antibody mechanism. (5) _TTP_ - microangiopathy from ADAMTS13 deficiency. Pharmacological intervention spans every step: aspirin (COX-1), P2Y12 (ADP receptor), GPIIb/IIIa antagonists (platelet aggregation), heparin (antithrombin enhancement), warfarin (vitamin K cycle), DOACs (Xa or thrombin direct), thrombolytics (plasminogen activators), antifibrinolytics (TXA, lysine analogues), specific replacement (factor concentrates, vWF, fibrinogen, PCC), genetic therapies (gene therapy for haemophilia). Modern haemostasis care integrates molecular detail (DOACs, gene therapy), pharmacology (precision targeting), and clinical management (anticoagulation duration, balance of risks). Each clinical syndrome is interpretable through the design framework. The haemostatic system illustrates how a critical physiological function is achieved through balanced activation and inhibition - tipped one way produces bleeding, the other thrombosis, both simultaneously catastrophic [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].
