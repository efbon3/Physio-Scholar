## Pass 1 — Vision

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The principal refractive structures of the eye, in order of their contribution to total refractive power, are:

Correct answer: Cornea (~40 D, fixed) > lens (~20 D, adjustable for accommodation) — together producing the ~60 D needed to focus distant images on the retina; the cornea contributes most because of the large refractive change at the air–cornea interface.

Distractors:

- "Lens > cornea." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the contribution. Correction: the cornea contributes more (~40 D) than the lens (~20 D); the air–cornea interface produces the largest refractive change.
- "Iris > cornea." — Reveals misconception: student treats the iris as a refractive structure. Correction: the iris controls pupil aperture and depth of focus but contributes little refractive power; the cornea is the principal refractive surface.
- "Vitreous > aqueous." — Reveals misconception: student picks intraocular fluids as the refractive elements. Correction: aqueous and vitreous have refractive indices similar to water and contribute little to refraction; the cornea and lens dominate.

Explanation: The cornea provides the largest single contribution to refraction because of its high refractive index (~1.376) at the air–cornea interface, where the refractive change is greatest [Guyton & Hall ch.50; Berne & Levy ch.7]. The crystalline lens adds variable power (accommodation). With age, the lens stiffens (presbyopia, typically appearing around age 40–45) and accommodation amplitude declines from ~14 D in childhood to <2 D by age 60 — necessitating reading glasses. Refractive errors include myopia (axial length too long or cornea too curved → image focused in front of retina; corrected with concave lenses), hyperopia (axial length too short → image focused behind retina; corrected with convex lenses), and astigmatism (irregular corneal curvature → multiple focal points). Modern refractive surgery (LASIK, PRK) reshapes the cornea to alter its refractive contribution.

Hints:

1. The fixed refractive surface contributes most; the adjustable one contributes less.
2. Look for the largest refractive-index step — air to tissue.
3. Total eye power is ~60 D.

QUESTION 2
Type: application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Accommodation for near vision involves:

Correct answer: Parasympathetic activation via CN III (Edinger-Westphal nucleus) → ciliary muscle contraction → relaxation of zonular fibres → lens becomes more spherical and refractive power increases — accompanied by pupillary constriction and convergence (the "near triad").

Distractors:

- "Sympathetic activation." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong autonomic limb. Correction: accommodation, miosis, and convergence are parasympathetic — driven by the Edinger-Westphal nucleus via CN III; sympathetic activity dilates the pupil.
- "Direct lens elasticity changes." — Reveals misconception: student forgets the role of zonular tension. Correction: the lens is held flatter by zonular tension at rest; ciliary muscle contraction _relaxes_ the zonules, allowing the elastic lens to become more spherical.
- "Movement of the cornea." — Reveals misconception: student attributes accommodation to corneal change. Correction: the cornea is fixed; accommodation alters the curvature of the lens, not the cornea.

Explanation: The "near triad" comprises accommodation (lens thickening), miosis (pupillary constriction increases depth of focus and reduces aberrations), and convergence (medial rectus contraction aligns both eyes on the near target) [Guyton & Hall ch.50; Ganong]. All three are parasympathetic via CN III, with central coordination at the Edinger-Westphal nucleus and visual cortex feedback. The Argyll Robertson pupil of tertiary syphilis demonstrates dissociation: pupils accommodate to near but do not constrict to light — pathognomonic for neurosyphilis. With age, the lens stiffens, and accommodation amplitude declines (presbyopia).

Hints:

1. The relevant cranial nerve carries parasympathetic fibres to the eye.
2. Tightening the ciliary muscle releases the zonular fibres.
3. Three things happen together: lens thickening, pupil constriction, and convergence.

QUESTION 3
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The retinal photoreceptors are organised into:

Correct answer: Rods (~120 million, peripheral retina, mediating low-light/scotopic vision, monochromatic, single rhodopsin pigment) and cones (~6 million, concentrated in the fovea, mediating photopic and colour vision via three opsin pigments — S/blue, M/green, L/red).

Distractors:

- "Rods (in the fovea) and cones (in the periphery)." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the spatial distribution. Correction: cones predominate in the fovea (the area of highest acuity), while rods are concentrated peripherally; this is the opposite of the option above.
- "Rods only." — Reveals misconception: student denies cones. Correction: photoreceptors are _both_ rods and cones; cones drive colour and high-acuity vision in daylight.
- "Cones only." — Reveals misconception: student denies rods. Correction: rods provide the high-sensitivity, low-light vision missing in the cones-only model.

Explanation: Rods have high sensitivity, low resolution, and are monochromatic, concentrated in peripheral retina, predominating at low light [Guyton & Hall ch.51; Berne & Levy ch.7]. Cones have lower sensitivity but higher resolution and colour discrimination, concentrated in the fovea (which has only cones, no rods, and no overlying neural layers — providing maximum visual acuity). Three cone types exist: S-cones (~420 nm, blue), M-cones (~530 nm, green), and L-cones (~560 nm, red). Trichromatic colour vision arises from comparing cone outputs. Colour blindness typically reflects defects in M or L cone genes (X-linked, ~8% of men). Age-related macular degeneration preferentially destroys central cones, producing loss of central vision and reading and face-recognition difficulty.

Hints:

1. Two photoreceptor classes do completely different jobs.
2. The high-acuity colour cells live in the fovea.
3. Numerically, the dim-light receptors outnumber the colour receptors by ~20-fold.

QUESTION 4
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Phototransduction in retinal photoreceptors involves:

Correct answer: Light isomerises 11-cis-retinal to all-trans-retinal in opsin, activating the G-protein transducin → cGMP phosphodiesterase → cGMP hydrolysis → cGMP-gated cation (CNG) channels close → photoreceptor _hyperpolarises_ → reduced glutamate release onto bipolar cells.

Distractors:

- "Light directly opens cation channels." — Reveals misconception: student picks a direct-gating mechanism. Correction: photoreceptor channels are not directly gated by light; they are gated by cGMP, which falls when light activates the transducin–PDE cascade.
- "Light depolarises photoreceptors." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the direction of the light response. Correction: photoreceptors are unique in that they _hyperpolarise_ to light — channels close, the dark-current Na⁺ influx stops, and glutamate release decreases.
- "Light directly fires action potentials." — Reveals misconception: student gives photoreceptors action potentials. Correction: rods and cones use _graded_ potentials, not action potentials, to signal to bipolar cells; APs first appear at the ganglion cell.

Explanation: Photoreceptors hyperpolarise in response to light — opposite to most sensory receptors [Guyton & Hall ch.51; Boron & Boulpaep ch.15]. In darkness, cGMP keeps CNG channels open → Na⁺ influx ("dark current") → tonic depolarisation (~−40 mV) → continuous glutamate release. Light-activated opsin engages transducin (Gαt) → cGMP-PDE → cGMP falls → CNG channels close → membrane hyperpolarises → glutamate release decreases. Bipolar cells respond to this _decrease_ in glutamate (ON-bipolars depolarise via metabotropic mGluR6; OFF-bipolars hyperpolarise via ionotropic glutamate receptors), creating on-centre/off-centre receptive fields. The cascade provides massive amplification: one photon → one rhodopsin → ~500 transducin molecules → degradation of ~500,000 cGMP molecules → closure of many channels — the basis of single-photon detection by rods.

Hints:

1. Light closes channels rather than opening them.
2. The photoreceptor hyperpolarises and decreases glutamate release.
3. The G-protein in the cascade is named transducin.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The visual pathway from retina to primary visual cortex follows:

Correct answer: Retina → optic nerve → optic chiasm (nasal fibres decussate, temporal fibres remain ipsilateral) → optic tract → lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of thalamus → optic radiations → primary visual cortex (V1, calcarine sulcus, occipital lobe).

Distractors:

- "Retina → optic nerve → visual cortex directly." — Reveals misconception: student skips the chiasm and the LGN. Correction: visual information must pass through the optic chiasm (decussation) and the LGN (thalamic relay) before reaching V1.
- "Retina → cerebellum → cortex." — Reveals misconception: student inserts the cerebellum into a sensory pathway. Correction: the cerebellum is not a primary visual relay; visual signals reach cortex via the LGN.
- "Retina → midbrain → temporal lobe." — Reveals misconception: student misroutes the pathway. Correction: while some retinal fibres reach midbrain (superior colliculus, pretectal area for reflexes), conscious visual information goes via LGN to _occipital_ cortex.

Explanation: At the optic chiasm, nasal retinal fibres (carrying images from the temporal visual field) cross to the contralateral side; temporal retinal fibres (carrying nasal visual field) remain ipsilateral [Guyton & Hall ch.52; Berne & Levy ch.7]. After the chiasm, each optic tract carries information from the contralateral visual hemifield (right tract = left visual field). LGN has six layers — magnocellular (layers 1, 2; large fields, motion, low resolution), parvocellular (layers 3–6; colour, fine detail), and koniocellular (between layers; blue-yellow, blob projections). V1 is at the calcarine sulcus; layer IV receives LGN input. Hierarchical processing then proceeds through V2, V3, V4 (colour, shape), and V5/MT (motion), with the dorsal "where/how" stream to parietal lobe and the ventral "what" stream to temporal lobe (face/object recognition).

Hints:

1. The decussation happens at the optic chiasm.
2. The thalamic relay for vision is the LGN.
3. Primary visual cortex sits in the occipital lobe at the calcarine sulcus.

Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A patient with bitemporal hemianopia (loss of both temporal visual fields) most likely has a lesion at the:

Correct answer: Optic chiasm — commonly compressed by a pituitary macroadenoma from below or a craniopharyngioma from above, interrupting the decussating nasal retinal fibres that carry the temporal visual fields of both eyes.

Distractors:

- "Right optic tract." — Reveals misconception: student picks a post-chiasmal lesion. Correction: a right optic tract lesion produces _left homonymous_ hemianopia (same-side loss in both eyes), not a bitemporal pattern.
- "Right LGN." — Reveals misconception: student picks a thalamic relay lesion. Correction: an LGN lesion produces a contralateral homonymous hemianopia, not bitemporal hemianopia.
- "Right occipital cortex." — Reveals misconception: student picks the cortical end of the pathway. Correction: an occipital cortex lesion produces a contralateral homonymous hemianopia (often with macular sparing), not bitemporal hemianopia.

Explanation: Bitemporal hemianopia is anatomically diagnostic of an optic chiasm lesion [Guyton & Hall ch.52; Ganong]. Nasal retinal fibres (which decussate) carry images from the temporal visual field; chiasmal compression preferentially affects these. Causes include pituitary macroadenoma (most common — pressing upward), craniopharyngioma (more common in children, pressing downward), meningioma, and aneurysm. Pituitary tumours often present with bitemporal hemianopia plus hormonal effects (prolactinoma → galactorrhoea, amenorrhoea; growth hormone excess → acromegaly). MRI confirms; treatment is endoscopic transsphenoidal resection or, for prolactinomas, dopamine agonists (bromocriptine, cabergoline). Other lesion–defect pairs: anterior optic nerve → monocular blindness; optic tract → contralateral homonymous hemianopia; Meyer's loop (temporal) → "pie in the sky" upper quadrantanopia; parietal radiation → "pie on the floor"; occipital cortex → homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing.

Hints:

1. The defect is symmetric on both sides of the visual world.
2. The lesion must therefore be at the point where fibres from both eyes converge.
3. A common cause sits just below this point in the sella turcica.

QUESTION 7
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The visual field defect produced by a lesion of the right optic tract is:

Correct answer: Left homonymous hemianopia — loss of the left visual field of both eyes (left half of right eye's field plus left half of left eye's field, both carried in the right optic tract after chiasmal decussation).

Distractors:

- "Right monocular blindness." — Reveals misconception: student picks an anterior optic nerve lesion. Correction: monocular blindness is from a pre-chiasmal optic nerve lesion; the right optic tract lies post-chiasm and produces contralateral homonymous loss.
- "Bitemporal hemianopia." — Reveals misconception: student picks the chiasmal pattern. Correction: bitemporal hemianopia comes from chiasmal compression; tract lesions produce homonymous, not heteronymous, defects.
- "Left binasal hemianopia." — Reveals misconception: student picks an unusual pattern. Correction: binasal hemianopia is rare and arises from bilateral lesions affecting non-decussating temporal retinal fibres; right optic tract lesion gives left _homonymous_ hemianopia.

Explanation: After the optic chiasm, each optic tract carries information from the _contralateral_ visual hemifield [Guyton & Hall ch.52]. A right optic tract lesion → loss of the left visual field in both eyes (homonymous = same-side defects). Causes include stroke, tumour, demyelination, and trauma. Visual field testing localises lesions systematically: anterior optic nerve → monocular blindness; chiasm → bitemporal hemianopia; tract → contralateral homonymous (often _incongruous_); LGN/optic radiations → contralateral homonymous (more _congruous_ further posteriorly); Meyer's loop in temporal lobe → contralateral upper quadrantanopia ("pie in the sky"); parietal radiation → contralateral lower quadrantanopia ("pie on the floor"); occipital cortex → contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing (PCA stroke; macular cortex receives dual blood supply from MCA and PCA).

Hints:

1. The lesion is post-chiasmal.
2. Each post-chiasmal tract carries the contralateral hemifield.
3. The defect is identical in both eyes (homonymous).

QUESTION 8
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Aqueous humour is produced by the [blank] and drains predominantly via the [blank]:

Correct answer: Produced by the ciliary body epithelium (active Na⁺ secretion via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, with HCO₃⁻ and Cl⁻ following and water osmotically); drains via the trabecular meshwork → canal of Schlemm → episcleral veins (~85%, with the remainder via the uveoscleral route).

Distractors:

- "Retina; choroid." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong source and route. Correction: aqueous humour is produced by ciliary body epithelium and drains at the iridocorneal angle, not by the retina or choroid.
- "Cornea; vitreous." — Reveals misconception: student conflates aqueous and vitreous compartments. Correction: vitreous is a separate, mostly static compartment; aqueous humour is produced by ciliary body and drained via the angle, not into the vitreous.
- "Iris; pupil." — Reveals misconception: student uses iris/pupil terms loosely. Correction: the iris does not produce aqueous; the pupil is a passage between posterior and anterior chambers, not the drainage outflow.

Explanation: Aqueous humour is produced by the ciliary body's non-pigmented epithelium (~2.5 µL/min) [Guyton & Hall ch.50]. The fluid flows from posterior chamber → through the pupil → anterior chamber → drains predominantly via the trabecular meshwork (~85%) at the iridocorneal angle → canal of Schlemm → episcleral veins → systemic venous drainage; a smaller fraction takes the uveoscleral route. Glaucoma results from impaired drainage (most commonly), raising intraocular pressure (normal 10–21 mm Hg) and producing optic nerve damage and progressive visual field loss. Two main forms: open-angle (gradual, painless) and angle-closure (acute, painful, an ophthalmologic emergency). Treatments include prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost — increase uveoscleral outflow), β-blockers (timolol — reduce production), α₂ agonists (brimonidine), carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (acetazolamide), pilocarpine (miotic; opens angle in angle-closure), and surgical options (trabeculectomy, laser trabeculoplasty, peripheral iridotomy).

Hints:

1. The fluid is _secreted_, not filtered passively.
2. The drainage outflow lies at the angle between iris and cornea.
3. Glaucoma is the disease of impaired drainage.

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The fovea (the area of highest visual acuity) is characterised by:

Correct answer: An exclusively cone population (no rods), with overlying neural cell layers pushed peripherally, direct light access to the photoreceptors, and a 1:1:1 wiring ratio (each cone connects to its own bipolar and ganglion cell — no convergence) — together giving maximum spatial resolution.

Distractors:

- "Highest density of rods." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the fovea's photoreceptor composition. Correction: the fovea contains _only_ cones; rods are concentrated in the periphery.
- "Convergence of many photoreceptors onto a single ganglion cell." — Reveals misconception: student attributes peripheral wiring to the fovea. Correction: peripheral retina has high convergence (sacrificing resolution for sensitivity); the fovea has 1:1:1 wiring (sacrificing sensitivity for resolution).
- "Absence of pigment epithelium." — Reveals misconception: student denies the RPE under the fovea. Correction: the retinal pigment epithelium is present everywhere, including under the fovea — it is essential for rhodopsin recycling and photoreceptor support.

Explanation: The fovea (~1.5 mm diameter) sits at the centre of the macula [Berne & Levy ch.7; Guyton & Hall ch.51]. Adaptations for maximum acuity include cones only (highest density ~150,000/mm² in the foveal centre), no overlying neural layers (bipolar/ganglion cells are pushed laterally so light reaches photoreceptors directly), no convergence (each foveal cone has its own bipolar and ganglion cell), and an avascular foveolar zone (no retinal blood vessels in the central fovea — nutrition from underlying choriocapillaris). The trade-off is that foveal cones have low sensitivity (need photopic light) but maximum spatial discrimination; peripheral rods are sensitive enough to detect single photons but converge, sacrificing resolution for sensitivity — explaining why faint stars are best seen by looking just _off_ them. Age-related macular degeneration preferentially affects macula and fovea, producing central vision loss; wet AMD (neovascularisation) is treated with anti-VEGF agents (bevacizumab, ranibizumab, aflibercept).

Hints:

1. The fovea is wired for resolution, not sensitivity.
2. There are no rods there.
3. The neural layers are anatomically displaced so that light reaches photoreceptors directly.

QUESTION 10
Type: application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Dark adaptation (transition from photopic to scotopic vision when entering a dark room) involves:

Correct answer: Pupillary dilation (rapid, sympathetic, ~seconds), regeneration of bleached photopigments (slow — 20–30 min for rods, faster for cones), and neural changes (lateral inhibition adjustment, ON/OFF circuit reconfiguration); rhodopsin regeneration requires vitamin A (retinal precursor).

Distractors:

- "Pupillary dilation alone." — Reveals misconception: student narrows adaptation to a single mechanism. Correction: adaptation has rapid, slow, and neural components; pupil dilation is only the first.
- "Cone hypertrophy." — Reveals misconception: student invokes anatomic hypertrophy. Correction: cones do not hypertrophy with darkness; the slow component is photopigment regeneration in _rods_, not cone enlargement.
- "Pure neural adjustment." — Reveals misconception: student strips out pigment and pupil. Correction: dark adaptation requires both photochemical regeneration and pupillary/neural change; "pure neural" misses the dominant rhodopsin component.

Explanation: Dark adaptation has multiple components on different timescales [Guyton & Hall ch.51]. Pupil dilation (α₁-mediated; ~seconds) increases light gathering. Photopigment regeneration is slow — rhodopsin regenerates from the bleached state, requiring vitamin A as a retinal precursor; vitamin A deficiency produces night blindness (nyctalopia). The cone-rod transition occurs at low light when cones become non-functional and rods take over, marked by the "rod-cone break" in the dark adaptation curve (~5–10 min in). Neural reorganisation completes the picture (lateral inhibition adjusts; receptive field properties change). Light adaptation is much faster, dominated by rapid pupillary constriction and pigment bleaching reaching new steady state. Clinical relevance: night blindness from vitamin A deficiency, retinitis pigmentosa, and congenital rod dystrophies; cone dystrophies cause day blindness and photophobia.

Hints:

1. There are at least three different mechanisms with three different timescales.
2. The slowest (~20–30 min) is rhodopsin regeneration.
3. The dietary nutrient required is vitamin A.

QUESTION 11
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Colour vision is best explained by:

Correct answer: A combination of the trichromatic theory at the receptor level (three cone types — S, M, L) and the opponent-process theory at the retinal/cortical level (red–green and blue–yellow opponent channels created by lateral inhibition between cone types) — both correct at different processing stages.

Distractors:

- "A single pigment with multiple sensitivities." — Reveals misconception: student denies cone diversity. Correction: there are three distinct cone opsins (S, M, L); a single pigment cannot mediate trichromatic colour vision.
- "Wavelength-specific neurons in the retina only." — Reveals misconception: student strips out cortical processing. Correction: colour processing extends beyond retina — V4 in cortex is critical, and cerebral achromatopsia from V4 lesions proves the cortical contribution.
- "Random firing patterns." — Reveals misconception: student denies any organised mechanism. Correction: colour vision is highly organised, encoded by cone-specific opsins and downstream opponent channels.

Explanation: Two complementary theories explain colour vision at different levels [Guyton & Hall ch.51; Berne & Levy ch.7]. The trichromatic theory (Young–Helmholtz) — three cone types (S ~420 nm, M ~530 nm, L ~560 nm) with overlapping but distinct spectral sensitivities; any colour can be encoded by their relative responses; verified by molecular cloning of opsin genes. The opponent-process theory (Hering) — retinal ganglion cells and LGN neurons show opponent responses (red-green and blue-yellow opponent channels, plus a luminance channel), created by lateral inhibition; explains afterimages and why we never see "reddish-green" or "bluish-yellow". Colour blindness includes protanopia (L cone defect, "red-blind"), deuteranopia (M cone defect, most common — Daltonism), tritanopia (S cone defect, rare), and achromatopsia (no functional cones). L and M cone genes are X-linked, so colour blindness affects ~8% of men. V4 lesions cause cerebral achromatopsia — patients can detect colours but cannot identify them.

Hints:

1. There are three pigments at the receptor level.
2. There are opponent channels at the post-receptor level.
3. Both theories are correct at their respective stages.

Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Glaucoma — the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide — most often presents as:

Correct answer: Open-angle glaucoma — gradual, painless, progressive optic nerve damage from raised intraocular pressure (>21 mm Hg) due to impaired aqueous outflow at the trabecular meshwork; visual field loss begins peripherally (nasal step, then arcuate scotoma) and progresses centrally; often asymptomatic until late.

Distractors:

- "Sudden severe pain only." — Reveals misconception: student picks angle-closure as the typical presentation. Correction: open-angle glaucoma is the most common form (~90%) and is characteristically painless and gradual; angle-closure is acute and painful but much less common.
- "Sudden blindness with no warning." — Reveals misconception: student treats glaucoma as a flash event. Correction: glaucoma typically erodes vision over years; sudden blindness suggests other causes (retinal artery occlusion, retinal detachment, optic neuritis).
- "Recovery without treatment." — Reveals misconception: student treats glaucoma as self-limiting. Correction: glaucomatous optic neuropathy is irreversible; without treatment, visual field loss progresses, and IOP control is the only modifiable factor.

Explanation: Open-angle glaucoma accounts for ~90% of glaucoma cases and is painless and asymptomatic until late, with progressive peripheral-to-central visual field loss and optic disc cupping on fundoscopy [Guyton & Hall ch.50]. Treatment lowers IOP — first-line prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost), then β-blockers, α₂ agonists, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, and pilocarpine; laser trabeculoplasty and surgical trabeculectomy for resistant cases. Risk factors include age, family history, African ancestry, high myopia, diabetes, and steroid use. Angle-closure glaucoma is acute and painful (severe pain, halos, nausea, mid-dilated unreactive pupil), an ophthalmologic emergency, treated with immediate IOP-lowering plus laser peripheral iridotomy. Normal-tension glaucoma shows optic nerve damage with normal IOP — vascular factors are implicated; treatment still aims at further IOP reduction. Glaucoma is the "silent thief of sight" — early detection through screening (especially after age 40) is critical.

Hints:

1. The most common form is painless and progressive.
2. Visual field loss starts peripherally, often unnoticed.
3. Lowering intraocular pressure is the only modifiable factor.

## Pass 2 — Hearing and the vestibular system

QUESTION 13
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The principal mechanism by which the middle ear ossicles match impedance between low-impedance air and high-impedance cochlear fluid is:

Correct answer: The area ratio between tympanic membrane (~55 mm²) and oval window (~3.2 mm²) — about 17:1 — concentrates force on a smaller area, plus the lever advantage of malleus–incus (~1.3:1), achieving ~22-fold amplification (~26 dB) — roughly matching the ~3000-fold air–fluid impedance mismatch.

Distractors:

- "The ossicles amplify the signal electronically." — Reveals misconception: student invokes electronic amplification. Correction: the middle ear amplifies _mechanically_ via area ratio and lever advantage; there is no electronic amplification in the ossicular chain.
- "The ossicles produce sound directly." — Reveals misconception: student treats the ossicles as sound generators. Correction: ossicles transmit, not generate, sound; impedance matching is achieved by mechanical leverage and area concentration.
- "Bone conduction alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks an alternative pathway as the primary mechanism. Correction: bone conduction bypasses the middle ear entirely and is used in special hearing tests (Rinne, Weber); normal sound transmission uses ossicular impedance matching.

Explanation: Without impedance matching, ~99.9% of sound energy would be reflected at the air–fluid interface [Guyton & Hall ch.53; Berne & Levy ch.7]. The middle ear achieves matching through the area ratio (TM ~17× larger than oval window — pressure increased 17×) and lever advantage of the malleus and incus (~1.3×), giving ~22× total amplification (~26 dB). The stapedius muscle (CN VII) and tensor tympani (CN V) reflexively dampen the ossicular chain in response to loud sounds — protecting the cochlea (acoustic reflex) and reducing self-perception of one's voice during speech; reflex latency is ~50–100 ms, too slow for sudden impulse noises (gunshots, explosions). Conductive hearing loss arises from outer or middle ear lesions (cerumen, otitis media, otosclerosis, ossicular discontinuity) and is treated with hearing aids or surgery; sensorineural hearing loss arises from cochlea or auditory nerve disease (presbycusis, noise, ototoxicity, congenital). Rinne and Weber tests distinguish: Rinne — air > bone normally; if BC > AC, conductive loss. Weber — lateralises to the affected ear in conductive loss and away from the affected ear in sensorineural loss.

Hints:

1. The principle is purely mechanical, not electrical.
2. Pressure rises when force is applied to a smaller area.
3. The ossicles also act as a lever.

Stem: The cochlear basilar membrane displays tonotopic organisation:

Correct answer: High frequencies at the base (narrow, stiff portion near the oval window), low frequencies at the apex (wide, compliant portion) — sound waves of different frequencies produce maximal displacement at characteristic locations along the membrane (von Békésy's travelling wave; place principle of frequency coding).

Distractors:

- "High frequencies at apex, low at base." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the gradient. Correction: high frequencies peak at the _base_ (narrow, stiff); low frequencies peak at the _apex_ (wide, compliant).
- "All frequencies activate the entire membrane equally." — Reveals misconception: student denies tonotopic organisation. Correction: the basilar membrane has graded mechanical properties so different frequencies peak at different locations — the basis of place coding.
- "Frequency is encoded only by hair cell firing rate." — Reveals misconception: student substitutes pure rate coding for place coding. Correction: while rate (phase-locking) coding contributes at low frequencies, the dominant mechanism is place coding along the tonotopic basilar membrane.

Explanation: von Békésy's travelling-wave demonstration (Nobel 1961) showed that the basilar membrane has graded mechanical properties — narrow and stiff at the base, wide and compliant at the apex [Guyton & Hall ch.53; Boron & Boulpaep ch.15]. Sound entering the oval window produces a travelling wave that increases in amplitude and reaches maximum displacement at a frequency-specific location: high frequencies (~20,000 Hz) at the base, low frequencies (~20 Hz) at the apex, speech frequencies (300–3000 Hz) in the middle. This place principle is preserved throughout the auditory pathway — tonotopic maps in cochlear nuclei, superior olive, inferior colliculus, MGN, and primary auditory cortex (Heschl's gyrus). Inner hair cells (~3,500 in a single row) are the primary sensors — ~90% of afferent CN VIII fibres synapse on IHCs. Outer hair cells (~12,000 in three rows) act as active amplifiers, contracting and elongating with sound via prestin to sharpen tuning. Mechanoelectrical transduction: stereocilia deflection → tip-link opening of mechanically gated cation channels → K⁺ entry from endolymph → depolarisation → glutamate release. Endolymph (high K⁺ ~150 mM) is unusual in being intracellular-like and is produced by the stria vascularis; the +80 mV endocochlear potential drives K⁺ entry into hair cells.

Hints:

1. The base of the cochlea is stiff; the apex is flexible.
2. Higher frequencies vibrate the stiffer end best.
3. The place of maximal vibration codes for frequency.

Stem: The vestibular apparatus comprises:

Correct answer: Three semicircular canals (anterior, posterior, lateral) — each with an ampulla and crista, sensing angular acceleration in three orthogonal planes — plus the utricle (sensing horizontal linear acceleration and head tilt) and saccule (sensing vertical linear acceleration); hair cells use stereocilia plus a single kinocilium, and signal via CN VIII to vestibular nuclei.

- "The middle ear." — Reveals misconception: student locates the vestibular system in the middle ear. Correction: the vestibular apparatus is in the inner ear (bony and membranous labyrinth), not in the middle ear (which contains the ossicles).
- "The auditory cortex." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cortical structure as the apparatus. Correction: the apparatus is peripheral (inner ear); cortical processing of vestibular input occurs in parietoinsular vestibular cortex.

Head rotation makes endolymph lag behind (inertia), bending the cupula and deflecting hair cell stereocilia. Push-pull operation between paired canals (e.g., right horizontal canal activated by leftward head turn while the left horizontal is inhibited) sharpens directional sensitivity. **Otolith organs** sense linear acceleration and head tilt: the utricle (macula in horizontal plane) senses horizontal linear acceleration and head tilt around the longitudinal axis; the saccule (macula in vertical plane) senses vertical linear acceleration. Hair cell stereocilia are embedded in a gelatinous mass containing calcium-carbonate otoconia that lag with acceleration. Vestibular afferents project to vestibular nuclei (medulla/pons), then to spinal cord (vestibulospinal tract for posture), cerebellum (vestibulocerebellum for balance), CN III/IV/VI nuclei (vestibulo-ocular reflex — among the fastest reflexes, ~10 ms latency), and parietoinsular vestibular cortex.

Hints:

1. Two kinds of acceleration must be sensed: angular and linear.

Stem: Visual processing in V1 (primary visual cortex) is organised in:

Correct answer: Cortical columns — ocular dominance columns (alternating bands favouring left vs right eye), orientation columns (sensitive to specific bar/edge orientations), and "blobs" (cytochrome-oxidase-rich regions for colour processing); the hypercolumn contains all orientations and both eye preferences for one retinal location (Hubel and Wiesel, Nobel 1981).

- "Random arrangement." — Reveals misconception: student denies cortical organisation. Correction: V1 is one of the most precisely organised cortical areas, with columnar architecture revealed by Hubel and Wiesel's classic experiments.
- "Single neurons for each pixel." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a strict pixel-to-neuron mapping. Correction: V1 cells respond to oriented edges and bars, not to single pixels; receptive fields integrate input from many photoreceptors.
- "Holographic representation." — Reveals misconception: student picks a metaphor with no neuroanatomical basis. Correction: V1 has discrete columnar organisation, not holographic distribution.

Explanation: V1 (striate cortex, area 17, calcarine sulcus) shows elegant columnar organisation [Berne & Levy ch.7; Boron & Boulpaep ch.16]. Two streams: dorsal "where/how" (V1 → V5 → posterior parietal) and ventral "what" (V1 → V2 → V4 → inferior temporal — face recognition, prosopagnosia from FFA lesion). 1. Hubel and Wiesel's recordings revealed orderly columnar organisation.
2. Three kinds of columns: ocular dominance, orientation, and blobs.
3. A "hypercolumn" packs all of them for one piece of retina.

Stem: Optic neuritis, often heralding multiple sclerosis, characteristically presents with:

- "Painless gradual bilateral visual loss." — Reveals misconception: student picks a chronic bilateral pattern. Correction: optic neuritis is typically _acute_, _unilateral_, and _painful_ with eye movement; bilateral painless gradual loss suggests other causes (toxic, nutritional, hereditary).
- "Sudden complete blindness without pain." — Reveals misconception: student picks an arterial-occlusion pattern. Correction: optic neuritis evolves over hours to days with pain on eye movement; sudden painless blindness suggests central retinal artery occlusion or anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy.
- "Halos around lights with red painful eye." — Reveals misconception: student picks angle-closure glaucoma's pattern. Correction: angle-closure presents with halos, severe eye pain, and a red eye; optic neuritis presents with vision loss, pain on eye movement, and colour desaturation, without a red eye.

Correct answer: Loss of stapedius function on the affected side — CN VII (facial nerve) innervates stapedius, and its paresis allows unattenuated ossicular vibration, producing hyperacusis (sounds seem too loud); an absent acoustic reflex on impedance audiometry corroborates the lesion of CN VII.

- "Damage to the cochlea on the affected side." — Reveals misconception: student picks a sensorineural aetiology. Correction: cochlear damage causes _hearing loss_, not hyperacusis; Bell's palsy spares the cochlea but interrupts the stapedial reflex efferent.

Explanation: Bell's palsy is an acute idiopathic peripheral CN VII palsy with characteristic features: rapid onset (hours to a day), unilateral lower-motor-neuron facial weakness involving both the upper face (frontalis weakness with inability to wrinkle forehead) and lower face, often preceded by ear/postauricular pain, sometimes with altered taste on the anterior 2/3 of the tongue (chorda tympani lesion) and hyperacusis (stapedius paresis) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15; Ganong]. Stem: Maintaining balance integrates inputs from:

Correct answer: The vestibular apparatus (semicircular canals + otolith organs), the visual system (optic flow), and proprioception (joint and muscle afferents) — combined in the cerebellum, brainstem, and parietal cortex; loss of any one input can usually be compensated by the other two over weeks.

Hints:

1. Balance integrates more than one modality.
2. Each modality can compensate for the others to some extent.

Stem: Motion sickness arises from:

Correct answer: A mismatch between vestibular and visual inputs — for example, reading in a moving car (vestibular system signals motion, vision signals a stationary book) — leading to nausea, sweating, pallor, and vomiting; treated with anti-muscarinic (scopolamine), antihistaminic (meclizine, cyclizine), and antiemetic (ondansetron) agents.

- "A peripheral vestibular lesion." — Reveals misconception: student picks a structural lesion. Correction: motion sickness occurs in healthy people exposed to mismatched inputs; it does not require a vestibular lesion.
- "A purely psychogenic disorder." — Reveals misconception: student treats motion sickness as psychological. Correction: motion sickness has clear neurophysiological mechanisms (vestibular–visual mismatch), even though anxiety can amplify it.
- "Excess endolymph (hydrops)." — Reveals misconception: student picks the Ménière mechanism. Correction: endolymphatic hydrops is the proposed mechanism of Ménière disease; motion sickness is sensory mismatch, not hydrops.

Explanation: Motion sickness arises from a mismatch between vestibular and visual inputs (and sometimes proprioceptive) [Guyton & Hall ch.55; Ganong]. The classic example is reading in a moving car — the vestibular system reports acceleration while vision reports a stationary book; in seasickness, the cabin appears stable while the body senses the ship's motion; in flight simulators or VR, the eye reports motion while the vestibular system does not. The brain interprets this mismatch as a sign of toxin exposure (an evolutionary hypothesis — many neurotoxins disturb sensorimotor coordination, so vomiting is protective), generating nausea, sweating, pallor, and vomiting via vestibular projections to the area postrema and brainstem emetic centres. Treatment: anti-muscarinic agents such as scopolamine patches are first-line for prevention; antihistamines (meclizine, cyclizine, dimenhydrinate) are useful and have a sedating effect; antiemetics such as ondansetron treat established symptoms; behavioural strategies include staring at the horizon, reclining, fresh air, and avoiding reading. Adaptation occurs with repeated exposure (sailors gain "sea legs"; astronauts adapt to microgravity over days).

Hints:

1. Two sensory systems disagree about whether motion is happening.
2. The brain interprets the disagreement as if it were toxin exposure.
3. Anti-muscarinic and antihistamine drugs are the mainstays of prevention.
