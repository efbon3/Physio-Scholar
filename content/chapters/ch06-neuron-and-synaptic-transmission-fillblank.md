# Chapter 6 — The Neuron and Synaptic Transmission — Fill-in-the-Blank Questions

---

QUESTION 1

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The synaptic delay at a typical chemical synapse — the interval between the arrival of the presynaptic action potential and the onset of the postsynaptic potential — is approximately **\_\_** ms.

Canonical answer: 0.5

Explanation: Synaptic delay reflects Ca²⁺ entry, vesicle fusion (~0.2 ms alone), transmitter diffusion across the cleft, receptor binding, and channel opening (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 2

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The synaptic cleft at a typical chemical synapse is about **\_\_** nm wide.

Canonical answer: 20

Explanation: Berne & Levy quote ~20 nm; Guyton & Hall give 200–300 Å (i.e., 20–30 nm). The cleft is wide enough to ensure cytoplasmic separation but narrow enough that diffusion across it is essentially instantaneous (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 3

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: At an electrical synapse, the apposed plasma membranes are separated by an intercellular gap of only about **\_\_** nm.

Canonical answer: 3 (≈3.5)

Explanation: Gap junctions narrow the intercellular space to ~3–3.5 nm, in striking contrast to the ~20 nm of a chemical synaptic cleft (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — gap junction figure).

---

QUESTION 4

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The pore diameter of a gap junction channel is approximately **\_\_** nm, large enough to permit passage of ions and small molecules up to ~1 kDa.

Canonical answer: 1–2

Explanation: Gap junction channels have a pore of 1–2 nm; their permeability to second messengers and metabolites (cAMP, IP₃, ATP) — not just ions — distinguishes them from any voltage- or ligand-gated channel (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 5

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Each connexon (gap junction hemichannel) is built from **\_\_** connexin subunits.

Canonical answer: 6 (a hexamer)

Explanation: Two connexons (one from each cell), each a hexamer of connexin protein, form one channel. Connexin 36 is the major neuronal connexin in the adult mammalian CNS (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — At the Cellular Level; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 6

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Each ACh-containing synaptic vesicle at the neuromuscular junction holds approximately **\_\_** molecules of acetylcholine.

Canonical answer: 2,000–10,000

Explanation: Guyton & Hall quote 2,000–10,000 ACh molecules per vesicle. The presynaptic terminal contains enough vesicles to support a few hundred to >10,000 action potentials before fatigue (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 7

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A single miniature end-plate potential (MEPP) at the human motor end-plate produces a depolarisation of approximately **\_\_** mV.

Canonical answer: 0.4

Explanation: Costanzo gives ~0.4 mV per MEPP (Berne & Levy classically quote ≤1 mV at the frog NMJ). The full EPP is an integer multiple of this quantum (Costanzo, 7e — NMJ; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 8

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The minimal EPSP that depolarises the axon initial segment to threshold for spike initiation is approximately **\_\_** mV above the resting potential.

Canonical answer: 10–20

Explanation: Because of its high density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (~7× the soma), the initial segment can be triggered by an EPSP of only +10 to +20 mV; the soma itself would need +30–40 mV (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 9

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A typical fast EPSP at a central neuron lasts about **\_\_** ms before decaying to baseline, even though the underlying EPSC lasts only 1–2 ms.

Canonical answer: 15

Explanation: Channel kinetics (the EPSC) determine the rising phase; the long decay reflects the postsynaptic membrane's RC time constant. This long tail enables temporal summation (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 10

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A single excitatory presynaptic terminal on a spinal motor neuron produces an EPSP of only about **\_\_** mV at the soma.

Canonical answer: 0.5–1.0

Explanation: Guyton & Hall quote 0.5–1.0 mV per terminal; reaching the ~10–20 mV needed at the initial segment therefore requires simultaneous (spatial) summation of many synapses — about 40–80 in a typical anterior motor neuron (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 11

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A spinal motor neuron carries roughly **\_\_** presynaptic terminals on its surface.

Canonical answer: 10,000–200,000

Explanation: Guyton & Hall's classic figure: 10,000 to 200,000 presynaptic terminals on the soma and dendrites of an anterior horn motor neuron, with 80–95% of them on dendrites (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 12

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The resting membrane potential of a typical spinal motor neuronal soma is approximately **\_\_** mV.

Canonical answer: −65

Explanation: Less negative than skeletal muscle (−90 mV) — this matters because it lies between the threshold for spiking and the Nernst potential for K⁺/Cl⁻, allowing both excitatory and inhibitory regulation (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 13

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Brief loss of cerebral blood flow produces unconsciousness within about **\_\_** seconds.

Canonical answer: 3–7

Explanation: Synaptic transmission depends critically on oxidative metabolism. Even brief interruption of cerebral blood flow rapidly depresses neuronal excitability — the basis of vasovagal syncope and immediate post-cardiac-arrest unconsciousness (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46 — Hypoxia).

---

QUESTION 14

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Maximal paired-pulse facilitation at most chemical synapses is observed when the two stimuli are separated by an interstimulus interval of about **\_\_** ms.

Canonical answer: 20

Explanation: Maximal facilitation peaks around 20 ms, then declines; with intervals of several hundred milliseconds the two responses are equal in size (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Paired-Pulse Facilitation).

---

QUESTION 15

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Excitatory neurotransmitter stores in many central synapses can sustain only about **\_\_** action potentials of rapid stimulation before fatigue from depletion of releasable vesicles.

Canonical answer: 10,000

Explanation: Guyton & Hall give 10,000 as a representative figure. Fatigue is a normal protective mechanism (e.g., terminating epileptic seizures) and contributes to short-term synaptic depression (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46 — Fatigue).

---

QUESTION 16

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The dendrites of an anterior horn motor neuron extend up to about **\_\_** μm from the soma in all directions.

Canonical answer: 500–1000

Explanation: This expanded surface area provides a vast spatial domain for synaptic input, and explains why most presynaptic terminals (80–95%) contact dendrites rather than the soma (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 17

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The proteins that physically zipper synaptic vesicle and plasma membranes together to drive fusion are collectively called the **\_\_** complex.

Canonical answer: SNARE

Explanation: SNARE = soluble N-ethylmaleimide-sensitive factor attachment protein receptor. The minimal complex is synaptobrevin (v-SNARE on the vesicle) plus syntaxin and SNAP-25 (t-SNAREs on the plasma membrane) (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Molecular Apparatus).

---

QUESTION 18

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The vesicle-membrane protein on which botulinum and tetanus toxins act to abolish neurotransmitter release is **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: synaptobrevin (also called VAMP)

Explanation: Synaptobrevin/VAMP is the vesicular SNARE; it is cleaved by tetanus toxin and by several botulinum toxin serotypes (B, D, F, G). BoNT/A and /E cleave SNAP-25 instead (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

---

QUESTION 19

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The presynaptic Ca²⁺ sensor whose binding triggers fast vesicle fusion is named **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: synaptotagmin

Explanation: Synaptotagmin's two cytoplasmic C2 domains bind Ca²⁺ and trigger the final fusion step. Different synaptotagmin isoforms with different Ca²⁺ kinetics tune release properties at different synapses (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 20

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The smallest unit of neurotransmitter release — corresponding to the contents of one synaptic vesicle — is called a **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: quantum

Explanation: The quantal hypothesis (Katz) states that transmitter is released in discrete packets equal to the contents of single vesicles. The amplitudes of evoked responses are integer multiples of the miniature (single-quantum) response (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Costanzo, 7e — NMJ).

---

QUESTION 21

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The local depolarisation of the postsynaptic membrane produced by an excitatory transmitter is called the **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: EPSP (excitatory postsynaptic potential)

Explanation: EPSPs depolarise the cell and increase its probability of firing. At the neuromuscular junction the equivalent depolarisation is termed the end-plate potential (EPP) (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 22

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A change in membrane potential produced by a transmitter that decreases the probability of postsynaptic firing — usually by hyperpolarising the membrane — is called the **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: IPSP (inhibitory postsynaptic potential)

Explanation: IPSPs typically open Cl⁻ or K⁺ channels. Even when ECl equals Vrest, the IPSP can still inhibit by shunting subsequent EPSCs (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 23

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A presynaptic receptor activated by the cell's own released neurotransmitter is called an **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: autoreceptor

Explanation: Autoreceptors typically inhibit further release (negative feedback) — e.g., 5-HT₁A receptors on raphe neurons, α₂-adrenoceptors on noradrenergic terminals (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Presynaptic Receptors).

---

QUESTION 24

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The reduction in transmitter release produced by activation of presynaptic receptors that decrease invasion of the action potential into the active zone is termed presynaptic **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: inhibition

Explanation: Presynaptic inhibition is mediated by axoaxonic synapses. Mechanisms include shunting, partial Na⁺-channel inactivation by depolarisation, and direct G-protein coupled modulation of presynaptic Ca²⁺/K⁺ channels (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 25

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The simultaneous summation at the axon hillock of EPSPs generated by different synapses active at the same time is termed **\_\_** summation.

Canonical answer: spatial

Explanation: Distinct from temporal summation, which is the addition of successive EPSPs from the same synapse before each has decayed (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 26

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The ratio of evoked EPSP amplitude to the depolarisation needed to fire an action potential is termed the **\_\_** of a synapse.

Canonical answer: safety factor

Explanation: The neuromuscular junction has a high safety factor (>1) and normally always fires the muscle fibre; most CNS synapses have low safety factors (<1) and rely on summation. Myasthenia gravis lowers the NMJ safety factor toward 1 (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 27

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The receptor type that is itself an ion channel and produces fast (millisecond-scale) postsynaptic responses is called **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: ionotropic

Explanation: Ionotropic receptors include the nicotinic ACh receptor, GABA-A, glycine, AMPA, NMDA, kainate, and 5-HT₃ receptors. They are typically pentameric or tetrameric ligand-gated channels (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

---

QUESTION 28

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The receptor type that is coupled to a G protein and activates intracellular second-messenger cascades — producing slower, longer-lasting effects — is called **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: metabotropic

Explanation: Examples include muscarinic ACh, GABA-B, mGluRs, dopamine, adrenergic, and most peptide receptors (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Receptors).

---

QUESTION 29

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A transient increase in postsynaptic response after a brief train of high-frequency presynaptic stimulation, lasting tens of seconds to several minutes, is called **\_\_** potentiation.

Canonical answer: posttetanic (PTP)

Explanation: PTP is presynaptic, reflecting residual Ca²⁺ in the terminal. It is distinct from long-term potentiation (LTP), which lasts hours to days and involves both pre- and postsynaptic changes (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 30

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The persistent (hours to days) activity-dependent strengthening of synapses thought to underlie certain forms of learning and memory is called **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: long-term potentiation (LTP)

Explanation: LTP at hippocampal CA3-CA1 synapses is NMDA-receptor-dependent and Ca²⁺-CaMKII-driven, with both presynaptic (release) and postsynaptic (AMPA receptor insertion) components (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 31

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The soma and proximal dendrites of a typical CNS neuron are connected to the axon by a specialised low-threshold region called the axon **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: initial segment (axon hillock and adjacent first node region)

Explanation: The axon initial segment (AIS) has the highest density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (~7× the somal density) and is therefore the spike trigger zone for the cell (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 32

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Acetylcholine is synthesised in the cholinergic terminal from acetyl-CoA and choline by the enzyme **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: choline acetyltransferase (ChAT)

Explanation: ChAT in the cytoplasm of cholinergic terminals catalyses ACh synthesis; ACh is concentrated into vesicles by the vesicular acetylcholine transporter (VAChT). After release, acetylcholinesterase in the cleft hydrolyses ACh to acetate and choline; choline is recovered by an Na⁺-coupled high-affinity choline transporter (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Costanzo, 7e — NMJ).

---

QUESTION 33

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The amount of neurotransmitter released from a presynaptic terminal is steeply dependent on the entry of **\_\_** ions through voltage-gated channels in the active zone.

Canonical answer: Ca²⁺ (calcium)

Explanation: Release is roughly proportional to [Ca²⁺]ᵢ raised to the fourth power, reflecting cooperative binding to synaptotagmin. Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome — autoantibodies against presynaptic P/Q-type voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels — produces failure of release on this basis (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 34

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Glutamate released into the cleft is taken up mainly by **\_\_** cells and converted to glutamine before being shuttled back to the neuron.

Canonical answer: astrocytes (glia)

Explanation: EAAT1/GLAST and EAAT2/GLT-1 on astrocytes are the dominant glutamate transporters; astrocytic glutamine synthase converts glutamate to glutamine, which is exported back to neurons for reuse — the glutamate-glutamine cycle (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Glutamate Transport Cycle).

---

QUESTION 35

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The choline released by cleft hydrolysis of acetylcholine is recovered into the presynaptic terminal by an Na⁺-coupled **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: high-affinity choline transporter (CHT1)

Explanation: Hemicholinium-3 blocks this transporter and depletes presynaptic ACh stores — a classic experimental tool. Cleft acetylcholinesterase hydrolyses ACh to acetate and choline; the rate-limiting step in resynthesis is choline uptake (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Costanzo, 7e — NMJ).

---

QUESTION 36

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The effect of opening Cl⁻ channels in a mature CNS neuron is normally an inhibitory hyperpolarisation because the Nernst potential for chloride is more **\_\_** than the resting membrane potential.

Canonical answer: negative

Explanation: ECl ≈ −70 mV; Vrest ≈ −65 mV. Cl⁻ flows in, hyperpolarising the cell. In immature neurons with high intracellular Cl⁻, ECl is positive to Vrest and GABA-A activation is depolarising — explaining why GABA acts as an excitatory transmitter early in development (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 37

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The NMDA channel is blocked at resting potentials by extracellular **\_\_**, and this block is relieved only when the postsynaptic membrane is depolarised.

Canonical answer: Mg²⁺ (magnesium)

Explanation: This voltage-dependent Mg²⁺ block makes the NMDA receptor a coincidence detector — it opens only when glutamate is bound AND the membrane is depolarised (typically by AMPA-mediated current). Ca²⁺ entry through NMDA receptors is the proximal trigger for LTP induction (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

---

QUESTION 38

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Botulinum toxin and tetanus toxin both inhibit synaptic transmission by cleaving **\_\_** proteins of the vesicle-fusion machinery.

Canonical answer: SNARE

Explanation: Both are zinc-dependent metalloproteases. BoNT acts at peripheral cholinergic terminals → flaccid paralysis; TeNT is taken up at the NMJ but transported retrogradely to spinal inhibitory interneurons, where it abolishes glycine/GABA release → spastic paralysis (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

---

QUESTION 39

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The mechanism of action of a nondepolarising muscle relaxant such as d-tubocurarine is competitive antagonism at the postsynaptic **\_\_** receptor at the motor end-plate.

Canonical answer: nicotinic acetylcholine

Explanation: Curare reduces EPP amplitude by occupying ACh-binding sites without opening the channel. α-Bungarotoxin binds the same receptor irreversibly and is used experimentally to label it. Anticholinesterases reverse curare-induced block by raising cleft ACh (Costanzo, 7e — NMJ; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

---

QUESTION 40

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The convulsant strychnine produces unopposed motor neuron excitation in the spinal cord by competitively blocking the postsynaptic **\_\_** receptor.

Canonical answer: glycine

Explanation: Glycine is the principal inhibitory transmitter in the spinal cord and brainstem, acting on a Cl⁻-permeable ionotropic receptor. Loss of glycinergic inhibition leaves motor neurons in unopposed excitatory drive, producing tonic spasms (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 41

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The therapeutic effect of pyridostigmine in myasthenia gravis is to inhibit cleft **\_\_**, thereby prolonging ACh action at the partially depleted postsynaptic receptor pool.

Canonical answer: acetylcholinesterase

Explanation: With fewer functional postsynaptic AChRs (autoantibody-mediated), the safety factor is reduced. Slowing ACh hydrolysis raises cleft ACh and lengthens its dwell time, restoring suprathreshold EPPs at the residual receptors (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — In the Clinic; Costanzo, 7e — Box 1.5).

---

QUESTION 42

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Benzodiazepines such as diazepam exert their anxiolytic, sedative, and anticonvulsant effects by binding an allosteric site on the **\_\_** receptor and increasing the frequency of Cl⁻-channel opening in the presence of GABA.

Canonical answer: GABA-A

Explanation: Benzodiazepines are positive allosteric modulators (not direct agonists) — they increase channel-opening frequency. Barbiturates bind a different site and prolong channel-open time. Both drug classes amplify GABAergic inhibition and depress neuronal excitability (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13; Ganong, 26e).

---

QUESTION 43

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Selective serotonin-reuptake inhibitors block the **\_\_**, raising synaptic 5-HT, but their full antidepressant effect requires weeks because of slow desensitisation of inhibitory presynaptic 5-HT₁A autoreceptors.

Canonical answer: serotonin transporter (SERT, SLC6A4)

Explanation: SSRIs raise extracellular 5-HT within hours, but the clinical benefit takes 2–6 weeks — paralleling autoreceptor desensitisation, postsynaptic receptor adaptation, BDNF up-regulation, and downstream gene-expression changes (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13; Ganong, 26e).

QUESTION 44

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The major source of widely projecting noradrenergic axons in the brain is the small brainstem nucleus called the **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: locus coeruleus

Explanation: Located in the dorsolateral pons; its diffuse projections regulate arousal, attention, and mood. Similar widely projecting modulatory systems include raphe nuclei (serotonin), substantia nigra/VTA (dopamine), and basal forebrain nuclei (acetylcholine) (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

---

QUESTION 45

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Dopaminergic neurons whose loss produces the motor features of Parkinson's disease project from the substantia nigra (pars compacta) to the **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: striatum (caudate and putamen)

Explanation: The nigrostriatal projection is the system whose degeneration underlies the bradykinesia, rigidity, and tremor of Parkinson's disease. Loss of dopaminergic input shifts the basal ganglia output toward the indirect pathway, causing excessive thalamo-cortical inhibition (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).
