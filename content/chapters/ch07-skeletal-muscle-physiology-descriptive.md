## Pass 1 — Comprehension and reasoning

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Trace the sequence of events from arrival of an action potential at the neuromuscular junction to mechanical shortening of a skeletal-muscle sarcomere.

Model answer: A complete answer organises the chain into three discrete domains, each linked to the next. Domain A — Excitation: ACh release at the NMJ → binding to nicotinic receptors at the end-plate → cation flux → end-plate potential → suprathreshold depolarisation → propagated muscle action potential along the sarcolemma and into T tubules. Domain B — Coupling: T-tubule depolarisation → conformational change in the DHPR (skeletal Cav1.1) — the voltage sensor — → mechanical opening of the apposed RyR1 in the SR terminal cisternae → Ca²⁺ flow from SR lumen (where it is stored bound to calsequestrin) into the cytosol, raising cytosolic [Ca²⁺] from ~10⁻⁷ M (resting) to ~10⁻⁵ M. Domain C — Contraction: Ca²⁺ binds troponin C → conformational change displaces tropomyosin off myosin-binding sites on actin → cross-bridge cycle begins (ATP-bound myosin head detaches; ATP hydrolysed to ADP·P_i re-cocks the head; head binds new actin site; P_i release triggers ~10-nm power stroke; ADP release returns to rigor). Sarcomere shortens (A band constant, H and I bands shorten) → muscle generates force/shortens. Termination/relaxation: SR repolarises; SERCA hydrolyses ATP to pump 2 Ca²⁺ back into the SR per cycle; cytosolic Ca²⁺ falls; troponin C releases Ca²⁺; tropomyosin re-blocks actin; cross-bridges cycle off. ATP is required at three points: cross-bridge cycling, SERCA-mediated Ca²⁺ reuptake, and Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase restoration of ionic gradients [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Guyton & Hall ch.6–7].

Elaborative explanation: The chain has feed-forward logic — once the AP reaches the T tubule, all downstream steps are automatic provided ATP is available. This is why ATP depletion (rigor mortis) freezes the system in the bound state, and why diseases at any single step (NMJ in myasthenia, RyR1 in MH or central core disease, SERCA1 in Brody disease, dystrophin in DMD) produce characteristic phenotypes. The sliding filament theory is the integrative endpoint — A band stays constant in width while H and I bands shorten — and was originally inferred from changes seen with light microscopy across different sarcomere lengths.

Hints:

1. The chain has three domains: excitation, coupling, contraction.
2. Each domain has a specific molecular substrate (NMJ → DHPR/RyR1 → cross-bridge cycle).
3. Termination requires SERCA-mediated Ca²⁺ reuptake.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the NMJ as the starting point and explains how the muscle AP is generated.
- Names the DHPR–RyR1 mechanical coupling at the triad and the role of T-tubule depolarisation.
- Describes Ca²⁺ release from SR, binding to troponin C, and tropomyosin displacement.
- Walks through the cross-bridge cycle (ATP detaches; hydrolysis re-cocks; P_i release powers the stroke; ADP release returns to rigor).
- Includes termination via SERCA-mediated Ca²⁺ reuptake.
- Notes that ATP is required for cross-bridge cycling, SERCA, and Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase.

A Yellow answer covers the high-level sequence but omits the specific molecular detail at one of the three domains (e.g., does not name DHPR/RyR1 or troponin C).

A Red answer treats contraction as triggered directly by ACh in the muscle (skipping the AP and ECC steps), or omits Ca²⁺ entirely.

Common misconceptions:

- "ACh directly binds troponin C to trigger contraction.": Correction: ACh acts at the NMJ AChR; the contraction trigger is Ca²⁺ released from the SR via the DHPR–RyR1 coupling, binding to troponin C.
- "Contraction stops because the muscle runs out of energy.": Correction: relaxation is an _active_ process requiring SERCA-mediated Ca²⁺ reuptake (and ATP); ATP depletion _prevents_ relaxation and leads to rigor.

QUESTION 2
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Explain the molecular basis of the length–tension relationship for skeletal muscle. Why does active force peak at sarcomere lengths of ~2.0–2.2 µm and fall to zero at ~3.6 µm?

Model answer: The length–tension curve has three regions, each with a clear molecular interpretation. (1) Ascending limb (1.27–2.0 µm) — active force _increases_ with stretching. Below ~2 µm, thin filaments from opposite Z lines overlap or collide, interfering with cross-bridge formation. At very short lengths the thick filament physically butts against the Z line. (2) Plateau (2.0–2.2 µm, optimal L_o) — maximal active force. The thin filaments overlap the bare cross-bridge-bearing region of the thick filament along its full length, while staying away from the central H-zone area without cross-bridges. Every available cross-bridge can engage actin. (3) Descending limb (2.2–3.6 µm) — active force falls because thick–thin overlap, and hence the number of cross-bridges that can form, decreases. At ~3.6 µm there is no overlap and active force = 0. Passive (titin-mediated) tension rises rapidly only beyond L_o, contributing the resistance felt when stretching a relaxed muscle. Total tension = active + passive. In whole-muscle preparations, the descending limb is rarely used in vivo because skeletal connections constrain operating length close to L_o — a design feature. The length–tension curve is therefore a direct consequence of the sliding filament theory: because force is generated only by cross-bridges, and cross-bridges can form only where overlap exists, force must be a geometric function of overlap [Berne & Levy ch.12; Costanzo].

Elaborative explanation: The body avoids the descending limb by anatomical design — joint range of motion is constrained so muscles operate near L_o. This is why pathological hyperextension (joint dislocation, surgical lengthening) can produce weakness disproportionate to fibre count: the muscle is operating on the descending limb. Length–tension also explains how muscles work optimally at specific positions (e.g., grip strength is maximal at a specific wrist angle that places finger flexors near L_o). Cardiac muscle's length–tension curve underlies the Frank–Starling mechanism — increased preload stretches sarcomeres toward L_o, increasing force per beat.

Hints:

1. The curve has an ascending limb, a plateau, and a descending limb.
2. The plateau corresponds to optimal thick–thin overlap.
3. Beyond ~3.6 µm, no overlap = no force.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Describes all three regions of the curve (ascending, plateau, descending).
- Explains the molecular basis of each region (overlap geometry).
- Quotes the optimal length range (~2.0–2.2 µm) and the upper limit (~3.6 µm).
- Distinguishes active tension from passive (titin) tension.
- Connects the curve to the sliding filament theory.

A Yellow answer covers the curve but omits the molecular interpretation of each region or the role of titin in passive tension.

A Red answer claims that force increases monotonically with length, denies the ascending limb, or treats the curve as static rather than length-dependent.

Common misconceptions:

- "Force always increases with stretch.": Correction: force _peaks_ at the plateau and falls beyond ~2.2 µm because thick–thin overlap decreases.
- "The descending limb is the normal physiological operating range.": Correction: the body operates near L_o (the plateau); the descending limb is unusual and produces measurable weakness when reached.

QUESTION 3
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Compare the mechanism of excitation–contraction coupling in skeletal versus cardiac muscle. Which features are conserved, and which differ — and why does cardiac ECC require Ca²⁺ entry from the extracellular space while skeletal ECC does not?

Model answer: Conserved features: both involve a T-tubule action potential opening voltage-gated DHPRs that lie close to RyRs in the SR; both end with cytosolic [Ca²⁺] rise → troponin C activation → cross-bridge cycling; both terminate via SERCA-mediated Ca²⁺ reuptake. Different features: coupling type — skeletal uses _mechanical/conformational coupling_ (the DHPR/Cav1.1 directly contacts RyR1 across the ~15-nm triad gap; depolarisation alone, even without Ca²⁺ entry, mechanically opens RyR1); cardiac uses _Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release (CICR)_ (Ca²⁺ entering through cardiac DHPR/Cav1.2 into the dyadic cleft binds and opens RyR2, amplifying the initial Ca²⁺ signal). Anatomical apposition — skeletal: triads at A–I junction; cardiac: dyads at the Z line. Source of Ca²⁺ — skeletal: virtually all from SR; cardiac: ~75% from SR + ~25% from extracellular fluid via Cav1.2 (proportions species-dependent). Dependence on extracellular Ca²⁺ — skeletal: independent (a muscle can twitch in zero extracellular Ca²⁺ for a time); cardiac: critically dependent (zero extracellular Ca²⁺ stops the heart almost immediately). Action potential duration and refractoriness — skeletal: short AP (~5 ms), brief refractory period, tetanus possible; cardiac: long Ca²⁺-driven plateau (~200 ms), long refractory period, tetanus impossible — protecting diastolic filling. Why the difference? Cardiac muscle must contract in synchrony from a single pacemaker initiator and must protect itself from tetany; therefore CICR provides a graded, modulable response that scales with extracellular Ca²⁺ (e.g., during sympathetic stimulation: PKA phosphorylates Cav1.2 → more Ca²⁺ entry → more CICR → stronger contraction). Skeletal muscle, recruited by motor neurons that already grade force via firing rate and motor-unit number, can afford the simpler, faster, all-or-none mechanical coupling [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Costanzo].

Elaborative explanation: The functional payoff of the design difference is huge. Cardiac graded inotropy (varying force by varying extracellular Ca²⁺ entry) is essential to autonomic regulation — sympathetic stimulation increases Cav1.2 Ca²⁺ entry, parasympathetic decreases it. Skeletal muscle, by contrast, has its force grading externalised to the CNS (motor unit recruitment, rate coding). This logic also explains why cardiac glycosides (digoxin) work: they raise intracellular Na⁺ via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase inhibition, slowing Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchange, and so raise cardiac Ca²⁺ stores and contractility — but they have no effect on skeletal muscle force, because skeletal muscle does not depend on extracellular/SR Ca²⁺ balance the same way.

Hints:

1. Cardiac uses CICR; skeletal uses mechanical coupling.
2. The difference is dictated by what each muscle must do.
3. Cardiac is heavily modulable; skeletal grades force at the CNS level.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names mechanical (skeletal) vs CICR (cardiac) coupling.
- Identifies DHPR isoforms (Cav1.1 in skeletal; Cav1.2 in cardiac).
- Notes the anatomical difference (triad vs dyad).
- Quotes the source-of-Ca²⁺ split (~100% SR vs ~75/25%).
- Explains the AP duration and refractoriness difference and its functional consequence (tetany prevented in cardiac).
- Articulates why each muscle's design suits its function (graded inotropy vs CNS-graded force).

A Yellow answer correctly compares the coupling mechanisms but omits one of the secondary differences (anatomy, Ca²⁺ source, AP duration) or the functional rationale.

A Red answer claims both muscles use CICR or both use mechanical coupling, or denies the dependence of cardiac contraction on extracellular Ca²⁺.

Common misconceptions:

- "Cardiac and skeletal ECC are identical.": Correction: they share the conserved trigger logic but differ in coupling type, Ca²⁺ source, AP duration, and modulability.
- "Skeletal muscle needs extracellular Ca²⁺ to contract.": Correction: skeletal ECC works in zero extracellular Ca²⁺ because the DHPR–RyR1 coupling is mechanical, not Ca²⁺-flux-mediated.

QUESTION 4
Type: application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Describe the cross-bridge cycle in detail. At which step is ATP bound? Hydrolysed? Released as ADP? What is the rigor state, and why do muscles enter rigor without ATP?

Model answer: The cross-bridge cycle is a four-state cyclic mechano-chemical process. Starting from the rigor configuration (myosin head tightly bound to actin, no nucleotide). (1) ATP binding — a new ATP molecule binds the catalytic site on the myosin head. This binding allosterically reduces myosin's affinity for actin, causing the head to _detach_. (Step blocked in rigor mortis: no ATP, no detachment.) (2) ATP hydrolysis — the detached head hydrolyses ATP to ADP·P*i; the products remain bound. The energy of hydrolysis "cocks" the head into the high-energy 90° configuration, ready for a new power stroke. (3) Weak binding to a new actin site — the cocked, ADP·P_i-bound head re-attaches to actin one step further along the thin filament. The binding is weak until P_i is released. (4) Power stroke (P_i release → ADP release) — P_i release triggers a conformational change that swings the lever arm by ~70°, sliding the actin filament ~10 nm toward the M line. ADP release follows; the head returns to the strong-binding rigor configuration. Without ATP available to bind, the cycle stops here — the rigor state. Rigor state: myosin is tightly bound to actin and resists detachment. Living muscle is never fully in rigor because ATP is continuously available; rigor mortis occurs after death because no ATP is regenerated, so existing cross-bridges cannot detach, and failing SERCA allows SR Ca²⁺ leak that engages all cross-bridges. Rigor breaks ~24–48 hours later through proteolytic decomposition. Pharmacological/clinical anchors: cardiac troponin I/T are released into blood when myocytes are damaged (acute MI). Botulinum toxin acts on the \_upstream* NMJ — not the cross-bridge cycle. The cross-bridge cycle is also exploited in the diagnosis of malignant hyperthermia (in-vitro caffeine/halothane contracture testing) [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9].

Elaborative explanation: The cycle's energetic efficiency is ~20–25% of ATP energy converted to mechanical work — comparable to an internal combustion engine. The remaining energy appears as heat (a useful byproduct in shivering thermogenesis but a problem in malignant hyperthermia). Drugs targeting myosin kinetics include omecamtiv mecarbil (prolongs actin-bound state, investigated for systolic heart failure) and mavacamten (reduces super-relaxed myosin, treats hypertrophic cardiomyopathy). The cycle is also a beautiful demonstration of how a chemical reaction (ATP hydrolysis) can be coupled to mechanical work via specific protein conformational changes — a paradigm now extended to understand many other ATP-driven motors (kinesin, dynein, F1-ATPase).

Hints:

1. Four states: rigor → detached → cocked → re-attached → power-stroke → rigor.
2. ATP _binding_ (not hydrolysis) detaches the head.
3. P_i release triggers the power stroke.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the four states/steps clearly.
- States that ATP binding (not hydrolysis) detaches the head.
- States that ATP hydrolysis re-cocks the head into the high-energy state.
- States that P_i release triggers the power stroke; ADP release returns to rigor.
- Explains rigor state (no ATP → cannot detach) and links to rigor mortis.
- Mentions energetic efficiency (~20–25%) or notes that heat is the byproduct.

A Yellow answer covers the cycle but confuses ATP binding with hydrolysis as the detachment step, or omits the role of P_i release.

A Red answer claims rigor occurs because of _too much_ ATP, or denies that ATP binding causes detachment.

Common misconceptions:

- "ATP hydrolysis (not binding) detaches the head.": Correction: ATP _binding_ detaches the head; _hydrolysis_ of the bound ATP re-cocks it into the high-energy pre-power-stroke state.
- "Rigor mortis is caused by accumulated lactic acid.": Correction: rigor mortis is caused by _ATP depletion_ — without ATP, myosin cannot detach from actin.

QUESTION 5
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Explain how the force–velocity relationship arises from the underlying cross-bridge biophysics, and why it explains the design observation that "fast" muscles have high V_max but lower endurance.

Model answer: The force–velocity curve plots shortening velocity (v) against external load (F). It is hyperbolic: v = V_max at zero load, falls progressively as load rises, and reaches zero at the maximum isometric tension P0 (where the load equals the most the muscle can generate). Hill's equation: (P + a)(v + b) = (P0 + a)b describes it. Molecular basis: at any moment, only a fraction of cross-bridges are attached (force generators); others are detached or cycling. Total force = number of attached cross-bridges × force per cross-bridge. At zero load, cross-bridges complete the power stroke as fast as they can detach and recycle. V_max is set by the rate of cross-bridge cycling, which is dominated by myosin ATPase activity. As load increases, more cross-bridges must be attached simultaneously to support the load. To do so, attached time per cross-bridge lengthens (a back-step occurs more often), lowering net velocity. At P0, all available cross-bridges are simultaneously bearing load; net displacement is zero (isometric). Why fast vs slow muscles differ: Type II (fast) — high-ATPase myosin isoforms cycle quickly → high V_max. Per cycle, more ATP is hydrolysed → high energetic cost → poor endurance unless oxidative capacity is also high (Type IIa) or anaerobic glycolysis can support short bursts (Type IIx/IIb). Type I (slow) — low-ATPase myosin → low V_max → low ATP cost → economical isometric postural work, high endurance. The tetraisoform repertoire (Type I, IIa, IIx, IIb) allows muscles to be tuned for tasks: postural soleus is mostly Type I; sprinter quadriceps biopsies show >70% Type II; mixed muscles can shift their proportions over months of training. Power output (P × v) is maximal at intermediate loads — about 30% of P0 — which is why most efficient locomotor and pumping designs operate at this load fraction, not at maximal force or maximal speed [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9].

Elaborative explanation: The force–velocity curve was characterised by A.V. Hill (Nobel 1922) as one of the first quantitative laws of muscle physiology. It generalises beyond skeletal muscle — cardiac papillary muscle force–velocity curves underpin clinical concepts of contractility, and the ventricular pressure–volume loop is essentially a force–velocity curve in disguise. The training implication is that if your goal is power output, you should train at intermediate load (~30% of 1RM with high velocity) — the same prescription used by Olympic athletes for power-event preparation.

Hints:

1. Plot shortening velocity against load: hyperbolic.
2. V_max set by myosin ATPase rate; P0 set by cross-bridge number.
3. Power = force × velocity, maximal at intermediate load.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Describes the hyperbolic force–velocity relationship and Hill's equation.
- Locates V_max at zero load and P0 at zero velocity.
- Explains V_max as set by myosin ATPase rate (rate of cross-bridge cycling).
- Explains why fast fibres have higher V_max and lower endurance.
- Notes that power is maximal at intermediate loads (~30% of P0).
- Mentions fibre-type contributions (Type I, IIa, IIx).

A Yellow answer describes the curve but omits the molecular basis (ATPase rate, cycle kinetics) or the power-output corollary.

A Red answer claims force and velocity are independent of each other, or claims V_max is reached at maximum load.

Common misconceptions:

- "Fast muscle is always better than slow muscle.": Correction: fast muscle is good for _brief, high-power_ tasks but uses more ATP per cycle and fatigues faster; slow muscle is _economical_ for sustained postural and endurance work.
- "Power output is maximal at zero load.": Correction: at zero load, force is zero, so power (force × velocity) is zero; power peaks at intermediate loads.

QUESTION 6
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The skeletal muscle fibre is a multinucleate syncytium with a unique cytoskeletal architecture. Describe the roles of titin, nebulin, dystrophin, and desmin in linking sarcomere mechanics to force transmission and structural integrity.

Model answer: A complete answer maps each protein to its location and function. Titin — spans Z line to M line; the largest known protein. Two roles: (i) elastic spring — its PEVK and Ig-domain regions provide passive tension at long sarcomere lengths and re-centre the thick filament between Z lines; (ii) mechanosensor — strain on titin recruits MAPK and other signalling pathways implicated in hypertrophic responses. Mutations cause certain dilated cardiomyopathies (truncating variants) and tibial muscular dystrophy. Nebulin — a long, thin actin-binding protein co-extending with the thin filament from Z line to its tip; functions as a length ruler and stabiliser of thin filaments. Loss-of-function mutations cause nemaline myopathy. Dystrophin — sub-sarcolemmal, anchoring intracellular F-actin to the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex (DGC) embedded in the sarcolemma. The DGC connects through α/β-dystroglycan and laminin to the basal lamina, mechanically transmitting force from the sarcomere to the extracellular matrix while protecting the membrane from contraction-induced shear damage. Loss → Duchenne muscular dystrophy: membrane fragility, repeated micro-injury, Ca²⁺ leak, fibrosis, progressive weakness. Desmin — cytoskeletal intermediate filament that aligns adjacent myofibrils at the Z lines, links Z lines to mitochondria and to the nucleus, and connects to costameres at the sarcolemma. Desmin mutations cause myofibrillar/desminopathic myopathies and cardiomyopathy. Integrative point: sarcomere force is transmitted laterally as well as longitudinally. The longitudinal route (Z line → tendon → bone) is what most lay descriptions emphasise, but lateral force transmission through costameres (DGC/dystrophin and the integrin–vinculin–talin axes) is equally important — without it, ~30–40% of force production fails to reach the tendon. Diseases that disrupt costameric proteins thus produce weakness even when contractile machinery is intact. Serum creatine kinase (CK-MM) is dramatically elevated in DMD and other dystrophies because of leak through fragile membranes — a useful biomarker [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: The four proteins together solve four different problems: titin provides elasticity and re-centring; nebulin sets thin-filament length; dystrophin transmits force laterally and protects the membrane; desmin organises myofibrils into a coherent unit and connects them to mitochondria and the nucleus. Disrupting any one produces a characteristic disease phenotype, and identifying the protein from the clinical pattern is a foundational skill in neuromuscular medicine. The understanding of lateral force transmission through costameres has revised the textbook view that force flows simply from sarcomere to tendon — much of it actually flows sideways through the DGC into the extracellular matrix.

Hints:

1. Four proteins, four roles.
2. Titin = elastic spring; nebulin = ruler; dystrophin = sarcolemmal anchor; desmin = myofibril organiser.
3. Loss of each produces a characteristic clinical disease.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names all four proteins and their roles.
- States titin's location (Z to M) and its dual mechanical/mechanosensor function.
- States nebulin's role as actin-binding and length ruler.
- States dystrophin's role at the sub-sarcolemma anchoring F-actin to the DGC and to laminin.
- States desmin's role at Z lines connecting myofibrils, mitochondria, and the nucleus.
- Connects each to a clinical disease (DMD for dystrophin; nemaline myopathy for nebulin; titin-related cardiomyopathy; desminopathies).
- Mentions lateral force transmission through costameres.

A Yellow answer describes 2–3 of the proteins but omits one or fails to link to clinical disease.

A Red answer treats all four as interchangeable or denies their distinct roles.

Common misconceptions:

- "Force is transmitted only longitudinally from Z to Z to tendon.": Correction: substantial force is transmitted _laterally_ through the DGC and costameres into the extracellular matrix — disrupting these produces weakness even with intact sarcomeres.
- "Titin is just a passive spring.": Correction: titin is also a mechanosensor whose strain activates downstream signalling (MAPK, others) and contributes to hypertrophic responses to mechanical load.

Stem: A 5-year-old boy is brought in with delayed motor milestones, a waddling gait, calf pseudohypertrophy, and a Gowers' sign. CK is 12,000 IU/L. Decompose the pathophysiology in terms of skeletal-muscle architecture and physiology.

Model answer: This is Duchenne muscular dystrophy — X-linked recessive, mutations in DMD (Xp21). (1) Genetic / molecular lesion — out-of-frame deletions / nonsense mutations result in absent or near-absent dystrophin, the sub-sarcolemmal protein that links F-actin to the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex (DGC) and thence to the basal lamina. (Becker MD = in-frame deletions producing a partially functional truncated dystrophin → milder, later-onset phenotype.) (2) Mechanical consequence — without intact DGC, sarcolemmal integrity is compromised. Each contraction shears the membrane, allowing extracellular Ca²⁺ entry and intracellular component leak (CK, myoglobin). Lateral force transmission through costameres is impaired, contributing to weakness disproportionate to fibre count. (3) Cellular cascade — sustained intracellular Ca²⁺ overload activates calpains and phospholipases, damages mitochondria, drives oxidative stress, and triggers fibre necrosis. Repeated cycles of necrosis and regeneration deplete satellite-cell capacity. Replacement by fibrofatty tissue produces the calf pseudohypertrophy. (4) Clinical manifestations as predicted by physiology — proximal weakness → waddling gait, Gowers' sign (using hands to climb up the legs to stand because hip extensors are weak); calf pseudohypertrophy → fatty/fibrotic replacement of damaged muscle; markedly elevated CK → continual leak from fragile membranes; cardiomyopathy → dystrophin is also expressed in cardiac muscle, dilated cardiomyopathy emerges in adolescence; respiratory failure → diaphragmatic weakness in the second decade is the leading cause of death in untreated DMD. (5) Therapeutic principles — glucocorticoids (prednisone, deflazacort) slow disease progression; exon-skipping antisense oligonucleotides (eteplirsen for exon 51 skipping; others for 53, 45) restore the open reading frame to produce a Becker-like internally truncated dystrophin; stop-codon read-through (ataluren) for nonsense mutations; gene therapy with micro-dystrophin AAV-vector approaches now FDA-approved for some patients; multidisciplinary care including non-invasive ventilation, ACE inhibitors / β-blockers for cardiomyopathy, physiotherapy, scoliosis surgery [Berne & Levy ch.12; Guyton & Hall ch.6; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: DMD is the prototype of a disease in which a single gene's mutation produces a complete physiological cascade — membrane fragility → Ca²⁺ overload → necrosis → fibrosis → loss of force, in every cycle of contraction. This is also why interventions at multiple levels are partially effective: glucocorticoids stabilise membranes, anti-fibrotic agents reduce scarring, gene therapy restores some dystrophin. The pseudohypertrophy of the calves is a clinical clue: muscles look big but are weak because much of the mass is fat and fibrosis, not contractile tissue. Carrier mothers can have mild manifestations (mild weakness, raised CK, cardiomyopathy) due to skewed X-inactivation.

Hints:

1. The lesion is in dystrophin, X-linked recessive.
2. Membrane fragility produces Ca²⁺ overload and necrosis with each contraction.
3. Treatment is now multimodal: steroids, exon-skipping, gene therapy.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies DMD as the diagnosis.
- States the genetic lesion (X-linked, dystrophin gene mutations).
- Explains the mechanical consequence (loss of DGC link, sarcolemmal fragility, lateral force transmission).
- Describes the cellular cascade (Ca²⁺ overload → necrosis → fibrosis → pseudohypertrophy).
- Predicts clinical features from physiology (proximal weakness, Gowers' sign, calf pseudohypertrophy, cardiomyopathy, respiratory failure).
- Outlines therapeutic strategies (steroids, exon-skipping, gene therapy, multidisciplinary care).
- Distinguishes DMD from Becker MD (in-frame vs out-of-frame mutations).

A Yellow answer identifies DMD and the dystrophin lesion but omits the lateral-force-transmission point or one of the therapeutic categories.

A Red answer treats DMD as a primary contractile or metabolic disorder rather than a structural sub-sarcolemmal disease.

Common misconceptions:

- "Calf pseudohypertrophy means the muscle is strong.": Correction: the bulk is fat and fibrous tissue replacement; the muscle is _weaker_, not stronger.
- "DMD only affects skeletal muscle.": Correction: dystrophin is also expressed in cardiac muscle, smooth muscle, and brain; cardiomyopathy and cognitive features are part of the disease.

QUESTION 8
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: A 24-year-old man undergoing elective surgery becomes hyperthermic (40 °C), develops masseter rigidity after succinylcholine, and shows acidosis, hyperkalaemia, and rapidly rising end-tidal CO₂. Explain the molecular pathophysiology of this crisis and the rationale for dantrolene.

Model answer: This is malignant hyperthermia — a pharmacogenetic disorder of skeletal-muscle Ca²⁺ release. (1) Genetic basis — most cases involve gain-of-function mutations in RYR1 (the skeletal SR Ca²⁺-release channel), with a smaller proportion in CACNA1S (DHPR α₁s subunit). Inheritance is autosomal dominant with variable penetrance. (2) Trigger — volatile inhalational anaesthetics (halothane, sevoflurane, isoflurane, desflurane) and the depolarising muscle relaxant succinylcholine destabilise the mutant RyR1, lowering its activation threshold. (3) The cascade — excessive, sustained Ca²⁺ release from the SR raises cytosolic [Ca²⁺] → continuous cross-bridge cycling and rigidity → uncontrolled ATP hydrolysis → ATP regeneration via creatine kinase, glycolysis, and oxidative phosphorylation runs at maximum → enormous heat production (hyperthermia, often >40 °C, rising 1 °C per 5 min) → lactate accumulates (metabolic acidosis) → CO₂ production rises (raised end-tidal CO₂ — often the earliest sign on the ventilator) → membrane damage releases K⁺ (hyperkalaemia) and creatine kinase / myoglobin (rhabdomyolysis, dark urine, acute kidney injury). (4) Diagnostic clues — end-tidal CO₂ rises despite increased minute ventilation (most sensitive early sign); masseter rigidity post-succinylcholine (pathognomonic in this context); rapid temperature rise, sinus tachycardia, mottling, mixed metabolic and respiratory acidosis on ABG. (5) Treatment rationale — discontinue triggers, hyperventilate with 100% O₂ through fresh circuit and tubing (volatile anaesthetic adsorbs into rubber); dantrolene (the specific therapy) binds RyR1 and reduces Ca²⁺ release, breaking the runaway cycle (initial dose 2.5 mg/kg IV, repeated up to 10 mg/kg or more); cardiac RyR2 is largely unaffected, so cardiac contractility is preserved; aggressive cooling (cold IV fluids, ice packs, peritoneal/gastric lavage with cold saline); manage hyperkalaemia, acidosis, arrhythmias; monitor for rhabdomyolysis-induced acute kidney injury (alkaline diuresis, careful fluid management). Subsequent counselling: first-degree relatives must be advised of the risk; in-vitro halothane-caffeine contracture test or genetic testing may be offered [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9].

Elaborative explanation: MH is the prototype pharmacogenetic crisis. Mortality has fallen from ~80% (pre-dantrolene) to <5% with prompt recognition and treatment — a dramatic example of how a single specific antidote, deployed quickly, can transform an outcome. Recognition is the rate-limiting step: end-tidal CO₂ rise is more sensitive and earlier than temperature rise. Newer dantrolene formulations (Ryanodex) reconstitute much faster than the original Dantrium, allowing quicker administration in crisis. The susceptibility is permanent — patients and families must be counselled and identified to anaesthetists before any subsequent surgery. The condition also illustrates a general principle: a "silent" genetic variant can be unmasked by a specific drug exposure, with catastrophic results.

Hints:

1. The genetic substrate is RYR1 (or CACNA1S) gain-of-function.
2. The cascade is Ca²⁺ release → continuous cross-bridge cycling → heat, lactate, K⁺ leak.
3. Dantrolene specifically blocks RyR1.

A Yellow answer identifies MH and treatment but omits the genetic basis or one of the cascade steps.

Stem: A patient with painless cramping after sustained voluntary effort, with delayed muscle relaxation on EMG, is found to have a genetic defect in SERCA1. Explain why this produces the clinical picture and how it differs from myotonia congenita.

Model answer: This is Brody disease — autosomal recessive loss-of-function mutations in ATP2A1 encoding SERCA1 (the fast-twitch SR Ca²⁺-ATPase). (1) Mechanism in Brody disease — without effective SERCA1, cytosolic Ca²⁺ in fast-twitch fibres cannot be returned to the SR efficiently after contraction. With repeated stimulation (sustained or repeated effort), cytosolic Ca²⁺ stays elevated → cross-bridges remain engaged → muscle fails to relax promptly. Slow-twitch fibres (which use SERCA2a) are spared, so resting tone and isometric postural function are normal. Features: painless cramping after exercise, delayed relaxation, weakness with sustained effort, especially affecting fast-twitch-rich muscles (limb muscles, eyelids). (2) Contrast with myotonia congenita — myotonia congenita (CLCN1 mutations, autosomal recessive Becker form or dominant Thomsen form) involves loss of skeletal-muscle Cl⁻ conductance. The Cl⁻ channel normally stabilises the sarcolemma after the AP; its loss leads to repetitive AP firing in response to a single voluntary stimulus → sustained contraction → painless stiffness. Crucially: Brody — problem with Ca²⁺ removal → delayed relaxation that is EMG-silent during the impaired relaxation (no ongoing electrical activity); myotonia congenita — problem with membrane stabilisation → delayed relaxation accompanied by continued electrical activity ("warm-up" phenomenon as repeated activity reduces stiffness because of slow recovery of remaining channels). (3) Treatment — Brody has no specific therapy; symptoms ease with avoidance of provocative effort and dantrolene (variable effect). Myotonia congenita responds to membrane-stabilising agents (mexiletine, phenytoin, carbamazepine). (4) Why this distinction matters pedagogically — Brody and myotonia both produce delayed muscle relaxation, but the lesion is at completely different physiological steps (SR Ca²⁺ reuptake vs sarcolemmal Cl⁻ conductance). EMG distinguishes them; the conceptual lesson is that "stiffness" is not a single disease — anywhere along the path from membrane → SR → cross-bridge can produce it [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Skeletal Muscle Diseases; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: This pair of diseases is a beautiful pedagogical example because both produce the _same superficial clinical phenotype_ (delayed relaxation after sustained effort) from _opposite physiological origins_. Brody is "Ca²⁺ stays in the cytosol too long because reuptake fails"; myotonia congenita is "the AP keeps firing because the membrane won't repolarise fully". The EMG is the discriminator: silent in Brody (because the muscle is not firing — it's just slow to relax); active in myotonia (because spontaneous AP discharges drive the contraction). Other "slow-relaxation" syndromes include hypothyroid pseudomyotonia (Hoffmann syndrome — myxoedema-related slow tendon reflex) and rippling muscle disease (caveolin-3 mutations).

1. Brody = Ca²⁺ removal failure; myotonia = membrane stabilisation failure.
2. EMG is silent in Brody but active in myotonia.
3. The same superficial phenotype can arise from completely different lesions.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

Stem: A patient on simvastatin for hyperlipidaemia develops bilateral proximal myalgia, weakness, and a CK level 15× the upper limit of normal. Explain the physiological mechanisms by which statins can cause skeletal-muscle injury, and why the risk is increased by certain co-medications.

(1) Statin pharmacology — statins competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol biosynthesis. In addition to lowering hepatic cholesterol output, they reduce production of mevalonate-pathway intermediates beyond cholesterol, including ubiquinone (CoQ10), heme A, dolichols, and prenylated proteins. (2) Proposed mechanisms of muscle injury — CoQ10 depletion → impaired mitochondrial electron transport chain function → reduced ATP production, increased ROS → mitochondrial dysfunction in fast-glycolytic and oxidative fibres alike; reduced isoprenylation of small GTPases (Rab, Rho, Ras) → impaired vesicle traffic and intracellular signalling in muscle; membrane cholesterol depletion → altered sarcolemmal integrity and ion-channel function (notably ryanodine-receptor-associated Ca²⁺ leak); statin-induced necrotising autoimmune myopathy (SINAM) — a rare immune-mediated subtype with anti-HMGCR antibodies, persisting after statin withdrawal and requiring immunosuppression. The autoimmune subtype (SINAM) with anti-HMGCR antibodies is rare but instructive — it shows that drug exposure can unmask autoimmunity in susceptible individuals, with the immune attack on HMGCR persisting even after statin withdrawal. The treatment for SINAM is immunosuppression (corticosteroids, methotrexate, IVIG, rituximab) — quite different from simple statin myopathy.

1. The mevalonate pathway has many products beyond cholesterol.
2. CoQ10 depletion and prenylation loss are the leading mechanisms.
3. Drug interactions through CYP3A4 and OATP1B1 magnify exposure.

Stem: A 70-year-old woman is bedbound after a stroke for 3 weeks. During an attempted endotracheal intubation requiring succinylcholine, her serum K⁺ rises from 4.2 to 7.8 mEq/L within minutes. Explain the underlying physiology.

Model answer: This presentation is succinylcholine-induced hyperkalaemia from denervation/disuse supersensitivity. (1) Normal NMJ ACh receptor distribution — in healthy adult skeletal muscle, the nicotinic ACh receptors are tightly clustered at the motor end-plate, accounting for <0.1% of the sarcolemmal area. The sarcolemma elsewhere is essentially receptor-free. (2) What changes with denervation, disuse, burns, or critical illness — loss of trophic input from the motor nerve (or prolonged absence of normal contraction) triggers transcriptional reprogramming. Embryonic-form γ-subunit-containing AChRs and α7-AChRs spread along the entire sarcolemma — "extrajunctional receptors". These receptors are present in much greater numbers than at the original NMJ and have prolonged open-channel time and are more sensitive to depolarising agonists like succinylcholine. (3) Succinylcholine's effect — succinylcholine is a depolarising NMJ blocker that mimics ACh. Normally it briefly depolarises the end-plate and produces flaccid paralysis after fasciculations. In a patient with widespread extrajunctional receptors, however, the agent depolarises the entire sarcolemma, driving massive K⁺ efflux through the open ACh receptor channel pores. The result: serum K⁺ rises 1–4 mEq/L within a minute, with risk of cardiac arrest from hyperkalaemia. (4) Patient populations at risk — denervation injury (spinal cord injury, peripheral nerve transection, stroke with dense paresis): risk begins ~24–72 hours post-event, peaks at 1–4 weeks, persists for up to 6–12 months; burns (>10% TBSA): risk peaks at days 5–30; prolonged immobilisation, severe sepsis, critical illness myopathy; pre-existing myopathies (DMD, dermatomyositis). (5) Avoidance — use a non-depolarising muscle relaxant (rocuronium, vecuronium) in any of the above patient categories. The specific antidote for rocuronium is sugammadex. (6) Pedagogical lesson — the motor neuron exerts continuous trophic influence on the muscle fibre; denervation reveals an entire silenced developmental programme (extrajunctional AChR expression, embryonic isoforms). The resulting "denervation supersensitivity" was first described by Cannon and Rosenblueth in 1949 and is a general feature of the autonomic-target relationship (e.g., supersensitivity of denervated heart to norepinephrine) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: This case is a high-yield anaesthesia teaching point that recurs in every USMLE/PLAB-style examination because it illustrates several principles at once: (a) the trophic influence of nerves on muscle gene expression, (b) how a normally safe drug becomes lethal in a specific patient population, and (c) the importance of taking a focused history before drug administration. The 24-hour cutoff is conservative — the safe period is ~24 hours from the precipitating event, but waiting longer is wise if possible, and the risk persists for months. Sugammadex's introduction has made rocuronium the modern alternative to succinylcholine for rapid sequence intubation in many centres, eliminating this risk altogether.

Hints:

1. Denervation triggers extrajunctional AChR expression.
2. Succinylcholine then depolarises the _entire_ sarcolemma, not just the end-plate.
3. The K⁺ release is massive and life-threatening.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the syndrome as succinylcholine-induced hyperkalaemia in denervation/disuse.
- Explains the normal NMJ AChR distribution and contrasts it with extrajunctional AChR expression after denervation.
- States that embryonic γ-subunit-containing receptors return.
- Explains why succinylcholine produces massive K⁺ efflux through these receptors.
- Lists at-risk patient populations (denervation, burns, prolonged immobilisation, myopathies).
- Recommends non-depolarising blockers (rocuronium) with sugammadex as antidote.

A Yellow answer identifies the syndrome but omits the molecular mechanism (extrajunctional AChRs) or the at-risk populations.

A Red answer attributes the K⁺ rise to renal failure or unrelated causes.

- "Succinylcholine is safe in any patient.": Correction: it is contraindicated in denervation, burns >10% TBSA, prolonged immobilisation, malignant hyperthermia susceptibility, and many myopathies.

Stem: Why does someone who exercises to exhaustion experience muscle ache 24–48 hours later (delayed-onset muscle soreness, DOMS) — but cramps and burning sensation during exercise are immediate? Distinguish the physiological mechanisms.

Model answer: Two distinct phenomena, two distinct mechanisms. Acute exercise-related discomfort (immediate, during or just after exercise): local accumulation of H⁺, P_i, K⁺, lactate, and ATP catabolites in working muscle stimulates muscle group III/IV afferents ("ergoreceptors") — small, often unmyelinated nerve fibres that signal "metabolic discomfort" and burning; drives the exercise pressor reflex (rising heart rate and blood pressure) and the sense of fatigue; cramps during exercise are largely neurogenic — abnormal motor-neuron firing in dehydrated, electrolyte-disturbed, or fatigued conditions (the previous "lactic acid" theory of cramps is now considered inadequate); these signals resolve within minutes to hours of stopping exercise as metabolites are cleared. Delayed-onset muscle soreness (24–72 hours later): caused predominantly by eccentric muscle contractions (lengthening under load — descending stairs, downhill running, lowering weights); eccentric contractions produce greater strain per cross-bridge than concentric; mechanical microdamage — streaming of Z lines, sarcomere "popping" (asymmetric stretching of weakest sarcomeres), fragmentation of titin and other cytoskeletal proteins; inflammatory cascade — damage releases damage-associated molecular patterns; macrophages and neutrophils invade; cytokines (IL-1, TNF, IL-6) and prostaglandins (COX-2 mediated) sensitise nociceptors (this is the source of the soreness — inflammatory, not mechanical or metabolic per se); peak 24–72 hours after the bout because the inflammatory response takes time to develop; resolves over a week. Repeated exposures induce repeated-bout effect — the muscle adapts (cytoskeletal reinforcement, protective remodelling), and the same workload produces less DOMS next time. Implications: NSAIDs (COX inhibitors) modestly reduce DOMS but may also blunt the adaptive remodelling; "no pain, no gain" is a useful slogan only insofar as pain reflects mechanical strain that drives adaptation; pain that persists, localises, or is associated with weakness disproportionate to work suggests true injury (rhabdomyolysis, exertional or compartment syndromes). The acute and delayed phenomena are easily conflated by lay audiences but mechanistically have nothing in common: one is metabolite-driven afferent signalling, the other is inflammation following mechanical microdamage [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Fatigue; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: The contrast also dispels several common misconceptions — that lactate or "lactic acid" causes DOMS (it does not; lactate is cleared within an hour), that DOMS-free training is suboptimal (the repeated-bout effect means experienced athletes get less DOMS at the same workload, not because they're training poorly), and that NSAIDs are universally helpful (they may dampen the very inflammatory signals that drive adaptive remodelling). Eccentric contractions are particularly important because they produce supra-physiological strain per cross-bridge — useful for injury prevention training (Nordic hamstring curls reduce hamstring strain risk) but also responsible for most exercise-induced microinjury.

Hints:

1. Acute discomfort = metabolites stimulating group III/IV afferents.
2. DOMS = mechanical microdamage from eccentric work + delayed inflammatory response.
3. They look similar but have completely different mechanisms.

- Explains the timing mismatch (peak DOMS at 24–72 h is too late for lactate).
- Mentions the repeated-bout effect.
- Notes the NSAIDs trade-off (relief vs adaptation).

A Yellow answer distinguishes the two phenomena but doesn't articulate the inflammatory cascade or the eccentric contraction emphasis.

Stem: Common misconception: "Lactic acid build-up causes muscle fatigue, soreness, and cramps." Critique this widely held belief using contemporary muscle physiology.

Model answer: This claim, while still pervasive in lay culture and even some textbooks, is largely incorrect. A careful response distinguishes three claims. (1) "Lactic acid causes muscle fatigue." — Partially incorrect. Lactate itself is not a major cause of fatigue; in fact, lactate is a useful intercellular and intracellular fuel. The proton (H⁺) accumulation that accompanies anaerobic glycolysis can lower intramuscular pH and contribute to reduced myofibrillar Ca²⁺ sensitivity, but recent work (Lamb, Westerblad, others) shows that even intracellular acidosis has a modest effect on force at physiological temperatures. The dominant contributors to fatigue during sustained intense exercise are now considered to be: accumulation of inorganic phosphate (P_i) — impairs P_i release and the power stroke step of the cross-bridge cycle; reduced SR Ca²⁺ release (RyR1 dysfunction, depleted SR Ca²⁺); reduced myofibrillar Ca²⁺ sensitivity; glycogen depletion in long-duration exercise; central fatigue (reduced motoneuronal drive from CNS). (2) "Lactic acid causes delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS)." — Demonstrably incorrect. Lactate is cleared from muscle within ~1 hour of exercise. DOMS peaks at 24–72 hours, when lactate is long gone. DOMS is caused by mechanical microdamage from eccentric contractions and the subsequent inflammatory response, not by acid retention. (3) "Lactic acid causes muscle cramps." — Cramps occur in many situations without notable lactate (sleeping; exercising at low intensity; pathological cramps in heat exhaustion or McArdle's disease). The leading hypotheses are neuromuscular: abnormal motor-neuron firing in the setting of dehydration, electrolyte disturbance, fatigue, or altered spinal reflex modulation. A more accurate framing: lactate is a useful metabolic fuel (the lactate shuttle hypothesis — Brooks). It is exported from active glycolytic muscle via MCT4, taken up via MCT1 by oxidative muscle, heart, and brain, and reconverted to pyruvate as a substrate for oxidative phosphorylation. The "lactate threshold" in exercise testing reflects the point at which production exceeds clearance, not a toxic effect of lactate itself. Pedagogical lesson: beware causal claims based on temporal correlation (lactate rises during exercise; fatigue occurs during exercise; therefore lactate causes fatigue). Rigorous mechanistic dissection — pH alone vs P_i alone vs central fatigue — is required to identify the actual contributors. The history of physiology is full of plausible-sounding but ultimately incorrect "stories" that survived because they were catchy [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Fatigue; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: McArdle disease (myophosphorylase deficiency, glycogen storage disease type V) is a beautiful negative control — patients cannot generate lactate during anaerobic exercise (they cannot break down glycogen), yet they develop severe muscle pain and contractures with exercise. This shows that lactate is not necessary for exercise-induced cramp/contracture; energy substrate failure (PCr exhaustion in McArdle's case) is the actual cause. The "second wind" phenomenon in McArdle's — in which fatty acid oxidation kicks in after a brief rest — is another striking illustration that the muscle's fuel logic is more subtle than "more lactate = more fatigue".

Hints:

1. Lactate is a metabolic fuel, not a toxin.
2. P_i, reduced SR Ca²⁺ release, glycogen depletion, and central fatigue dominate.
3. McArdle disease is a natural experiment that disproves the lactate-causes-fatigue claim.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States the misconception clearly.
- Critiques the three sub-claims (fatigue, DOMS, cramps) separately.
- Names the actual contributors to fatigue (P_i, reduced SR Ca²⁺ release, glycogen depletion, central fatigue).
- Notes that DOMS peaks at 24–72 h when lactate has long been cleared.
- Mentions the lactate shuttle and lactate as fuel.
- Optionally: cites McArdle disease as a natural experiment.

A Yellow answer critiques the lactate myth but omits one of the three sub-claims or fails to name the actual contributors.

A Red answer defends the misconception or denies that lactate has any metabolic value.

Common misconceptions:

- "Lactate is a waste product.": Correction: lactate is a useful metabolic fuel, taken up by oxidative muscle, heart, brain, and other tissues via MCT1 and reconverted to pyruvate.
- "Acid pH directly causes fatigue.": Correction: at physiological temperatures, intracellular acidosis has a modest effect; P_i accumulation and reduced SR Ca²⁺ release are more important.

QUESTION 15
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Common misconception: "Strength training builds muscle by increasing the number of muscle fibres." Critique this and explain the actual cellular mechanisms of training-induced hypertrophy.

Model answer: In adult mammals, including humans, strength training increases muscle size almost entirely by hypertrophy (more contractile protein per fibre), not by hyperplasia (more fibres). Some splitting of fibres has been demonstrated experimentally in extreme conditions, but this is a minor contribution to physiological adult training adaptations. Mechanisms of hypertrophy: (1) Mechanical signalling — active contraction against resistance produces strain detected by mechanosensors (titin, costameric proteins, integrin-linked kinase, and stretch-activated channels). This activates mTOR Complex 1 (mTORC1) — the master regulator of protein synthesis. (2) mTORC1 pathway — IGF-1 (locally and systemically) and mechanical signals converge on PI3K → Akt → TSC1/2 → Rheb → mTORC1. mTORC1 phosphorylates 4E-BP1 (releasing eIF4E) and S6K1, dramatically increasing translation initiation and ribosomal biogenesis. (3) Satellite cell recruitment — mature myofibres are post-mitotic, but satellite cells (Pax7⁺ muscle stem cells) lying beneath the basal lamina proliferate in response to exercise and fuse with existing fibres, donating new myonuclei. This is essential when fibre size approaches the "myonuclear domain ceiling" — the cytoplasmic volume one nucleus can support. (4) Decreased proteolysis — the atrophy-promoting FoxO/MuRF1/atrogin-1 pathway is suppressed during training; ubiquitin-proteasome degradation of contractile proteins is reduced. Net protein balance shifts positive. (5) Sarcoplasmic vs sarcomeric hypertrophy — bodybuilding-style high-volume training produces relatively more sarcoplasmic hypertrophy (cytoplasmic volume); strength-style heavy training produces relatively more sarcomeric hypertrophy (added contractile protein). Specific gains depend on stimulus. Atrophy (the converse) follows the opposite logic: disuse, denervation, glucocorticoids, cytokines (TNF, IL-6 in cachexia), and myostatin-pathway activation drive FoxO and MuRF1/atrogin-1, increasing protein degradation. mTORC1 signalling is suppressed. Within days to weeks, fibre cross-sectional area falls. Astronauts in microgravity lose ~15% of muscle mass in a 6-month mission despite exercise countermeasures. Why no hyperplasia? Adult mammalian myofibres are post-mitotic. New myofibres are formed only by satellite-cell proliferation, fusion, and differentiation — and the resulting product is integrated into existing fibres or replaces damaged ones, not added as net new fibres. Mathematical analyses of biopsy fibre counts confirm no significant change in fibre number with training. Take-home: "build muscle" colloquially, but physiologically what trainees are actually doing is building protein within fibres, with the limit imposed by mTOR signalling capacity and myonuclear domain [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Hypertrophy/Atrophy].

Elaborative explanation: The myonuclear domain hypothesis explains why satellite cell incorporation matters — each nucleus can support only a limited cytoplasmic volume, so substantial hypertrophy requires adding nuclei. This has clinical implications: chronic glucocorticoid use depletes satellite cells (in addition to driving proteolysis), making recovery from disuse atrophy slower in steroid-treated patients. The opposite extreme — myostatin loss-of-function (natural in some cattle breeds, occasionally in humans with double mutations) — produces dramatic muscle hypertrophy through both increased fibre size and possibly some hyperplasia, suggesting that the limit on hyperplasia in normal adults is myostatin-imposed.

Hints:

1. Hypertrophy = bigger fibres; hyperplasia = more fibres.
2. mTORC1 is the central pro-hypertrophic signalling node.
3. Satellite cells donate nuclei to support enlarged cytoplasm.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Distinguishes hypertrophy from hyperplasia and states that adult training is dominated by hypertrophy.
- Names mTORC1 as the master regulator and the upstream signalling (IGF-1, PI3K/Akt, mechanical signals).
- Describes satellite cell recruitment and the myonuclear domain hypothesis.
- Mentions decreased proteolysis (FoxO/MuRF1/atrogin-1 suppression).
- Distinguishes sarcoplasmic vs sarcomeric hypertrophy.
- Contrasts atrophy (disuse, glucocorticoids, microgravity) and its mechanisms.

A Yellow answer covers hypertrophy but omits the satellite cell role, the mTORC1 detail, or the atrophy contrast.

A Red answer claims training produces hyperplasia, or denies that protein synthesis is the dominant mechanism.

Common misconceptions:

- "Adults can grow new muscle fibres with training.": Correction: hyperplasia is minimal in adult mammals; training produces hypertrophy of existing fibres.
- "Bigger muscles always mean more contractile protein.": Correction: bodybuilding-style training favours sarcoplasmic hypertrophy (cytoplasm, glycogen, water); strength-style training favours sarcomeric hypertrophy (contractile protein).

QUESTION 16
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Discuss the plasticity of skeletal-muscle fibre type. Can endurance training convert fast-twitch fibres to slow-twitch, or vice versa? How does motor-neuron innervation influence fibre type, and what is the role of MEF2/calcineurin signalling?

Model answer: Skeletal-muscle fibre type is not fixed; it is shaped continuously by neural activity pattern, hormonal milieu, and mechanical demands. (1) Innervation determines fibre type — classic cross-innervation experiments (Buller, Eccles, Eccles 1960) showed that surgically connecting a slow muscle's nerve to a fast muscle, and vice versa, eventually converts each muscle's fibre-type composition toward the new nerve's pattern. The conclusion: the firing pattern of the motor neuron, not the muscle fibre's intrinsic identity, dominates fibre-type expression. All fibres in one motor unit are of the same type because they share a single motor neuron's firing pattern. (2) Tonic vs phasic firing patterns — slow motor neurons fire tonically at low frequency (5–25 Hz); fast motor neurons fire in brief, infrequent, high-frequency bursts (50–150 Hz). The pattern, more than the absolute number of impulses, determines fibre type. (3) Molecular mechanism — calcineurin/NFAT and MEF2 — tonic, sustained low-amplitude Ca²⁺ transients (slow-fibre pattern) activate calcineurin, the Ca²⁺/calmodulin-dependent phosphatase. Calcineurin dephosphorylates NFAT (nuclear factor of activated T-cells), allowing it to enter the nucleus and partner with MEF2 to drive transcription of slow-fibre genes (slow MyHC-I, mitochondrial biogenesis, oxidative enzymes). High-frequency, brief Ca²⁺ transients (fast pattern) do not sustain Ca²⁺/calmodulin/calcineurin activation; NFAT remains phosphorylated, cytoplasmic, and inactive. Other transcription factors (e.g., MyoD active in fast fibres) drive fast-isoform expression. (4) PGC-1α — a master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis and the slow-fibre programme. Endurance exercise increases PGC-1α via AMPK and CaMKII activation; PGC-1α drives the cellular shift toward the slow-oxidative phenotype. (5) Practical training adaptations — endurance training (running, cycling) increases mitochondrial content, capillary density, oxidative enzymes, and shifts MyHC expression toward IIa (and limited shifts toward I); pure I↔II conversion is rare in humans on physiological time scales — IIx/IIb often shifts toward IIa, but I:II ratios change less. Resistance training primarily induces hypertrophy (especially of Type II); shifts MyHC slightly toward IIa from IIx. Detraining and bed rest rapidly increase Type IIx expression and decrease oxidative capacity. (6) Clinical relevance — in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, exercise-trained quadriceps show measurable shifts toward Type I and improved fatigue resistance; in ageing, sarcopenia preferentially affects Type II fibres, and resistance training can attenuate this loss; genetic mutations in the slow-fibre programme produce specific myopathies. (7) Take-home — fibre type is "neural-firing-pattern-encoded, transcriptionally implemented, hormonally tunable". It is plastic but slowly so (weeks to months), and the switches are reversible. Training shifts dominate the phenotype within physiological limits, and absolute conversion (e.g., from IIx to I) is uncommon [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9].

Elaborative explanation: The cross-innervation experiment is one of the most elegant demonstrations of activity-dependent gene expression in physiology: the same muscle fibre, exposed to a different firing pattern, eventually rewrites its transcriptional programme. The clinical translation is the use of chronic electrical stimulation to drive slow-oxidative phenotype in denervated muscle (functional electrical stimulation in spinal cord injury) and the rationale for tonic low-frequency stimulation as part of cardiac resynchronisation. Myostatin loss-of-function and follistatin overexpression are emerging therapeutic strategies for sarcopenia and muscular dystrophies.

Hints:

1. The motor neuron's firing pattern, not fibre identity, dominates.
2. Calcineurin–NFAT–MEF2 is the slow-fibre transcriptional programme.
3. Training shifts fibre type within limits, but I↔II conversion is uncommon.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States that innervation pattern determines fibre type (cross-innervation experiments).
- Distinguishes tonic (slow) from phasic (fast) motor neuron firing patterns.
- Names the calcineurin–NFAT–MEF2 pathway driving slow-fibre gene expression.
- Mentions PGC-1α as a master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis.
- Describes practical training shifts (endurance → IIx → IIa; limited I↔II) and detraining effects.
- Notes the clinical relevance (COPD, ageing sarcopenia).

A Yellow answer covers basic fibre-type shifts but omits the calcineurin–NFAT–MEF2 mechanism or the cross-innervation evidence.

A Red answer claims fibre type is genetically fixed at birth and cannot change.

Common misconceptions:

- "Fibre type is genetically fixed.": Correction: fibre type is plastic, driven by motor-neuron firing pattern and environmental signals; cross-innervation, training, and disuse all shift it.
- "Endurance training converts fast fibres to slow.": Correction: substantial IIx → IIa shifts occur with training, but pure II → I conversion is uncommon in humans on physiological time scales.

QUESTION 17
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Compare skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle, and smooth muscle on a single integrative axis: structure (sarcomeric vs not), Ca²⁺ source for contraction, regulatory protein for contraction (troponin vs calmodulin), tetanus capability, autonomic responsiveness, and energy metabolism. Why are these design choices appropriate for each tissue's function?

Model answer: Skeletal muscle: sarcomeric striations; triad arrangement at the A–I junction; Ca²⁺ from SR almost exclusively (mechanical DHPR–RyR1 coupling); regulated by troponin C (thin-filament regulation); tetanus is possible (short ~5 ms AP, brief refractory period); voluntary control via somatic α motor neurons; mixed energy metabolism (fibre-type-specific — Type I oxidative, Type IIx glycolytic, Type IIa intermediate); long, multinucleate syncytium per fibre; no electrical coupling between fibres; dystrophin/DGC critical. Cardiac muscle: sarcomeric (less regular striations); dyad at the Z line; Ca²⁺ from ~75% SR + ~25% extracellular via Cav1.2 (CICR); regulated by troponin C (cardiac isoform); tetanus _not_ possible (long ~200 ms Ca²⁺-plateau AP, long refractory period); autonomic responsiveness — sympathetic raises rate and contractility, parasympathetic lowers rate; almost exclusively oxidative metabolism (fatty acids 60–70%, glucose, lactate, ketones); branched cells, single nucleus, electrically coupled by gap junctions at intercalated discs; pacemaker (SA node) initiates each beat. Smooth muscle: no sarcomeric striations; no triad/dyad (caveolae plus SR with IP₃R and RyR3); Ca²⁺ from a mix of SR and extracellular (Cav, store-operated, receptor-operated channels); regulated by calmodulin → MLCK (thick-filament regulation; phosphorylation of myosin light chain); largely tonic, no classical tetanus; sympathetic + parasympathetic on visceral/vascular smooth muscle plus paracrine and hormonal regulation; primarily oxidative metabolism with very low ATP cost in the latch state; spindle-shaped, single-nucleus, often gap-junction-coupled (unitary smooth muscle); variable AP triggering (spontaneous slow waves, neurally driven, hormonally driven). Why these design choices? Skeletal muscle must respond rapidly and graded force is set by descending CNS commands. Short AP enables tetany; mechanical ECC is fastest; troponin-based regulation is fast and on/off. Voluntary control allows precise behaviour. Cardiac muscle must contract rhythmically without fatigue and must not tetanise (which would prevent diastolic filling). Long Ca²⁺-plateau AP enforces a long refractory period. CICR allows fine modulation by sympathetic/parasympathetic inputs (more Ca²⁺ entry → more CICR → stronger contraction). Branched cells and gap junctions ensure synchronous contraction. Smooth muscle must sustain prolonged, low-energy contractions in vessels, gut, airways, bladder, uterus. Calmodulin–MLCK regulation is slower but allows the "latch state" — sustained force at very low ATP cost. Lack of striations and slower cross-bridge cycling fit the requirement for tonic force. Multiple Ca²⁺ entry/release pathways allow sensitive responses to neural, hormonal, mechanical, and paracrine signals. No single muscle design is "better." Each muscle type is exquisitely matched to the task it performs. The same conserved sliding-filament/cross-bridge core, with different regulation, geometry, and energy-management layers, supports voluntary movement, life-long pumping, and visceral homeostasis [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Costanzo].

Elaborative explanation: This three-way comparison is one of the most clarifying integrative exercises in muscle physiology. Each design choice — sarcomeric vs not, troponin vs calmodulin, tetanus vs not, oxidative vs mixed, syncytium vs gap-junction-coupled — maps onto a functional requirement. The same conserved cross-bridge cycle is "tuned" by different regulation, geometry, AP duration, energy-management strategies, and hormonal/neural inputs to fit each task. Latch state in smooth muscle is particularly interesting: dephosphorylation of myosin light chain while still attached to actin produces sustained force at minimal ATP cost — the design feature that lets vascular smooth muscle hold blood-pressure-relevant contraction for hours without exhausting energy stores.

Hints:

1. Six axes: structure, Ca²⁺ source, regulator, tetanus capability, autonomic responsiveness, energy metabolism.
2. Each muscle's design fits its functional demands.
3. The cross-bridge core is conserved; the wiring differs.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Compares all three muscle types across the requested axes.
- Identifies the source of Ca²⁺ (skeletal SR; cardiac ~75/25 SR/ECF; smooth mixed).
- Identifies the regulatory mechanism (troponin C in striated; calmodulin–MLCK in smooth).
- States tetanus capability (yes in skeletal; no in cardiac; tonic in smooth).
- Connects each design choice to functional requirement (rapid graded for skeletal; rhythmic non-tetanic for cardiac; tonic low-energy for smooth).
- Mentions latch state as the smooth-muscle distinctive feature.

A Yellow answer compares skeletal and cardiac well but is thin on smooth muscle.

A Red answer treats all three as essentially the same or denies the different regulatory mechanisms.

Common misconceptions:

- "Smooth muscle has troponin like skeletal/cardiac.": Correction: smooth muscle uses _thick-filament_ regulation via calmodulin and MLCK; troponin is absent.
- "Cardiac muscle can tetanise under stress.": Correction: the long Ca²⁺-plateau AP and prolonged absolute refractory period prevent tetany — protecting diastolic filling.

QUESTION 18
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Why is the skeletal-muscle action potential brief (~5 ms) while the cardiac action potential is long (~200 ms)? Frame this as a design problem solved by selecting different sets of ion channels.

Model answer: The contrast in action-potential durations between skeletal and cardiac muscle is one of the clearest examples in physiology of channel composition tailoring tissue function. The skeletal-muscle action potential is essentially a nerve-like spike: voltage-gated Na⁺ channel opening drives a rapid upstroke (~+30 mV peak), Na⁺ inactivation closes the upstroke, and voltage-gated K⁺ channels (delayed rectifier) repolarise the membrane within 2–5 ms. Total duration: ~5 ms. Brief refractory period. Tetanus possible. Why brief? Because graded force in skeletal muscle is implemented at the level of motor-unit recruitment and rate coding by the CNS — the muscle fibre itself just needs to fire reliably and recover quickly. The cardiac ventricular action potential has the same Na⁺-channel-driven phase 0 upstroke. But then: phase 1 (transient repolarisation) — brief opening of transient outward K⁺ current (Ito); phase 2 (plateau, ~150–200 ms) — inward Ca²⁺ current through L-type Ca²⁺ channels (Cav1.2) is balanced by outward K⁺ currents (mainly delayed rectifier IKs). The membrane sits near 0 mV for ~200 ms. Phase 3 (repolarisation) — Ca²⁺ channels inactivate; rapid delayed rectifier (IKr) and slow delayed rectifier (IKs) take over; membrane repolarises. Phase 4 (resting) — K⁺ inward rectifier (IK1) sets the resting potential. Why a long plateau? It serves three concurrent purposes. (1) Long refractory period prevents tetany — the cardiac cell cannot be re-excited until the plateau ends, ensuring time for full ventricular filling between beats. Without this, ventricular tachyarrhythmia could approach a tetanic state, dropping cardiac output to zero. (2) Sustained Ca²⁺ entry triggers CICR — the Ca²⁺ that enters through Cav1.2 during the plateau triggers SR Ca²⁺ release and is itself a substantial contributor to contractile Ca²⁺. Without the plateau, contraction would be feeble. (3) Modulation point for autonomic regulation — sympathetic (PKA-mediated) phosphorylation of Cav1.2 channels increases plateau Ca²⁺ entry → bigger CICR → stronger contraction (positive inotropy). Parasympathetic stimulation increases K⁺ current (IK,ACh) → shortens plateau → reduces Ca²⁺ entry → weakens contraction. Pharmacological corollary — drugs that prolong the cardiac AP (Class III antiarrhythmics like amiodarone, dofetilide) target IKr; loss-of-function mutations in IKs and IKr genes (KCNQ1, KCNH2) cause congenital long-QT syndrome. Drugs that shorten the AP (e.g., digoxin via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase inhibition; β-adrenergic antagonists) modulate it the other way. None of these manoeuvres are relevant to skeletal muscle, where the channel repertoire is different — illustrating how channel pharmacology is tissue-specific because channel expression is tissue-specific. Take-home design lesson: the shape of an action potential is not a universal constant — it is a feature evolved (and selected) to fit a function. Brief APs for tetanic, voluntary striated muscle; long plateau APs for rhythmic, fatigue-free cardiac pumping; even slower, often graded electrical events in unitary smooth muscle for tonic visceral functions. The same Hodgkin–Huxley framework explains all three; the "wiring" in each is what differs [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Costanzo — Cardiac Muscle].

Elaborative explanation: This question crystallises a profound design principle: channel expression is the language by which evolution writes the electrical "personality" of each tissue. The same Na⁺ channels (with subtle isoform differences) produce both skeletal and cardiac upstrokes, but the K⁺ and Ca²⁺ channel complements differ dramatically — and those differences produce all the downstream functional contrast. This logic also applies to neurons: pyramidal cells, Purkinje cells, motor neurons, and dorsal root ganglion neurons each have characteristic AP shapes that reflect their specific channel repertoires, and disease states often involve channel mutations or altered channel expression.

Hints:

1. Skeletal AP is a quick spike (~5 ms); cardiac AP has a long plateau (~200 ms).
2. The plateau is generated by Ca²⁺ entry through Cav1.2 balanced by K⁺ efflux.
3. The plateau prevents tetany, drives CICR, and is the autonomic modulation point.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Describes the brief skeletal AP (~5 ms) and its mechanism (Na⁺ upstroke, K⁺ delayed rectifier repolarisation).
- Describes the cardiac AP phases (0, 1, 2, 3, 4) and the channels for each.
- Explains the plateau (phase 2) as Ca²⁺ entry balanced by K⁺ efflux.
- Articulates three functional purposes of the plateau (tetany prevention, CICR support, autonomic modulation).
- Names a Class III antiarrhythmic (amiodarone, dofetilide) and its target (IKr).
- Mentions long-QT syndrome from IKs/IKr mutations.

A Yellow answer describes the AP shapes but omits the specific channels or the functional rationale for the plateau.

A Red answer claims the two APs are essentially the same or denies that the cardiac plateau prevents tetany.

Common misconceptions:

- "AP shape is universal across all muscle.": Correction: AP shape depends on the channel repertoire, which is tissue-specific; skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle have very different APs.
- "Cardiac muscle can be tetanised by fast pacing.": Correction: the long Ca²⁺-plateau AP and prolonged absolute refractory period make true tetany impossible — a design feature that protects diastolic filling.
