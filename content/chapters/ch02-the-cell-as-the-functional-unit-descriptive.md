## Pass 1 — Comprehension and reasoning

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Explain in your own words why phospholipid molecules spontaneously form bilayers when placed in an aqueous solution, and why this property is the structural basis of every cell membrane in the body.

Model answer: Phospholipids are _amphipathic_ molecules — each contains a polar (hydrophilic) head group and two non-polar (hydrophobic) fatty acyl tails [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. In an aqueous solution, the hydrophobic tails cannot interact favourably with water; their interaction with water is energetically unfavourable, and water molecules around them lose entropy by forming an ordered "cage." The system minimises this energetic cost by orienting phospholipids so that the hydrophobic tails are sequestered from water. In a confined volume with sufficient phospholipid, the most stable arrangement is a bilayer: two leaflets of phospholipids with their hydrophilic heads facing the aqueous solutions on either side, and their hydrophobic tails facing each other in the membrane interior. This arrangement excludes water from the bilayer core while exposing the polar heads to it. The bilayer is therefore self-assembling and self-sealing — small breaches close spontaneously because exposed hydrophobic tails will quickly re-orient. This property is the structural basis of every biological membrane: the plasma membrane, organelle membranes (nuclear envelope, ER, Golgi, mitochondria, lysosomes), and the membranes of secretory vesicles. Without amphipathic lipids, eukaryotic cellular compartmentalisation would not be possible.

Elaborative explanation: The same physical principle operates at every scale. Soap bubbles, micelles, and cell membranes all reflect the spontaneous self-organisation of amphipathic molecules in water. The bilayer is also the reason the membrane is _fluid_ rather than rigid — phospholipids are held in place by hydrophobic interactions, not covalent bonds, so they can diffuse laterally within their leaflet. The energetic cost of trans-leaflet flip-flop is high (the hydrophilic head must transit the hydrophobic core), so flip-flop is rare without dedicated enzymes (flippases).

Hints:

1. Phospholipids have two ends with different relationships to water — what happens when they are dropped into water?
2. The driving force is energetic: water "prefers" to be away from the fatty tails.
3. The geometry that satisfies this for many molecules at once is _two layers stacked tail-to-tail_.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies phospholipids as amphipathic (hydrophilic head + hydrophobic tails).
- Explains the energetic driver: hydrophobic tails are sequestered from water.
- Describes the resulting geometry: hydrophilic heads facing water on both sides; hydrophobic tails meeting in the middle.
- Notes that the bilayer is self-assembling and self-sealing.
- Connects the principle to the structural basis of all biological membranes (plasma + organelle membranes).

A Yellow answer correctly describes amphipathic structure and bilayer geometry but does not articulate the energetic driver, or does not generalise from plasma membrane to other membranes.

A Red answer treats bilayer formation as requiring active machinery (e.g., enzymes), or claims the bilayer holds together because of covalent bonds, or describes the wrong geometry (e.g., heads facing each other in the middle).

Common misconceptions:

- "Bilayers are held together by covalent bonds." Correction: the bilayer is held together by non-covalent hydrophobic interactions; this is what makes it self-assembling and self-sealing.
- "Phospholipids form bilayers because something pushes them together." Correction: bilayer formation is _spontaneous_ — it requires no energy input — because it is the lowest-free-energy arrangement of amphipathic molecules in water.

QUESTION 2
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Why is membrane fluidity functionally important to cells, and what factors determine the fluidity of a given plasma membrane? Explain how a cell could in principle adjust its membrane fluidity in response to environmental change.

Model answer: Membrane fluidity is essential for several functions [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. (1) Lateral diffusion of integral proteins lets receptors cluster with their downstream effectors after ligand binding — this is critical for signal transduction. (2) Membrane fusion events (exocytosis, endocytosis, vesicle trafficking) require local membrane reorganisation, which a rigid membrane cannot achieve. (3) Cell shape change, motility, and cytokinesis depend on the membrane being able to deform. (4) Membrane recycling and repair require flexibility. Two principal factors determine fluidity: _temperature_ — fluidity increases with temperature; and _lipid composition_ — phospholipids with more unsaturated fatty acyl chains pack less tightly (because the cis-double bonds put kinks in the chains), increasing fluidity. Cholesterol acts as a "fluidity buffer": at high temperatures it reduces fluidity by dampening phospholipid motion, and at low temperatures it prevents tight packing of phospholipids and so prevents the membrane from becoming too rigid. Cells can adjust their membrane fluidity by changing the saturated-to-unsaturated fatty acid ratio of their membrane phospholipids — for example, organisms (and cell types) adapted to lower temperatures have more unsaturated fatty acids in their membranes (homeoviscous adaptation), and cells can also remodel their cholesterol content. These are slower adjustments than the transient changes that occur with temperature itself.

Elaborative explanation: Loss of fluidity is functionally damaging — at temperatures below the lipid phase-transition point, membranes become gel-like and lose normal function. Anaesthetics and ethanol increase membrane fluidity, which is part of how they disrupt neural function. In sickle cell disease, abnormal hemoglobin polymerisation distorts membrane mechanics and contributes to the cell's lack of flexibility — a related but distinct concept (mechanical rigidity due to internal contents, not lipid composition).

Hints:

1. Membrane proteins must be able to _move_ to do their jobs — fluidity is what permits this.
2. Two main factors set fluidity at any given moment: temperature and which kind of lipid?
3. The acyl chain property that influences fluidity is whether the chains are _straight_ or _kinked_ — what does saturation determine here?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names at least two functions that depend on membrane fluidity (lateral diffusion of receptors, vesicle trafficking, cell shape change, repair).
- Identifies temperature as a key factor.
- Identifies lipid composition (especially the saturated/unsaturated ratio) as a key factor and explains the kink-in-chain mechanism.
- Mentions cholesterol's role as a "fluidity buffer."
- Notes that cells can adjust fluidity by remodelling lipid composition.

A Yellow answer covers the factors but does not connect them to functional consequences, or omits cholesterol's buffering role.

A Red answer treats the membrane as rigid by default, or inverts the saturated-vs-unsaturated relationship (claiming saturated chains increase fluidity), or attributes fluidity to protein composition only.

QUESTION 3
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Compare the three principal classes of cytoskeletal filaments (microfilaments, intermediate filaments, microtubules) by structure and function. For each class, name the protein subunit, the approximate diameter, and one functional role.

Model answer: The three cytoskeletal classes are distinguished by size, protein subunit, and function [Rapid Review ch.1; Berne ch.1].

_Microfilaments_ are the smallest (5–9 nm in diameter), flexible helical polymers of G-actin (globular actin). They form the cortical layer just beneath the plasma membrane and function in cell shape, motility (e.g., crawling), organelle transport, cytokinesis (the contractile ring during cell division), and muscle contraction (interacting with myosin).

_Intermediate filaments_ are mid-sized (about 10 nm), formed from a heterogeneous family of proteins that differ by cell type — keratins in epithelial cells, vimentin in mesenchymal cells, neurofilaments in neurons, glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) in glial cells, and lamins in the nuclear envelope. Their principal function is mechanical stability under tension; they are particularly important in epithelial cells, where they form desmosomes (cell–cell anchors) and hemidesmosomes (cell–extracellular-matrix anchors).

_Microtubules_ are the largest (25 nm), rigid hollow cylinders of α/β-tubulin dimers. They extend from the centrosome, the major microtubule-organising centre. Functions include intracellular transport (kinesin and dynein motor proteins walk along them carrying cargo), formation of the mitotic spindle that separates chromosomes during cell division, and structural support of cilia and flagella (where the 9 + 2 axoneme of microtubules drives motility).

The three classes have complementary roles: microfilaments handle dynamic, motile, contractile events; intermediate filaments provide passive mechanical resilience; microtubules supply tracks for transport and form the apparatus of cell division.

Elaborative explanation: The clinical correspondences are useful for memory: defects in microfilament regulators are exploited by _Listeria monocytogenes_ for cell-to-cell spread; intermediate-filament gene mutations cause epidermolysis bullosa (keratin defects in skin) and laminopathies (lamin A/C defects causing premature aging or muscular dystrophy); microtubule defects underlie immotile cilia syndrome (Kartagener), and microtubule-targeting drugs (colchicine, vinca alkaloids, paclitaxel) are core to the treatment of gout, leukemias, and solid tumours.

Hints:

1. Three classes; three sizes; three protein subunits.
2. The smallest is the most flexible; the largest is the most rigid.
3. Each class has a different role: motility, mechanical resilience, intracellular transport.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names microfilaments (actin, ~5–9 nm) and gives at least one functional role.
- Names intermediate filaments (~10 nm, heterogeneous proteins) and gives at least one functional role.
- Names microtubules (tubulin, ~25 nm) and gives at least one functional role.
- States the size hierarchy correctly (microfilaments < intermediate filaments < microtubules).
- Optional: mentions that cilia/flagella are built from microtubules and the spindle is made of microtubules.

A Yellow answer correctly names the three classes but mismatches one protein with its filament class, or gets the size hierarchy wrong.

A Red answer reduces all cytoskeletal filaments to a single class, or mismatches multiple protein–filament pairings, or names myosin as a structural cytoskeletal protein (it is a motor protein that walks on actin).

QUESTION 4
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The phospholipid composition of the plasma membrane is asymmetric between the two leaflets — phosphatidylcholine and sphingomyelin predominate in the outer leaflet, while phosphatidylethanolamine, phosphatidylserine, and phosphatidylinositol predominate in the inner leaflet. Why is this asymmetry functionally important? Give two specific examples where the asymmetry matters.

Model answer: Leaflet asymmetry matters because it gives the two faces of the membrane different functional properties [Berne ch.1]. The cytoplasmic face is poised to interact with cytoplasmic signalling and structural machinery; the extracellular face is poised to interact with the external environment, neighbouring cells, and the extracellular matrix. Two specific functional consequences:

_Signal transduction._ Phosphatidylinositol (PI) is concentrated on the inner leaflet. Its inositol head group is the precursor of two important second messengers: inositol trisphosphate (IP₃, which releases Ca²⁺ from the endoplasmic reticulum) and diacylglycerol (DAG, which activates protein kinase C). Phosphorylation of PI to PIP₂ and PIP₃ produces additional signalling lipids that recruit specific proteins to the inner leaflet. PI's location on the cytoplasmic face places it where the relevant kinases, phospholipases, and downstream effectors operate.

_Apoptosis recognition._ Phosphatidylserine (PS) is normally hidden on the inner leaflet. During apoptosis, scramblases redistribute PS to the outer leaflet — _PS exposure_ is one of the canonical "eat-me" signals that allows phagocytes to recognise and clear dying cells without triggering inflammation. The asymmetry is therefore actively maintained in healthy cells (by ATP-dependent flippases) and selectively dismantled in dying cells.

Maintaining the asymmetry against passive flip-flop requires energy (ATP-dependent flippases) — when ATP fails, asymmetry collapses, and PS exposure on the outer leaflet contributes to the recognition of stressed and dying cells.

Elaborative explanation: A third application worth noting: the inner-leaflet enrichment of negatively charged phospholipids (PS, PI) gives the cytoplasmic face a net negative charge, which can influence the binding of positively charged regions of cytoplasmic proteins. This electrostatic asymmetry is exploited in the localisation of several membrane-associated signalling molecules.

Hints:

1. The two leaflets meet two different worlds (cytoplasm and extracellular space). What does each side need to do?
2. One asymmetric lipid is a signalling molecule precursor. Which one, and on which leaflet?
3. The other asymmetric lipid becomes an "eat-me" signal when it appears on the wrong side.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States that asymmetry gives the two leaflets different functional roles.
- Gives at least one specific example: phosphatidylinositol on the inner leaflet → signal transduction (IP₃, DAG).
- Gives a second specific example: phosphatidylserine externalisation → apoptosis recognition.
- States that asymmetry is actively maintained (e.g., by flippases) — it is not a passive equilibrium.

A Yellow answer states asymmetry exists and gives one solid example, but does not provide a second example or does not articulate that asymmetry is actively maintained.

A Red answer treats the leaflets as functionally equivalent, or claims asymmetry has no functional consequence, or inverts the location of either example lipid.

QUESTION 5
Type: comprehension
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Lysosomal hydrolytic enzymes function efficiently inside the lysosomal lumen but are largely inactive in the cytosol. Explain the basis for this difference, and describe why this property is also a _cellular safety feature_.

Model answer: Lysosomal hydrolases — proteases, nucleases, lipases, glycosidases — have _acidic pH optima_: they are most active at pH ~4.5–5 and progressively lose activity as pH rises [Rapid Review ch.1]. The lysosomal lumen is maintained at approximately pH 4.8 by a vacuolar H⁺ pump (V-ATPase) in the lysosomal membrane, so lysosomal enzymes operate near their pH optimum inside the lysosome. The cytosol, in contrast, is at approximately pH 7.1–7.2 — far above the enzymes' optima — so lysosomal hydrolases that escape into the cytosol have very low catalytic activity. This pH-dependent activation is a cellular safety feature in two ways. First, accidental enzyme leakage into the cytosol is contained: a small amount of leaked hydrolase will not cause significant cytosolic damage because it is largely inactive at neutral pH. Second, when lysosomes are damaged (e.g., by oxidative stress, certain drugs, or particulate matter such as silica), large-scale enzyme release into the cytosol can occur — this is one mechanism of programmed cell death (lysosomal membrane permeabilisation triggered cell death). The pH-dependence acts as a kind of "kill switch" that activates the enzymes only when they are properly contained.

Elaborative explanation: The same architectural principle — pH-dependent activation as a containment strategy — is seen with other digestive enzymes. Pancreatic proteases (trypsin, chymotrypsin) are stored as inactive zymogens until they reach the duodenum; gastric pepsin is most active at the very low pH of the stomach lumen and is inactive once it reaches the more neutral small intestine. In each case, enzyme activity is restricted to the compartment where it is needed.

Hints:

1. Enzyme activity depends on pH. Where in the cell is the pH most acidic?
2. Where else in the cell is the pH neutral or near-neutral?
3. What is the consequence if a hydrolase strays from one compartment to the other?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States that lysosomal enzymes have acidic pH optima.
- States the lysosomal pH (~4.5–5) and the cytosolic pH (~7.1–7.2).
- Explains that the enzymes are largely inactive in the neutral cytosol.
- Articulates the safety implication: accidental leakage causes minimal damage because the enzymes are inactivated at cytosolic pH.
- Optional: notes that large-scale lysosomal release can override this safeguard and cause cell death.

A Yellow answer states the pH optima but does not articulate the safety logic, or omits the cytosolic pH comparison.

A Red answer claims lysosomal enzymes are constitutively active regardless of pH, or that the cytosol is acidic, or that lysosomal enzymes function in the gut lumen.

QUESTION 6
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A patient with hereditary spherocytosis presents with anaemia, jaundice, and splenomegaly. Decompose the molecular events that link a defective ankyrin gene to the clinical picture, and explain why splenectomy is functionally curative.

Model answer: The molecular chain in hereditary spherocytosis runs from a single defective gene to a clinical triad through a sequence of consequences [Rapid Review ch.1].

(1) _Genetic defect._ Most cases involve mutations in the _ankyrin_ gene (other affected proteins include spectrin, band 3, and protein 4.2). Ankyrin normally anchors the spectrin cytoskeletal network to the integral membrane protein band 3 on the inner face of the red-cell membrane.

(2) _Cytoskeletal failure._ With defective ankyrin, the spectrin network detaches from the membrane in patches. The membrane patches that lose their cytoskeletal scaffolding are mechanically unsupported.

(3) _Membrane vesiculation._ The unsupported membrane buds off as small vesicles, reducing the cell's surface area. The cell volume stays roughly the same, but the surface-area-to-volume ratio falls.

(4) _Spherical morphology._ The lowest surface-to-volume shape is a sphere; the cell adopts this shape because it has lost membrane in proportion to volume. Spherical red cells are also more rigid than biconcave discs because they cannot deform without increasing internal pressure.

(5) _Splenic destruction._ Spherical, inflexible red cells cannot navigate the narrow gaps in the splenic cords and sinusoids. They are detained, exposed to macrophages, and phagocytosed — extravascular hemolysis.

(6) _Clinical features._ Hemolysis produces unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia (jaundice) and anaemia (low red cell count). The spleen, working overtime, becomes enlarged (splenomegaly). Other features include reticulocytosis (compensatory marrow response), increased mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (the spheres are slightly dehydrated), and over years, pigment gallstones (from chronic bilirubin overload).

(7) _Splenectomy._ Removing the spleen eliminates the site of red-cell destruction. The cells remain spherical (the underlying defect is unchanged), but they survive normally in circulation because the macrophage system in the spleen — which had been the principal removal site — is gone. Patients become asymptomatic, though they retain the membrane defect and the lifelong risk of pigment stones from any residual hemolysis.

Elaborative explanation: This question illustrates a general pattern in cellular pathology: a single molecular defect (in this case, in a cytoskeletal anchoring protein) propagates through structural, mechanical, and physiological consequences to produce a multi-symptom clinical picture. Recognising the chain — molecular → structural → mechanical → physiological → clinical — is essential to understanding inherited disease.

Hints:

1. Start at the molecular defect: what does ankyrin do, and what fails when it's defective?
2. Trace the consequence to membrane structure: what happens to the supporting cytoskeleton?
3. Connect membrane structure to cell shape; cell shape to splenic transit; splenic transit to anaemia.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the function of ankyrin (anchors spectrin to membrane).
- Describes the consequence of ankyrin loss: spectrin detaches, membrane patches lack support, vesicles bud off.
- Connects vesiculation to reduced surface-to-volume ratio and spherical morphology.
- Explains why spherical, rigid cells are destroyed in the spleen.
- Names the clinical features (anaemia, jaundice, splenomegaly) and connects each to the underlying mechanism.
- Explains why splenectomy is curative (removes the destruction site, not the underlying defect).

A Yellow answer correctly traces the chain from defect to clinical features but does not articulate why splenectomy is curative, or omits the surface-to-volume reasoning.

A Red answer claims hereditary spherocytosis is an autoimmune disorder, or attributes the spherical shape to hemoglobin abnormality (sickle cell logic), or states that splenectomy "fixes" the underlying defect.

QUESTION 7
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: In I-cell disease (mucolipidosis II), lysosomal enzymes are _secreted_ from cells instead of being delivered to lysosomes. Decompose the molecular trafficking failure that produces this phenotype, and explain why the disease causes multi-organ dysfunction.

Model answer: I-cell disease arises from a single trafficking failure in the Golgi apparatus, with cascading consequences [Rapid Review ch.1].

(1) _Normal trafficking._ Lysosomal enzymes, like all secretory-pathway proteins, are synthesised on rough ER ribosomes and transit the Golgi apparatus. In the _cis_-Golgi, a phosphorylating enzyme — N-acetylglucosamine-1-phosphate transferase — adds a _mannose-6-phosphate (M6P)_ tag to mannose residues on lysosomal enzyme precursors. This tag is the address: M6P receptors in the _trans_-Golgi recognise it and divert the tagged enzymes into clathrin-coated vesicles destined for endosomes and lysosomes. Untagged secretory proteins continue along the constitutive pathway and are exported from the cell.

(2) _Defect in I-cell disease._ The N-acetylglucosamine-1-phosphate transferase is deficient or absent. Lysosomal enzyme precursors therefore _do not receive the M6P tag_.

(3) _Mistargeting._ Without the M6P address, the lysosomal enzymes are not recognised by M6P receptors. They follow the default fate of unmarked secretory-pathway proteins: they are exported from the cell into the extracellular space.

(4) _Lysosomal hydrolase deficiency._ Lysosomes therefore receive almost none of their normal complement of hydrolytic enzymes. They cannot digest the substrates that ordinarily turn over within them — substrates that arrive via endocytosis, autophagy, or general protein turnover.

(5) _Cellular consequences._ Undigested substrates accumulate within lysosomes and engulfing vesicles, forming the characteristic _cytoplasmic inclusions_ that give the disease its name ("I-cell" = inclusion-cell). Cells become engorged and dysfunctional.

(6) _Multi-organ dysfunction._ Because every cell uses lysosomes for routine turnover, every tissue is potentially affected. The most clinically prominent manifestations involve tissues with high turnover demand or limited tolerance for accumulated substrate — connective tissue (coarse facial features, skeletal abnormalities), heart valves (inclusion accumulation causing valvular dysfunction), brain (developmental delay), and others. Death typically results in childhood from cardiopulmonary complications.

(7) _Diagnostic signature._ Patients have _high_ plasma levels of lysosomal enzymes (because they are secreted instead of stored intracellularly) — the inverse of what one might naively expect for a "lysosomal storage disease." Measurement of plasma lysosomal enzymes is a useful diagnostic test.

Elaborative explanation: The disease elegantly illustrates how a single trafficking defect can produce a generalised storage syndrome. It also clarifies the mechanism behind enzyme replacement therapy for some lysosomal storage diseases — administered enzymes carrying intact M6P tags are taken up by M6P receptors on the cell surface and delivered to lysosomes, replacing missing activity. (Enzyme replacement is more straightforward in non-CNS tissues; brain delivery is limited by the blood–brain barrier.)

Hints:

1. The Golgi adds a specific tag that is the address for lysosomal delivery. What is this tag?
2. What happens to a protein that is intended for lysosomes but lacks the address?
3. Untagged proteins follow the default secretory route — where do they end up?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the M6P tag and its role as the lysosomal address.
- Names the deficient enzyme (the M6P phosphotransferase) and where it acts (Golgi).
- Traces the consequence: missing tag → mistargeting → lysosomal enzyme secretion.
- Explains the cellular pathology: undigested substrate accumulates in cellular inclusions.
- Connects multi-organ involvement to the universal role of lysosomes in cellular turnover.
- Optional: notes the diagnostic finding of elevated plasma lysosomal enzymes.

A Yellow answer traces most of the chain but does not articulate why the disease is multi-organ, or does not identify the specific enzyme deficiency.

A Red answer attributes I-cell disease to a missing lysosomal hydrolase (the typical lysosomal storage disease pattern, not I-cell), or claims the enzymes never leave the ER, or describes mistargeting to mitochondria.

QUESTION 8
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Lysosomal storage diseases (Tay-Sachs, Niemann-Pick, Gaucher, Fabry, Hurler, Hunter, Krabbe, and others) present with strikingly different clinical features despite sharing a common architectural pattern. Explain the unifying architecture, and account for why the clinical presentations differ so much.

Model answer: All lysosomal storage diseases share the same architecture [Rapid Review ch.1].

_The common pattern._ A single hydrolytic enzyme normally resident in the lysosome is deficient (typically due to a recessive genetic mutation). The substrate that this enzyme normally degrades therefore accumulates progressively within lysosomes. Over time the accumulation impairs the function of cells that depend most heavily on turnover of that substrate. Cellular dysfunction produces tissue dysfunction; tissue dysfunction produces clinical disease.

_Why presentations differ._ Three independent variables determine the phenotype.

(1) _Which enzyme is missing._ Each missing enzyme produces a different missing reaction in lipid, glycoprotein, or glycosaminoglycan catabolism — and therefore a different accumulating substrate. Tay-Sachs lacks hexosaminidase A and accumulates GM2 ganglioside. Gaucher lacks β-glucocerebrosidase and accumulates glucocerebroside. Fabry lacks α-galactosidase A and accumulates ceramide trihexoside. Hurler lacks α-L-iduronidase and accumulates dermatan and heparan sulfate. Each substrate is metabolically and physically distinct.

(2) _Where the substrate is normally turned over._ Different substrates are catabolised most actively in different tissues. GM2 ganglioside turns over rapidly in neurons (so Tay-Sachs is predominantly neurodegenerative — cherry-red spot, weakness, blindness, early death). Glucocerebroside turns over in macrophages, especially in liver, spleen, and bone marrow (so Gaucher presents with hepatosplenomegaly and bone disease). Ceramide trihexoside accumulates in vascular endothelium (so Fabry causes renal failure, cardiac disease, and acroparaesthesiae). Glycosaminoglycans accumulate in connective tissue and cornea (so Hurler causes coarse facial features and corneal clouding).

(3) _Severity of the enzyme defect._ Some patients retain partial enzyme activity, producing milder, later-onset phenotypes; others have complete deficiency, producing severe early-onset disease. Hunter syndrome (X-linked) and Hurler syndrome (autosomal recessive) involve different enzymes but produce overlapping phenotypes; within Hurler, allelic variants produce a spectrum from severe to attenuated.

_Therapeutic implications._ Recognising the unifying architecture explains why enzyme replacement therapy works in principle: replacing the missing enzyme stops further substrate accumulation. In practice, replacement is more effective for non-CNS tissues; the blood–brain barrier limits CNS access, which is why the encephalopathic forms (Tay-Sachs, severe Hurler) remain difficult to treat.

Elaborative explanation: The same architectural pattern — _missing enzyme → substrate accumulation in a specific organelle → tissue-specific dysfunction_ — generalises beyond lysosomes. Peroxisomal disorders (Zellweger syndrome, X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy) follow the same pattern with peroxisomes; mitochondrial disorders follow it with mitochondria. The architectural lens turns dozens of disparate-sounding diseases into a small number of comprehensible categories.

Hints:

1. Despite the variety, every disease in the group has the same architectural pattern. What is it?
2. Two variables explain the different clinical pictures: which enzyme is missing, and where its substrate normally turns over.
3. Different substrates have different "home tissues" — that's where the symptoms localise.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the unifying architecture: missing enzyme → substrate accumulation in lysosomes.
- Explains that _which enzyme_ is missing determines _which substrate_ accumulates.
- Explains that _where the substrate normally turns over_ determines _which tissues_ show pathology.
- Provides at least two specific examples linking enzyme → substrate → tissue → clinical features (e.g., Tay-Sachs → neuronal; Gaucher → hepatic/macrophage).
- Optional: discusses severity and partial-deficiency phenotypes.

A Yellow answer correctly identifies the unifying pattern and gives at least one example, but does not articulate that tissue distribution of the substrate determines clinical localisation.

A Red answer claims the diseases are unrelated, or that they all present identically, or that the pattern is gain-of-function (over-activity) rather than loss-of-function.

QUESTION 9
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A patient with Kartagener syndrome presents with the classical clinical triad of bronchiectasis, chronic sinusitis, and situs inversus. Decompose the cytoskeletal defect that underlies this disorder and explain how a single defect produces these three apparently unrelated features. Why does the same defect also cause male infertility?

Model answer: Kartagener syndrome (a form of primary ciliary dyskinesia) arises from a defect in the dynein arms of the ciliary microtubule core [Rapid Review ch.1]. A single architectural problem — defective cilia and flagella — produces a clinical picture that initially looks unrelated.

_The cytoskeletal defect._ Cilia and flagella are built around a microtubule axoneme arranged in a 9 + 2 pattern (nine peripheral microtubule doublets surrounding two central microtubules). Movement is generated by _dynein_ motor proteins on the outer arms of the doublets, which slide adjacent doublets relative to one another. In Kartagener syndrome, the dynein arms are absent or defective, so cilia cannot beat — the structure is present but immotile.

_Bronchiectasis._ Mucociliary clearance is the principal defence mechanism for the lower airways: ciliated epithelium beats mucus (with trapped pathogens and debris) up the bronchial tree to be swallowed or expectorated. With immotile cilia, mucus accumulates, becomes infected (chronic and recurrent bacterial bronchitis), and the inflammatory damage to bronchial walls produces irreversible dilation — bronchiectasis.

_Chronic sinusitis._ The same mucociliary apparatus operates in the paranasal sinuses; immotile cilia cause stasis of sinus secretions, recurrent infections, and chronic inflammation of the sinus mucosa.

_Situs inversus._ Embryonic left–right asymmetry depends on motile cilia at the ventral node of the early embryo. These nodal cilia rotate in a specific pattern, generating a leftward fluid flow that establishes left–right patterning. With immotile nodal cilia, there is no leftward flow and left–right asymmetry is randomised: about 50% of patients have _situs inversus totalis_ (organs reversed left-to-right). Note: situs inversus on its own is not pathological — it is a developmental marker indicating the underlying cilial defect.

_Male infertility._ Sperm motility depends on the flagellar microtubule axoneme — the same architecture as cilia, with the same dynein-driven sliding. With defective dynein arms, sperm flagella are immotile, and sperm cannot reach the ovum. (Female fertility is generally preserved because oocyte transport along the fallopian tube depends on tubal cilia, which in some patients have residual function; reduced fertility is reported but is not absolute.)

_The unifying lesson._ Multiple seemingly unrelated clinical features (lung, sinus, abdomen, gonad) are linked by the _same architectural element_ — the microtubule axoneme of motile cilia and flagella, expressed in many tissues with different functions. A single molecular defect propagates wherever that architectural element is needed.

Elaborative explanation: The diagnostic pearl: any patient with situs inversus and recurrent respiratory tract infections should be evaluated for primary ciliary dyskinesia. Conversely, a patient with bronchiectasis and infertility but normal organ situs may still have ciliary dyskinesia (without the situs inversus, since that occurs in only about half of cases). Definitive diagnosis is by electron microscopy of cilial structure.

Hints:

1. One cytoskeletal element is the underlying problem. Which one, and what specifically about it?
2. Cilia operate in many places — not just airways. Where else?
3. Embryonic development depends on cilia at one specific stage; what happens when those cilia don't beat?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the cytoskeletal defect: dynein-arm dysfunction in the ciliary microtubule axoneme.
- Connects bronchiectasis to impaired mucociliary clearance.
- Connects chronic sinusitis to the same mechanism in sinus mucosa.
- Explains situs inversus through nodal cilia and their role in embryonic left–right patterning.

- States the unifying principle: the same architectural defect appears wherever cilia/flagella are needed.

A Yellow answer correctly explains 2–3 of the four clinical features but misses one (typically the situs inversus / nodal cilia link, which is more obscure).

Stem: A 28-year-old woman has a maternal uncle and maternal grandmother who both died young of an unexplained mitochondrial myopathy. She is concerned about her own and her future children's risk. Explain the inheritance pattern of mitochondrial DNA disorders, and use it to advise on transmission risk: (a) to her own children, (b) to children of her brother, and (c) why mtDNA disorders sometimes show variable severity even within the same family.

Model answer: Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) is inherited _exclusively from the mother_ because the mitochondria of the embryo derive almost entirely from the egg cell; sperm mitochondria are excluded shortly after fertilisation [Rapid Review ch.1]. Inheritance therefore follows a strict maternal pattern: an affected mother transmits the mutation to _all_ her offspring; an affected father transmits to _none_ (through mtDNA — though a father can pass on Mendelian-inherited disorders involving nuclear genes that encode mitochondrial proteins).

_(a) Risk to her own children._ Because the maternal grandmother and maternal uncle are affected, the mutation comes through the maternal lineage. The patient's mother is therefore likely a carrier (or affected), and the patient herself likely carries the mutation. If she is a carrier of the mtDNA mutation, _all_ her children — sons and daughters alike — will inherit it. The risk of transmission is therefore essentially 100% for genotype, though the _severity_ in any given child can vary (see below).

_(b) Risk to her brother's children._ The brother, if a carrier, _will not transmit_ the mutation to any of his children, because he passes only nuclear DNA (mtDNA from his sperm is excluded after fertilisation). The risk of mtDNA transmission to his children is essentially zero (assuming the disorder is mtDNA-encoded).

_(c) Variable severity._ Three factors explain why mtDNA disorders vary in severity even within a family. (i) _Heteroplasmy_: each cell contains many mitochondria, and each mitochondrion contains multiple mtDNA molecules. Carriers usually have a mix of mutant and wild-type mtDNA in each cell ("heteroplasmy"), and the proportion varies between cells, tissues, and individuals. The proportion of mutant mtDNA in a child depends on how the egg's mitochondria are distributed during oogenesis (the _mitochondrial bottleneck_) — this is partly stochastic. (ii) _Threshold effect_: most mitochondrial diseases have a threshold proportion of mutant mtDNA above which cells become dysfunctional. Tissues with high energy demand (brain, heart, muscle) have lower thresholds. (iii) _Tissue distribution_: even at a given heteroplasmy level, the distribution between tissues varies — some children may have most of their mutant mtDNA in muscle (presenting with myopathy), others in nervous tissue (presenting with encephalopathy).

_Counselling implications._ The patient should know that all her children may inherit the mutation but will have unpredictable severity; her brother's children are not at risk through this inheritance route. Confirmatory mtDNA testing of the patient and prenatal counselling (including discussion of heteroplasmy, threshold effects, and reproductive options including donor eggs or mitochondrial replacement therapy where legal) are appropriate.

Elaborative explanation: The maternal-only pattern is one of the most distinctive features of clinical genetics — recognising it on a pedigree (transmission only through females, all offspring of affected mothers at risk, no transmission through affected males) immediately suggests mtDNA inheritance. Disorders following this pattern include Leber hereditary optic neuropathy (LHON), MELAS, MERRF, and mitochondrial myopathy. Distinguishing mtDNA disorders from nuclear-encoded mitochondrial disorders matters because the latter follow ordinary Mendelian patterns and have entirely different recurrence risks.

Hints:

1. Mitochondrial DNA is inherited from one parent only. Which parent?
2. If the proband's brother is also a carrier, what happens when he passes his genome to his children?
3. Within a family, why might siblings inheriting the same mutation have different clinical severity?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States that mtDNA is inherited exclusively maternally.
- Explains transmission risk to the patient's own children: 100% if she is a carrier (genotype), regardless of child sex.
- Explains transmission risk through her brother: zero (he does not pass mtDNA).
- Explains at least one mechanism for variable severity (heteroplasmy and the bottleneck effect, or threshold effect, or tissue distribution).
- Provides clear genetic counselling implications for each scenario.

A Yellow answer correctly states maternal inheritance but does not explain the heteroplasmy / variable severity component, or does not address all three sub-questions.

A Red answer treats mtDNA inheritance as Mendelian (biparental) or as paternal, or claims the brother can transmit the mutation, or denies that severity can vary.

Stem: Eukaryotic cells are characterised by extensive _compartmentalisation_ — many organelles bounded by membranes. Argue why this compartmentalisation is a foundational design principle for complex cells, using at least three specific organelle examples to illustrate the benefit.

Model answer: Compartmentalisation is foundational because it allows incompatible biochemistries to operate simultaneously in the same cell, isolates dangerous activities from sensitive ones, and concentrates reactants for efficiency [Berne ch.1; Rapid Review ch.1].

_The general principle._ A cell that performs only one biochemistry — say, glycolysis — does not need many compartments; bacteria operate this way. A eukaryotic cell, by contrast, simultaneously performs hundreds of incompatible reactions: it must oxidise glucose, synthesise membrane lipids, degrade misfolded proteins, transcribe DNA, secrete proteins, regulate calcium, and many more. Many of these reactions cannot share the same chemical environment without interfering with one another. Compartmentalisation — separating each activity into its own membrane-bounded organelle with its own internal pH, ionic composition, and resident proteins — solves this problem.

_Lysosome._ Lysosomes contain hydrolytic enzymes with acidic pH optima. The enzymes function inside the lysosome at pH ~4.8 but are largely inactive at cytosolic pH. Compartmentalisation here serves as both a _concentration mechanism_ (substrate and enzyme are concentrated together) and a _safety feature_ (enzymes that escape are inactivated by the cytosol's neutral pH).

_Mitochondrion._ The inner mitochondrial membrane houses the electron transport chain, which produces a steep proton gradient across the membrane (the proton-motive force). This gradient drives ATP synthesis. Without compartmentalisation, the proton gradient could not be maintained — protons would diffuse away into the cytosol — and oxidative phosphorylation could not occur. The double-membrane architecture also keeps mitochondrial DNA, ribosomes, and proteins separated from the rest of the cell.

_Endoplasmic reticulum._ The ER provides a membrane-bound compartment whose lumen is continuous with the secretory pathway. Proteins destined for the cell surface, secretion, or other organelles are folded and modified inside the ER lumen, which has its own redox state (more oxidising than the cytosol, favouring disulfide-bond formation). Cytosolic proteins, which cannot tolerate this oxidising environment, are made on free ribosomes in the cytosol — a clear separation enabled by compartmentalisation.

_The unifying lesson._ Each organelle is in effect a "specialty room" optimised for a particular kind of biochemistry: low pH (lysosome), proton gradient (mitochondrion), oxidising lumen (ER). The membrane that bounds each organelle is what makes specialty conditions possible. Compartmentalisation is therefore not merely organisational neatness; it is the architectural prerequisite for a cell that can do many incompatible things at once.

Elaborative explanation: Compartmentalisation is also the basis for _targeted protein delivery_. Each organelle has its own targeting signals — N-terminal signal sequence for ER (and onward through the secretory pathway), mitochondrial targeting sequence, nuclear localisation signal, peroxisomal targeting signal — and each signal is recognised by dedicated machinery. The system as a whole is one of the most elegant features of eukaryotic cell biology.

Hints:

1. What kinds of activities does a eukaryotic cell perform that bacteria don't?
2. Why can incompatible biochemistries not all share the same cytosolic environment?
3. Pick three organelles whose internal environment is _different_ from the cytosol — what is different in each?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Articulates the general principle: compartmentalisation lets incompatible biochemistries coexist in one cell.
- Provides at least three specific organelle examples, each with a different internal environment (e.g., lysosomal pH, mitochondrial proton gradient, ER oxidising lumen).
- For each example, names the specific biochemistry that requires that environment.
- Notes the safety implication for at least one example (typically the lysosome).
- Optional: links compartmentalisation to targeted protein trafficking with organelle-specific targeting signals.

A Yellow answer gives the principle and one or two examples but does not articulate why the _internal environment_ of each organelle is different, or stops at "organelles do different jobs" without explaining why membranes are necessary.

A Red answer treats compartmentalisation as merely a way of organising structures spatially, without addressing the biochemical specialty of each compartment, or gives examples that are not membrane-bounded (e.g., ribosomes).

QUESTION 12
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The plasma membrane is described as a _selectively permeable barrier_. Explain how a single structure can simultaneously serve as both a barrier (excluding most molecules) and a regulated transport interface (permitting some molecules in and others out).

Model answer: The combination of barrier function and selective transport is achieved through the architectural separation of lipid bilayer (the barrier) and embedded proteins (the regulated transporters) [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1; Rapid Review ch.1].

_The barrier._ The phospholipid bilayer has a hydrophobic interior approximately 3 nm thick (the fatty-acyl portion of the 5 nm bilayer, minus the head groups). Most polar and charged molecules — ions, glucose, amino acids, water (to a partial extent) — cannot cross this hydrophobic interior because they cannot shed their hydration shells without enormous energetic cost. The bilayer therefore _passively excludes_ essentially all the substances that the cell needs to regulate (ions, sugars, amino acids, peptides, nucleic acids). At the same time, hydrophobic substances — gases (O₂, CO₂, N₂), small lipid-soluble molecules (steroid hormones, fatty acids), and some uncharged small molecules — _can_ freely diffuse across the bilayer because they dissolve in the hydrophobic core. The bilayer therefore selects for hydrophobic permeants and against hydrophilic ones, by default.

_The regulated transporters._ Embedded in the bilayer are integral membrane proteins that provide _specific_ pathways for selected hydrophilic substances. Three principal classes: (1) _Channels_ — pores that, when open, allow specific ions or molecules to pass down their electrochemical gradients (e.g., voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in axons; aquaporins for water). (2) _Carriers_ — proteins that bind a substance, undergo a conformational change, and release it on the other side; these can move substrates either down their gradient (facilitated diffusion, e.g., GLUT family for glucose) or against it (active transport, when coupled to an energy source). (3) _Pumps_ — ATP-driven carriers that move substances against their gradients (e.g., Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, Ca²⁺-ATPase, H⁺-K⁺-ATPase). Regulation of which transporters are present, in what number, and in what activity state determines what the membrane will permit at any given moment.

_The combination._ The bilayer is the _default barrier_ — it excludes hydrophilic substances. The embedded proteins are _regulated exceptions_ — they create specific, controllable pathways through the otherwise impermeable bilayer. The cell can adjust permeability by inserting or removing transporters, by phosphorylating or dephosphorylating them, by allosteric ligand binding, by membrane potential changes (for voltage-gated channels), and by ligand binding (for ligand-gated channels). The result is a membrane that is mostly impermeable but _selectively, controllably permeable_ to specific substances at specific times.

Elaborative explanation: This dual architecture is what allows the cell to maintain steep ionic gradients across its membrane (low intracellular Na⁺, high intracellular K⁺, low intracellular Ca²⁺) while simultaneously allowing rapid changes (action potentials, secretion, contraction). The same architectural principle — bilayer barrier with embedded regulated transporters — operates at every membrane in the cell, including organelle membranes.

Hints:

1. The bilayer and the embedded proteins do different jobs. What does each do?
2. The bilayer's barrier property comes from a physical chemistry feature — what is it about phospholipid tails that excludes most substances?
3. Selective permeability depends on the proteins, not the lipid. What kinds of proteins serve this function?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the lipid bilayer as the _default barrier_, due to its hydrophobic interior.
- States which substances pass freely (small uncharged hydrophobic molecules — gases, steroids) and which do not (ions, sugars, amino acids).
- Identifies integral membrane proteins as the regulated _transport pathways_.
- Names at least two protein classes (channels, carriers, pumps).
- States that regulation occurs by changing transporter abundance, activity, or gating.

A Yellow answer captures the barrier–transporter division but does not articulate which substances cross the bilayer freely, or does not name protein classes.

A Red answer treats the membrane as uniformly permeable or impermeable, or claims that the lipid bilayer itself does the selective transport, or omits the role of proteins entirely.

QUESTION 13
Type: misconception-targeted
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A first-year medical student says: "All ribosomes do the same thing — they make proteins. It doesn't matter whether the ribosome is free in the cytosol or attached to the rough endoplasmic reticulum." Identify the misconception and correct it. In your answer, explain how the cell decides where a particular protein will be made, and why this decision matters.

Model answer: The misconception is that ribosomal location is irrelevant to protein fate [Berne ch.1; Rapid Review ch.1]. The correction: while _all_ ribosomes catalyse the same biochemical reaction (peptide-bond formation during translation), the _destination_ of the resulting protein depends critically on whether translation occurs on a free cytosolic ribosome or on a ribosome attached to the rough ER. Free cytosolic ribosomes synthesise proteins that remain in the cytosol or are imported post-translationally into the nucleus, mitochondria, or peroxisomes (each via specific targeting sequences). Rough-ER-bound ribosomes synthesise proteins that enter the secretory pathway: secreted proteins, plasma-membrane proteins, and proteins of the ER, Golgi, and lysosomes. The ribosome itself is not pre-specified for one fate or the other; the same ribosome can do either job. _The decision is made early during translation_, by the protein being made.

_How the decision is made._ Many proteins destined for the secretory pathway begin with an N-terminal _signal sequence_ — a short stretch of hydrophobic amino acids that emerges first from the ribosome. The signal sequence is recognised by the _signal recognition particle (SRP)_, a ribonucleoprotein that binds it as it emerges. SRP binding pauses translation and docks the entire ribosome–nascent chain–SRP complex onto the SRP receptor on the rough ER membrane. Translation then resumes with the nascent polypeptide threading directly into the ER lumen. The signal sequence is cleaved during translocation; the rest of the protein is folded and modified in the ER lumen and proceeds through the secretory pathway. Proteins lacking a signal sequence are translated to completion in the cytosol; if they bear a different targeting signal (e.g., a nuclear localisation signal, a mitochondrial targeting sequence, or a peroxisomal targeting signal), they are imported into the relevant organelle post-translationally.

_Why the decision matters._ The cell separates _cytosolic_ and _secretory-pathway_ protein synthesis because the two compartments have different chemistries. Cytosolic proteins fold in a reducing environment with abundant chaperones; secretory-pathway proteins fold in the more oxidising ER lumen, where disulfide bonds form and N-linked glycosylation is added. A protein folded in the wrong compartment would not fold correctly. Mistargeting — the failure to direct a protein to the right compartment — produces clinical disease: I-cell disease is one example (lysosomal enzymes are made on rough ER but missorted because their M6P address tag is missing); cystic fibrosis (with the most common ΔF508 mutation) involves misfolding of CFTR in the ER and degradation rather than delivery to the plasma membrane. Recognising that protein destination depends on initial ribosome location and downstream sorting steps is therefore foundational to understanding both normal cell physiology and many genetic diseases.

Elaborative explanation: The signal-sequence/SRP system is one of the most elegant universal features of eukaryotic biology — the same mechanism operates from yeast to humans. It is also the basis for an important pharmacological observation: bacterial ribosomes use a related but distinct system, which is why some antibiotics (e.g., streptomycin) target bacterial ribosomes specifically without affecting eukaryotic ribosomes.

Hints:

1. The student is right that all ribosomes catalyse the same reaction. What's _not_ the same?
2. What signal at the start of a protein determines whether translation continues on a free ribosome or moves to the rough ER?
3. Cells that secrete many proteins (e.g., plasma cells, exocrine pancreas) have abundant rough ER. Why?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the misconception: that ribosomal location does not affect protein fate.
- Corrects: free ribosomes make cytosolic / nuclear / mitochondrial / peroxisomal proteins; rough-ER-bound ribosomes make secretory-pathway proteins.
- Names the signal sequence and SRP system as the mechanism that decides ribosome location.
- Explains why the compartments matter: different folding chemistries (oxidising ER lumen vs reducing cytosol).
- Provides at least one clinical consequence of mistargeting (e.g., I-cell disease, CFTR ΔF508 misfolding).

A Yellow answer correctly distinguishes the two ribosome populations and their products but does not explain the SRP-mediated mechanism, or does not connect to clinical consequence.

A Red answer agrees with the student, or attributes the difference between free and bound ribosomes to differences in the ribosomes themselves rather than to the proteins they translate.

QUESTION 14
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The cytoskeleton is universal in eukaryotic cells but is specialised differently in different cell types. Compare the cytoskeleton of the _red blood cell_, the _intestinal epithelial cell_, and the _skeletal muscle fibre_. For each, explain which cytoskeletal class is most prominent and why that specialisation suits the cell's function.

Model answer: Each of the three cell types has a cytoskeleton specialised to its function [Rapid Review ch.1; Berne ch.1].

_Red blood cell._ The dominant cytoskeletal element is a _cortical network of spectrin and actin_ anchored to the inner face of the plasma membrane via ankyrin and band 3. The network has a quasi-hexagonal lattice geometry. This specialisation suits the red cell's job of squeezing through capillary microvasculature: the spectrin lattice provides mechanical resilience under repeated deformation while remaining flexible enough to allow the cell to be folded into narrow capillaries. Microtubules and intermediate filaments are essentially absent in mature red cells, which have lost their nucleus and almost all internal organelles. The clinical correspondence: defects in this lattice (ankyrin in hereditary spherocytosis, β-spectrin in hereditary elliptocytosis) produce mechanically compromised red cells that cannot survive splenic transit.

_Intestinal epithelial cell._ All three cytoskeletal classes are prominent, each in a specialised role. Microfilaments form the core of _microvilli_ on the apical surface, increasing the surface area for absorption. Intermediate filaments (specifically keratins) form a dense network anchored at _desmosomes_ (cell–cell junctions) and _hemidesmosomes_ (cell–basement-membrane junctions), giving the epithelial sheet its mechanical resilience under shear stress in the intestinal lumen. Microtubules run apex-to-base and serve as tracks for vesicle trafficking — for example, of digestive enzymes and absorptive transporters. The combination is what allows the cell to maintain polarity (different apical and basolateral membrane compositions), absorb efficiently, and resist mechanical disruption.

_Skeletal muscle fibre._ The dominant element is a _highly organised actin–myosin contractile apparatus_ arranged into sarcomeres — the most specialised use of the microfilament system in the body. Z-discs (actin-anchoring structures), I-bands, A-bands, and M-lines define a regular repeating unit of actin (thin filaments) interdigitating with myosin (thick filaments). Sliding of the two filament systems past each other, driven by ATP-dependent myosin power strokes, produces contraction. Intermediate filaments (desmin) connect adjacent Z-discs and link sarcomeres to the cell membrane and to neighbouring myofibrils, distributing force evenly. Microtubules play a smaller role, important during muscle development and nuclear positioning.

_The unifying lesson._ The cytoskeleton's three classes form a kit that different cell types assemble in different proportions and arrangements. Red cells need mechanical resilience under deformation; epithelial cells need polarity, attachment, and organised vesicle traffic; muscle cells need a contractile apparatus. The same molecular building blocks produce profoundly different functional outcomes through specialised arrangement.

Elaborative explanation: The same principle generalises to other cell types. Neurons have specialised microtubule arrays running the length of axons (carrying organelles via kinesin and dynein) and a dense actin cortex at synapses. Cardiomyocytes have a contractile apparatus similar to skeletal muscle but with intercalated discs that link adjacent cells through desmosomes and gap junctions. Recognising the general pattern — same toolkit, different specialised arrangements — is the most economical way to understand cytoskeletal diversity.

Hints:

1. Red blood cells need to deform repeatedly. What kind of cytoskeletal arrangement does this require?
2. Epithelial cells stack into sheets and have polarised apical and basal surfaces. Which cytoskeletal classes contribute to each function?
3. Muscle cells _contract_. Which cytoskeletal class becomes the contractile apparatus, and how is it organised?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Red cell: dominant cortical spectrin–actin lattice anchored by ankyrin; provides mechanical resilience for repeated deformation.
- Intestinal epithelial cell: actin in microvilli (apical), keratin intermediate filaments at desmosomes/hemidesmosomes, microtubules as tracks for vesicle trafficking.
- Skeletal muscle: actin–myosin sarcomeres for contraction, desmin intermediate filaments connecting sarcomeres.
- Connects each specialised arrangement to the cell's function (deformation / barrier-and-absorption / contraction).
- Optional: states the unifying principle that the cytoskeleton's three classes form a kit assembled differently by each cell type.

A Yellow answer gets two of the three cell types right but makes an error in one (e.g., names tubulin instead of actin in the red cell, or misses desmin in muscle).

A Red answer treats all three cells as having the same cytoskeleton, or names the wrong dominant cytoskeletal element in two or more cells, or misidentifies which protein carries the contractile function in muscle.

Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze

Stem: The Singer-Nicolson _fluid mosaic model_ (1972) describes the plasma membrane as a fluid lipid bilayer with proteins free to diffuse laterally. The _lipid raft_ concept (1990s onward) has refined this picture. Explain what lipid rafts are, and how the existence of rafts both extends and complicates the original fluid mosaic model.

Model answer: The fluid mosaic model captured two foundational properties of the membrane: it is _fluid_ (lipids and proteins diffuse laterally) and it is a _mosaic_ (heterogeneous mixture of components) [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. Subsequent decades of biophysical and biochemical work showed that the membrane is more _organised_ than the original model suggested. Lipid rafts are one important refinement.

_What lipid rafts are._ Lipid rafts are small, dynamic membrane microdomains enriched in _sphingomyelin_ and _cholesterol_, which together pack more tightly than surrounding glycerophospholipids and form a more ordered, gel-like phase ("liquid-ordered" or Lo phase) within the otherwise more fluid ("liquid-disordered" or Ld phase) bulk membrane. Rafts often contain specific protein cargoes — many GPI-anchored proteins, some signalling receptors, and various kinases — which are excluded from non-raft regions. Rafts are typically tens of nanometres across and short-lived (millisecond to second timescale), although they can be stabilised and enlarged by protein–protein interactions or by clustering with cytoskeletal elements.

_How rafts extend the fluid mosaic model._ Rafts add three layers of organisation to the membrane that the original model did not capture. (1) _Lateral heterogeneity_: components are not uniformly mixed but partition into raft and non-raft regions. (2) _Functional clustering_: rafts concentrate signalling components into the same patch, increasing the efficiency of signal transduction by making relevant receptors, transducers, and effectors collide more frequently. (3) _Cargo selection in trafficking_: raft and non-raft regions can be sorted into different vesicles during endocytosis and exocytosis (e.g., apical sorting in epithelial cells uses raft-associated cargoes).

_How rafts complicate the model._ The classic fluid mosaic predicts that membrane components are free to diffuse laterally and mix randomly. Rafts show that this freedom is constrained: some components are concentrated in specific microdomains, and those microdomains can be relatively stable. The membrane is therefore _partially mixed and partially organised_ — a refinement, not a replacement of the original model. Many features of the fluid mosaic remain correct: most lipids and proteins do diffuse laterally; the bilayer is fluid; trans-leaflet flip-flop is rare. What changes is the assumption that diffusion is unrestricted: cytoskeletal anchoring (the actin cortex limits protein diffusion in patches), lipid–lipid interactions (rafts), and lipid–protein interactions (palmitoylation targets proteins to rafts) all impose order on the apparent fluidity.

_Clinical and pharmacological relevance._ Rafts are important targets for several pathogens — HIV uses rafts for assembly and budding; cholera and tetanus toxins use raft-associated gangliosides for cellular entry. Statins, by lowering membrane cholesterol, can disrupt raft function and affect raft-dependent signalling pathways — one of several proposed mechanisms for the pleiotropic clinical effects of statins beyond LDL lowering.

Elaborative explanation: The history of the fluid mosaic model is itself instructive. It was a _correct_ model that captured the essential physical chemistry of the membrane in 1972. The raft refinement does not refute it; it adds detail that became visible only with newer techniques (single-particle tracking, super-resolution microscopy, biochemical detergent fractionation). This pattern — a foundational model later refined by additional layers of complexity — is common in biology, and physiologists should expect models to be refined rather than abandoned as new data accumulate.

Hints:

1. The original fluid mosaic model treated lipids and proteins as freely diffusing. What feature of the real membrane does this miss?
2. Rafts are made of two specific lipids — which two? — that pack tightly compared with the surrounding membrane.
3. Rafts cluster certain proteins together. What functional advantage does this clustering provide?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines lipid rafts: sphingomyelin- and cholesterol-enriched microdomains, more tightly packed than the surrounding membrane.
- States that rafts contain specific protein cargoes (often GPI-anchored proteins or signalling proteins).
- Explains how rafts extend the fluid mosaic model: lateral heterogeneity, functional clustering, cargo sorting.
- Articulates that rafts _refine_ rather than refute the fluid mosaic — most of the original model remains correct.
- Optional: gives a clinical or pharmacological example (HIV, cholera toxin, statins).

A Yellow answer correctly defines rafts and gives one example of how they extend the model but does not articulate that the membrane is now understood as _partially mixed and partially organised_ (a refinement).

A Red answer treats rafts as identical to the bulk membrane, or claims that the fluid mosaic model is wrong, or describes rafts as separate vesicles rather than membrane microdomains.
