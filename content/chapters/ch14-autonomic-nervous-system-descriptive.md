The functional logic of these differences:

Sympathetic — designed for systemic mobilisation:

- Short pre + long post (with chain ganglia) → broad output from limited preganglionic neurons.
- High divergence ratio → whole-body coordinated response.
- Adrenal medulla as modified ganglion → blood-borne mobilisation reaching every organ with appropriate receptors.
- Catecholamines have multiple receptor subtypes (α, β1, β2, β3) — different organs respond differently to same circulating hormone.
- Net effect: rapidly mobilise body resources for action (HR ↑, CO ↑, BP ↑, glucose ↑, FFA ↑, bronchodilate, redirect blood to muscles, dilate pupils, suppress GI).

Parasympathetic — designed for localised effects:

- Long pre + short post (with target-near ganglia) → discrete control.
- Low divergence → fine local control.
- Single transmitter (ACh) at single receptor family (muscarinic) — but with 5 subtypes for tissue specificity.
- Net effect: organ-specific responses (e.g., vagal slowing of heart without affecting other organs simultaneously).

Enteric — designed for autonomous gut control:

- ~100 million neurons — comparable to spinal cord.
- Functionally autonomous; can run gut even with extrinsic denervation.
- Multiple transmitters allow local fine control of motility, secretion, blood flow.

Stem: Why is the adrenal medulla called a "modified sympathetic ganglion"? Discuss its embryology, innervation, and the consequences of its hormonal output.

Model answer: The adrenal medulla has unique features that justify the "modified sympathetic ganglion" designation, while differing in important ways from typical ganglia.

Embryology:

- Adrenal cortex (steroid hormones) — Mesoderm.
- Adrenal medulla — Neural crest origin, like all postganglionic sympathetic neurons.
- Chromaffin cells migrate from neural crest, become incorporated into the developing adrenal gland.
- This shared embryology with sympathetic ganglia is the basis for the "modified ganglion" view.

Innervation:

- Preganglionic sympathetic axons from T5–T11 (greater splanchnic nerve) → directly innervate chromaffin cells.
- NO postganglionic neurons in the medulla — chromaffin cells ARE the postganglionic equivalent.
- Preganglionic axons release ACh on nicotinic receptors of chromaffin cells.

Cell types and output:

Chromaffin cells secrete catecholamines into adrenal venous blood:

- ~80% adrenaline (epinephrine).
- ~20% noradrenaline (norepinephrine).
- Small amounts of dopamine, opioid peptides, chromogranin A, neuropeptide Y.

The dominance of adrenaline reflects the activity of phenylethanolamine-N-methyltransferase (PNMT), which converts NE → adrenaline. PNMT is highly expressed in chromaffin cells (induced by glucocorticoids — high cortisol from adrenal cortex bathes medullary cells) and rare elsewhere.

Why does this matter?

1. Hormonal vs neurotransmitter action:

- Postganglionic sympathetic terminals release NE locally at synapses → focal, brief effect (synaptic timescale: milliseconds; reuptake by NET dominates).
- Adrenal medulla releases catecholamines into blood → systemic, longer-lasting effect (reaches every tissue with appropriate receptors via circulation; clearance via COMT/MAO over minutes).

2. Different receptor profile:

- NE — α1 > α2 > β1 > β2 (almost no β2 effect at typical concentrations).
- Adrenaline — α1, α2, β1, β2, β3 — broader effects.
- This means adrenaline produces additional β2-mediated effects (vasodilation in skeletal muscle/coronary; bronchodilation; gluconeogenesis; lipolysis) that NE alone cannot fully produce.

3. Different roles in stress response:

- Sympathetic neural (postganglionic): Acute, focal, immediate (split-second timing for fight/flight).
- Adrenal medullary: Sustained systemic mobilisation (minutes to hours of catecholamine action).

Together they produce the complete sympathoadrenal response to stress.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Describe the receptor pharmacology of the autonomic nervous system. How do α1, α2, β1, β2, β3, M1-M5, and N receptors mediate effects?

Model answer: ANS uses two transmitters (ACh, NA) acting on multiple receptor classes. _Adrenergic_ (G-protein-coupled): α1 (Gq -> IP3/DAG -> Ca²⁺) - vasoconstriction, mydriasis, urinary sphincter contraction, hepatic glycogenolysis; α2 (Gi -> reduced cAMP) - presynaptic NE inhibition, CNS sympathetic outflow reduction (clonidine target), platelet aggregation, pancreatic insulin inhibition; β1 (Gs -> cAMP -> PKA) - cardiac inotropy/chronotropy/lusitropy, renin release; β2 (Gs -> cAMP) - bronchodilation, vasodilation in skeletal muscle, uterine relaxation, hepatic glycogenolysis, insulin release, K⁺ uptake into cells; β3 (Gs) - lipolysis, brown fat thermogenesis, urinary detrusor relaxation. _Cholinergic_: nicotinic (ligand-gated cation channels) - NN at autonomic ganglia and adrenal medulla, NM at NMJ; muscarinic (G-protein-coupled): M1 (Gq) - CNS, ENS; M2 (Gi) - cardiac (negative chrono/inotropy via reduced cAMP); M3 (Gq) - smooth muscle contraction (gut, bladder detrusor, bronchial), gland secretion, vasodilation (NO via endothelial M3); M4 (Gi) - CNS, basal ganglia; M5 (Gq) - CNS dopaminergic, cerebral vessel dilation. Pharmacological exploitation: β1 antagonists (metoprolol, bisoprolol) for HF/IHD; β2 agonists (salbutamol) for asthma; α1 blockers (doxazosin) for BPH/HTN; α2 agonist (clonidine) for HTN/withdrawal; M3 antagonists (tiotropium, ipratropium) for COPD/asthma; M3 antagonists (oxybutynin) for overactive bladder; nicotinic antagonists at NMJ (curare-type) for paralysis [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Ganong ch.5].

Elaborative explanation: ANS receptor pharmacology is the foundation of large swathes of clinical medicine. The challenge for student and clinician is knowing which subtype mediates which effect, because tissue-selective drugs depend on this. The same chemical (NA) can produce vasoconstriction (α1) or vasodilation (β2) depending on receptor distribution.

Hints:

1. Adrenergic: α1, α2, β1, β2, β3 - all GPCRs.
2. Cholinergic: nicotinic (ligand-gated cation channel), muscarinic M1-M5 (GPCRs).
3. Drug specificity is achieved by receptor subtype targeting.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names ≥ 4 adrenergic and ≥ 3 muscarinic subtypes with their primary functions.
- Identifies G-protein coupling (Gs, Gi, Gq) for at least 3 receptors.
- Maps ≥ 3 drugs to receptor subtypes (β1 blocker, β2 agonist, α1 blocker, M3 antagonist).
- Distinguishes nicotinic (ligand-gated) from muscarinic (GPCR).
- Notes tissue-selective effects from differential receptor expression.

A Yellow answer covers some receptors but misses subtype specificity.

A Red answer treats all adrenergic or all cholinergic effects as identical.

Common misconceptions:

- "All α receptors do the same thing.": Correction: α1 contracts smooth muscle (Gq); α2 inhibits NE release presynaptically (Gi) and reduces sympathetic outflow centrally - opposite functions.
- "Beta-blockers are interchangeable.": Correction: β1-selective (metoprolol, bisoprolol) preferred for cardiac indications to avoid β2-mediated bronchospasm; non-selective (propranolol) blocks both.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe central autonomic control: what brain regions integrate autonomic responses, and how do they coordinate sympathetic and parasympathetic outflow?

Model answer: Autonomic nervous system is centrally controlled by a hierarchy of integrative regions. (1) _Cortex_ (especially insular, medial prefrontal, anterior cingulate) - emotional and cognitive influence on autonomic state (eg, blushing with embarrassment, heart rate increase with anxiety). (2) _Limbic system_ (amygdala, hippocampus) - stress responses, fear/flight, fight-or-flight integration with autonomic, endocrine (HPA axis), and behavioural outputs. (3) _Hypothalamus_ - master integrator: paraventricular nucleus (PVN) coordinates ANS + endocrine + behavioural responses; suprachiasmatic nucleus drives circadian rhythms; arcuate/lateral hypothalamus integrate metabolic state with autonomic outflow; preoptic area thermoregulation. (4) _Brainstem_ - executive autonomic centres: rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM) - tonically active sympathetic premotor neurons (the brainstem's "sympathetic generator"); nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) - primary integrator of visceral afferent input (baroreceptors, chemoreceptors, cardiac, gut); nucleus ambiguus and dorsal motor nucleus of vagus - parasympathetic preganglionic outflow; ventral medullary respiratory groups; PAG - integrates autonomic with pain/defensive responses. (5) _Spinal cord_ - segmental sympathetic outflow (T1-L2 IML); sacral parasympathetic outflow (S2-S4); spinal reflex circuits (genital, micturition, defaecation) modulated by descending autonomic pathways. Coordination of sympathetic/parasympathetic: (a) Reciprocal - typically one increases as the other decreases (eg, exercise: ↑ sympathetic, ↓ vagal tone -> tachycardia); (b) Selective - some organs receive only one (sweat glands, peripheral vessels mostly sympathetic); (c) Co-activation in some functions (sexual response - parasympathetic erection + sympathetic ejaculation); (d) Visceral afferent feedback drives reflex adjustments (baroreflex, chemoreflex, lung volume reflexes). Clinical: hypothalamic lesions disrupt thermoregulation, satiety, sleep; brainstem stroke can produce autonomic instability, sleep apnoea, neurogenic shock; spinal cord injury above T6 produces autonomic dysreflexia (paroxysmal hypertension to noxious stimulus below the lesion). Key principle: ANS is _organised hierarchically_ with cortex/limbic giving emotional/cognitive context, hypothalamus integrating with endocrine and metabolic state, brainstem providing rhythm and reflex control, and spinal cord executing segmental outflow [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: Central autonomic control reveals that the ANS is not just "automatic" peripheral reflexes but a _centrally orchestrated system_ integrating cognition, emotion, and homeostasis. The hypothalamus is the master integrator; the brainstem provides moment-to-moment reflex control; cortex and limbic structures shape the autonomic state by top-down modulation. Lesions at each level produce characteristic autonomic syndromes.

Hints:

1. Hierarchical: cortex/limbic -> hypothalamus -> brainstem -> spinal cord.
2. Hypothalamus = master integrator (PVN, SCN, etc).
3. Brainstem RVLM, NTS, nucleus ambiguus.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names cortex, limbic, hypothalamus, brainstem, and spinal cord as the hierarchy.
- Identifies hypothalamus (PVN, SCN) as master integrator.
- Names ≥ 2 brainstem nuclei (RVLM, NTS, nucleus ambiguus) and their roles.
- Distinguishes reciprocal vs selective vs co-activated patterns of sympathetic/parasympathetic interaction.
- Names ≥ 1 clinical syndrome (autonomic dysreflexia, neurogenic shock).

A Yellow answer covers central control but misses the hierarchy or specific nuclei.

A Red answer treats ANS as purely peripheral reflexes.

Common misconceptions:

- "ANS is automatic and unaffected by cortex.": Correction: Cortex (insula, cingulate) and limbic structures (amygdala) produce strong autonomic effects in emotion, stress, and cognition - the basis of psychosomatic medicine and biofeedback.
- "Hypothalamus only controls hormones.": Correction: It integrates ANS, endocrine, and behavioural outputs simultaneously - a single integrative organ for homeostasis.

QUESTION 5
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the baroreflex. How does it operate, and what happens when it fails?

Model answer: _Baroreflex_ - the rapid neural feedback regulating arterial blood pressure. Sensors: stretch receptors in carotid sinus (CN IX, glossopharyngeal) and aortic arch (CN X, vagus); fire proportionally to BP. Afferent: input to nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) in medulla. Central processing: NTS excites caudal ventrolateral medulla (CVLM, inhibitory) -> CVLM inhibits rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM, sympathetic premotor); NTS also excites nucleus ambiguus and dorsal motor nucleus of vagus (parasympathetic). Efferent: sympathetic outflow (cardiac, vascular) and parasympathetic outflow (cardiac via vagus). Effects: rising BP -> increased baroreceptor firing -> reduced sympathetic outflow + increased parasympathetic outflow -> bradycardia, vasodilation, reduced contractility -> BP falls back toward set-point. Falling BP -> opposite. Time scale: seconds. Set-point: ~ 90-110 mm Hg MAP normally; resets in chronic hypertension to a higher set-point (right shift). Failure modes and clinical syndromes: (1) _Baroreflex failure_ (after bilateral carotid endarterectomy, neck radiation, lesions of NTS) - severe BP lability with surges to 200+ and crashes; treatment with clonidine to dampen sympathetic outflow centrally. (2) _Orthostatic hypotension_ - failure to adjust BP on standing; common in elderly, diabetic autonomic neuropathy, Parkinson disease (especially MSA), pure autonomic failure; treat with non-pharmacological measures (rise slowly, compression stockings, salt) and drugs (fludrocortisone, midodrine, droxidopa, pyridostigmine). (3) _Carotid sinus hypersensitivity_ - exaggerated bradycardia/hypotension to mild carotid stimulation (collar pressure, head turning); causes syncope; pacemaker for cardioinhibitory type. (4) _Vasovagal (neurocardiogenic) syncope_ - inappropriate baroreflex activation in response to triggers (prolonged standing, emotion, pain) -> bradycardia + vasodilation -> syncope; treat by avoidance, hydration, salt, sometimes midodrine, fludrocortisone, β-blocker, isometric counter-pressure manoeuvres. (5) _Neurogenic shock_ - acute spinal cord injury above T6 -> loss of sympathetic outflow -> hypotension + bradycardia (vs hypovolaemic shock with tachycardia); treat with fluids, vasopressors. (6) _Autonomic dysreflexia_ (high cord injury) - noxious stimulus below lesion (eg, distended bladder) -> reflex sympathetic surge unopposed by descending modulation -> severe paroxysmal hypertension; can cause stroke; treat by removing trigger and short-acting antihypertensive (nifedipine, GTN). Pharmacology: β-blockers reduce baroreflex sensitivity less than clonidine; ACE-I/ARBs preserve baroreflex; many drugs that lower BP do so through reduced central sympathetic outflow [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14].

Elaborative explanation: The baroreflex is a textbook example of negative feedback control. Its failure modes (rare but clinically important) and pharmacological exploitation (clonidine, β-blockers) illustrate why understanding the loop matters. Vasovagal syncope - the most common cause of syncope - is essentially baroreflex misbehaviour.

Hints:

1. Sensors: carotid sinus + aortic arch.
2. NTS -> CVLM -> RVLM (sympathetic) and nucleus ambiguus (parasympathetic).
3. Failure produces orthostatic hypotension, syncope, neurogenic shock.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the sensors, afferents, integration, and efferents of the baroreflex.
- Identifies NTS as the afferent integration point.
- Names ≥ 2 baroreflex failure syndromes (orthostatic hypotension, syncope, neurogenic shock, autonomic dysreflexia).
- Mentions pharmacology (β-blocker, clonidine, midodrine, fludrocortisone).
- Notes the seconds-scale time course.

A Yellow answer covers the baroreflex anatomy but misses the failure syndromes.

A Red answer treats syncope and hypotension as isolated entities.

Common misconceptions:

- "All syncope is cardiac.": Correction: Vasovagal syncope (baroreflex inappropriate activation) is the most common cause; differentiate by history, ECG, tilt-table, careful evaluation.
- "Baroreflex set-point is fixed.": Correction: It resets in chronic hypertension (right shift); aggressive BP lowering can drop pressures below the new set-point and cause hypoperfusion.

Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: Describe diabetic autonomic neuropathy. What are its clinical manifestations and management implications?

Model answer: _Diabetic autonomic neuropathy (DAN)_ - common complication of diabetes (~ 50% with > 10 years duration), affecting cardiovascular, GI, GU, sudomotor, and pupillary function. Pathophysiology: hyperglycaemia-induced microvascular damage to vasa nervorum, accumulation of advanced glycation end-products, polyol pathway flux, oxidative stress -> small-fibre nerve dysfunction (autonomic + small-fibre sensory). Cardiovascular DAN: (1) Resting tachycardia (loss of vagal tone -> baseline HR 90-100); (2) Reduced HR variability on deep breathing (early sign); (3) Orthostatic hypotension; (4) Silent myocardial ischaemia (loss of afferent pain signals); (5) Increased mortality (~ 5-fold increase in cardiovascular events); (6) Prolonged QT, sudden cardiac death risk. GI DAN: (1) _Gastroparesis_ - delayed gastric emptying -> postprandial fullness, nausea, vomiting, erratic glycaemic control; treatment with prokinetics (metoclopramide - limited duration due to tardive dyskinesia risk; domperidone; erythromycin), small frequent low-fat low-fibre meals, gastric pacemaker in refractory cases; (2) Diabetic diarrhoea (often nocturnal); (3) Faecal incontinence; (4) Constipation. GU DAN: (1) _Erectile dysfunction_ - early sign; multifactorial (autonomic + vascular + endothelial); treat with PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil), intracavernosal therapy, prostheses; (2) Bladder dysfunction (overflow incontinence, recurrent UTIs); (3) Female sexual dysfunction. Sudomotor: (1) Distal anhidrosis -> dry feet (cracks, infection risk); (2) Compensatory hyperhidrosis on trunk/face; (3) Gustatory sweating after eating. Pupillary: reduced response to light, especially dilation in dark; impaired night vision adaptation. Diagnosis: clinical features + bedside cardiovascular autonomic tests (HR response to deep breathing, Valsalva, standing); 24-h ambulatory BP; gastric emptying scintigraphy; urodynamic studies. Management: (1) Tight glycaemic control - prevents/slows progression in type 1 (DCCT); modest benefit in type 2; (2) Symptomatic - as above; (3) Cardiovascular protection - statins, ACE-I, BP control; (4) Foot care, infection prevention; (5) Avoid drugs that worsen autonomic failure (anticholinergics, opioids, TCAs in some cases). Prognosis: cardiovascular DAN strongly predicts mortality; gastroparesis significantly impacts QoL and glycaemic control [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: DAN illustrates how diabetes - apparently a metabolic disease - has profound consequences for autonomic function across organs. The cardiovascular component is the most life-threatening; the GI/GU components have major QoL impact. Recognising DAN allows targeted symptom management and informs perioperative and pharmacological choices.

Hints:

1. DAN affects CV, GI, GU, sudomotor, pupillary systems.
2. Reduced HR variability - early CV sign.
3. Gastroparesis disrupts glycaemic control.

Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 50-year-old presents with episodic severe hypertension, headaches, sweating, and palpitations. Decompose phaeochromocytoma.

Model answer: _Phaeochromocytoma_ - rare catecholamine-producing tumour of chromaffin cells, most often arising from adrenal medulla; ~10% extra-adrenal (paragangliomas, often along sympathetic chain or organ of Zuckerkandl). The "rule of 10s" (now revised): ~10% extra-adrenal, ~10% bilateral, ~10% malignant, ~25-40% familial (genetic syndromes - higher than originally thought). Genetics: MEN2A/2B (RET), VHL, NF1, succinate dehydrogenase mutations (SDH-A, B, C, D, AF2), TMEM127, MAX, FH. Pathophysiology: chromaffin cells synthesise and secrete catecholamines (NA, adrenaline, dopamine); episodic or sustained release produces clinical syndrome. Clinical features: (1) _Triad_ - episodic headache, sweating, palpitations - present in ~50% of cases; (2) Hypertension - often severe and paroxysmal; sometimes orthostatic (variable BP); (3) Other - tremor, anxiety, pallor, weight loss, glucose intolerance, cardiomyopathy (catecholamine-induced), fever; (4) Triggers - postural change, palpation of abdomen, anaesthetic induction, surgery, certain drugs. Differential: thyrotoxicosis, anxiety disorder, carcinoid, drug effects (cocaine, amphetamines, MAOIs), essential hypertension. Diagnosis: (1) _Plasma free metanephrines_ (or 24-h urine fractionated metanephrines) - first-line; high sensitivity > 95%; metanephrine = methylated metabolite, more reliable than catecholamines themselves; (2) Imaging localisation - CT or MRI abdomen/pelvis (T2 hyperintense "lightbulb" appearance on MRI); MIBG scintigraphy for occult or extra-adrenal/metastatic; PET-CT (¹⁸F-FDG, ⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE for SDH mutations); (3) Genetic testing in selected patients. Management: (1) _α-blockade FIRST_ - phenoxybenzamine (irreversible, non-selective) or doxazosin (selective α1) for 2-3 weeks pre-op to control BP and expand intravascular volume; (2) _β-blockade only after α-blockade_ - prevents unopposed α-mediated vasoconstriction (which can cause hypertensive crisis); (3) Volume expansion with high-salt diet pre-op; (4) Definitive treatment - laparoscopic adrenalectomy in experienced centres; (5) Intra-op anaesthetic management - careful monitoring, vasodilators, vasopressors as needed; (6) Post-op - hypotension common (sudden withdrawal of catecholamines + alpha blockade); glucose monitoring; (7) Lifelong follow-up - 10-15% recur or develop second tumours; annual metanephrines; family screening. Crisis management (hypertensive crisis): IV phentolamine (α-blocker) or sodium nitroprusside; never β-blockade alone [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14].

Elaborative explanation: Phaeochromocytoma is a classical case of "rare but important" - it is uncommon but the diagnosis is life-saving (cardiovascular catastrophe risk) and is one of the great clinical pharmacology lessons (α-block before β-block to prevent hypertensive crisis from unopposed α-mediated vasoconstriction). Genetic counselling is increasingly important.

Hints:

1. Catecholamine-secreting chromaffin tumour, ~25-40% familial.
2. Diagnose with plasma metanephrines.
3. α-block first, then β-block; never β-block alone.

Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the role of the autonomic nervous system in the cardiovascular response to exercise. How are central command, exercise pressor reflex, and arterial baroreflex coordinated?

Model answer: Exercise produces large coordinated cardiovascular adjustments through three interacting neural mechanisms. (1) _Central command_ - feedforward signal from motor cortex/premotor areas projecting to brainstem cardiovascular centres; activates sympathetic outflow and withdraws vagal tone in proportion to perceived effort; produces the _anticipatory_ cardiovascular response (HR rises before exercise begins). (2) _Exercise pressor reflex_ - feedback from skeletal muscle: (a) mechanoreceptors (group III, lightly myelinated; respond to muscle stretch/contraction); (b) metaboreceptors (group IV, unmyelinated; respond to lactate, H+, K+, bradykinin, adenosine accumulating in working muscle). Afferent input via dorsal horn -> brainstem -> sympathetic activation, vagal withdrawal. Reinforces and matches cardiovascular response to actual muscle metabolic demand. (3) _Arterial baroreflex_ - resets to a higher set-point during exercise ("baroreflex resetting") to allow exercise hypertension without inappropriate baroreflex-mediated bradycardia. Coordinated effects: (a) ↑ HR (vagal withdrawal initially, sympathetic later) - contributing to ↑ CO; (b) ↑ contractility (β1-mediated); (c) ↑ stroke volume; (d) Vasodilation in working muscle (metabolic - adenosine, lactate, K+, H+; endothelial NO; β2 sympathetic cholinergic in some species; in humans, withdrawal of vasoconstrictor tone + metabolic vasodilation dominates); (e) Vasoconstriction in non-working tissue (gut, kidney, non-active muscle - α1 sympathetic) preserves perfusion pressure; (f) ↑ MAP (modest in dynamic exercise; large in isometric exercise); (g) ↑ pulse pressure; (h) Skin vasodilation later for thermoregulation (cholinergic sympathetic). Other autonomic effects: ↑ ventilation (CNS + pulmonary stretch + chemoreceptor); pupillary dilation; bronchodilation; sweating; reduced gut motility. Clinical implications: (1) Patients with autonomic failure (PD, MSA, DAN) have blunted exercise response and exercise intolerance; (2) Exercise pressor reflex is exaggerated in heart failure (perceived as fatigue/dyspnoea), hypertension; (3) β-blockers attenuate the HR response - useful in some conditions, may impair exercise capacity; (4) The exercise hypertension response may be excessive in some hypertensive patients. Heart rate recovery (the rate of HR fall after exercise) reflects vagal reactivation and is a powerful prognostic marker (slow recovery -> increased mortality risk). The integrated cardiovascular response to exercise is one of the best examples of feedforward + feedback neural control matching circulatory output to metabolic demand [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: Exercise cardiovascular control illustrates how feedforward (central command) and feedback (exercise pressor reflex, baroreflex) mechanisms cooperate. Disruption of any limb (eg, autonomic failure) compromises exercise capacity. The exercise pressor reflex is also a target in chronic heart failure, where overactivity contributes to symptoms.

Hints:

1. Central command (feedforward) + exercise pressor reflex (feedback) + baroreflex resetting.
2. Sympathetic activation + vagal withdrawal -> ↑ HR, contractility, vasoconstriction in non-working beds.
3. Local metabolic vasodilation in working muscle.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the three integrated mechanisms.
- Distinguishes central command from exercise pressor reflex.
- Identifies metaboreceptors (group IV) and mechanoreceptors (group III) in muscle.
- Describes ≥ 4 cardiovascular adjustments (HR, contractility, vasoconstriction non-working, vasodilation working).
- Mentions baroreflex resetting during exercise.

A Yellow answer covers some mechanisms but misses central command or pressor reflex.

A Red answer treats exercise response as a single feedback loop.

Common misconceptions:

- "Heart rate rise during exercise is purely from sympathetic activation.": Correction: The first phase (initial 30s) is largely vagal withdrawal; sympathetic activation dominates later; the rapid initial response reflects feedforward central command.
- "Vasodilation in working muscle is purely metabolic.": Correction: Local metabolites are dominant, but withdrawal of sympathetic vasoconstriction also contributes; β2 adrenergic vasodilation is modest in humans.

Stem: Describe the autonomic and somatic neural control of micturition. How does the system fail in spinal cord injury and other lesions?

Anatomy: bladder detrusor (smooth muscle); internal urethral sphincter (smooth muscle, autonomic); external urethral sphincter (striated muscle, somatic via pudendal nerve). Storage phase: low intravesical pressure as bladder fills (compliance); sympathetic outflow from T11-L2 (hypogastric nerve) -> β3 receptors relax detrusor + α1 contracts internal sphincter; somatic outflow (Onuf nucleus, S2-S4) via pudendal nerve -> contracts external sphincter; parasympathetic activity is suppressed. Voiding phase (when bladder volume reaches threshold ~ 300-400 mL, social context permits): pontine micturition centre (Barrington nucleus, dorsal pons) coordinates simultaneous: parasympathetic activation (S2-S4 pelvic nerves) -> M3-mediated detrusor contraction; sympathetic withdrawal -> internal sphincter opens; somatic withdrawal -> external sphincter relaxes. Volitional control via prefrontal cortex/anterior cingulate -> hypothalamus -> PMC. Failure modes: (1) _Suprapontine lesion_ (eg, stroke, Alzheimer, MS plaques) - loss of cortical inhibition -> _urge incontinence_, detrusor overactivity, but coordinated voiding preserved. (2) _Pontine/suprasacral spinal cord lesion_ (above S2; eg, MS, spinal trauma, tumour) - _detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia_ (detrusor contracts but sphincter does not relax) -> high pressures, incomplete voiding, hydronephrosis risk; spastic bladder. (3) _Sacral cord or cauda equina lesion_ (eg, conus medullaris injury, cauda equina syndrome) - _areflexic flaccid bladder_ + lax external sphincter -> overflow incontinence, retention. (4) _Peripheral nerve lesion_ (eg, diabetic, prolonged catheter, post-surgical) - bladder hypotonia, large residual volumes. Goals: bladder protection (low-pressure storage), continence, complete emptying, infection prevention [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14].

Elaborative explanation: Micturition is a classic example of integrated autonomic + somatic + cortical control. The pontine micturition centre coordinates the simultaneous switch from storage to voiding mode. Lesion patterns localise to the level of disruption, and management requires understanding which arm of the control system has failed.

Hints:

1. Storage = sympathetic + somatic active; voiding = parasympathetic active.

Stem: Describe the autonomic control of male sexual response. How does it integrate parasympathetic and sympathetic outflows?

(2) _Emission_ - sympathetic from T10-L2 (hypogastric nerve) -> contraction of vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate -> propulsion of seminal fluid into posterior urethra. (3) _Ejaculation_ - somatic via pudendal nerve (S2-S4) -> rhythmic contraction of bulbospongiosus and ischiocavernosus muscles -> propulsion of seminal fluid. Internal urethral sphincter contracts (sympathetic, α1) to prevent retrograde ejaculation. "Shoot" - sympathetic + somatic for emission/ejaculation. Elaborative explanation: The male sexual response illustrates how different autonomic divisions sequentially produce a complex behaviour. PDE5 inhibitors are a triumph of physiology-driven therapy - identifying NO/cGMP/PDE5 as the molecular pathway and inhibiting the breakdown enzyme. ED as a marker for cardiovascular disease has changed clinical practice (early evaluation of CV risk).

1. Erection - parasympathetic, NO/cGMP, PDE5 inhibitors.
2. Emission - sympathetic, hypogastric nerve.
3. Ejaculation - somatic, pudendal nerve.

Stem: Describe the autonomic control of body temperature. How are heat conservation and heat dissipation coordinated?

Model answer: Thermoregulation is a homeostatic system maintaining core temperature ~ 37°C with diurnal variation (lower at night, higher mid-evening). Sensors: (1) Cutaneous - cold receptors (TRPM8) and warm receptors (TRPV3, TRPV4) in skin signalling environmental temperature. (2) Deep core - hypothalamic preoptic area neurons (warm-sensitive and cold-sensitive); also thoracic, abdominal, spinal cord receptors. Integration: preoptic area / anterior hypothalamus is the central thermostat; integrates afferent input and generates effector responses. Set-point temperature normally ~ 37°C; raised in fever (cytokine-induced PGE2 raises set-point); lowered in some hibernating mammals. Heat conservation (cold exposure or core cooling): (1) _Cutaneous vasoconstriction_ (sympathetic α1) reduces heat loss to environment; (2) _Piloerection_ (sympathetic α1; cosmetic in humans, more functional in furry mammals); (3) _Behavioural_ (clothing, posture, seek warmth); (4) _Shivering_ thermogenesis - somatic motor activation of skeletal muscle producing heat from ATP hydrolysis; sustained fine tremor; large heat output; (5) _Non-shivering thermogenesis_ - sympathetic β3-mediated activation of brown adipose tissue (UCP1 uncouples mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation -> heat instead of ATP); important in neonates and recently in adults; thyroid hormone and catecholamines support; (6) Reduced sweating. Heat dissipation (heat exposure or core warming): (1) _Cutaneous vasodilation_ (withdrawal of sympathetic tone + active sympathetic cholinergic vasodilation in some species) increases heat loss; (2) _Sweating_ (sympathetic cholinergic - exception to the rule that postganglionic sympathetic fibres are noradrenergic; M3 receptors on eccrine sweat glands); evaporative cooling (~ 600 kcal per L sweat); (3) Behavioural (seek shade, reduce activity); (4) Reduced metabolic heat production. Integration: hypothalamic neurons drive coordinated combinations - eg, vasoconstriction + shivering for cold; vasodilation + sweating for heat. Clinical: (1) _Hyperthermia_ - failure of dissipation - heat stroke (core > 40°C, altered mental state, organ failure); rapid cooling life-saving (cold water immersion, evaporative cooling, ice packs to neck/groin/axilla, IV fluids; not antipyretics - they don't work in hyperthermia from environmental causes). (2) _Hypothermia_ - failure of conservation - core < 35°C; impaired consciousness, arrhythmias, coagulopathy ("trauma triad"), "reverse J" wave on ECG (Osborn wave); rewarming gradual; resuscitation continued until rewarmed. (3) _Fever_ - regulated set-point change driven by IL-1, IL-6, TNF -> hypothalamic PGE2 -> raised set-point; antipyretics (paracetamol, NSAIDs) inhibit COX -> reduce PGE2 -> lower set-point. (4) _Malignant hyperthermia_ - rare anaesthetic complication; uncontrolled Ca²⁺ release from SR via mutant RyR1 -> sustained muscle contraction and heat production; treat with dantrolene. (5) _Hot flushes_ - vasomotor symptom of menopause (oestrogen withdrawal alters thermoregulatory set-point). Drugs affecting thermoregulation: antipsychotics (impair both heat and cold tolerance), anticholinergics ("hot as a hare" - reduce sweating), opioids, antidepressants, alcohol [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: Thermoregulation illustrates a beautifully integrated negative feedback control system spanning sensors, central comparator (hypothalamic set-point), and multiple effector arms (vascular, metabolic, behavioural, sweat). Failures produce defined clinical syndromes; pharmacology targets specific arms (antipyretics, dantrolene, anti-shivering agents).

Hints:

1. Hypothalamic set-point ~ 37°C; raised in fever.
2. Cold response: vasoconstriction + shivering + non-shivering thermogenesis.
3. Heat response: vasodilation + sweating (sympathetic cholinergic).

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies hypothalamic preoptic area as central thermostat.
- Names ≥ 3 cold-response effectors (vasoconstriction, shivering, BAT, behavioural).
- Names sweating as sympathetic _cholinergic_ (exception to noradrenergic rule).
- Distinguishes hyperthermia (heat stroke - failure to dissipate) from fever (regulated set-point change).
- Mentions ≥ 2 thermoregulatory disorders (heat stroke, hypothermia, MH, hot flushes).

A Yellow answer covers thermoregulation but misses the specific autonomic effectors.

A Red answer treats temperature as passive heat exchange.

Common misconceptions:

- "All elevated temperatures are fever.": Correction: Distinguish _fever_ (set-point shift; antipyretics work) from _hyperthermia_ (uncontrolled rise; cooling required, antipyretics ineffective).
- "Sweating is cholinergic but unrelated to sympathetic.": Correction: Sweat glands receive _sympathetic cholinergic_ fibres - the major exception to the rule that postganglionic sympathetic fibres are noradrenergic.

Stem: Describe the pupillary light reflex. How do lesions at different levels (afferent, efferent, central) produce specific pupillary syndromes?

Model answer: _Pupillary light reflex_ - light shone in one eye produces _bilateral_ pupil constriction. Afferent: retinal ganglion cells (especially intrinsically photosensitive ipRGCs expressing melanopsin) -> optic nerve -> partial decussation at chiasm -> pretectal nucleus (midbrain) - bilateral projection of each eye's input. Efferent: pretectal nucleus -> bilateral Edinger-Westphal nuclei (parasympathetic component of CN III) -> CN III -> ciliary ganglion -> short ciliary nerves -> sphincter pupillae (M3) -> miosis. Direct response = ipsilateral eye constricts when light shone in same eye; consensual = contralateral eye constricts when light shone in fellow eye. Both should be equal because of bilateral pretectal projection. Lesion patterns: (1) _Optic nerve lesion (afferent defect)_ - reduced afferent signal -> diminished direct + diminished consensual when shone in affected eye; preserved direct + consensual when shone in fellow eye. _Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD / Marcus Gunn pupil)_ - swinging-flashlight test reveals paradoxical _dilation_ of affected pupil when light moved to it (afferent input weaker than to fellow eye). (2) _Efferent CN III lesion_ - ipsilateral pupil dilated and unreactive (both directly and consensually); often with ptosis and ophthalmoplegia (parasympathetic fibres on surface of CN III - compressed first in uncal herniation, posterior communicating artery aneurysm); ischaemic CN III palsy (diabetic) typically _spares_ pupil because vasa nervorum supplies central motor fibres but surface PSNS fibres are spared. (3) _Horner syndrome_ - sympathetic disruption -> miosis (smaller pupil), ptosis (mild, due to Müller muscle), anhidrosis (face/neck), enophthalmos. Causes: lateral medullary stroke (Wallenberg), apical lung tumour (Pancoast), carotid dissection (painful Horner), neck trauma. Triad of features depends on lesion level (1st-order central, 2nd-order preganglionic, 3rd-order postganglionic). Pharmacological testing (cocaine - blocks NA reuptake, dilates normal pupil but not Horner; hydroxyamphetamine - dilates 1st/2nd-order Horner but not 3rd-order; apraclonidine - reverse anisocoria in Horner). (4) _Argyll Robertson pupil_ - small, irregular pupils that constrict with accommodation but _not_ light ("prostitute's pupil - accommodates but does not react"). Classically neurosyphilis; also seen in diabetes, MS, sarcoidosis, Lyme. Lesion in pretectal area or rostral midbrain. (5) _Adie tonic pupil_ - large pupil with poor light response, slow (tonic) accommodation response, light-near dissociation; postganglionic parasympathetic denervation (ciliary ganglion); often with absent reflexes (Adie syndrome); supersensitive to dilute pilocarpine 0.1%. (6) _Bilateral fixed dilated pupils_ - severe brain injury, hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy, anticholinergic poisoning, brainstem death. Pinpoint pupils - opioid overdose (also pontine haemorrhage, organophosphates). Clinical bedside test (RAPD, anisocoria, light-near dissociation) gives huge localising information [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: Pupillary examination is one of the highest-yield bedside tests in neurology. Each abnormality maps to a specific anatomical-pharmacological lesion. The swinging-flashlight test for RAPD distinguishes optic from oculomotor pathology; Horner syndrome localises to the sympathetic chain; Argyll Robertson and Adie tonic pupils have distinct light-near dissociation patterns.

Hints:

1. Afferent: optic nerve -> pretectal -> bilateral EW nuclei.
2. Efferent: CN III parasympathetic -> ciliary ganglion -> sphincter.
3. RAPD = afferent defect; Horner = sympathetic disruption; Argyll Robertson and Adie = light-near dissociation.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Maps the afferent and efferent limbs of the pupillary reflex.
- Distinguishes RAPD from CN III palsy from Horner syndrome.
- Names ≥ 2 light-near dissociation syndromes (Argyll Robertson, Adie tonic).
- Identifies pupil-sparing in diabetic CN III palsy.
- Notes pinpoint vs dilated bilateral pupils in toxidromes (opioids, anticholinergics).

A Yellow answer covers the reflex but misses one or two clinical syndromes.

A Red answer treats pupillary signs as nonspecific.

Common misconceptions:

- "Pupil sparing rules out CN III pathology.": Correction: Pupil-sparing CN III palsy suggests _ischaemic_ (diabetic) cause and is generally benign; pupil-involving CN III palsy is concerning for compressive lesion (PCom aneurysm, uncal herniation).
- "All anisocoria is pathological.": Correction: Physiological anisocoria (< 1 mm asymmetry) is common; relevant clinical anisocoria has features (sluggish reactivity, ptosis, eye movement abnormalities, dissociation).

Stem: Describe autonomic dysreflexia. Why does it occur in spinal cord injury, and how is it managed?

Model answer: _Autonomic dysreflexia (AD)_ - life-threatening syndrome of paroxysmal hypertension occurring in patients with spinal cord injury _at or above T6_. Pathophysiology: noxious stimulus below the level of injury (most commonly bladder distension - 80%; bowel distension/constipation; pressure ulcer; ingrown toenail; UTI; childbirth labour; sexual activity) -> ascending afferent input via spinal cord -> activates segmental sympathetic outflow below the lesion -> _unopposed sympathetic-mediated peripheral vasoconstriction_ (descending modulation from RVLM is blocked at the spinal level of injury) -> severe hypertension (often SBP > 200, sometimes 240+). Carotid baroreceptors detect the rising BP -> reflex bradycardia (vagal) and vasodilation _above the lesion_ (preserved descending parasympathetic) - flushing of head and neck, sweating above lesion, nasal congestion, pounding headache. Below the lesion: pale, cool, piloerection, dry. Critical: T6 is the threshold because segmental sympathetic outflow above T6 (controlling cardiac and splanchnic vasoconstriction) is needed to drive systemic hypertension; lower lesions don't produce dysreflexia. Severity: SBP rises 20-40 mm Hg above patient's normal baseline (which is often low in chronic SCI; 120 may already be 'crisis'); risk of hypertensive emergency, intracerebral haemorrhage, seizures, encephalopathy, retinal haemorrhage, pulmonary oedema, MI, cardiac arrest. Clinical: triad of severe headache + hypertension + sweating above lesion in SCI patient. Management: (1) Sit patient _upright_ (not supine) to lower BP via orthostatic effect; (2) Loosen tight clothing; (3) Identify and remove noxious trigger - check bladder (catheterise / unblock), bowel (digital evacuation), skin; trigger removal often resolves rapidly; (4) Antihypertensive if BP remains > 150 systolic - short-acting agents (sublingual nifedipine, GTN spray/patch, captopril, hydralazine IV); avoid long-acting agents (BP can crash once trigger removed); (5) Monitor BP every 5 min until resolved; (6) Investigate underlying cause (UTI, calculi). Prevention: bladder routine (intermittent catheterisation, antimuscarinics, BTX in selected cases), bowel routine, skin care, education (patient and family), medical alert. AD is a _medical emergency_ in pregnancy/labour for women with SCI - epidural blocks afferents and prevents AD. Patient education and prevention are key - many AD episodes are preventable [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14].

Elaborative explanation: AD illustrates the consequences of disrupting central modulation of spinal sympathetic reflexes. The bizarre clinical pattern (severe hypertension + bradycardia + flushing above lesion + pallor below) directly maps to anatomy. AD is one of the most teachable examples of why understanding neuroanatomy and physiology produces accurate clinical diagnosis.

Hints:

1. SCI at or above T6 + noxious stimulus below = paroxysmal HTN.
2. Triad: headache + HTN + sweating above lesion.
3. Sit up + remove trigger + short-acting antiHTN.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names AD with the SCI ≥ T6 prerequisite.
- Identifies bladder distension as most common trigger.
- Describes the unopposed-sympathetic mechanism below the lesion.
- Names management steps in order (sit up, remove trigger, BP control).
- Mentions risks (hypertensive emergency, ICH, MI).

A Yellow answer covers AD but misses the T6 threshold or specific management.

A Red answer treats this as essential hypertension.

- "AD is uncommon and not urgent.": Correction: It is a hypertensive emergency in SCI patients; rapid identification and trigger removal are life-saving.

Stem: Describe the gut-brain axis. How does the enteric nervous system communicate with the CNS, and what are the clinical implications?

Model answer: _Gut-brain axis_ - bidirectional communication between the enteric nervous system (ENS) and the CNS through neural, endocrine, immune, and microbial pathways. ENS: ~ 500 million neurons in the GI wall (submucosal Meissner plexus, myenteric Auerbach plexus); the "second brain"; capable of autonomous control of peristalsis, secretion, blood flow, and immunity. Communication: (1) _Vagal afferents_ (~ 80% of vagal fibres are afferent) - mechanoreceptors (gut distension), chemoreceptors (nutrients, bacterial products), inflammation; project to nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS); important in satiety (CCK-mediated), nausea, malaise, sickness behaviour. (2) _Spinal visceral afferents_ (sympathetic) - pain, distension, ischaemia. (3) _Enteric hormones_ - GLP-1, PYY, ghrelin, CCK released from gut entero-endocrine cells; act locally and circulate to act on hypothalamic and brainstem nuclei (ARC, NTS) regulating appetite and metabolism. (4) _Microbiota_ - gut bacteria produce neurotransmitter precursors (tryptophan -> serotonin; glutamate; SCFAs - butyrate, propionate, acetate) and modulate the immune system; communicate via enteroendocrine cells, immune cells, vagal afferents, and circulating metabolites. (5) _Immune system_ - intestinal IgA and immune cells respond to microbial signals; cytokines feedback on CNS. (6) _Endocrine_ - HPA axis activated by stress affects gut motility and microbiota. Functional consequences: (1) Mood-gut interactions - 95% of body serotonin in gut; depression and anxiety co-morbid with IBS; SSRIs effective for both; (2) Microbiota and behaviour - germ-free mice have altered behaviour, anxiety, social cognition; faecal microbiota transplantation alters host phenotype; (3) Sickness behaviour - vagal afferents communicate inflammation -> CNS produces malaise, fatigue, anorexia; (4) Stress and gut function - HPA + sympathetic activation -> reduced gut motility, altered secretion, altered microbiome composition, intestinal permeability changes ("leaky gut"); (5) Neurodegenerative disease - alpha-synuclein appears in gut years before brain (Parkinson disease); vagotomy reduces PD risk. Clinical implications: (1) _Irritable bowel syndrome_ (IBS) - functional GI disorder with abdominal pain + altered bowel habit; gut-brain axis dysfunction; treatment includes diet (low-FODMAP), antispasmodics, antidepressants (TCAs, SSRIs), psychotherapy; (2) _Inflammatory bowel disease_ affects mood and cognition via systemic inflammation; (3) _Obesity_ - dysregulated gut hormones (reduced PYY/GLP-1, raised ghrelin); GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide) are paradigm-shifting; (4) _Fecal microbiota transplant_ - approved for recurrent C. difficile; under investigation for IBD, autism, depression; (5) _Coeliac disease_ - immune-mediated; can present with neurological features. Pharmacology: GLP-1 agonists for diabetes and obesity (semaglutide, liraglutide); dual GIP/GLP-1 (tirzepatide); these act partially on gut-brain axis to reduce appetite and food reward. Vagal nerve stimulation - approved for refractory epilepsy and depression; under investigation for IBD, obesity, heart failure [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14].

Elaborative explanation: The gut-brain axis is one of the most rapidly developing areas in physiology. The realisation that gut microbiota substantially influence brain function and behaviour has broad implications for psychiatry, neurology, metabolism, and immunology. GLP-1 agonists are an early therapeutic triumph from this conceptual revolution.

Hints:

1. ENS = 500 million neurons; second brain.
2. Vagal afferents (~80% of vagus) carry visceral information to NTS.
3. Microbiota influences mood, behaviour, metabolism.

Stem: Describe anaphylaxis. What is the autonomic and immune pathophysiology, and how is it managed?

Model answer: _Anaphylaxis_ - severe, life-threatening systemic hypersensitivity reaction. Pathophysiology: IgE-mediated mast cell + basophil degranulation (most common; allergen specific); also non-IgE mast cell activation (NSAIDs, contrast media, opioids, exercise); release of histamine, tryptase, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, platelet-activating factor, cytokines. Effects: (1) Vascular - vasodilation (low MAP), increased vascular permeability (intravascular volume to interstitium), distributive shock (initially), then potentially hypovolaemia from fluid loss; (2) Bronchial - bronchoconstriction, mucus, airway oedema; (3) GI - smooth muscle spasm, vomiting, diarrhoea; (4) Skin - urticaria, angioedema, flushing; (5) Cardiac - direct effects + reflex tachycardia (sometimes paradoxical bradycardia from intense parasympathetic activation - Bezold-Jarisch reflex); arrhythmias. Clinical features: rapidly progressive (minutes-1h) involving ≥ 2 systems; (a) Skin/mucosa (~ 90%); (b) Respiratory (cough, stridor, wheeze, dyspnoea); (c) Cardiovascular (hypotension, syncope, shock); (d) GI (vomiting, abdominal pain). Severity grading: mild (skin/mild GI), moderate (cardiorespiratory features stable), severe (shock, severe airway). Triggers: foods (peanut, tree nut, shellfish, milk, egg), drugs (penicillin, NSAIDs, contrast), insect venoms (bees, wasps), latex, exercise (food-dependent or independent), idiopathic. Management - acute (adult): (1) _IM adrenaline (epinephrine) 500 mcg (0.5 mL of 1:1000) in anterolateral thigh, repeat every 5 min as needed_ - first-line; α1 vasoconstriction reverses hypotension; β2 bronchodilation; β1 inotropy/chronotropy; mast cell stabilisation; (2) Position - lay flat with legs elevated (or pregnant left lateral); never sitting up if hypotensive; (3) Airway - secure if compromised, oxygen, intubate if severe airway oedema; (4) IV access - large-bore; rapid crystalloid 1-2 L; (5) Adjunctive - antihistamines (chlorphenamine, ranitidine - help skin/itch but don't replace adrenaline); corticosteroids (hydrocortisone - delayed onset; may reduce biphasic reactions but evidence weak); β2 agonist (salbutamol nebulised) for bronchospasm; glucagon if on β-blocker; vasopressin or pure α agonist if refractory shock; (6) Observe ≥ 4-6 h (biphasic reactions in ~5%); admit severe/persistent cases; (7) Discharge with adrenaline auto-injector education + allergy referral. Long-term: identify trigger (skin testing, specific IgE, oral challenge); avoidance; immunotherapy (venom, in some cases food); patient education on signs and auto-injector use; medic alert. _Adrenaline auto-injector education saves lives_ - delayed adrenaline administration is the most common reversible factor in fatal anaphylaxis [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: Anaphylaxis is a clinical emergency where rapid IM adrenaline is the only intervention proven to save lives. The autonomic and pharmacological logic - α1 for vasoconstriction, β2 for bronchodilation, β1 for cardiac support, mast cell stabilisation - explains why adrenaline alone covers the multi-system pathophysiology. Antihistamines and steroids are _adjuncts_, not substitutes.

Hints:

1. Mast cell degranulation -> multi-system reaction.
2. IM adrenaline is _the_ life-saving intervention.
3. Antihistamines and steroids are adjunctive.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names anaphylaxis criteria (rapid, multi-system).
- Identifies IM adrenaline 500 mcg as first-line.
- Lists ≥ 3 mechanisms by which adrenaline helps (α1, β2, β1, mast cell).
- Names ≥ 2 common triggers (foods, drugs, venom).
- Mentions biphasic reactions and post-resolution observation period.

A Yellow answer covers anaphylaxis but misses the role-of-adrenaline detail.

A Red answer treats anaphylaxis as a simple allergy.

Common misconceptions:

- "Antihistamines treat anaphylaxis.": Correction: Antihistamines help skin and itching but do not address the cardiovascular or respiratory crisis; _only adrenaline_ is life-saving.
- "Adrenaline IV is preferred.": Correction: IM adrenaline (anterolateral thigh) is preferred for anaphylaxis - faster, safer, more reliable than subcutaneous; IV adrenaline reserved for refractory shock with monitoring.

Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Discuss the autonomic adaptations of pregnancy. How does the maternal cardiovascular and autonomic system change to accommodate the developing fetus?

Model answer: Pregnancy produces remarkable maternal cardiovascular and autonomic adaptations. Cardiovascular: (1) Cardiac output rises ~ 30-50% (peaks late 2nd trimester); (2) Heart rate rises ~ 10-20%; (3) Stroke volume rises ~ 30%; (4) Plasma volume rises ~ 50%, RBC mass ~ 30% -> dilutional anaemia; (5) Systemic vascular resistance falls ~ 30% (progesterone, NO, prostaglandins) -> diastolic BP often _falls_ ~ 5-10 mm Hg in 1st-2nd trimester despite ↑ CO; SBP slightly down; (6) Late pregnancy: aortocaval compression by gravid uterus when supine -> ↓ venous return -> ↓ CO and BP -> _supine hypotensive syndrome_; sleep on left side; (7) Coagulation factors rise -> hypercoagulable state (DVT/PE risk). Renal: increased renal blood flow + GFR ~ 50%; lower urea/creatinine; physiological glucosuria. Respiratory: progesterone-mediated chronic respiratory alkalosis with bicarbonate compensation; tidal volume up; FRC down. Autonomic adaptations: (1) Vagal tone modulated; HR variability changes; (2) Sympathetic activation in 3rd trimester (compensatory); (3) Baroreflex resetting accommodates higher BP variability; (4) Pre-eclampsia involves abnormal vasoreactivity, endothelial dysfunction, and altered RAAS - sympathetic overactivity contributes; (5) Cardiac autonomic dysregulation in peripartum cardiomyopathy. Pregnancy-specific autonomic phenomena: (1) Morning sickness - vagal-mediated nausea, possibly involving area postrema and elevated hCG; (2) Hyperemesis gravidarum - severe form requiring IV fluids/antiemetics; (3) Increased gut motility issues - reflux (LOS relaxation by progesterone), constipation, haemorrhoids; (4) Bladder - increased frequency from ↑ GFR and uterine pressure; (5) Skin - increased pigmentation (MSH, melanocyte sensitivity), spider naevi, palmar erythema; (6) Vascular - varicose veins, supine hypotension. Labour and delivery autonomic changes: (1) Pain triggers massive sympathetic activation -> hypertension, tachycardia (epidural blunts this); (2) Valsalva during pushing -> baroreflex changes; (3) Post-delivery - autotransfusion of uterine blood (~ 500 mL) -> transient ↑ preload; (4) Lactation - oxytocin release with suckling; prolactin elevated; (5) Postpartum - haemodynamic recovery over weeks. Pre-existing autonomic conditions: (1) Spinal cord injury - autonomic dysreflexia risk during labour (epidural blocks afferents); (2) Postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome - often improves but may worsen; (3) Diabetes - autonomic neuropathy worsens; gestational diabetes; (4) Hypertension - chronic and pregnancy-induced; antihypertensive choice critical (ACE-I/ARB contraindicated; methyldopa, labetalol, nifedipine safer). Autonomic considerations for anaesthesia: pregnant patients have more rapid desaturation (reduced FRC, increased O2 consumption); higher risk of regurgitation (LOS relaxation, gravid uterus); supine hypotension; epidural sympathetic block may cause profound hypotension. Pre-eclampsia and eclampsia - distinct hypertensive disorders with multi-system involvement and seizure risk; magnesium for prevention/treatment. The maternal physiology adaptation is one of the most striking examples of integrated autonomic, cardiovascular, renal, and endocrine remodelling in a healthy individual [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: Pregnancy is a profound integrated physiological adaptation. Understanding these changes is essential for managing pregnant patients and for recognising pathological deviations (pre-eclampsia, peripartum cardiomyopathy, gestational diabetes). The autonomic adjustments support the dramatic cardiovascular shifts.

Hints:

1. CO ↑ 30-50%; SVR ↓ 30%; HR ↑ 10-20%.
2. Supine hypotensive syndrome from aortocaval compression.
3. Hypercoagulable state -> DVT/PE risk.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the major cardiovascular changes (CO, SVR, HR, BP) with magnitudes.
- Identifies aortocaval compression and supine hypotensive syndrome.
- Notes hypercoagulability and DVT/PE risk.
- Mentions ≥ 2 pregnancy-specific autonomic phenomena.
- Recognises ACE-I/ARB contraindication and safer alternatives.

A Yellow answer covers some changes but misses the integration.

A Red answer treats pregnancy as a simple state without specific adaptations.

Common misconceptions:

- "Blood pressure rises during pregnancy.": Correction: Diastolic BP typically _falls_ in early-mid pregnancy due to SVR decrease; rising BP after 20 weeks is concerning for pre-eclampsia.
- "All antihypertensives are safe in pregnancy.": Correction: ACE-I and ARBs are contraindicated (renal/skull defects); methyldopa, labetalol, nifedipine are first-line in pregnancy.

QUESTION 17
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: Why is the autonomic nervous system so heavily targeted by pharmacology? Synthesise the major drug classes acting on ANS receptors and their clinical applications.

Model answer: ANS pharmacology spans much of clinical medicine because ANS receptors control so many vital functions. Major drug classes: (1) _β1-selective antagonists_ (metoprolol, bisoprolol, atenolol) - HF, angina, post-MI, hypertension, arrhythmia; reduce mortality in HFrEF and post-MI. (2) _Non-selective β-antagonists_ (propranolol) - hypertension, anxiety/tremor, hyperthyroidism (controls symptoms before definitive therapy), portal hypertension (reduces variceal bleeding), migraine prevention, infantile haemangioma. Avoid in asthma. (3) _β2 agonists_ (salbutamol, salmeterol, formoterol) - asthma/COPD bronchodilation; tocolysis; β2-mediated K+ shift in hyperkalaemia. (4) _β3 agonist_ (mirabegron) - overactive bladder. (5) _α1 antagonists_ (doxazosin, prazosin, terazosin) - hypertension (less popular due to ALLHAT findings), BPH, Raynaud, PTSD-related nightmares. (6) _Selective α1A antagonists_ (tamsulosin, alfuzosin) - BPH (prostate-selective, less hypotension). (7) _α2 agonists_ (clonidine, dexmedetomidine, methyldopa) - hypertension (especially pregnancy with methyldopa), opioid/alcohol withdrawal, sedation in ICU (dexmedetomidine), spasticity (tizanidine). (8) _Mixed α/β agonists_ (adrenaline, noradrenaline) - anaphylaxis, septic shock, cardiac arrest. (9) _Pure α agonists_ (phenylephrine) - mydriasis, decongestion, hypotension. (10) _Indirect sympathomimetics_ (ephedrine, pseudoephedrine, amphetamines) - hypotension/decongestion; ADHD (methylphenidate, dextroamphetamine - increase synaptic NA/DA). (11) _Catecholamine reuptake inhibitors_ - TCAs (depression, neuropathic pain); SNRIs (depression, neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia); cocaine (drug of abuse). (12) _MAO inhibitors_ (selegiline, phenelzine) - PD, depression. (13) _Catecholamine synthesis inhibitors_ (metyrosine) - phaeochromocytoma. (14) _Reuptake/release inhibitors at sympathetic terminal_ (reserpine - historical antihypertensive; depletes vesicular monoamines). (15) _Cholinesterase inhibitors_ - pyridostigmine (myasthenia), donepezil/rivastigmine/galantamine (Alzheimer dementia), neostigmine (NMB reversal), physostigmine (anticholinergic toxicity), insecticides (organophosphates - toxic). (16) _M-receptor agonists_ (pilocarpine - glaucoma, dry mouth in Sjogren; bethanechol - urinary retention, ileus). (17) _M-receptor antagonists_ - atropine (bradycardia, cholinergic poisoning, pre-anaesthetic, mydriatic); ipratropium/tiotropium (COPD/asthma bronchodilation); tropicamide (mydriasis); oxybutynin/solifenacin/tolterodine (overactive bladder); hyoscine (motion sickness, secretions, palliative care); pirenzepine (peptic ulcer - M1 selective). (18) _Nicotinic ganglion blockers_ (hexamethonium - historical, no clinical use); _NMJ blockers_ (depolarising sux, non-depolarising rocuronium/vecuronium - anaesthesia). (19) _Botulinum toxin_ - SNARE cleavage at presynaptic terminals (NMJ + cholinergic ANS); cosmetic, dystonia, spasticity, hyperhidrosis, overactive bladder, migraine prophylaxis. (20) _Anti-CGRP antibodies_ (erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab) - migraine. Clinical principle: ANS pharmacology achieves selectivity through receptor subtype + tissue distribution + delivery route. Mastery of ANS pharmacology is essential for cardiology, pulmonology, ophthalmology, urology, neurology, anaesthesia, and emergency medicine [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14].

Elaborative explanation: ANS pharmacology is the largest single pharmacological domain because the ANS regulates cardiovascular, respiratory, urinary, GI, ocular, and many other functions. The same receptors are targeted by treatments for hypertension, asthma, BPH, glaucoma, dementia, depression, Parkinson, anaphylaxis, and more. Mastery of ANS pharmacology unlocks much of clinical medicine.

Hints:

1. β-blockers, α-blockers, agonists, anticholinergics - cover huge therapy areas.
2. Selectivity through subtype + tissue + route.
3. Same receptor targeted differently for different conditions.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names ≥ 5 drug classes acting on ANS receptors.
- Maps each class to its receptor subtype and clinical indications.
- Identifies ≥ 3 cross-disciplinary applications (cardiology, pulmonology, urology, etc).
- Discusses subtype selectivity (β1 vs β2; M3 vs M1; α1 vs α2).
- Mentions newer classes (β3 agonist, anti-CGRP, GLP-1).

A Yellow answer covers some classes but misses the breadth.

A Red answer treats ANS pharmacology as one homogeneous group.

Common misconceptions:

- "β-blockers are the same.": Correction: Subtype selectivity matters: β1-selective for cardiac use to avoid β2 bronchospasm; non-selective for migraine/anxiety; β-blockers with α-blocking activity (carvedilol, labetalol).
- "Anticholinergics have one effect.": Correction: M3 antagonists (bladder), M3+M2 (COPD), M1 (peptic), CNS-penetrant (motion sickness); selectivity defines clinical use and side-effect profile.

QUESTION 18
Type: synthesis
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Synthesise the chapter: what design principles emerge from the autonomic nervous system, and how do clinical syndromes test our understanding?

Model answer: Design principles from ANS organisation: (1) _Two-neuron architecture_ - preganglionic + postganglionic provides ganglionic divergence (1:30+ for sympathetic) supporting mass discharge, and integration at ganglia. (2) _Anatomical separation of divisions_ - sympathetic (T1-L2 thoracolumbar) for fight-or-flight; parasympathetic (cranial + S2-S4 sacral) for rest-and-digest; allows reciprocal control. (3) _Receptor diversity_ - many subtypes (α1/2, β1/2/3, M1-5, NN/NM) allow tissue-specific drug action despite shared transmitters. (4) _Feedforward + feedback_ - central command (cortex/limbic/hypothalamus) anticipates demand; visceral afferents and baroreflexes feedback. (5) _Hierarchical central control_ - cortex/limbic, hypothalamus, brainstem, spinal cord layered for cognitive-emotional integration with reflex execution. (6) _Bidirectional communication_ - ENS as semi-autonomous "second brain" interfaces with CNS (gut-brain axis) via neural, endocrine, and microbial signals. (7) _Co-transmission and modulation_ - ATP, NPY, NO, VIP, and others act alongside ACh/NA, fine-tuning responses. (8) _Tissue-specific expression_ - same transmitter (NA) produces vasoconstriction (α1) or vasodilation (β2) by tissue receptor distribution. (9) _Resilience_ - some functions controlled bilaterally (cardiac vagal); some by single division. (10) _Graded responses_ - ANS rarely all-or-none; nuanced response to demand. Disease-mechanism mapping (each clinical syndrome reveals a design feature): (i) _Phaeochromocytoma_ - excess catecholamine production; mass discharge dysregulated; α-then-β rule reflects unopposed α vasoconstriction. (ii) _Diabetic autonomic neuropathy_ - small-fibre dysfunction across multiple systems; reveals shared molecular vulnerability of long autonomic axons. (iii) _Autonomic dysreflexia_ - SCI ≥ T6 disrupts descending modulation; spinal sympathetic reflexes go unopposed. (iv) _Orthostatic hypotension_ - failure of baroreflex (afferent or efferent or central); test with tilt-table and HR variability. (v) _Anaphylaxis_ - mast cell mediators target multiple ANS receptors; adrenaline targets α1, β1, β2, β3 simultaneously - one drug for multi-system crisis. (vi) _Vasovagal syncope_ - paradoxical baroreflex activation; brief bradycardia + vasodilation. (vii) _Multiple system atrophy_ - widespread autonomic + cerebellar + parkinsonian degeneration; compares with PD (more localised). (viii) _Horner syndrome_ - sympathetic chain interruption; localising value depends on level (1st/2nd/3rd order). (ix) _Drug toxidromes_ - anticholinergic ("hot, dry, mad"), sympathomimetic ("speedy"), cholinergic (SLUDGE), opioid (miosis, respiratory depression) - each a targeted ANS imbalance. (x) _Erectile dysfunction_ - multi-axis failure (vascular + neural + endocrine); PDE5 inhibitors (NO/cGMP pathway) elegant pharmacological intervention. Pedagogical insight: the ANS demonstrates how a relatively small set of building blocks (two transmitters, multiple receptor subtypes, two-neuron architecture, central control hierarchy) generates exquisite tissue-specific responses through the combinatorial logic of receptor distribution and central modulation. Most of clinical pharmacology in cardiology, pulmonology, urology, ophthalmology, gastroenterology, anaesthesia, and emergency medicine is essentially ANS pharmacology. Mastery of the ANS unlocks broad clinical reasoning [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: The ANS is one of the most pedagogically rewarding domains in physiology - small set of principles, vast clinical implications. Each clinical syndrome maps cleanly to a design feature; each drug class exploits a specific receptor subtype; each pharmacological principle (selectivity, tissue specificity, hierarchical control) is illustrated. Studying the ANS is essentially studying an integrative framework for clinical medicine.

Hints:

1. Two-neuron + receptor diversity + hierarchical control + tissue specificity.
2. Each syndrome reveals a design feature.
3. ANS pharmacology underlies much of clinical medicine.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Articulates ≥ 5 design principles.
- Maps ≥ 5 clinical syndromes to ANS design features.
- Discusses combinatorial logic of receptor subtype + tissue distribution.
- Highlights cross-disciplinary clinical relevance.
- Synthesises rather than listing facts.

A Yellow answer covers some principles but misses the integration.

A Red answer treats ANS as a list of unrelated facts.

Common misconceptions:

- "ANS is just another organ system.": Correction: It is an integrative framework that interfaces with virtually all organ systems and underpins much of clinical pharmacology and physiology.
- "Clinical reasoning in ANS disorders is intuitive.": Correction: It requires systematic localisation (which division? which level? afferent or efferent?) and pharmacological reasoning - structured ANS thinking.
