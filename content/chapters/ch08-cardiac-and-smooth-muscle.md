## Pass 1 — Cardiac muscle architecture, action potential, CICR, autonomic modulation

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The structure that physically and electrically couples adjacent cardiac myocytes, allowing the heart to function as a syncytium, is the:

Correct answer: Intercalated disc — combining mechanical anchor sites (fascia adherens, desmosomes) with electrical coupling via gap-junction (connexin-43) channels.

Distractors:

- "T tubule." — Reveals misconception: student confuses an EC-coupling membrane invagination with a cell–cell junction. Correction: T tubules conduct depolarisation deep into individual cells; they are not the structure that couples adjacent cells.
- "Sarcolemma alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks the cell membrane in general rather than the specialised junction. Correction: bare sarcolemma does not provide electrical coupling between cells; the intercalated disc — a specialised region of the sarcolemma — does.
- "Z line." — Reveals misconception: student picks a sarcomere boundary structure. Correction: Z lines anchor thin filaments within a single cell; they do not couple adjacent cells.

Explanation: Intercalated discs at the longitudinal cell–cell junction contain (i) **fascia adherens** (anchor sites for actin filaments — analogous to a Z line); (ii) **desmosomes** (anchor intermediate filaments and prevent cells from pulling apart); and (iii) **gap junctions** (connexin-43 channels) that electrically couple adjacent cells [Berne & Levy ch.13; Guyton & Hall ch.9]. Together they make the heart a "functional syncytium" — a single AP can propagate through all coupled cells. Mutations in desmosomal proteins (plakoglobin, desmoplakin, plakophilin-2) cause arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy (ARVC); connexin-43 abnormalities contribute to arrhythmogenesis after ischaemia.

Hints:

1. Three classes of junction sit side-by-side at this structure.
2. The electrical coupling element is a gap junction (connexin-43).
3. The mechanical elements include desmosomes and fascia adherens.

QUESTION 2
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The trigger for Ca²⁺ release from the SR in cardiac muscle excitation–contraction coupling is:

Correct answer: Ca²⁺ influx through L-type Ca²⁺ channels (Cav1.2 / DHPR) during the plateau, which binds RyR2 and triggers Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release (CICR).

Distractors:

- "A direct mechanical conformational change in DHPR (as in skeletal muscle)." — Reveals misconception: student applies the skeletal mechanism to cardiac muscle. Correction: skeletal-muscle ECC is mechanically coupled and does not require Ca²⁺ entry; cardiac ECC absolutely _requires_ trigger Ca²⁺ entry through Cav1.2.
- "IP₃-mediated Ca²⁺ release alone." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a metabotropic-pathway-driven release to cardiac ECC. Correction: although cardiac myocytes have some IP3 receptors (mostly atrial), these contribute modestly to ECC; the dominant trigger is Ca²⁺ entry via Cav1.2 acting on RyR2.
- "Membrane depolarisation alone." — Reveals misconception: student treats RyR2 as voltage-gated. Correction: RyR2 is _not_ directly voltage-gated; its opening depends on Ca²⁺ binding (CICR). Depolarisation is necessary to open Cav1.2 but is not the direct trigger of SR release.

Explanation: Cardiac ECC is by **Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release (CICR)**. During the plateau of the cardiac AP, Ca²⁺ enters via L-type Ca²⁺ channels (Cav1.2) [Berne & Levy ch.13; Costanzo ch.4]. This "trigger Ca²⁺" diffuses to the apposed RyR2 in the SR membrane (separated by ~12 nm at the dyad), binds, and opens RyR2 — releasing the much larger SR Ca²⁺ store. Removal of cytosolic Ca²⁺ involves SERCA2a (back into SR; PLB-regulated), Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (extrudes ~25% per beat — must match the trigger Ca²⁺ entered, otherwise Ca²⁺ accumulates), PMCA (small contribution), and mitochondrial uniporter (slow). This contrasts with skeletal muscle, where ECC is mechanical (DHPR–RyR1 direct coupling) and does not require Ca²⁺ entry. Ca²⁺ channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem; less so dihydropyridines at the heart) reduce CICR and produce negative inotropy.

Hints:

1. The mechanism shares a name with the trigger.
2. The trigger involves Ca²⁺ entry, unlike skeletal muscle.
3. The release channel is RyR2, not RyR1.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The long plateau (~200 ms) of the ventricular cardiac action potential is produced primarily by:

Correct answer: Sustained inward Ca²⁺ current through L-type Ca²⁺ channels (Cav1.2) balanced by outward K⁺ currents (delayed rectifiers IKr and IKs) — phase 2.

Distractors:

- "Sustained inward Na⁺ current." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the plateau to a Na⁺ current. Correction: phase 0 (the upstroke) is Na⁺-mediated; the plateau is Ca²⁺-mediated. Sustained Na⁺ current ("late INa") is a small contribution and a target in some long-QT pathology and ranolazine therapy.
- "Closure of all K⁺ channels." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the plateau to total K⁺-channel closure. Correction: K⁺ currents (IKr, IKs) are not absent during the plateau — they are present but balanced by inward Ca²⁺ current; phase 3 begins when Ca²⁺ inactivation tips the balance toward K⁺ outward.
- "Sustained Cl⁻ entry." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the plateau to a Cl⁻ current. Correction: Cl⁻ contributes a transient outward current (Ito) component to phase 1 in some species but is not the major plateau current.

Explanation: Phase 0 is a Na⁺-driven upstroke (similar to nerve / skeletal muscle); phase 1 is brief Ito-mediated repolarisation; phase 2 (the plateau) is characterised by _inward_ Ca²⁺ current through Cav1.2 balanced by _outward_ K⁺ current (delayed rectifier IKr / IKs); phase 3 begins when Ca²⁺ channels inactivate, leaving K⁺ currents to repolarise; phase 4 is set by IK1 (inward rectifier) [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. The plateau both feeds CICR (sustained Ca²⁺ entry → SR Ca²⁺ release) and produces a long absolute refractory period — preventing tetanus and protecting diastolic filling. Class III antiarrhythmics (amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide) block IKr to prolong the AP and refractoriness; they can produce torsades de pointes. SA / AV nodal cells lack the Na⁺-driven phase 0 and use Ca²⁺-mediated upstroke (slow response).

Hints:

1. Two opposing currents balance during the plateau.
2. The inward current is Ca²⁺ (Cav1.2).
3. The outward current is K⁺ (delayed rectifier).

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The "funny current" (If) in sinoatrial node cells responsible for diastolic depolarisation is carried predominantly by:

Correct answer: Na⁺ entry through HCN (hyperpolarisation-activated cyclic nucleotide-gated) channels — opens at hyperpolarised voltages, accelerated by cAMP binding (basis of β-adrenergic chronotropy); ivabradine selectively blocks.

Distractors:

- "K⁺ efflux only." — Reveals misconception: student attributes diastolic depolarisation to a K⁺ current. Correction: K⁺ efflux _opposes_ depolarisation; the "funny current" is a depolarising inward current carried mainly by Na⁺.
- "Ca²⁺ alone through T-type channels." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the entire diastolic depolarisation to T-type Ca²⁺ current. Correction: T-type Ca²⁺ channels (Cav3) contribute to the late phase of diastolic depolarisation, but the early/initial phase 4 depolarisation is dominated by If.
- "Cl⁻ entry." — Reveals misconception: student attributes diastolic depolarisation to a Cl⁻ current. Correction: Cl⁻ is not a major contributor to SA node pacemaking.

Explanation: SA node cells lack a stable resting potential. After repolarisation, **HCN channels** (encoded by HCN4 in the SA node) open at hyperpolarised voltages and conduct mainly inward Na⁺ (with some K⁺), driving slow diastolic depolarisation [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Ganong 26e]. Cyclic AMP binds HCN channels and shifts their activation toward more positive voltages — providing the molecular basis of β-adrenergic acceleration (cAMP up → If up → faster pacemaker). Once threshold is reached, T-type and then L-type Ca²⁺ channels carry the AP upstroke (slow response). **Ivabradine** selectively blocks If, slowing heart rate without affecting contractility — used in chronic HFrEF on maximal β-blocker with HR ≥70 in sinus rhythm (SHIFT trial). Vagal stimulation acts via M2 → Gαi → reduces cAMP (slowing If) and Gβγ → opens GIRK / IK,ACh (hyperpolarising further) — slowing the pacemaker.

Hints:

1. The current is "funny" because it is inward, not outward.
2. The channel family is HCN; the key molecule is cAMP.
3. The clinical drug ivabradine acts here.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The sodium–calcium exchanger (NCX) on the cardiac sarcolemma is electrogenic, exchanging:

Correct answer: 3 Na⁺ for 1 Ca²⁺, generating a net inward current when extruding Ca²⁺ (or net outward current when running in reverse during the plateau).

Distractors:

- "1 Na⁺ for 1 Ca²⁺." — Reveals misconception: student picks an electroneutral 1:1 exchange. Correction: NCX is electrogenic with a 3:1 stoichiometry, not 1:1.
- "2 Na⁺ for 1 Ca²⁺." — Reveals misconception: student picks an electroneutral stoichiometry. Correction: 2:1 would be electroneutral; NCX is 3:1, and electrogenic — making one net positive charge per cycle.
- "1 ATP per Ca²⁺." — Reveals misconception: student conflates NCX with PMCA. Correction: NCX is a _secondary_ active transporter using the Na⁺ gradient; it does _not_ directly hydrolyse ATP. PMCA is the primary-active Ca²⁺-ATPase that uses ATP.

Explanation: NCX (NCX1 in cardiac muscle) exchanges 3 Na⁺ for 1 Ca²⁺ — electrogenic (one net positive charge per cycle). In its forward mode (during diastole), it extrudes ~25% of the cytosolic Ca²⁺ that entered during the AP, balancing Cav1.2 Ca²⁺ entry [Berne & Levy ch.13; Ganong 26e]. NCX can run in _reverse mode_ (Ca²⁺ in, Na⁺ out) when intracellular Na⁺ rises (e.g., in digoxin therapy, or briefly during the AP plateau when the membrane is depolarised) — the basis of cardiac glycoside positive inotropy: digoxin inhibits the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → intracellular Na⁺ rises → NCX reverses (or extrudes less Ca²⁺) → SR Ca²⁺ load rises → stronger contractions. Excessive Ca²⁺ accumulation (digoxin toxicity, Ca²⁺ overload from ischaemia) → delayed afterdepolarisations → arrhythmias.

Hints:

1. The stoichiometry is 3:1 in favour of Na⁺.
2. The exchanger is electrogenic — one net positive charge moves per cycle.
3. It can reverse direction depending on driving forces.

Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The cardiac SR Ca²⁺ release channel (the cardiac ryanodine receptor) is encoded by:

Correct answer: RYR2 — mutations cause catecholaminergic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (CPVT), driven by SR Ca²⁺ leak and delayed afterdepolarisations on adrenergic stress.

Distractors:

- "RYR1." — Reveals misconception: student picks the skeletal-muscle isoform. Correction: RYR1 is the skeletal-muscle isoform (mutations cause malignant hyperthermia and central core disease); RYR2 is cardiac.
- "RYR3." — Reveals misconception: student picks an isoform with broad but lesser expression. Correction: RyR3 is widely expressed (brain, smooth muscle, some skeletal muscle) but is not the cardiac CICR channel.
- "ITPR2." — Reveals misconception: student picks an IP3 receptor. Correction: ITPR2 (IP3R2) is an IP3 receptor (Ca²⁺ release channel activated by IP3, not Ca²⁺); it has a minor role in cardiac atrial myocytes but is not the principal CICR channel.

Explanation: RyR2 is the cardiac isoform; RyR1 is skeletal; RyR3 is widely distributed [Berne & Levy ch.13]. Mutations in RYR2 cause **catecholaminergic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (CPVT)** — exercise- or emotion-triggered ventricular arrhythmias driven by abnormal Ca²⁺ leak from the SR, producing delayed afterdepolarisations (DADs) and triggered activity. CPVT classically presents with exercise-induced syncope or sudden death in childhood/adolescence; ECG at rest is normal. β-blockers (especially nadolol) and **flecainide** (which blocks RyR2 leak in addition to Na⁺ channels) reduce arrhythmia risk; severe / refractory cases require ICD or left cardiac sympathetic denervation. Calsequestrin-2 (CASQ2) mutations produce a similar phenotype (CASQ2-CPVT).

Hints:

1. The cardiac and skeletal isoforms are different.
2. The number after RYR matches the second of the three isoforms.
3. RYR2 mutations cause CPVT.

QUESTION 7
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: β-Adrenergic stimulation produces positive inotropy in cardiac muscle by:

Correct answer: cAMP / PKA-mediated phosphorylation of L-type Ca²⁺ channels (more Ca²⁺ entry per beat), phospholamban (relieving inhibition of SERCA2a → faster relaxation and larger SR Ca²⁺ load), and troponin I (decreasing myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity to speed relaxation) — net result is stronger, briefer, more frequent contractions.

Distractors:

- "Direct activation of MLCK." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a smooth-muscle mechanism to cardiac muscle. Correction: cardiac muscle uses the troponin / tropomyosin thin-filament regulatory system; MLCK regulates _smooth-muscle_ myosin light-chain phosphorylation.
- "Direct closure of K⁺ channels." — Reveals misconception: student attributes inotropy to a K⁺-channel-closure mechanism. Correction: β-adrenergic stimulation does enhance some K⁺ currents (IKs phosphorylation accelerates repolarisation in tachycardia), but inotropy is driven by Cav1.2 / PLB / TnI phosphorylation, not by K⁺-channel closure.
- "Inhibition of NCX." — Reveals misconception: student attributes inotropy to NCX inhibition. Correction: β-adrenergic activation does not directly inhibit NCX; the key mechanism is cAMP/PKA enhancing Ca²⁺ entry through Cav1.2 and reuptake by SERCA2a (via PLB phosphorylation).

Explanation: Sympathetic activation: noradrenaline / adrenaline → β1-receptor → Gs → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA. PKA phosphorylates: (i) **Cav1.2** (L-type Ca²⁺ channel) → bigger Ca²⁺ transient (positive inotropy); (ii) **phospholamban** → relieves PLB inhibition of SERCA2a → faster Ca²⁺ reuptake (positive lusitropy) and increased SR Ca²⁺ load for the next beat; (iii) **troponin I** → lower myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity → faster cross-bridge release (positive lusitropy); (iv) **HCN / If** in SA node → faster pacemaker (positive chronotropy); (v) **RyR2** (controversial — may sensitise SR release) [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. Net effect: stronger (positive inotropy), briefer (positive lusitropy), more frequent (positive chronotropy) contractions. β1-blockers (bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate, carvedilol) reverse these effects and are foundational therapy in HFrEF; β2-selective agonists (salbutamol) are airway-targeted and have lesser cardiac effect.

Hints:

1. The signalling cascade culminates in PKA phosphorylation.
2. Three principal targets are Cav1.2, phospholamban, and troponin I.
3. The same pathway produces positive inotropy _and_ positive lusitropy.

QUESTION 8
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The cardiac muscle absolute refractory period spans most of the contraction cycle. The functional consequence is:

Correct answer: The heart cannot be tetanised — the long refractory period (paralleling the AP plateau, ~200 ms) prevents summation, allowing alternating contraction–relaxation and protecting diastolic filling.

Distractors:

- "The heart can be tetanised." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the consequence of the long refractory period. Correction: tetany would prevent diastolic filling and drop cardiac output to zero; the long cardiac refractory period prevents this.
- "Tetanus is possible only at very high heart rates." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a hypothetical tetanic state to extreme tachycardia. Correction: even at extreme tachycardia, the cardiac AP refractory period prevents tetanisation; if it were violated, fibrillation would ensue (which is uncoordinated, not tetanic).
- "The refractory period is shorter than that of skeletal muscle." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the comparison. Correction: cardiac refractoriness (~200 ms) is much _longer_ than skeletal (~5 ms); skeletal muscle is designed to tetanise, cardiac muscle is not.

Explanation: The cardiac AP plateau (~200 ms) imposes an absolute refractory period that lasts roughly the entire contraction. New action potentials cannot be triggered until repolarisation is well underway — preventing summation and tetany [Berne & Levy ch.13; Costanzo ch.4]. This is essential because tetany would prevent diastolic filling, dropping cardiac output to zero. Skeletal muscle, in contrast, has brief refractoriness (~5 ms) and is designed to tetanise. **Effective refractory period (ERP)** corresponds to most of the AP duration; a stimulus during the relative refractory period (the late part of phase 3) can elicit a propagating but abnormal response — which can initiate ventricular tachyarrhythmias (e.g., R-on-T phenomenon). Class III antiarrhythmics deliberately prolong AP and ERP to increase the post-repolarisation refractoriness window.

Hints:

1. The duration of the refractory period roughly matches the duration of contraction.
2. Tetany would prevent diastolic filling.
3. Cardiac and skeletal muscles differ greatly in this property.

Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Cardiac muscle obtains the bulk of its ATP from:

Correct answer: Oxidative phosphorylation, using a wide range of substrates (fatty acids ~60–70%, glucose, lactate, ketones); mitochondria occupy ~30% of cell volume.

Distractors:

- "Anaerobic glycolysis." — Reveals misconception: student attributes cardiac energy production to anaerobic metabolism. Correction: cardiac muscle relies almost exclusively on aerobic / oxidative metabolism; anaerobic glycolysis contributes only briefly during severe ischaemia.
- "Phosphocreatine alone." — Reveals misconception: student attributes sustained energy supply to PCr buffer. Correction: PCr provides a small immediate buffer; sustained ATP supply is from oxidative phosphorylation.
- "Glucose-only oxidation." — Reveals misconception: student names a single substrate. Correction: cardiac muscle is metabolically flexible — fatty acids dominate at rest, but glucose, lactate, and ketones all contribute substantially under different conditions.

Explanation: Healthy cardiac muscle runs almost exclusively on oxidative phosphorylation. Fatty acids account for 60–70% of ATP at rest; glucose, lactate, and ketones provide the remainder [Berne & Levy ch.13 — Cardiac Muscle Metabolism]. Mitochondria occupy ~30% of cell volume — among the highest of any tissue. The myocardium relies on continuous coronary flow — an interruption produces ischaemia within seconds and infarction within minutes; cardiac muscle has minimal capacity to use anaerobic glycolysis under sustained workload. **Substrate utilisation shifts** in heart failure (toward glucose and ketones), in diabetes (toward fatty acids), and in ischaemia (toward glucose if oxygen is limited; lactate accumulates). **SGLT2 inhibitors** (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) appear to provide cardiac benefit partly through ketone body utilisation and metabolic efficiency. Carnitine deficiency impairs fatty acid mitochondrial entry and can cause cardiomyopathy.

Hints:

1. The dominant pathway uses oxygen.
2. Multiple substrates are used; one dominates at rest.
3. Mitochondria are abundant in the cardiac myocyte.

QUESTION 10
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Cardiac muscle hypertrophy in response to chronic pressure overload (e.g., hypertension, aortic stenosis) is best characterised as:

Correct answer: Concentric hypertrophy — sarcomeres added in parallel, thickened walls, and reduced or normal chamber volume. The increased wall thickness reduces wall stress (Laplace: T = P × r / (2 × h)) and is initially compensatory.

Distractors:

- "Eccentric hypertrophy with increased chamber volume." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the response to pressure vs volume overload. Correction: eccentric hypertrophy (sarcomeres added in series, dilated chamber) is the response to _volume_ overload (regurgitant valve disease), not pressure overload.
- "Pure hyperplasia." — Reveals misconception: student attributes cardiac growth to fibre proliferation. Correction: adult cardiomyocytes have very limited replicative capacity; cardiac hypertrophy is essentially purely cellular hypertrophy, not hyperplasia.
- "Atrophy with fibrosis only." — Reveals misconception: student picks the late decompensated picture only. Correction: chronic pressure overload first produces compensatory hypertrophy; only later (decompensation) does fibrosis dominate, with eventual systolic dysfunction.

Explanation: **Pressure overload** (hypertension, aortic stenosis) → high systolic wall stress → sarcomeres added in parallel → **concentric hypertrophy** (thicker wall, normal/reduced cavity volume) [Berne & Levy ch.13 — Hypertrophy; Ganong 26e]. **Volume overload** (regurgitant valve disease — AR, MR; chronic anaemia; AVF) → high diastolic wall stress → sarcomeres added in series → **eccentric hypertrophy** (dilated chamber). Pathological hypertrophy is associated with re-expression of "fetal" gene programmes (β-MyHC, ANP, BNP, skeletal α-actin) and progresses to heart failure if unrelieved, with fibrosis, apoptosis, and contractile dysfunction. **Physiological hypertrophy** (athletic heart, pregnancy) involves a different signalling profile — IGF-1 / PI3K / Akt — and is reversible. RAAS inhibition (ACEi / ARB / ARNi) and β-blockade reduce pathological hypertrophy and improve outcomes.

Hints:

1. Pressure overload adds sarcomeres in _parallel_.
2. Volume overload adds sarcomeres in _series_.
3. The Laplace relation explains why thicker walls reduce wall stress.

Stem: The Frank-Starling law states that, within physiological limits, the stroke volume of the heart:

Correct answer: Increases as end-diastolic volume (preload) increases — the heart pumps what it receives. Mechanism: stretch increases myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity (length-dependent activation) and improves thick–thin filament overlap.

Distractors:

- "Decreases as end-diastolic volume increases." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the Frank-Starling relationship. Correction: within physiological limits, more preload produces _more_ stroke volume; the relationship is monotonic and ascending.
- "Is independent of preload." — Reveals misconception: student denies a fundamental physiological law. Correction: the heart's intrinsic Frank-Starling response makes stroke volume tightly preload-dependent on a beat-to-beat basis.
- "Is determined exclusively by afterload." — Reveals misconception: student picks one determinant exclusively. Correction: stroke volume reflects preload, afterload, and contractility together; the Frank-Starling law specifically describes the preload contribution.

Explanation: Within the operating range, an increase in end-diastolic fibre length (preload) increases stroke volume by enhancing both myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity (length-dependent activation — increased TnC affinity for Ca²⁺ at longer sarcomere length, plus reduced inter-filament spacing bringing actin closer to myosin) and overlap of thick-thin filaments [Berne & Levy ch.13; Costanzo ch.4]. The intrinsic Frank-Starling response operates beat-to-beat, matching right and left ventricular outputs and adjusting to changes in venous return. Ventricular function curves shift up with positive inotropy (sympathetic stimulation, digoxin) and down with negative inotropy (β-blocker, ischaemia, HFrEF). Frank-Starling allows the denervated transplanted heart to increase output during exercise primarily via increased venous return.

Hints:

1. The relationship is intrinsic — independent of nerves or hormones.
2. Increased preload → increased stroke volume.
3. Length-dependent activation is the molecular basis.

QUESTION 12
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The cardiac length–tension curve operates almost entirely on its ascending limb because:

Correct answer: Resting cardiac sarcomere length is constrained near 1.9–2.0 µm by stiff cardiac titin and the rich extracellular matrix, so the heart never reaches the descending limb of overlap-loss; Frank-Starling response is therefore monotonically ascending.

Distractors:

- "The heart is normally short." — Reveals misconception: student offers a vague non-mechanistic answer. Correction: the heart's chamber and fibre lengths span normal physiological ranges; the answer lies in the constraints on sarcomere stretching, not on overall heart size.
- "Cardiac actin is shorter." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the property to a shorter thin filament. Correction: cardiac and skeletal thin-filament lengths are similar; the difference is in titin stiffness and ECM constraint of sarcomere stretching.
- "Troponin C is absent in cardiac muscle." — Reveals misconception: student denies a thin-filament regulatory protein. Correction: cardiac muscle has its own TnC isoform (cTnC); the regulatory system is intact and Ca²⁺-dependent.

Explanation: Unlike skeletal muscle, cardiac sarcomeres rarely exceed ~2.2 µm because of cardiac titin (which is stiffer than skeletal titin, with shorter compliant elements) and the rich extracellular matrix that constrains diastolic stretch [Berne & Levy ch.13; Costanzo ch.4]. This means an overstretched heart does not enter the descending limb of overlap-loss within the physiological range. The functional implication: the Frank-Starling response is monotonic — more preload (within limits) always gives more force. **In severe pathological dilatation** (e.g., end-stage cardiomyopathy with very high LVEDV), other geometric Laplace effects (large cavity radius → high wall stress) reduce wall efficiency, but this is not the result of crossing the descending limb of length-tension. Cardiac myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity is also notably steeper and more length-dependent than skeletal — augmenting the Frank-Starling effect.

Hints:

1. The constraint is mechanical, not biochemical.
2. Cardiac titin and ECM are stiffer than skeletal counterparts.
3. The heart never operates on the descending limb under physiological conditions.

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Positive lusitropy refers to:

Correct answer: Increased rate of relaxation — faster Ca²⁺ removal during diastole, allowing more rapid diastolic filling at higher heart rates.

Distractors:

- "Increased force of contraction." — Reveals misconception: student confuses lusitropy with inotropy. Correction: inotropy = force; lusitropy = relaxation rate; chronotropy = heart rate; dromotropy = conduction speed. Don't conflate the suffixes.
- "Increased heart rate." — Reveals misconception: student conflates lusitropy with chronotropy. Correction: chronotropy is heart rate; lusitropy is relaxation rate.
- "Increased atrial filling." — Reveals misconception: student picks a downstream consequence rather than the property itself. Correction: improved diastolic relaxation does facilitate atrial filling, but lusitropy is the molecular property of faster relaxation.

Explanation: **Lusitropy** = relaxation rate. Positive lusitropy = faster relaxation, allowing more rapid diastolic filling at higher heart rates [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. β-Adrenergic stimulation produces positive lusitropy by PKA-mediated phosphorylation of: (i) **phospholamban** — relieves PLB inhibition of SERCA2a → faster Ca²⁺ reuptake into SR; (ii) **troponin I** — lowers myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity → faster cross-bridge release at the same falling Ca²⁺ level. The pairing of positive inotropy AND positive lusitropy is essential during exercise: the heart must contract harder _and_ relax faster to fit more cycles into the shortened cardiac cycle of tachycardia. Impaired lusitropy contributes to HFpEF (the stiff non-compliant ventricle of diastolic dysfunction).

Hints:

1. Suffix "-tropy" with prefix "lusi-" (Latin for play / loosen).
2. The opposite of contraction force; refers to relaxation.
3. β-adrenergic stimulation produces it via PLB and TnI phosphorylation.

- "Aspirin alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks an antiplatelet. Correction: aspirin is appropriate for prevention of atherothrombosis but does not relax vasospasm; CCB and nitrates address the smooth-muscle pathophysiology.
- "Digoxin." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cardiac glycoside unrelated to vasospasm. Correction: digoxin has no role in coronary vasospasm; it is used for AF rate control and HFrEF symptom relief.

Explanation: Variant / Prinzmetal angina is caused by transient coronary artery vasospasm at rest (often nocturnal, ST elevation on ECG during episodes, normal coronaries on angiography or non-obstructive plaque) [Berne & Levy ch.14; Ganong 26e]. **Treatment**: **calcium channel blockers** (dihydropyridines such as amlodipine, or non-DHPs such as diltiazem) and **long-acting nitrates** (isosorbide mononitrate). β-Blockers can paradoxically worsen vasospasm by leaving α1-adrenergic vasoconstriction unopposed, and are not first-line. **Risk factors**: smoking (single most important modifiable), cocaine use (potent coronary vasospasm trigger), severe hyperventilation, autonomic dysfunction, magnesium deficiency. **Smoking cessation** and **lipid management** are also important. Provocation testing with ergonovine or acetylcholine is sometimes used during angiography to confirm diagnosis.

Hints:

1. The pathophysiology is vasospasm, not fixed atherosclerosis.
2. CCB relaxes vascular smooth muscle by blocking Ca²⁺ entry.
3. β-Blockers can paradoxically worsen the condition.

QUESTION 28
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The clinical phenomenon by which a hollow viscus (e.g., bladder, gut) accommodates increased volume without a proportionate rise in pressure — owing to the time-dependent yielding of smooth muscle to stretch — is called:

Correct answer: Stress-relaxation (also called plasticity) — after an initial rise in tension upon filling, tension falls back over seconds-to-minutes as the smooth muscle "yields" to the new length.

Distractors:

- "Tetanus." — Reveals misconception: student picks a sustained-contraction phenomenon from skeletal muscle. Correction: tetanus is sustained force from rapid stimulation in skeletal muscle; stress-relaxation is the _opposite_ — yielding of force despite sustained stretch.
- "Latch state." — Reveals misconception: student picks a related smooth-muscle phenomenon. Correction: latch is sustained low-energy force _despite_ low Ca²⁺; stress-relaxation is yielding of force during sustained stretch — distinct phenomena.
- "Frank-Starling." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cardiac phenomenon (length-dependent activation increasing force). Correction: Frank-Starling is the heart's increased force at increased preload; stress-relaxation is smooth muscle's _decreased_ force at sustained stretch.

Explanation: **Stress-relaxation** allows smooth muscle to adapt to sustained stretch; after an initial rise in tension upon filling, tension falls back over seconds-to-minutes as the muscle "yields." This is critical for bladder and intestinal storage function — the bladder can fill from 0 to 300 mL with only modest rise in detrusor pressure (compliance) [Guyton & Hall ch.8 — Stress-Relaxation; Berne & Levy ch.14]. The opposite phenomenon — **reverse stress-relaxation** — occurs when load is reduced (the muscle slowly "tightens up"). Both reflect the slow cross-bridge cycling and re-arrangement of contractile filaments and dense bodies in smooth muscle. **Clinical correlates**: detrusor overactivity (loss of compliance — small bladder pressures rise rapidly; treated with antimuscarinics, β3 agonist mirabegron, intradetrusor botulinum toxin); intestinal accommodation reflex after a meal (vasoactive intestinal peptide-mediated relaxation); pregnancy uterine accommodation; aortic Windkessel function (an analogous phenomenon in vascular elastic tissue).

Hints:

1. The phenomenon allows accommodation without pressure rise.
2. The bladder is the classic clinical example.
3. The smooth muscle yields to sustained stretch.
