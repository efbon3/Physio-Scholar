## Pass 1 — Comprehension and reasoning

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Describe the production, circulation, and absorption of cerebrospinal fluid. What functional roles does CSF play, and how does the system maintain steady-state volume?

Model answer: Production: ~500 mL/day (~0.35 mL/min) by the choroid plexuses in the lateral, third, and fourth ventricles; modified ependymal epithelium with apical Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase actively pumps Na⁺ into the ventricular lumen, with Cl⁻ following passively and water by aquaporin-1. Production is largely independent of ICP within physiological range. Circulation: lateral ventricles → foramina of Monro → third ventricle → aqueduct of Sylvius → fourth ventricle → foramina of Luschka (lateral) and Magendie (midline) → cisterna magna and subarachnoid space; CSF then bathes the brain and spinal cord. Absorption: arachnoid villi (granulations) projecting into dural venous sinuses (especially superior sagittal sinus) act as one-way pressure-sensitive valves opening at ~1.5 mm Hg gradient; ~85–90% of CSF is reabsorbed here; the remainder via lymphatic drainage along cranial nerves and the glymphatic system to cervical lymph nodes. Functional roles: cushioning (brain floats, apparent weight reduced from 1400 g to ~50 g); chemical homeostasis (stable ionic environment, low protein); waste clearance via the glymphatic system; pressure buffering through the Monro–Kellie doctrine. Steady state: ~150 mL pool replaced 3–4× daily; ICP-independent production with ICP-sensitive absorption [Guyton & Hall ch.62; Boron & Boulpaep ch.10].

Elaborative explanation: The system is engineered for _constant production with feedback-modulated absorption_ — disruption of production (choroid plexus tumour), circulation (aqueductal stenosis), or absorption (post-meningitic communicating hydrocephalus) all produce hydrocephalus.

Hints:

1. Production at choroid plexus, ICP-independent.
2. Circulation through Monro → Sylvius → Luschka/Magendie.
3. Absorption at arachnoid villi, ICP-sensitive.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Quantifies production rate (~500 mL/day) and steady-state volume (~150 mL).
- Names the four CSF circulation foramina (Monro, Sylvius, Luschka, Magendie).
- Identifies arachnoid villi/superior sagittal sinus as the major absorption site.
- Lists ≥ 3 functions (cushioning, chemical homeostasis, waste clearance, pressure buffering).
- Notes ICP-independent production vs ICP-sensitive absorption.

A Yellow answer covers production and circulation but misses the absorption mechanism or functional roles.

A Red answer treats CSF as static or omits the choroid plexus / villi pathway.

Common misconceptions:

- "CSF production is ICP-dependent.": Correction: production is largely ICP-independent; _absorption_ is the ICP-sensitive arm of the homeostatic loop.
- "All CSF drains through arachnoid villi.": Correction: ~85–90% via villi, with a smaller contribution from glymphatic drainage to cervical lymph nodes.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Explain the structure and function of the blood–brain barrier. Why does the BBB exist, and how does it shape drug development and clinical pharmacology?

Model answer: Structure: the BBB is formed by tight junctions (claudin-5, occludin, ZO-1) between cerebral capillary endothelial cells, supported by pericytes and astrocyte end-feet — the _neurovascular unit_. Endothelial cells lack fenestrations and have low pinocytotic activity; ATP-dependent efflux pumps (P-glycoprotein, BCRP, MRPs) actively extrude xenobiotics. Function: (i) protects the brain from blood-borne toxins, ions, and pathogens; (ii) maintains a stable ionic milieu (low protein, K⁺, Ca²⁺) for predictable neuronal excitability; (iii) regulates passage of nutrients via specific transporters (GLUT1 for glucose, LAT-1 for large neutral amino acids including L-DOPA, MCT1 for monocarboxylates, choline transporter, transferrin receptor for iron); (iv) clears metabolic waste. Why exists: neuronal firing depends on tight ionic control; even small fluctuations in extracellular K⁺ would alter excitability; the BBB isolates the CNS from the systemic ionic noise. Lipophilic molecules (O₂, CO₂, ethanol, anaesthetics, opioids, steroid hormones) cross freely; water-soluble molecules require transporters. Drug-development implications: most polar drugs cannot reach therapeutic concentrations in the CNS — limits delivery of antibiotics (e.g., aminoglycosides), chemotherapy, and protein therapeutics. Strategies: (i) lipophilic prodrugs (heroin → morphine; L-DOPA → dopamine); (ii) transporter-targeted molecules (LAT-1 substrates); (iii) BBB disruption (osmotic mannitol, focused ultrasound); (iv) bypass via intrathecal/intraventricular delivery; (v) inhibit P-gp efflux to raise drug concentrations. Disruption: stroke, inflammation, tumours, hypertensive crises increase BBB permeability — explains contrast enhancement on imaging and the access of anti-cancer agents to brain tumours [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10].

Elaborative explanation: The BBB is best thought of as a _regulated interface_, not a wall. Specific transporters provide selective access; efflux pumps actively remove unwanted molecules; the barrier is dynamically modulated in disease. Modern CNS drug discovery is essentially the science of crossing this interface.

Hints:

1. Tight junctions + pericytes + astrocytes = neurovascular unit.
2. Lipophilic free, polar requires transporters.
3. P-gp efflux limits many drugs.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the molecular components of the tight junction (claudin-5 or equivalent).
- Identifies pericytes and astrocyte end-feet as supporting elements.
- Names ≥ 2 BBB transporters with their substrates (GLUT1/glucose, LAT-1/L-DOPA).
- Articulates ≥ 2 drug-development strategies (prodrug, BBB disruption, transporter targeting).
- Mentions P-gp efflux as a barrier to many drugs.

A Yellow answer covers structure and function but misses the drug-development implications.

A Red answer treats the BBB as a passive wall without acknowledging its regulated, transporter-dependent permeability.

Common misconceptions:

- "The BBB is impermeable.": Correction: it is a _selective_ interface — lipophilic molecules cross freely, polar molecules require transporters, and disease modulates permeability.
- "Disruption of the BBB is always pathological.": Correction: focused-ultrasound or osmotic mannitol disruption is _therapeutic_ in selected contexts (chemotherapy delivery, gene therapy).

Stem: Describe how cerebral blood flow is regulated. Discuss the relative contributions of metabolic, myogenic, neurogenic, and chemical control, and explain why PaCO₂ is the dominant physiological regulator.

Model answer: Mechanisms: (i) Metabolic — local activity-dependent vasodilation via H⁺, K⁺, adenosine, NO, lactate, and arachidonic-acid metabolites released by neurons and astrocytes (neurovascular coupling); the basis of fMRI BOLD signals. (ii) Myogenic — Bayliss response: arteriolar smooth muscle contracts when stretched by rising transmural pressure, helping maintain CBF on the autoregulatory plateau (MAP 60–150 mm Hg). (iii) Neurogenic — sympathetic and parasympathetic innervation modulates large vessels (e.g., circle of Willis), contributing to autoregulation upper limit and protecting against hypertensive surges; less important than peripheral vascular beds. (iv) Chemical — PaCO₂ is the dominant regulator: CBF rises ~3–4% per mm Hg PaCO₂ rise; doubling PaCO₂ approximately doubles CBF. Mechanism: CO₂ crosses the BBB freely → hydrates to H₂CO₃ → dissociates to H⁺; perivascular H⁺ relaxes arteriolar smooth muscle. PaO₂ is a much weaker regulator: cerebral vasodilation begins only when PaO₂ falls below ~50–60 mm Hg. Why PaCO₂ dominates: (i) CO₂ is the most sensitive metabolic indicator (small changes signify changing brain metabolism or ventilation); (ii) PaO₂ is more tightly defended by ventilation/Hb saturation, making it a poor real-time indicator; (iii) CO₂ readily crosses the BBB (HCO₃⁻ does not, so blood pH changes alone do not directly affect cerebral vessels); (iv) the H⁺ vasodilator signal is generated _locally_ in proportion to local PaCO₂. Clinical exploitation: therapeutic hyperventilation (lowers PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduces brain volume → transiently lowers ICP) in head injury; permissive hypercapnia in mechanical ventilation must be used cautiously when ICP is at risk [Guyton & Hall ch.62; Boron & Boulpaep ch.10].

Elaborative explanation: The four mechanisms operate at different scales: metabolic at the local arteriole (matches flow to need), myogenic at the systemic level (buffers against perfusion-pressure changes), neurogenic provides global tone modulation, and chemical (PaCO₂) integrates ventilation and metabolism. Together they provide highly regulated CBF with rapid responsiveness.

Hints:

1. Four mechanisms: metabolic, myogenic, neurogenic, chemical.
2. CO₂ produces H⁺ → vasodilation; ~3–4% per mm Hg.
3. PaO₂ is a weak regulator until very low.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Describes all four regulatory mechanisms with their key mediators.
- Quantifies the CO₂ sensitivity (~3–4% per mm Hg).
- Names the autoregulatory range (60–150 mm Hg MAP).
- Identifies H⁺ as the proximal vasodilator from CO₂ hydration.
- Explains why PaCO₂ dominates (sensitivity, BBB permeability, local H⁺ signal).
- Mentions therapeutic hyperventilation as a clinical application.

A Yellow answer covers PaCO₂ and autoregulation but misses one of the other three mechanisms.

A Red answer treats CBF as passively pressure-driven without acknowledging the regulatory mechanisms.

Common misconceptions:

- "Hypoxia is the main driver of cerebral vasodilation.": Correction: PaCO₂ is dominant; PaO₂ matters only at < ~50 mm Hg.
- "Blood pH directly modulates cerebral vessels.": Correction: HCO₃⁻ does not cross the BBB easily; the cerebral vasodilatory signal is _local_ H⁺ generated from CO₂ hydration.

Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: The Monro–Kellie doctrine constrains how the brain handles changes in intracranial volume. Explain the doctrine, the compliance curve, and why slowly growing lesions can be silent for months while small acute changes can be catastrophic.

Model answer: Doctrine: the cranial vault is a _rigid, fixed-volume container_ enclosing three components — brain (~80%), blood (~10%), and CSF (~10%). Total volume must remain constant; an increase in any one component must be compensated by a decrease in another, or ICP rises. Compliance curve: ICP plotted against added volume is biphasic — initially nearly flat (high compliance — small added volume produces little ICP rise), then steeply rising (low compliance — small added volume produces large ICP rise). Compensatory mechanisms: (i) CSF displacement to spinal subarachnoid space (the most rapid and effective); (ii) reduction in cerebral venous blood (cerebral venous pressure can be lowered by venous compression); (iii) reduced CSF production (modest effect); (iv) brain tissue shift (only at later stages). Slow lesions vs acute lesions: slow-growing lesions (meningioma, low-grade glioma) develop over months — compensation operates throughout, displacement of CSF and venous blood maintains low ICP; the compliance curve is traversed gradually, and the tumour may reach 50–100 mL before symptoms appear. Acute lesions (haematoma after trauma, ruptured aneurysm) add volume rapidly — compensation cannot keep up; the patient is on the steep portion of the curve, and a 10–20 mL haematoma can produce critical ICP rise. Once the steep portion is reached, even small _additional_ volumes (post-operative bleeding, cerebral oedema, hypoventilation-induced cerebral vasodilation) produce dramatic ICP elevation. Clinical implications: imaging may show large slow-growing tumours in awake, asymptomatic patients; conversely, small acute haemorrhages mandate urgent intervention; ICP monitoring is essential when compensation is exhausted; treatment targets each component (CSF drainage via EVD; brain volume via osmotic agents and hyperventilation; blood volume via head elevation, deep sedation, and barbiturates) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Guyton & Hall ch.62].

Elaborative explanation: The doctrine is essentially a budget constraint. The brain's response to a new mass effect is to "spend" its compensatory reserves first; once exhausted, every further volume increase must be paid for in pressure. The compliance curve's S-shape explains the _clinical phenomenon_ of long latency followed by rapid deterioration — patients can appear well, then crash within hours.

Hints:

1. Brain + blood + CSF = constant.
2. Initial compensation (CSF displacement) is silent; steep curve follows.
3. Slow tumours have more time to compensate than acute haematomas.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States the rigid vault + three components principle.
- Describes the biphasic compliance curve (flat then steep).
- Names ≥ 2 compensatory mechanisms (CSF displacement, venous reduction).
- Contrasts slow vs acute lesion behaviour using the curve.
- Identifies clinical implications (imaging silence, urgent intervention for acute lesions).

A Yellow answer states the doctrine but misses the compliance-curve dynamics.

A Red answer treats ICP as linearly proportional to added volume.

Common misconceptions:

- "Larger lesions always cause more raised ICP.": Correction: a slow 100 mL meningioma may have low ICP because compensation has had time; a 30 mL acute haematoma may have life-threatening ICP because the steep curve is reached suddenly.
- "Mannitol works by reducing CSF production.": Correction: mannitol acts as an osmotic diuretic drawing water from brain _across the intact BBB_, reducing brain-tissue volume.

Stem: Describe the classification, distribution, and physiology of the major glial cell populations in the central nervous system. How does each type contribute to normal CNS function and to disease?

Model answer: Four major populations: (1) Astrocytes — most numerous CNS glia; protoplasmic in grey matter (closer to synapses), fibrous in white matter (closer to axon tracts); functions: K⁺ buffering (Kir4.1 channels prevent extracellular K⁺ accumulation during firing), glutamate uptake (EAAT1/2 transporters), water homeostasis (AQP4), BBB support via end-feet, neurovascular coupling (release vasodilators in response to neuronal activity), metabolic support (lactate shuttle to neurons), tripartite synapse modulation. Disease: reactive gliosis (GFAP+) after CNS injury creates scar tissue that impedes regeneration; astrocytic K⁺ buffer failure contributes to seizure spread; AQP4 antibodies cause neuromyelitis optica. (2) Oligodendrocytes — myelinate CNS axons; one cell ensheaths up to ~50 internodes (vs Schwann cells, one cell per internode in PNS). Function: faster saltatory conduction, energy efficiency, axonal trophic support (glycolytic supply via monocarboxylate transporters). Disease: multiple sclerosis (autoimmune attack on CNS myelin), leukodystrophies (inherited myelin disorders), progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (JC virus-mediated oligodendrocyte killing). (3) Microglia — resident CNS immune cells; yolk-sac origin, distinct from circulating monocytes; ramified processes in healthy state, become amoeboid on activation; functions: immune surveillance, phagocytosis of debris and pathogens, synaptic pruning during development and learning, cytokine release. Disease: chronic activation contributes to Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, multiple sclerosis, traumatic brain injury, neurodegeneration; microglial dysfunction in white-matter disease. (4) Ependymal cells — line ventricles and central canal; modified at choroid plexus to secrete CSF; specialised tanycytes in third ventricle bridge CSF and median eminence (neuroendocrine sensing). Disease: ependymomas, hydrocephalus from blocked ependymal-lined aqueducts. Plus minor: NG2 glia (oligodendrocyte progenitors), perivascular macrophages [Berne & Levy ch.4; Boron & Boulpaep ch.10].

Elaborative explanation: Glia outnumber neurons in the CNS but historically were viewed only as "support" cells. Modern neuroscience recognises that essentially every neurological disorder has a glial component — sometimes primary (MS, leukodystrophies, NMO), sometimes contributory (Alzheimer's microglial activation). Pharmacological targeting of glia (anti-CD20 ocrelizumab in MS, microglial modulators in trial) is a frontier of CNS therapy.

Hints:

1. Four major types: astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, ependymal cells.
2. Each has a primary role (K⁺ buffer, myelination, immunity, lining).
3. Each links to specific diseases.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the four major glial types and their distributions.
- Lists ≥ 3 astrocytic functions (K⁺ buffer, glutamate uptake, BBB support, neurovascular coupling).
- Distinguishes oligodendrocyte (CNS) from Schwann cell (PNS) myelination.
- Identifies microglial yolk-sac origin and dual role (surveillance, neurodegeneration).
- Maps ≥ 3 glial diseases (MS, NMO, Alzheimer's microglial activation).

A Yellow answer covers astrocytes and oligodendrocytes but misses microglial or ependymal details.

A Red answer treats glia as undifferentiated "support cells" with no functional or pathological specificity.

Common misconceptions:

- "Glial cells are passive support cells.": Correction: glia actively regulate K⁺, glutamate, water, blood flow, and myelination; many neurological diseases are _primarily_ glial.
- "Microglia are derived from circulating monocytes.": Correction: microglia are _yolk-sac-derived_ tissue-resident immune cells distinct from circulating monocytes.

Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Explain the autonomic nervous system divisions and their general anatomical–functional principles (sympathetic, parasympathetic, enteric). How does the autonomic outflow differ from somatic motor outflow?

Model answer: The autonomic nervous system (ANS) controls visceral function — cardiovascular, respiratory, GI, urinary, reproductive, metabolic, thermoregulatory — through three divisions. (1) Sympathetic ("fight or flight"): preganglionic neurons in T1–L2 (thoracolumbar) intermediolateral cell column → short cholinergic preganglionic fibres → paravertebral chain ganglia (or prevertebral ganglia: coeliac, superior/inferior mesenteric) → long noradrenergic postganglionic fibres → target organs. Adrenal medulla is a sympathetic ganglion equivalent — preganglionic cholinergic fibres directly innervate chromaffin cells, which release adrenaline (~80%) and noradrenaline (~20%) into the blood. Effects: tachycardia, vasoconstriction, bronchodilation, mydriasis, sweating, glycogenolysis, lipolysis. (2) Parasympathetic ("rest and digest"): preganglionic neurons in cranial nerves III, VII, IX, X (CN X / vagus carries ~75% of parasympathetic outflow) and S2–S4 sacral (craniosacral) → long cholinergic preganglionic fibres → ganglia close to or within target organ → short cholinergic postganglionic fibres → target organs. Effects: bradycardia, bronchoconstriction, miosis, lacrimation, salivation, GI motility, micturition, erection. (3) Enteric: ~500 million neurons in submucosal (Meissner) and myenteric (Auerbach) plexuses of the GI wall; controls peristalsis, secretion, blood flow, immunity _autonomously_ (gut "brain"); modulated by sympathetic and parasympathetic input. Receptors: sympathetic uses α₁ (vasoconstriction), α₂ (presynaptic inhibition, CNS), β₁ (cardiac inotropy/chronotropy), β₂ (smooth-muscle relaxation), β₃ (lipolysis). Parasympathetic uses M₁–M₅ muscarinic receptors plus nicotinic at ganglia. Differences from somatic motor: somatic — single neuron (motor cortex/anterior horn → muscle), nicotinic ACh, voluntary, skeletal muscle only. Autonomic — two-neuron chain, dual transmitter (ACh at ganglion, NA or ACh at target), involuntary, all visceral targets [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: The two-neuron architecture of the ANS allows divergence (one preganglionic to many post-ganglionic fibres) and integration at ganglia. The sympathetic chain provides a coordinated mass response; the parasympathetic provides discrete organ-by-organ control. The enteric NS is functionally autonomous, capable of coordinating peristalsis without CNS input.

Hints:

1. Sympathetic = thoracolumbar; parasympathetic = craniosacral.
2. Two-neuron chain: pre + post-ganglionic.
3. Adrenal medulla = sympathetic ganglion analogue.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the three divisions and their craniospinal origins.
- Distinguishes pre-/post-ganglionic transmitter use (ACh nicotinic at all ganglia; NA at sympathetic targets except sweat glands; ACh at parasympathetic targets).
- Names ≥ 4 autonomic receptor subtypes (α₁, α₂, β₁, β₂, M₂, etc.).
- Identifies the enteric NS as semi-autonomous.
- Contrasts ANS with somatic motor architecture.

A Yellow answer covers sympathetic and parasympathetic but misses the enteric NS or the receptor pharmacology.

A Red answer treats the ANS as a single system without the sympathetic/parasympathetic distinction.

Common misconceptions:

- "All sympathetic postganglionic fibres release noradrenaline.": Correction: sympathetic postganglionic fibres to _sweat glands_ release _acetylcholine_ (cholinergic sympathetic) — a classic exception.
- "The vagus is mostly motor.": Correction: ~80% of vagal fibres are _afferent_ (carrying visceral sensation to the CNS); only ~20% are efferent.

Stem: The brain is uniquely vulnerable to circulatory and metabolic disruption. Explain why, and discuss the time courses of injury for hypoxia, hypoglycaemia, and combined hypoxic–ischaemic insult.

Model answer: Why uniquely vulnerable: (i) high metabolic rate — brain is ~2% of body mass but consumes ~20% of body O₂ and ~120 g/day of glucose; (ii) obligate aerobic glucose metabolism — neurons cannot store glucose or significantly use alternative fuels (except ketones in prolonged starvation); (iii) limited substrate reserves — endogenous glucose stores last ~2 minutes, ATP for ~10 seconds of ion-pumping at full activity; (iv) tightly regulated ionic environment — even small ATP failures collapse Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, leading to cellular swelling and excitotoxicity; (v) selective vulnerability — hippocampal CA1, cerebellar Purkinje cells, cortical layers 3 and 5, basal ganglia are most susceptible. Hypoxia (low PaO₂, normal flow): consciousness lost in 5–10 seconds at PaO₂ ~30 mm Hg; reversible if PaO₂ restored within minutes; selective neuronal injury at ~5–10 minutes; cellular ATP depletion → glutamate release → excitotoxicity → Ca²⁺ overload → mitochondrial failure → apoptosis/necrosis. Hypoglycaemia (low blood glucose, normal flow): symptoms (sweating, tremor, confusion) at glucose ~3 mmol/L; neuroglycopenia (impaired consciousness) at < 2.5 mmol/L; coma at < 1.5 mmol/L; permanent damage if hypoglycaemia is prolonged (> 30 min) at very low levels — particularly affects cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia. Combined hypoxic–ischaemic insult (cardiac arrest): worst case — no flow, no O₂, no glucose; loss of consciousness in 5–10 seconds; permanent neuronal injury beginning at 4–5 minutes at normothermia; widespread infarction by 10 minutes. Mechanism: ATP depletion → membrane-pump failure → cellular swelling → glutamate release → NMDA receptor overactivation → Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial dysfunction → reactive oxygen species → apoptosis. Therapeutic hypothermia (32–34°C for 24 h post-arrest) reduces CMRO₂ by ~6–8% per °C and limits secondary injury. Penumbra concept: in focal ischaemia (stroke), CBF 10–20 mL/100 g/min creates a viable but threatened "penumbra" surrounding an infarct core (CBF < 10) — reperfusion (tPA, thrombectomy) salvages penumbra if achieved within hours [Guyton & Hall ch.62; Boron & Boulpaep ch.10].

Elaborative explanation: The brain runs at the metabolic edge: any modest reduction in O₂ or glucose supply rapidly outpaces compensatory mechanisms. The clinical implication is the strict time-to-treatment urgency in cardiac arrest, stroke, and severe hypoglycaemia. Selective vulnerability of specific neuronal populations explains characteristic clinical patterns (e.g., hippocampal amnesia after global ischaemia).

Hints:

1. High demand + minimal reserves + tight regulation.
2. Hypoxia: minutes; hypoglycaemia: tens of minutes; combined: 4–5 min.
3. Therapeutic hypothermia and reperfusion exploit the time window.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Quotes the brain's metabolic share (~20% O₂, ~120 g glucose/day).
- Identifies obligate aerobic glucose metabolism and limited reserves as the vulnerability.
- Names the cellular cascade (ATP failure → pump failure → glutamate → Ca²⁺ → necrosis/apoptosis).
- Quantifies the time courses for hypoxia (~5–10 min) and combined H–I (~4–5 min).
- Mentions selective vulnerability (hippocampal CA1, Purkinje cells).
- Names therapeutic hypothermia and reperfusion as time-sensitive interventions.

A Yellow answer covers hypoxia and hypoglycaemia but misses the cellular mechanism or selective vulnerability.

A Red answer treats brain ischaemia as undifferentiated cell death without the cascade or time courses.

Common misconceptions:

- "The brain can use ketones as a primary fuel acutely.": Correction: ketone utilisation requires up-regulation of MCT transporters and metabolic adaptation over days; _acute_ ketone substitution is limited.
- "Hippocampal vulnerability is incidental.": Correction: CA1 pyramidal neurons have high NMDA receptor density and vulnerability to excitotoxicity — explaining post-arrest amnesia.

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: A 65-year-old man develops sudden-onset right-sided weakness, expressive aphasia, and gaze deviation to the left. CT shows no haemorrhage. Decompose this presentation in terms of cerebral blood flow physiology, ischaemic penumbra, and the rationale for emergency reperfusion.

Model answer: This is _acute left middle cerebral artery (MCA) ischaemic stroke_. Localisation: right-sided weakness (face/arm > leg) → contralateral motor cortex / corticospinal tract (left MCA territory); expressive aphasia → left-sided Broca's area (frontal operculum); gaze deviation to the left → left frontal eye field affected ("looking at the lesion"); no haemorrhage → ischaemic rather than haemorrhagic. Mechanism: thromboembolism occluding the left MCA (proximal M1 or distal M2/M3) → reduced flow to frontal–parietal–temporal cortex, internal capsule, and basal ganglia. CBF physiology: normal CBF ~50 mL/100 g/min; below ~20 mL/100 g/min electrical activity ceases (oligaemia); below ~10 mL/100 g/min infarction within minutes. Penumbra: tissue surrounding the infarct core where CBF is 10–20 mL/100 g/min — neurons are electrically silent but structurally viable for hours; the longer the duration of hypoperfusion, the more penumbra converts to infarct. Imaging: non-contrast CT excludes haemorrhage; CT perfusion or DWI/PWI MRI maps infarct core (low CBV, restricted diffusion) vs penumbra (low CBF, preserved CBV); CT/MR angiography identifies large-vessel occlusion. Rationale for emergency reperfusion: (i) thrombolysis with intravenous tPA (alteplase) within 4.5 hours of onset — pharmacologically lyses the clot, restores flow, salvages penumbra; (ii) endovascular thrombectomy for large-vessel occlusion within 6 hours (extended to 24 h with appropriate imaging mismatch criteria) — mechanically removes the clot. "Time is brain" — every minute, ~1.9 million neurons die. Adjuncts: blood-pressure management (avoid both hypotension which compromises penumbral CBF and severe hypertension which increases haemorrhagic risk after thrombolysis); supplemental O₂ if hypoxic; monitoring and management of glucose, temperature; secondary prevention (antiplatelet, statin, BP control, anticoagulation if AF, carotid endarterectomy if symptomatic carotid stenosis). Contraindications to tPA: recent surgery, active bleeding, severe hypertension uncontrollable, intracranial haemorrhage on CT, major stroke (NIHSS > 25 caution), recent stroke (< 3 months), known aneurysm/AVM [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Guyton & Hall ch.62].

Elaborative explanation: The penumbra concept revolutionised stroke care: it is not the _fact_ of a stroke that determines outcome but the _speed of reperfusion_. Modern stroke systems (telestroke networks, mobile stroke units, hub-and-spoke with thrombectomy centres) are engineered around this physiological insight. The CBF thresholds (50/20/10) directly translate into the imaging and clinical decision tree.

Hints:

1. Localise: left MCA, motor cortex, Broca's, frontal eye field.
2. Penumbra (CBF 10–20) is salvageable; core (< 10) is not.
3. tPA ≤ 4.5 h; thrombectomy ≤ 6–24 h with imaging.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Localises each finding to a left MCA-territory structure.
- Quantifies the three CBF thresholds (50/20/10).
- Defines the penumbra as electrically silent but viable tissue.
- Names tPA (≤ 4.5 h) and mechanical thrombectomy (≤ 6–24 h with imaging) as reperfusion therapies.
- Mentions blood-pressure caveats (avoid both extremes) and adjunctive care.
- Identifies ≥ 2 contraindications to tPA.

A Yellow answer localises the stroke but misses the penumbra concept or specific reperfusion windows.

A Red answer prescribes general supportive care without the urgent reperfusion logic.

Common misconceptions:

- "All ischaemic strokes can be treated with tPA.": Correction: tPA has a strict time window (≤ 4.5 h), exclusion criteria (recent surgery, anticoagulation, severe hypertension, large infarct), and risks (haemorrhage); careful patient selection is essential.
- "Aggressive BP lowering benefits all stroke patients.": Correction: lowering BP can drop CBF below the autoregulatory minimum and convert penumbra to infarct; BP is generally permitted up to 220/120 in non-thrombolysed strokes, ≤ 185/110 if thrombolysing.

QUESTION 9

Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 25-year-old woman with several months of progressive headache, blurred vision, papilloedema, and obesity presents with normal brain MRI. Lumbar puncture opening pressure is 380 mm H2O with normal CSF composition. Decompose this case.

Model answer: This is _idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH)_ — also called pseudotumour cerebri. Diagnostic criteria (Modified Dandy): (i) symptoms/signs of raised ICP (headache, papilloedema, transient visual obscurations, pulsatile tinnitus, sixth-nerve palsy); (ii) no localising neurological signs except CN VI palsy; (iii) raised CSF opening pressure (> 250 mm H2O in adults; > 280 in obese, lying lateral position) with normal CSF composition; (iv) normal neuroimaging (no mass, hydrocephalus, or venous sinus thrombosis); (v) no other identified cause. Demographics: classically obese women of reproductive age; incidence ~1-2/100,000 general population, ~20/100,000 obese women aged 20-44. Pathophysiology (proposed): impaired CSF absorption at arachnoid granulations; venous sinus stenosis (transverse sinus narrowing seen in many cases); raised CSF outflow resistance; possible obesity-related raised intra-abdominal/intracranial venous pressures. Clinical features: progressive headache (typically worse on lying, on Valsalva); transient visual obscurations (seconds, with postural change); progressive visual loss if untreated (papilloedema -> optic atrophy); pulsatile tinnitus; CN VI palsy from stretch (false-localising sign). Investigation: MRI brain + MR venography (rules out tumour, hydrocephalus, dural venous sinus thrombosis); LP with manometry (opening pressure raised, CSF composition normal); ophthalmological assessment (acuity, visual fields, optic disc photos, OCT for retinal nerve fibre layer). Management: (i) Weight loss - sustained loss of ~5-10% body weight often resolves IIH; (ii) Acetazolamide (carbonic anhydrase inhibitor reduces CSF production by ~50%; first-line drug therapy); (iii) Topiramate (alternative, also has weight-loss benefit); (iv) Repeated LPs (transient relief, used to bridge); (v) Surgical: optic-nerve sheath fenestration (if visual loss progressing), CSF diversion (lumboperitoneal or ventriculoperitoneal shunt), venous-sinus stenting (selected cases with documented stenosis); (vi) Bariatric surgery (refractory severe obesity-associated IIH). Prognosis: variable; most patients improve with weight loss and acetazolamide; ~25% have permanent visual loss; recurrence common with weight regain [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Guyton & Hall ch.62].

Elaborative explanation: IIH is the clinical embodiment of CSF-absorption physiology gone wrong. The opening pressure (380 mm H2O ~ 28 mm Hg, well above normal ~10 mm Hg) reflects the pressure required to force CSF through impaired arachnoid villi or stenotic venous sinuses. The therapy targets the imbalance: reduce production (acetazolamide), reduce systemic venous pressure (weight loss), or bypass absorption failure (shunt, fenestration, stenting).

Hints:

1. Diagnostic triad: raised ICP signs, normal imaging, normal CSF composition.
2. Obese woman of reproductive age - classical demographic.
3. Treatment: weight loss + acetazolamide + visual monitoring.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names IIH/pseudotumour cerebri as the diagnosis.
- States the Modified Dandy criteria (raised ICP, no localising signs, normal imaging, normal CSF composition, no other cause).
- Identifies the obese reproductive-age woman demographic.
- Names ≥ 2 management modalities (weight loss, acetazolamide, repeated LPs, surgical).
- Mentions the _visual loss_ as the principal long-term morbidity.

A Yellow answer makes the diagnosis but misses the management hierarchy or visual prognosis.

A Red answer treats this as a generic headache without identifying the IIH framework.

Common misconceptions:

- "Repeated LPs are curative.": Correction: repeated LPs provide _transient_ symptomatic relief; definitive therapy is weight loss, acetazolamide, or surgical CSF diversion.
- "IIH only causes headaches.": Correction: progressive _visual loss_ is the most serious sequela; ophthalmological monitoring is essential.

QUESTION 10
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: A previously healthy 22-year-old presents with fever, severe headache, neck stiffness, and progressive obtundation. CSF analysis shows: opening pressure 350 mm H2O, glucose 1.2 mmol/L (plasma 6.2), protein 1.8 g/L, WBC 4500 cells/mm3 (90% neutrophils), Gram-negative diplococci. Decompose this presentation.

Model answer: This is _acute bacterial meningitis_ — almost certainly _meningococcal_ (Neisseria meningitidis) given the Gram-negative diplococci and demographic. CSF pattern in bacterial meningitis: (i) raised opening pressure (often 200-400 mm H2O); (ii) markedly low CSF glucose (< 40% of plasma; here 1.2/6.2 ~ 19%) due to bacterial consumption and impaired GLUT1 transport across inflamed BBB; (iii) raised protein (often > 1 g/L) from BBB disruption; (iv) high WBC (> 1000/mm3) with neutrophil predominance (> 80% PMN); (v) organisms on Gram stain or PCR. Pathophysiology: organism colonises nasopharynx -> haematogenous spread (or direct extension from sinusitis/otitis) -> seeds choroid plexus/subarachnoid space -> activates innate immunity (TLR signalling, complement) -> intense inflammatory response with cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6) -> BBB disruption, brain oedema, cerebral vasculitis, raised ICP. Cellular damage: neutrophil-derived ROS and proteases injure neurons; bacterial cell-wall components (lipopolysaccharide, peptidoglycan) drive inflammation. Clinical: classic triad (fever, headache, neck stiffness) - present in only ~45%, but ~95% have at least two of fever/headache/neck stiffness/altered mental state; petechial rash in meningococcaemia (purpura fulminans); CN palsies; seizures; raised ICP signs; sepsis with shock and DIC in severe cases. Investigation: blood cultures (before antibiotics if no delay), LP (do urgently unless contraindicated by signs of mass effect, immunocompromise, focal neurological signs - CT first if any concern), CSF analysis as above, blood glucose simultaneously. Empirical management: (i) IV ceftriaxone 2 g 12-hourly + IV vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg 12-hourly (covers pneumococcus including resistant strains and meningococcus; add ampicillin if Listeria suspected - elderly, immunocompromised, neonate); (ii) Dexamethasone 10 mg IV 6-hourly for 4 days (started before or with first antibiotic dose; reduces hearing loss in pneumococcal meningitis); (iii) Public health: notify CD officer; contact tracing; chemoprophylaxis (ciprofloxacin or rifampicin) for close contacts; (iv) Supportive: airway, fluids, anti-epileptics if seizing, ICP management. Mortality: meningococcal ~10% (often early in fulminant cases); pneumococcal ~20-30%; survivors may have hearing loss, seizures, cognitive deficits, focal deficits [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong ch.5].

Elaborative explanation: The CSF profile in this case (low glucose, high protein, neutrophilic pleocytosis, organisms) is the classical bacterial pattern - vs viral meningitis (lymphocytic, normal glucose, modestly raised protein, no organisms) and TB/fungal meningitis (lymphocytic, low glucose, very high protein, slow course). Pattern recognition saves time when clinical decision must be rapid.

Hints:

1. CSF glucose < 40% plasma + neutrophilic pleocytosis + Gram stain.
2. Empirical ceftriaxone + vancomycin (+ ampicillin if Listeria possible).
3. Dexamethasone before/with first antibiotic dose.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies bacterial meningitis (with meningococcus given Gram-negative diplococci).
- Quotes the bacterial CSF profile (raised pressure, low glucose, high protein, neutrophil pleocytosis, organisms).
- Names empirical antibiotics (ceftriaxone + vancomycin ± ampicillin).
- Names dexamethasone with timing relative to antibiotics.
- Mentions public-health notification and chemoprophylaxis.
- Quotes mortality rates.

A Yellow answer makes the diagnosis but misses the empirical regimen or dexamethasone timing.

A Red answer treats this as encephalitis or viral meningitis without recognising the bacterial CSF pattern.

Common misconceptions:

- "Lumbar puncture should be deferred for CT in all suspected meningitis.": Correction: CT before LP is indicated only with focal neurological signs, immunocompromise, recent seizure, or signs of raised ICP; otherwise, prompt LP avoids treatment delay.
- "Steroids are contraindicated in infection.": Correction: dexamethasone _reduces_ mortality and hearing loss in pneumococcal meningitis when given before/with antibiotics.

QUESTION 11
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 70-year-old man on warfarin falls and develops headache, then becomes drowsy over hours. CT shows a crescent-shaped extra-axial collection over the left hemisphere. Decompose this presentation in terms of meningeal anatomy and the bridging veins.

Model answer: This is _acute subdural haematoma (SDH)_. Anatomy: the meninges comprise three layers - dura mater (outer, fibrous, adherent to skull), arachnoid (middle, avascular), pia mater (inner, vascular, on brain surface). Three potential spaces: (i) extradural (epidural) - between skull and dura; (ii) subdural - between dura and arachnoid; (iii) subarachnoid - between arachnoid and pia (contains CSF and arteries). Bridging veins: cerebral veins traverse the subdural space to drain into the dural venous sinuses (especially superior sagittal sinus). They are _unsupported_ between the brain and the dura - vulnerable to shearing forces from rapid acceleration/deceleration. With brain atrophy (elderly, alcoholism), the bridging veins become elongated, taut, and even more vulnerable. Pathophysiology of SDH: (i) head trauma (often minor in elderly with brain atrophy) -> brain shifts within skull -> stretches bridging veins -> rupture -> _venous_ (low-pressure) bleeding into the subdural space; (ii) blood collects between dura and arachnoid; _crescent-shaped_ on CT because it spreads along the curved cerebral convexity, _not_ limited by sutures (unlike epidural haematoma which is biconvex/lenticular and limited by sutures because dura is adherent to skull at sutures); (iii) progressive volume increase compresses brain - mass effect, midline shift, cerebral oedema, eventually transtentorial herniation. Risk factors: elderly, anticoagulation (warfarin/DOAC), antiplatelets, alcoholism, brain atrophy, falls. Course: _acute_ (< 3 days) - high pressure, severe symptoms, surgical emergency; _subacute_ (3-21 days); _chronic_ (> 21 days) - older, often less acute presentation, may be hypodense on CT (compared to hyperdense fresh blood). Imaging: non-contrast CT shows crescent-shaped extra-axial collection; mixed-density suggests acute-on-chronic; mass effect, midline shift, sulcal effacement quantified. Management: (i) reverse anticoagulation - warfarin reversed with prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) + IV vitamin K; DOACs reversed with specific antidotes (idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors) or PCC; (ii) neurosurgical consultation - acute SDH > 10 mm thickness or > 5 mm midline shift typically requires _craniotomy_ for evacuation; chronic SDH often drained via burr-hole evacuation; (iii) ICP management (head elevation, mannitol/hypertonic saline, ICP monitoring as needed); (iv) anti-epileptic prophylaxis (controversial; sometimes for acute SDH); (v) treat underlying cause (fall risk assessment, anticoagulation review). Prognosis: depends on age, GCS, midline shift, time to evacuation; mortality 30-90% in severe acute SDH [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong ch.5].

Elaborative explanation: The crescent shape of SDH directly follows from anatomy - bleeding spreads along the _subdural_ potential space along the cerebral convexity, unlimited by sutures because the _arachnoid_ (not dura) is the inner boundary, and sutures don't anchor the arachnoid. Contrast: epidural haematoma is _biconvex_ because dura is firmly attached to skull at sutures, restricting blood spread.

Hints:

1. Crescent extra-axial = subdural; biconvex = epidural.
2. Bridging veins between brain and dura - venous bleeding.
3. Reverse anticoagulation + neurosurgical evacuation.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names acute SDH and the bridging-vein mechanism.
- Distinguishes SDH (crescent, venous, not limited by sutures) from EDH (biconvex, arterial, limited by sutures).
- Identifies elderly/anticoagulated patients as the high-risk group.
- Names anticoagulation reversal (PCC + vitamin K for warfarin) as urgent therapy.
- Names craniotomy for evacuation in acute SDH > 10 mm or > 5 mm midline shift.

A Yellow answer makes the diagnosis but misses the anatomical reasoning behind the crescent shape.

A Red answer confuses SDH with EDH or omits the urgent anticoagulation reversal.

Common misconceptions:

- "All extra-axial bleeds are biconvex.": Correction: subdural is _crescent-shaped_ (spreads along convexity, not limited by sutures); epidural is _biconvex_ (limited by suture-anchored dura).
- "Warfarin reversal can wait for INR results.": Correction: in life-threatening intracranial haemorrhage, reverse empirically with PCC + vitamin K immediately; don't wait for INR.

QUESTION 12
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 45-year-old man with severe head injury has ICP rising to 30 mm Hg. Explain the principles of managing raised ICP, and discuss why each intervention works at the level of the Monro-Kellie doctrine.

Model answer: ICP > 20-22 mm Hg is the conventional threshold for treatment in head-injured patients. Goals: maintain CPP > 60 mm Hg (CPP = MAP - ICP); prevent secondary brain injury (ischaemia, herniation). Tiered management following the Monro-Kellie components: (1) Reduce _brain_ volume: (i) head elevation 30 degrees - improves cerebral venous outflow; (ii) sedation (propofol, fentanyl) - reduces CMRO2, agitation, intrathoracic pressure; (iii) osmotherapy: mannitol 0.25-1 g/kg IV (osmotic diuretic across intact BBB; rapid onset, transient effect; risk of rebound and hypovolaemia) OR hypertonic saline 3% bolus or infusion (similar mechanism, plus volume expansion - useful in hypovolaemic patients); (iv) corticosteroids - useful only for _vasogenic_ oedema around tumours and abscesses, NOT in traumatic brain injury (CRASH trial showed harm); (v) decompressive craniectomy - removal of skull flap converts the rigid container into a flexible one, allowing brain to expand outward; reduces ICP and mortality but functional outcomes mixed. (2) Reduce _blood_ volume: (i) head elevation (above); (ii) avoid jugular venous obstruction (ensure neck not flexed/rotated); (iii) hyperventilation to PaCO2 30-35 mm Hg (cerebral vasoconstriction via reduced perivascular H+ - rapid effect but transient; prolonged hyperventilation causes ischaemia and is not recommended as first-line); (iv) sedation/analgesia reduces metabolic demand and so reduces autoregulated blood-volume requirement; (v) anticonvulsants if seizures (seizures raise CMRO2 and CBF). (3) Reduce _CSF_ volume: (i) external ventricular drain (EVD) - direct ICP measurement and CSF drainage; useful in obstructive components and severe ICP elevation; (ii) less commonly, lumbar drainage (only when no risk of herniation). (4) Reduce metabolic demand: (i) targeted temperature management (mild therapeutic hypothermia 35-36°C reduces CMRO2 by ~6-8% per degree); (ii) barbiturates (pentobarbital/thiopental) for refractory ICP - dramatically reduce CMRO2 but cause hypotension and immunosuppression. (5) Maintain CPP: (i) ensure adequate MAP (pressors if needed); (ii) avoid both hypotension (compromises CBF) and excessive hypertension (worsens haemorrhage/oedema). Other adjuncts: glucose control, avoid hyperthermia, optimise oxygenation (PaO2 > 80), avoid hypercapnia (except brief therapeutic hyperventilation), nutritional support, DVT prophylaxis [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Guyton & Hall ch.62].

Elaborative explanation: The Monro-Kellie doctrine provides the framework: brain + blood + CSF must equal a constant volume; if one component rises pathologically (e.g., oedema after TBI), the others must fall - therapy reduces one or more components. The tiered approach starts with low-risk interventions (positioning, sedation) and escalates to higher-risk ones (osmotherapy, hyperventilation, barbiturates, decompressive craniectomy) only as needed.

Hints:

1. Monro-Kellie tells you what to reduce: brain, blood, or CSF.
2. Each intervention targets a specific component.
3. CPP = MAP - ICP - balance both ends.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States the ICP threshold (> 20-22) and CPP target (> 60).
- Maps interventions to Monro-Kellie components (brain volume, blood volume, CSF volume).
- Names osmotherapy (mannitol, hypertonic saline) with mechanism (osmotic diuresis across intact BBB).
- Names hyperventilation as a _brief_ adjunct (not first-line) with mechanism (CO2-driven vasoconstriction).
- Names EVD as both diagnostic (ICP monitoring) and therapeutic (CSF drainage).
- Mentions decompressive craniectomy and barbiturates as last-tier interventions.

A Yellow answer covers most interventions but misses the Monro-Kellie framework.

A Red answer prescribes treatments without explaining why each works.

Common misconceptions:

- "Steroids are first-line for raised ICP.": Correction: steroids help only with vasogenic oedema (tumour, abscess) and _worsen_ outcome in TBI (CRASH trial); they are not first-line for raised ICP.
- "Hyperventilation should be maintained.": Correction: prolonged hyperventilation causes ischaemia by sustained vasoconstriction; it is a _brief_ rescue measure for impending herniation, not a maintenance strategy.
