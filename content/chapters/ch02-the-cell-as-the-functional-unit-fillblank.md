## Pass 1 — The plasma membrane

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The plasma membrane of a typical eukaryotic cell is a lipid bilayer approximately \_\_\_ nm thick.

Canonical answer: 5 nm

Accepted variants:

- 5 nm
- 5
- 5 nanometres
- 5 nanometers
- ~5 nm

Tolerance: ±1 nm

Yellow conditions:

- Numeric value matches but unit (nm) missing → "Right value — include the unit (nm)."
- Value 0.5 or 50 — wrong order of magnitude → "Check the order of magnitude — the bilayer is in single-digit nanometres, not fractions or tens."

Explanation: The plasma membrane consists of a 5-nm-thick lipid bilayer with associated proteins [Berne ch.1]. This thinness allows transmembrane proteins to span the bilayer with relatively short hydrophobic segments (about 20 amino acids in an α-helix), and it limits diffusion distances for substances crossing the membrane.

Hints:

1. The dimension is in nanometres, not micrometres.
2. Two phospholipid leaflets stacked head-to-tail set the thickness.
3. The number is a single digit.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: As much as \_\_\_ % of the lipids found in the plasma membrane can be cholesterol.

Canonical answer: 50%

Accepted variants:

- 50%
- 50
- 0.5
- about 50%
- up to 50%

Tolerance: ±5 percentage points

Yellow conditions:

- Numeric value matches but the percent sign is missing → "Right value — include the percent sign."
- Value 5% or 5 — order-of-magnitude error → "Cholesterol is a quantitatively major lipid component, not a minor one. Check the order of magnitude."
- Value 80% — overestimate → "Close in spirit but too high — phospholipids remain the dominant lipid class. Cholesterol can reach about 50%."

Explanation: Cholesterol can constitute up to about 50% of the lipids in the plasma membrane [Berne ch.1]. It intercalates between phospholipids in both leaflets and serves as a "fluidity buffer" — at body temperature it dampens excessive phospholipid motion, preventing the membrane from becoming too fluid; at lower temperatures it prevents tight packing and rigidity.

Hints:

1. The fraction is far higher than students typically guess on first reading.
2. Cholesterol's role is structural — what does inserting a planar rigid molecule do between flexible phospholipids?
3. The percentage is half.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A major phospholipid found _predominantly in the inner (cytoplasmic) leaflet_ of the plasma membrane, and important in signal transduction, is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: phosphatidylinositol (PI)

Accepted variants:

- phosphatidylinositol
- PI
- PtdIns
- phosphatidyl inositol

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "phosphatidylcholine" or "PC" or "sphingomyelin" → "These are predominantly _outer_-leaflet phospholipids. The signal-transduction-related lipid lives on the inner leaflet."
- "phosphatidylserine" or "PS" → "Phosphatidylserine is also an inner-leaflet phospholipid (and serves as an apoptotic 'eat-me' signal when externalised), but the lipid most closely tied to signal transduction is the one whose head group is a sugar alcohol."

Explanation: In the erythrocyte plasma membrane, phosphatidylcholine and sphingomyelin are predominantly outer-leaflet; phosphatidylethanolamine, phosphatidylserine, and phosphatidylinositol are predominantly inner-leaflet [Berne ch.1]. Phosphatidylinositol's location on the inner leaflet places it within reach of cytoplasmic signal-transduction machinery — it is the precursor of inositol trisphosphate (IP₃) and diacylglycerol (DAG), key second messengers, and PI(4,5)P₂ is itself a signalling lipid.

Hints:

1. The phospholipid name ends in a six-carbon sugar alcohol.
2. Its head group is the precursor of two important second messengers (IP₃ and DAG).
3. The first three letters of the name share with "phosphatidyl-..."

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The lipid that anchors certain proteins to the _outer_ leaflet of the plasma membrane is glycosylphosphatidylinositol, abbreviated as \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: GPI

Accepted variants:

- GPI
- GPI anchor
- glycosylphosphatidylinositol
- glycosyl-phosphatidyl-inositol

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "PIP" or "PIP2" or "PIP3" → "Those are phosphorylated inositol lipids involved in signalling, not the protein anchor described. The anchor name has three letters."

Explanation: Some membrane proteins are attached to the bilayer not by spanning it but by being covalently linked to a lipid that is itself embedded in one leaflet [Berne ch.1]. Glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) is the lipid anchor for proteins on the _outer_ leaflet of the plasma membrane. Inner-leaflet proteins use different anchors — fatty acids (myristate, palmitate) at the amino-terminus, or prenyl groups (farnesyl, geranylgeranyl) at the carboxyl-terminus.

Hints:

1. The acronym has three letters.
2. The first letter stands for "glycosyl"; the last two for "phosphatidylinositol."
3. The full name describes the lipid: a sugar attached to a phospholipid.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Specialised membrane microdomains enriched in sphingomyelin and cholesterol that often segregate signalling proteins are called \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: lipid rafts

Accepted variants:

- lipid rafts
- lipid raft
- membrane rafts

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "vesicles" or "endosomes" → "Those are membrane-bounded compartments that move _between_ parts of the cell. The structure described stays within the plasma membrane as a microdomain."
- "lipid bilayer" → "That is the entire membrane structure. The microdomain is a specialised region within it."

Explanation: Lipid rafts are membrane microdomains enriched in sphingomyelin and cholesterol [Berne ch.1]. They function as platforms for signalling: by clustering related receptors, transducers, and effectors into the same membrane region, they increase the efficiency of signal transduction. Lipid rafts also have roles in membrane trafficking and pathogen entry — several viruses (including HIV) and bacterial toxins exploit rafts for cellular invasion.

Hints:

1. The structure is a small region within the membrane, not a separate vesicle.
2. The name evokes a small floating platform.
3. Two words.

QUESTION 6
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand

Stem: The phospholipid most abundant in the _outer_ leaflet of the erythrocyte plasma membrane is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: phosphatidylcholine (PC)

Accepted variants:

- phosphatidylcholine
- PC
- phosphatidyl choline
- lecithin

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "sphingomyelin" → "Sphingomyelin is also predominantly outer-leaflet, but the most abundant outer-leaflet phospholipid by class is the one with a choline head group."
- "phosphatidylserine" or "phosphatidylinositol" → "Those are inner-leaflet phospholipids."

The asymmetry is functionally important — for example, phosphatidylserine is normally hidden on the inner leaflet, and its appearance on the outer leaflet during apoptosis serves as an "eat-me" signal for phagocytes.

2. The head group is the same molecule that constitutes acetylcholine's quaternary-amine portion.
3. The name begins with "phosphatidyl-".

## Pass 2 — Organelles and named entities

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The interior of a lysosome is maintained by a vacuolar H⁺ pump at a pH of approximately \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: 4.8

Accepted variants:

- 4.8
- ~4.8
- 4.5–5.0
- about 5
- 5

Tolerance: ±0.5

Yellow conditions:

- Value 7.0–7.4 → "That is the cytosolic / plasma pH. Lysosomes are markedly more _acidic_."
- Value 1–3 → "That is closer to gastric lumen pH. Lysosomes sit between cytosol and gastric pH — around pH 5."

Explanation: The lysosomal lumen is maintained at approximately pH 4.8 by a vacuolar H⁺ pump (V-ATPase) [Rapid Review ch.1]. Lysosomal hydrolases — proteases, nucleases, lipases, glycosidases — have acidic pH optima, so they function efficiently at this low pH and are largely inactive in the neutral cytosol. The acidic lumen also removes M6P tags from incoming lysosomal enzymes (the targeting tag is no longer needed once the enzyme has arrived).

Hints:

1. Lysosomes are _acidic_, not neutral.
2. The pH is well below the cytosolic value of about 7.1.
3. The number lies between 4 and 5.

QUESTION 8
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The form of endoplasmic reticulum that _lacks_ ribosomes and is involved in lipid synthesis and drug detoxification is the \_\_\_ endoplasmic reticulum.

Canonical answer: smooth

Accepted variants:

- smooth
- smooth ER
- sER
- agranular

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "rough" or "rER" or "granular" → "That ER bears ribosomes and synthesises proteins. The form involved in lipid synthesis and drug detoxification has the opposite description."

Explanation: The smooth ER lacks ribosomes on its membrane and serves three principal roles [Rapid Review ch.1]: synthesis of lipids (including steroid hormones in steroid-producing cells), detoxification of drugs (especially in hepatocytes via the cytochrome P450 system), and storage and release of calcium (in muscle, where smooth ER is called sarcoplasmic reticulum). The rough ER, in contrast, bears ribosomes and synthesises proteins for the secretory pathway.

Hints:

1. The name describes its appearance under electron microscopy.
2. With ribosomes, the ER appears granular; without, it appears...?
3. The opposite of "rough."

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: In I-cell disease, the molecular tag normally added to lysosomal enzyme precursors in the Golgi apparatus — directing them to lysosomes — is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: mannose-6-phosphate (M6P)

Accepted variants:

- mannose-6-phosphate
- M6P
- mannose 6-phosphate
- mannose-6-P
- M-6-P

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "glucose-6-phosphate" or "G6P" → "Glucose-6-phosphate is a glycolytic intermediate, not a sorting tag. The lysosomal targeting tag uses a different sugar."
- "mannose" alone (without phosphate) → "Almost — the tag is a _phosphorylated_ mannose. Add the phosphate."

Explanation: Lysosomal enzymes are synthesised on the rough ER, then enter the Golgi apparatus for post-translational modification [Rapid Review ch.1]. In the Golgi, a phosphorylating enzyme adds a mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) tag to mannose residues on the enzyme; the M6P tag is recognised by M6P receptors that direct the enzyme into lysosome-bound vesicles. In I-cell disease, this phosphorylating enzyme is deficient — the tag is not added, and lysosomal enzymes are missorted into the constitutive secretory pathway, exiting the cell instead of reaching lysosomes.

Hints:

1. The tag is a phosphorylated sugar.
2. The sugar is a hexose, but not glucose.
3. The acronym is three characters: a letter, a digit, and another letter.

QUESTION 10
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Mitochondria and their DNA are inherited from one parent only; this pattern is called \_\_\_ inheritance.

Canonical answer: maternal

Accepted variants:

- maternal
- mitochondrial / maternal
- matrilineal

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "paternal" → "Inverted — sperm mitochondria are excluded shortly after fertilisation; the egg cell contributes the mitochondria."
- "Mendelian" → "Mendelian inheritance is biparental for nuclear genes. Mitochondrial DNA inheritance is _not_ Mendelian."

Explanation: Mitochondria — and the mitochondrial DNA they contain — are inherited exclusively from the mother, because only the egg's mitochondria persist after fertilisation [Rapid Review ch.1]. Disorders caused by mtDNA mutations therefore follow a non-Mendelian, strictly maternal transmission pattern: an affected mother passes the disease to all her offspring; only female offspring transmit it further; an affected father does not transmit through mtDNA. (Disorders caused by mutations in _nuclear_ genes encoding mitochondrial proteins do follow ordinary Mendelian inheritance.)

Hints:

1. The pattern is one-parent transmission.
2. The egg carries far more cytoplasm than the sperm.
3. The word relates to the mother.

Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Within the nucleus, the prominent dense body that synthesises ribosomal RNA and assembles ribosomal subunits is the \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: nucleolus

Accepted variants:

- nucleolus
- the nucleolus
- nucleoli (plural)

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "nucleus" → "The nucleus is the entire compartment. The structure inside the nucleus that specifically synthesises rRNA has a different name."
- "ribosome" → "The ribosome is the _product_ (along with rRNA), not the site of rRNA synthesis."

Explanation: The nucleolus is a dense, RNA-rich body within the nucleus that synthesises ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and assembles ribosomal subunits before they are exported to the cytoplasm [Rapid Review ch.1]. The size of the nucleolus reflects ribosomal-synthesis activity — cells with high protein-synthesis demand have large, prominent nucleoli; quiescent cells have smaller ones. mRNA is transcribed elsewhere in the nucleoplasm.

Hints:

1. The structure sits _inside_ the nucleus.
2. The name is built from the word for nucleus, with a diminutive suffix.
3. It synthesises one specific kind of RNA — ribosomal.

QUESTION 12
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The form of chromatin that is highly compacted and transcriptionally inactive is called \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: heterochromatin

Accepted variants:

- heterochromatin
- hetero-chromatin

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "euchromatin" → "Inverted — euchromatin is the _less compact_, transcriptionally _active_ form. Heterochromatin is the compact, inactive form."
- "chromatin" → "All DNA + histones is chromatin. The question asks specifically about the highly compacted, inactive subtype."

Explanation: Chromatin in the interphase nucleus exists in two forms [Rapid Review ch.1]. _Heterochromatin_ is highly compacted, electron-dense in micrographs, and transcriptionally inactive. _Euchromatin_ is less compact and contains transcriptionally active DNA. The relative proportions vary by cell type and metabolic state, and the condensation state is regulated by histone modifications (acetylation, methylation) — the molecular basis of epigenetic gene regulation.

Hints:

1. Two terms describe chromatin condensation states; one is _more_ condensed and inactive.
2. The prefix means "different" or "other" in Greek.
3. The opposite term begins with "eu-".

## Pass 3 — Cytoskeleton and clinical anchors

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Microfilaments — the smallest and most flexible class of cytoskeletal filaments — are polymers of the protein \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: actin

Accepted variants:

- actin
- G-actin
- F-actin
- globular actin
- filamentous actin

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "tubulin" → "Tubulin polymerises into microtubules (the _largest_ cytoskeletal class), not microfilaments."
- "myosin" → "Myosin is a motor protein that walks along microfilaments. The structural protein of the microfilament itself is different."
- "keratin" → "Keratin is an intermediate-filament protein in epithelial cells, not a microfilament protein."

Explanation: Microfilaments are 5–9 nm in diameter and are flexible, helical polymers of G-actin (globular actin) [Rapid Review ch.1]. They form the cortex layer just beneath the plasma membrane and function in cell shape, motility, organelle transport, cytokinesis, and (in muscle) contraction. The pathogen _Listeria monocytogenes_ exploits actin polymerisation to spread directly from cell to cell.

Hints:

1. The protein name has five letters and is famously paired with myosin in muscle.
2. It exists as a globular monomer (G-) that polymerises into a filament (F-).
3. The first three letters of the name spell a word meaning "performance."

QUESTION 14
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Microtubules — the largest and most rigid class of cytoskeletal filaments — are polymers of the protein \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: tubulin

Accepted variants:

- tubulin
- α/β tubulin
- α-tubulin and β-tubulin
- alpha tubulin
- alpha and beta tubulin

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "actin" → "Actin polymerises into microfilaments (the _smallest_ cytoskeletal class), not microtubules."
- "keratin" → "Keratin is an intermediate-filament protein, not a microtubule protein."
- "dynein" or "kinesin" → "Those are motor proteins that walk _along_ microtubules. The structural protein of the microtubule itself is the answer."

Explanation: Microtubules are 25 nm in diameter and are large, rigid, hollow cylinders formed by polymers of α-tubulin and β-tubulin dimers [Rapid Review ch.1]. They extend from the centrosome (the major microtubule-organising centre), serve as scaffolding for organelle and chromosome movement, and form the structural core of cilia and flagella. Antimitotic drugs (colchicine, vincristine, vinblastine) bind tubulin and disrupt microtubule polymerisation.

Hints:

1. The name shares its first three letters with the structure it forms.
2. The protein has α and β subunits.
3. It is the target of colchicine and the vinca alkaloids.

QUESTION 16
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Intermediate filaments have an approximate diameter of \_\_\_ nm — sitting between microfilaments and microtubules.

Canonical answer: 10 nm

Accepted variants:

- 10 nm
- 10
- 10 nanometres
- ~10 nm

Tolerance: ±2 nm

Yellow conditions:

- Numeric value matches but unit (nm) missing → "Right value — include the unit (nm)."
- Value 5 → "That is the lower bound for _microfilaments_. Intermediate filaments are larger."
- Value 25 → "That is _microtubules_, the largest class. Intermediate filaments are intermediate, not the largest."

Explanation: Intermediate filaments are about 10 nm in diameter — intermediate between microfilaments (5–9 nm) and microtubules (25 nm), which is what gives them their name [Rapid Review ch.1]. They are the most abundant cytoskeletal element in many cell types and are formed from a heterogeneous family of proteins (e.g., keratins in epithelial cells, vimentin in mesenchymal cells, neurofilaments in neurons, lamins in the nuclear envelope). They provide mechanical stability, especially in epithelial cells where they form desmosomes and hemidesmosomes.

Hints:

1. Their name reflects their position between the other two classes.
2. The size hierarchy in nanometres is 5 → ? → 25.
3. The number is the midpoint, more or less.

QUESTION 17
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The membrane cytoskeletal protein most commonly mutated in hereditary spherocytosis — causing impaired anchoring of the spectrin network to the red-cell membrane — is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: ankyrin

Accepted variants:

- ankyrin
- ANK1
- ankyrin-1

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "spectrin" → "Spectrin is part of the same cortical cytoskeleton, but most cases of hereditary spherocytosis arise from a defect in the protein that _anchors_ spectrin to the membrane. The name of that anchor begins with the letter A."
- "actin" → "Actin is part of the cortical network, but the mutated protein is the membrane-anchoring linker."
- "dystrophin" → "Dystrophin is the membrane-cytoskeletal protein affected in _Duchenne muscular dystrophy_, in skeletal muscle — not the protein most commonly defective in hereditary spherocytosis."

Explanation: Hereditary spherocytosis is caused most commonly by mutations in the _ankyrin_ gene (other affected proteins include spectrin, band 3, and protein 4.2) [Rapid Review ch.1]. Ankyrin and spectrin together form the cortical cytoskeletal network on the inner face of the red-cell plasma membrane; ankyrin specifically anchors spectrin to the integral membrane protein band 3. When ankyrin is defective, the spectrin network detaches from the membrane in patches, the membrane buds off as small vesicles, and the cell becomes spherical and rigid. Spherical, inflexible red cells are scavenged in the splenic cords, producing extravascular haemolysis, jaundice, splenomegaly, and anaemia. Splenectomy is curative.

Hints:

1. The protein anchors the spectrin network to the membrane.
2. The name begins with "A" and ends in "-in."
3. It is named after the Greek for "anchor."

QUESTION 18
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: An antimitotic drug that binds tubulin, is used in the treatment of acute gout, and disrupts microtubule polymerisation is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: colchicine

Accepted variants:

- colchicine
- Colchicine

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "vincristine" or "vinblastine" → "Those are also tubulin-binding antimitotic drugs (vinca alkaloids), but the agent used specifically in _acute gout_ is different."
- "paclitaxel" or "taxol" → "Paclitaxel is a tubulin-binding chemotherapy drug (it stabilises microtubules rather than preventing polymerisation), but is not used in gout."
- "allopurinol" → "Allopurinol is used in chronic gout (xanthine-oxidase inhibitor) but does not bind tubulin or disrupt microtubules."

Explanation: Colchicine binds tubulin and prevents microtubule polymerisation [Rapid Review ch.1]. In acute gout, this is therapeutic because microtubules are required for neutrophil migration into the joint and for phagocytosis of urate crystals — disrupting microtubules suppresses the inflammatory response. At higher doses colchicine is also antimitotic, although the _vinca_ alkaloids (vincristine, vinblastine) are the tubulin-binding drugs used in chemotherapy. Microtubule dysfunction can also produce immotile cilia syndrome (Kartagener syndrome) and male infertility.

Hints:

1. The drug is named after a plant — the autumn crocus (_Colchicum autumnale_).
2. It is the classical pharmacological agent for an acute attack of one rheumatological condition.
3. The name begins with "col-".

**Pedagogical choices worth noting:**

- _Single-blank discipline._ Several questions could have been written as multi-value fill-blanks (e.g., "The three cytoskeletal classes have diameters of **_ , _** , and \_\_\_ nm") but were split into separate questions per the prompt convention. This produced Q15 and Q16 as related-but-independent items.
- _Tolerance and Yellow conditions._ Numeric questions specify a tolerance window for Green grading; non-numeric (term recall) questions use Yellow conditions to catch the _predictable wrong term_ a student is most likely to type. The Yellow feedback messages explicitly name the misconception so the student understands what they confused.
- _Distribution of types._ 17 of 18 are recall (Type: recall); 1 is comparison-style recall (Q12 chromatin); 2 are clinical-application recall (Q17 ankyrin, Q18 colchicine). This skew matches the prompt's guidance that fill-blank should be dominated by recall and named-entity retrieval.
- _Aspects covered._ Plasma membrane structure and lipids (Q1–Q6); organelles and named tags (Q7–Q12); cytoskeleton dimensions and proteins (Q13–Q16); clinical applications (Q17, Q18). The set illuminates Ch. 2 broadly within the topics that current source extracts cover.
- _No questions on cell-cycle stage durations, telomere length, or molecular protein-synthesis values._ These would have been natural fill-blank topics, but the relevant source chapters were not extracted in this session.
