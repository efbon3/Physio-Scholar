Correct answer: Systole (ventricular contraction — isovolumic contraction → ejection) and diastole (ventricular relaxation — isovolumic relaxation → rapid filling → diastasis → atrial systole/atrial kick); diastole longer than systole at rest, shortens disproportionately with tachycardia

Explanation: **Cardiac cycle phases** (~0.8 s at 75 bpm):

**Diastole** (~0.5 s; reduces with tachycardia):

1. **Isovolumic relaxation** (~0.04 s): Ventricular pressure falls below aortic; aortic valve closes (S2). Mitral still closed. No volume change.
2. **Rapid filling** (~0.15 s): Mitral valve opens; ~70% of stroke volume transferred during this phase.
3. **Diastasis** (~0.15 s, slow filling): Pressure equalisation; minimal flow. Disappears at high heart rates.
4. **Atrial systole / atrial kick** (~0.1 s): Adds final ~25% to ventricular filling. Critical at high heart rates or with ventricular noncompliance.

**Systole** (~0.3 s): 5. **Isovolumic contraction** (~0.05 s): Mitral valve closes (S1). Aortic still closed. Pressure rises but volume constant. 6. **Ejection**: Rapid (~0.1 s) then reduced (~0.15 s).

**End-diastolic volume (EDV)** ~120 mL; **End-systolic volume (ESV)** ~50 mL; **Stroke volume (SV)** ~70 mL; **EF** ~55–70%; **CO** = SV × HR ~5 L/min at rest.

**Effects of HR**: Higher HR → diastole shortens disproportionately. Very high HR → inadequate filling → reduced SV/CO (e.g., AF with rapid response).

Explanation: **S1 — first heart sound**:

S1 is generated when the AV valves (mitral and tricuspid) close at the start of isovolumic contraction. Mitral closure (M1) precedes tricuspid closure (T1) by ~30 ms — usually heard as a single sound; only audibly split in RBBB or mitral stenosis.

**Loudness of S1** depends on:

- **Force of ventricular contraction** (loud in tachycardia, hyperthyroidism, hyperdynamic states).
- **Position of AV leaflets at start of systole** — wide-open leaflets close from far apart → louder S1.
- **Mitral stenosis** — loud, snapping S1 (mobile but thickened leaflet).
- **Severe MR or calcified rigid leaflets** — soft S1.
- **Long PR interval (1° AV block)** — soft S1 (leaflets drift partially closed during diastole).

(Costanzo, 7e — Ch.4.)

Stem: The physiological splitting of the second heart sound (S2) on inspiration is best explained by:

Correct answer: Inspiration → ↓ intrathoracic pressure → ↑ venous return to RV → delayed pulmonic valve closure (P2 later) and slightly earlier aortic valve closure (A2 earlier) → audible A2-P2 split, narrowing on expiration

- "Faster ventricular ejection on inspiration" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Reflex sympathetic activation" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Physiological splitting of S2**:

On inspiration, increased venous return to the RV prolongs RV ejection (longer "Hangout time"), delaying pulmonic valve closure (P2). Simultaneously, pulmonary venous return to the LV is briefly reduced (blood pooling in expanded pulmonary vasculature), shortening LV ejection slightly and bringing aortic closure (A2) earlier. The net result is an audible split (A2 before P2) that narrows or fuses on expiration.

**Abnormal splittings**:

- **Wide fixed splitting** (no respiratory variation) — Atrial septal defect (ASD); equalised filling between sides.
- **Wide physiological splitting** (still varies with respiration) — Conditions delaying RV emptying: RBBB, pulmonary stenosis, severe pulmonary HTN with RV failure.
- **Paradoxical (reversed) splitting** — P2 before A2; split widens on expiration. Caused by delayed LV ejection: LBBB, severe AS, HCM with LVOT obstruction.
- **Single S2** — Severe AS (immobile aortic valve), large VSD, Eisenmenger.

(Berne & Levy, 7e; Costanzo, 7e — Ch.4.)

Stem: On the LV pressure–volume (P–V) loop, the area enclosed within the loop represents:

Correct answer: External stroke work performed by the ventricle on the blood ejected during one cycle

Distractors:

- "Total cardiac output" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Stroke volume only" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Myocardial oxygen consumption alone" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **The pressure–volume loop**:

X-axis = LV volume; Y-axis = LV pressure. Four phases:

1. **Filling** (mitral open) — bottom segment, increasing volume at low pressure.
2. **Isovolumic contraction** — left vertical segment, rising pressure, constant volume.
3. **Ejection** (aortic open) — top segment, falling volume.
4. **Isovolumic relaxation** — right vertical segment, falling pressure, constant volume.

**Key derivations**:

- **Width of loop** = Stroke volume (SV).
- **Area enclosed** = External stroke work.
- **EDPVR** (end-diastolic P-V relationship) — passive filling curve; slope = stiffness (1/compliance).
- **ESPVR** (end-systolic P-V relationship) — line connecting end-systolic points across loading conditions; slope = contractility (Emax).
- **PVA** (pressure–volume area) = stroke work + potential energy stored in walls; correlates linearly with myocardial O₂ consumption.

(Costanzo, 7e — Ch.4; Berne & Levy, 7e.)

Correct answer: Loop shifts right (increased EDV); width increases (larger SV by Frank-Starling); ESV roughly unchanged at preserved contractility/afterload

- "Loop area decreases" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Effect of preload increase (volume loading)**:

- ↑ Venous return → ↑ EDV.
- By Frank-Starling, SV rises (loop widens).
- ESV largely unchanged at preserved afterload and contractility.
- ESPVR slope unchanged; new operating point sits along same ESPVR line.

- **↑ Contractility** — ESPVR steepens; ESV falls; SV rises at same EDV.

**Clinical correlate**: Frank-Starling is why a failing ventricle improves with diuresis only up to a point.

Explanation: **Effect of afterload increase (AS, severe HTN)**:

- Peak ventricular pressure rises to overcome the resistance.
- Aortic valve opens later (longer isovolumic contraction).
- Ejection slows; ESV rises.
- SV falls at unchanged contractility/preload.

**Decompensation**: Eventually contractility falls (afterload mismatch) → ventricle dilates → reduced EF and HFrEF.

(Costanzo, 7e — Ch.4.)
