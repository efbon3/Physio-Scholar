## Pass 1 — Comprehension and reasoning

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Trace the sequence from arrival of a propagated action potential at a ventricular cardiac myocyte to mechanical contraction. Highlight where Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release fits and how it differs from skeletal-muscle ECC.

Model answer: A complete answer organises the chain into excitation, coupling (CICR), and contraction. Excitation: AP propagates from neighbour via gap junctions in the intercalated disc → fast Na⁺ channels open → phase 0 upstroke (~+30 mV) → phase 1 brief Ito repolarisation → phase 2 plateau (~200 ms) sustained by inward Cav1.2 Ca²⁺ current balanced by outward delayed-rectifier K⁺ current → phase 3 repolarisation as Ca²⁺ channels inactivate and K⁺ efflux dominates → phase 4 resting potential set by IK1. Coupling — CICR: during the plateau, ~25% of contractile Ca²⁺ enters via Cav1.2 (DHPR) at the dyad. This trigger Ca²⁺ binds the cytoplasmic face of RyR2 (apposed across the ~12 nm dyadic gap) and opens it, releasing the much larger SR Ca²⁺ store (calsequestrin-stored). The result is a graded, modulable Ca²⁺ transient — bigger trigger Ca²⁺ produces a bigger overall transient. Contraction: cytosolic [Ca²⁺] rises 10⁻⁷ → 10⁻⁶ M → binds troponin C → tropomyosin shifts → cross-bridge cycle activates exactly as in skeletal muscle. Relaxation: SR Ca²⁺ reuptake by SERCA2a (regulated by phospholamban — phosphorylation by PKA relieves PLB inhibition for faster reuptake = positive lusitropy). Sarcolemmal Ca²⁺ extrusion by NCX (3 Na⁺ in : 1 Ca²⁺ out) and a small contribution from PMCA. Total Ca²⁺ extrusion across sarcolemma must equal Ca²⁺ entry to maintain steady state. Why CICR vs mechanical coupling? Cardiac muscle requires graded contractions modulable by autonomic and frequency inputs. CICR is graded (more trigger Ca²⁺ → more SR release), so β-adrenergic stimulation of Cav1.2 → larger trigger → larger transient → more force. Skeletal muscle, recruited at the level of motor units and rate coding, can use the simpler "all-or-none" mechanical coupling. Skeletal triads (A–I junction) versus cardiac dyads (Z line) reflect this difference in geometry and function [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9].

Elaborative explanation: The contrast between skeletal mechanical coupling and cardiac CICR is one of the most pedagogically useful comparisons in muscle physiology — it ties together AP shape, channel choice, anatomical geometry, and functional demand. Cardiac CICR is also why digoxin works (raised intracellular Na⁺ via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase inhibition slows NCX-mediated Ca²⁺ extrusion → SR Ca²⁺ load rises → bigger CICR → positive inotropy) and why Ca²⁺-channel blockers reduce contractility (blocking Cav1.2 → less trigger Ca²⁺ → less CICR). The skeletal mechanism, in contrast, is essentially insensitive to extracellular Ca²⁺.

Hints:

1. Six AP phases, then CICR at the dyad, then contraction.
2. Ca²⁺ entry through Cav1.2 triggers RyR2 across a ~12 nm gap.
3. Skeletal uses _mechanical_ coupling; cardiac uses _Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release_.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the five AP phases and their dominant currents.
- Locates the trigger Ca²⁺ entry in the plateau via Cav1.2.
- Describes CICR at the dyad (Cav1.2 → RyR2, ~12 nm).
- Quotes the Ca²⁺ source split (~25% sarcolemmal, ~75% SR).
- Outlines relaxation (SERCA2a + phospholamban; NCX + PMCA).
- Contrasts cardiac CICR with skeletal mechanical coupling and explains why each suits its function.

A Yellow answer covers most of the chain but omits the dyad geometry or the source split.

A Red answer treats cardiac ECC as identical to skeletal or denies the role of Cav1.2-derived trigger Ca²⁺.

Common misconceptions:

- "Cardiac contraction depends entirely on SR Ca²⁺.": Correction: ~25% of contractile Ca²⁺ enters via Cav1.2 across the sarcolemma; this trigger Ca²⁺ is essential for opening RyR2 in CICR.
- "DHPR mechanically opens RyR2 in cardiac muscle.": Correction: skeletal DHPR (Cav1.1) mechanically opens RyR1; cardiac DHPR (Cav1.2) must conduct Ca²⁺ to open RyR2 — CICR.

QUESTION 2
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Explain the Frank–Starling law of the heart at three levels — phenomenological, sarcomeric/molecular, and integrative.

Model answer: Phenomenological: at constant heart rate and contractility, an increase in end-diastolic volume (preload) increases stroke volume. The heart "pumps what it receives." This applies to both ventricles and matches their outputs. Sarcomeric/molecular: (1) Improved overlap — at normal physiological lengths, cardiac sarcomeres operate on the ascending limb of the length–tension curve. Stretch increases the actin-myosin overlap available for cross-bridge formation. (Cardiac titin and ECM constrain max sarcomere length to ~2.2 µm, so the heart never reaches the descending limb.) (2) Length-dependent activation — stretch increases myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity through reduced inter-filament spacing (bringing actin closer to myosin), titin-mediated allosteric effects on troponin C, and probable contribution from increased cooperativity of cross-bridge attachment. This means more force at the same Ca²⁺ transient. Integrative: Frank–Starling allows the heart to match its output to venous return on every beat, equilibrate left and right ventricular outputs (otherwise pulmonary congestion or peripheral pooling would develop), and adapt to abrupt changes in load (postural changes, Valsalva) before slower neurohumoral mechanisms engage. Curve family: a given Frank–Starling curve assumes constant inotropy. Sympathetic stimulation shifts the curve upward (more stroke volume at every preload — increased contractility); cardiogenic shock shifts it downward. Therapy of acute heart failure aims to reduce preload (diuretics, venodilators) where the patient is on a flat upper portion, or improve contractility where the curve has fallen. Limit: in severe pathological dilation, the heart can fall off Frank–Starling — geometric inefficiency (Laplace law: high wall stress at large radius) overwhelms the benefit of additional stretch. This is the failing-heart situation that diuretics and afterload reducers address [Berne & Levy ch.13; Costanzo].

Elaborative explanation: The "two-component" mechanism (geometric overlap plus length-dependent activation) is what distinguishes Frank–Starling from a purely passive elastic recoil — the heart's force does not just rise with stretch because of more overlap; it also rises because the cardiac troponin C becomes more Ca²⁺-sensitive at longer lengths. Modern molecular work has implicated the cardiac titin N2B isoform's stiffness and direct interaction with troponin in this length-dependent activation. The Frank–Starling curve also generalises across species — fish, frogs, mammals all use it — making it one of the most evolutionarily conserved cardiac control mechanisms.

Hints:

1. Phenomenological = the law itself; sarcomeric = the molecular basis; integrative = its physiological role.
2. Length-dependent activation is more than just geometric overlap.
3. Cardiac sarcomeres never reach the descending limb of the length–tension curve.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States the law phenomenologically (preload up → stroke volume up).
- Identifies the ascending-limb operation of cardiac sarcomeres.
- Describes length-dependent activation (Ca²⁺ sensitisation at longer lengths).
- Explains the integrative role (matches output to venous return, equilibrates RV/LV, rapid adaptation).
- Distinguishes the curve from inotropy (which shifts the whole curve).
- Mentions the limit in severe dilation (Laplace law geometric inefficiency).

A Yellow answer covers the law but omits the length-dependent activation mechanism or the curve-shift logic.

A Red answer claims Frank–Starling depends on the descending limb or denies the role of length-dependent activation.

Common misconceptions:

- "Frank–Starling is purely about thick–thin overlap.": Correction: length-dependent activation (stretch-induced Ca²⁺ sensitisation via titin/troponin) contributes substantially.
- "The heart always benefits from more preload.": Correction: in severely dilated failing hearts, Laplace's law (T = Pr/2h) means wall stress rises with chamber radius — preload reduction (diuretics, venodilators) is therapeutic.

QUESTION 3
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the autonomic regulation of cardiac function. How does sympathetic activation produce simultaneous positive chronotropy, inotropy, dromotropy, and lusitropy at the molecular level?

Model answer: Sympathetic activation: noradrenaline from cardiac nerves and adrenaline from adrenal medulla → β1 receptor (with a smaller β2 contribution) → Gαs → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA. PKA phosphorylates several substrates with the following effects. (1) Positive chronotropy (faster heart rate) — PKA phosphorylates HCN channels in SA node, shifting If activation toward more positive voltages. Rate of diastolic depolarisation rises → SA node fires sooner. Cyclic AMP itself binds HCN allosterically, providing an even faster mechanism. (2) Positive inotropy (stronger contraction) — PKA phosphorylates Cav1.2 (L-type Ca²⁺ channels), increasing Ca²⁺ entry per AP and bigger CICR-driven Ca²⁺ transients. RyR2 phosphorylation may also contribute (controversial). (3) Positive dromotropy (faster AV nodal conduction) — PKA phosphorylates Cav1.2 in AV nodal cells (where AP upstroke is Ca²⁺-driven). Faster phase 0 → faster propagation → shorter AV delay. (4) Positive lusitropy (faster relaxation) — PKA phosphorylates phospholamban, relieving its inhibition of SERCA2a → faster SR Ca²⁺ reuptake. PKA also phosphorylates troponin I, lowering myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity → faster cross-bridge release. Together: faster fall in cytosolic Ca²⁺ and faster diastolic relaxation, allowing more rapid filling at higher rates. Cellular consequence at the integrated level: stronger, briefer, more frequent contractions — the perfect match to the demands of fight-or-flight (more cardiac output without sacrificing diastolic filling). Parasympathetic (vagus → M2 → Gαi + Gβγ): Gαi lowers cAMP → reduces all four sympathetic effects; Gβγ directly opens GIRK (IK,ACh) channels → hyperpolarisation → slower diastolic depolarisation in SA node → bradycardia; AV nodal block at high vagal tone; selectively affects atria (densely vagally innervated) more than ventricles. The reciprocal autonomic control allows the heart to be tuned moment-to-moment to functional demands. Pharmacologically, β-blockers exploit this by attenuating sympathetic drive — used in CHF (slow remodelling), MI (reduce O₂ demand and arrhythmias), hypertension, and anxiety [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9].

Elaborative explanation: The four-fold sympathetic effect (chrono-, ino-, dromo-, lusi-tropy) is an elegant example of a single signalling pathway (PKA) producing coordinated changes across multiple effectors. The lusitropic effect is particularly important — without faster relaxation, fast heart rates would compromise diastolic filling. PKA phosphorylation of multiple substrates therefore solves a multi-objective control problem with a single signalling cascade. The principle generalises: many homeostatic responses use one upstream signal to coordinate multiple downstream effects, ensuring that integrated organ behaviour scales smoothly.

Hints:

1. β1 → Gs → cAMP → PKA → multiple substrates.
2. Four "tropies": chrono, ino, dromo, lusi.
3. Each substrate (HCN, Cav1.2, AV node Cav1.2, PLB + troponin I) corresponds to one tropy.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies β1-Gs-cAMP-PKA as the central pathway.
- Maps each PKA substrate to its corresponding tropy (HCN → chrono; Cav1.2 → ino; AV-node Cav1.2 → dromo; PLB and troponin I → lusi).
- Explains why lusitropy matters at high heart rates.
- Describes the parasympathetic counter (M2 → Gαi/Gβγ → lower cAMP and GIRK opening).
- Mentions a clinical β-blocker indication.

A Yellow answer covers the cAMP-PKA pathway but omits one of the four tropies or one of their molecular substrates.

A Red answer claims the sympathetic effect is mediated by Ca²⁺ entry alone or denies the role of PKA.

Common misconceptions:

- "Sympathetic stimulation just increases heart rate.": Correction: it produces simultaneous chronotropy, inotropy, dromotropy, and lusitropy — four coordinated effects via one PKA cascade.
- "β-blockers are bad for heart failure because they reduce contractility.": Correction: in chronic HFrEF, β-blockers improve outcome by reducing chronic catecholamine toxicity, slowing remodelling, and protecting against arrhythmias — despite acute negative inotropy.

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Compare excitation–contraction coupling in skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle on a single integrated framework. Identify what is conserved and what is unique to each.

Model answer: Conserved across all three: sliding-filament-driven shortening of actin–myosin assemblies; ATP hydrolysis at myosin head powering cross-bridge cycling; cytosolic [Ca²⁺] as the proximal regulatory signal; an SR-like internal Ca²⁺ store and a transmembrane Ca²⁺ pump (SERCA family). Distinctive features: skeletal — sarcomeric striations regular, triads at A–I junction, ~100% Ca²⁺ from SR via mechanical DHPR-RyR1 coupling, troponin C / TnI / TnT thin-filament regulation, modest Ca²⁺ sensitisation, tetanus possible, moderate-to-high energy cost, force regulated by motor-unit recruitment + rate coding, voluntary somatic control. Cardiac — sarcomeric (less regular) striations, dyads at Z line, ~75% SR + ~25% sarcolemmal Ca²⁺ via CICR (Cav1.2-RyR2), TnC (cardiac isoform) thin-filament regulation, length-dependent Ca²⁺ sensitisation (Frank–Starling), no tetanus due to long refractory period, continuous high energy cost, force regulated by HR + Frank–Starling + autonomic inotropy, direct β1/M2 fine control. Smooth — no sarcomeres (dense bodies/areas), no T tubule:SR triad/dyad (caveolae and SR network instead), Ca²⁺ from mix of SR (IP₃R, RyR3) and sarcolemma (Cav, ROCs, SOCs), multiple parallel triggers (depolarisation is one of several), calmodulin → MLCK → MLC phosphorylation (thick-filament regulation), large RhoA/Rho-kinase Ca²⁺ sensitisation, largely tonic (no classical tetanus), very low energy cost (latch state), force regulated by membrane potential + receptors + sensitisation, sympathetic + parasympathetic + paracrine + endocrine inputs. Why the design choices? Skeletal muscle delivers fast, voluntary, graded force — mechanical ECC is fastest; thin-filament regulation gives sharp on/off kinetics. Cardiac muscle must contract rhythmically without fatigue, must not tetanise, and must be modulable — long Ca²⁺ plateau enforces refractoriness; CICR provides graded force; thin-filament regulation gives fast kinetics suited to ~60 cycles/min pumping. Smooth muscle must sustain prolonged tone in vessels, gut, airways, and sphincters at minimum energy cost — thick-filament regulation (MLCK/MLCP) adds the latch state; multiple parallel Ca²⁺ pathways permit fine response to neural, hormonal, mechanical, and paracrine inputs. The same conserved mechano-chemical core has been re-purposed three times, with different regulatory architecture each time, to fit completely different functional demands [Berne & Levy ch.12–14; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Costanzo].

Elaborative explanation: This three-way comparison is the most clarifying integrative exercise in muscle physiology. Each design choice maps onto a functional requirement. The conserved cross-bridge cycle is "tuned" by different regulation, geometry, AP duration, energy-management strategies, and hormonal/neural inputs to fit each task. Latch state in smooth muscle is particularly elegant: dephosphorylation of myosin light chain while still attached to actin produces sustained force at minimal ATP cost — the design feature that lets vascular smooth muscle hold blood-pressure-relevant contraction for hours without exhausting energy stores.

Hints:

1. Same cross-bridge core, three different regulatory architectures.
2. Sarcomeres or no sarcomeres; triad/dyad/none; troponin or calmodulin.
3. Each design fits the functional demand.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Lists the conserved features (sliding filament, ATP hydrolysis, Ca²⁺ as trigger, SR/SERCA).
- Compares all three muscle types across the major axes (sarcomeres, T-tubule:SR, Ca²⁺ source, regulator, sensitisation, tetanus, energy economy, force regulation, autonomic).
- Distinguishes mechanical coupling (skeletal) from CICR (cardiac) from multiple parallel paths (smooth).
- Identifies the regulatory protein for each (TnC striated; calmodulin/MLCK smooth).
- Connects each design to functional demand.

A Yellow answer compares two of the three well but is thin on smooth muscle.

A Red answer claims all three muscle types use the same mechanism.

Common misconceptions:

- "All muscle uses troponin.": Correction: smooth muscle uses _thick-filament regulation_ via calmodulin and MLCK; troponin is absent.
- "All muscle uses CICR.": Correction: only cardiac muscle obligatorily uses CICR; skeletal uses mechanical coupling, smooth uses multiple parallel Ca²⁺ pathways.

QUESTION 5
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the molecular mechanism of smooth-muscle contraction. Why is the same actin-myosin mechano-chemistry slow but extremely energy-efficient compared with skeletal muscle?

Model answer: Activation sequence: (1) stimulus (depolarisation, hormone, paracrine signal, stretch, etc.) → cytosolic [Ca²⁺] rises; (2) Ca²⁺ (4 ions) binds calmodulin (CaM) → Ca²⁺/CaM complex; (3) Ca²⁺/CaM binds and activates myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK); (4) MLCK phosphorylates regulatory myosin light chain (MLC) at Ser19 on the thick filament; (5) phosphorylated MLC permits the myosin head to interact with actin → cross-bridge cycle begins → contraction. Inactivation: cytosolic Ca²⁺ falls → CaM dissociates → MLCK inactive; myosin light-chain phosphatase (MLCP) dephosphorylates MLC → cross-bridge cycle slows → relaxation. Ca²⁺-sensitisation — many GPCR pathways (α1, ETA, AT1, TXA2) signal via Gα12/13 → RhoA → Rho-kinase → phosphorylates MYPT1 (regulatory subunit of MLCP) → MLCP inhibited → balance shifts toward phosphorylated MLC → more contraction at the same Ca²⁺. Critical for sustained vascular tone. Why slow? Smooth-muscle myosin has very low ATPase activity (cycling rate ~100× slower than skeletal). The MLCK phosphorylation cascade adds further latency: contraction onset is in seconds, peak in tens of seconds. This is appropriate for tonic functions (vessel tone, sphincter holding, peristalsis), not for fast voluntary movement. Why energy-efficient? The latch state: when Ca²⁺ falls and MLCP dephosphorylates the cross-bridges, attached cross-bridges detach much more slowly than during active phosphorylation. They remain attached to actin and bear load with very little ATPase activity. This is why smooth muscle uses 1/10 to 1/300 the ATP of skeletal muscle for the same maintained tension. For sphincters and resistance vessels that must hold tone for hours, this is essential. Multiple parallel Ca²⁺ entry/release pathways in smooth muscle (Cav1.2, store-operated Ca²⁺ entry via STIM/Orai, receptor-operated channels, RyR3, IP₃R) allow integration of diverse inputs — depolarisation, hormones, stretch, and paracrine signals can each independently raise Ca²⁺ and produce contraction. Pharmacological corollaries: calcium channel blockers (dihydropyridines) block Cav1.2 → less Ca²⁺ entry → vasodilation; Rho-kinase inhibitors (fasudil) block sensitisation → vasodilation; NO/cGMP agents (nitrates, sildenafil) raise cGMP → PKG activation → MLCP activation + reduced cytosolic Ca²⁺; β2 agonists (salbutamol) raise cAMP → PKA → MLCK inhibition → bronchodilation [Berne & Levy ch.14; Costanzo; Guyton & Hall ch.8].

Elaborative explanation: The MLCK/MLCP regulatory architecture has two outstanding features. First, it allows graded force at the same Ca²⁺ via Ca²⁺-sensitisation — RhoA/Rho-kinase phosphorylation of MYPT1 inhibits MLCP, so phosphorylated MLC accumulates without any change in Ca²⁺ levels. This is what lets vascular smooth muscle sustain tone in response to chronic agonist stimulation without the cell being constantly Ca²⁺-loaded. Second, the latch state is unique to smooth muscle and is the metabolic basis of arterial tone — without it, vascular resistance would be unsustainable energetically. The dual ON/OFF balance (MLCK vs MLCP, Ca²⁺ vs sensitisation) is what makes smooth muscle so pharmacologically responsive.

Hints:

1. Ca²⁺ → CaM → MLCK → MLC phosphorylation → contraction.
2. Latch state explains the low ATP cost.
3. RhoA/Rho-kinase phosphorylates MYPT1 to inhibit MLCP — sensitisation.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the activation sequence (Ca²⁺ → CaM → MLCK → MLC phosphorylation).
- Identifies Ser19 as the regulatory phosphorylation site.
- Describes MLCP as the relaxation mechanism and Rho-kinase/MYPT1 as the sensitisation pathway.
- Explains slow onset (low myosin ATPase + MLCK cascade latency).
- Explains energy efficiency via the latch state.
- Mentions multiple parallel Ca²⁺ entry pathways and their pharmacological exploitation.

A Yellow answer covers MLCK/MLCP but omits sensitisation or latch state.

A Red answer treats smooth muscle as troponin-regulated or denies thick-filament regulation.

Common misconceptions:

- "Smooth muscle is slow because Ca²⁺ rises slowly.": Correction: low myosin ATPase plus the MLCK phosphorylation cascade latency are the main contributors; Ca²⁺ rise itself can be quite fast.
- "Latch state is ATP-free.": Correction: it has very low ATP cost but is not zero; cross-bridges still cycle slowly.

QUESTION 6
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The cardiac ventricular action potential is divided into five phases. Describe the dominant ionic current(s) of each phase, and explain how the design of the AP fits the pumping function of the heart.

Model answer: Phase 0 (upstroke): voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (Nav1.5) open at threshold (~−65 mV) → rapid inward Na⁺ → membrane reaches +30 mV. Steep upstroke (~200 V/s) propagates rapidly through the gap-junction-coupled syncytium. Phase 1 (initial repolarisation): Na⁺ channels inactivate; transient outward K⁺ current (Ito) produces a brief notch. Most prominent in epicardial cells; absent in endocardial cells — produces the transmural voltage gradient that contributes to the T wave on ECG. Phase 2 (plateau): inward L-type Ca²⁺ current (ICa,L through Cav1.2) is balanced by outward delayed rectifier K⁺ currents (IKr — rapid; IKs — slow). Net current is small; membrane potential remains near 0 mV for ~200 ms. This is the unique cardiac phase. Phase 3 (rapid repolarisation): Cav1.2 channels inactivate; outward IKr and IKs continue → rapid repolarisation. As repolarisation proceeds, IK1 (inward rectifier) re-opens and accelerates return to resting potential. Phase 4 (resting potential): set by IK1 — high K⁺ permeability holds Vm near EK ≈ −90 mV in working myocardium. (In SA/AV node, IK1 is absent and If, ICa,T, ICa,L produce spontaneous depolarisation.) Why this design fits the heart's function: (1) Long plateau enforces refractoriness — during phases 0–2, Na⁺ channels are inactivated; they recover only after sufficient repolarisation. This long absolute refractory period (~250 ms) prevents tetanus, which would abolish diastolic filling. Cardiac muscle physically cannot tetanise — a brilliant safety feature engineered at the channel-protein level. (2) Plateau Ca²⁺ entry triggers CICR — the same plateau that protects the heart from tetany also delivers the Ca²⁺ that triggers contraction. The two functions (refractoriness, CICR) share the same molecular substrate. (3) AP duration is rate-adaptive — at higher rates, the plateau shortens (largely via IKs and Ito kinetics), preserving diastolic filling time. Loss of this rate-dependent shortening (e.g., in some long-QT mutations) predisposes to arrhythmias at fast rates. (4) Modulability — β-adrenergic phosphorylation of Cav1.2 increases plateau Ca²⁺ entry → more CICR → more force. Vagal stimulation increases IK,ACh → shortens plateau (in atrial, less in ventricular) → less Ca²⁺ entry → less force. Multiple drugs target individual channels (Class III antiarrhythmics block IKr; Class I block Nav1.5; Class IV block Cav1.2) to remodel the AP for therapeutic ends. Pathology view: mutations in any of these channels (KCNQ1, KCNH2, SCN5A, KCNJ2) produce specific congenital arrhythmia syndromes (long-QT 1, 2, 3; short-QT; Andersen-Tawil) with characteristic AP and ECG signatures. Drugs that block IKr inadvertently (many antibiotics and antipsychotics) cause acquired QT prolongation and torsade de pointes [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: The cardiac AP is the prototypical "designed-to-fit-function" biological waveform. Five distinct phases driven by different channels with different time constants combine to produce a shape that simultaneously prevents tetany, triggers contraction, and remains modulable. The pharmacological landscape mirrors this design — every major class of antiarrhythmic drug targets one of the channels, and every congenital long-QT or Brugada mutation hits one of them too. The convergence of design, pharmacology, and disease genetics on the same set of channels makes the cardiac AP one of the most clinically relevant pieces of physiology.

Hints:

1. Five phases, each with a dominant current.
2. Phase 2 plateau is the unique cardiac feature.
3. The plateau both enforces refractoriness _and_ triggers CICR.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names all five phases and their dominant currents (Nav1.5, Ito, Cav1.2 + IKr/IKs, IKr + IK1 transition, IK1).
- Identifies the plateau as the unique cardiac phase.
- Explains the four functional purposes of the plateau (refractoriness, CICR trigger, rate-adaptation, modulability).
- Cites at least one channelopathy (LQT1/2/3, Brugada) and at least one drug class (Class I/III/IV).

A Yellow answer covers the phases but omits the functional rationale or the channel-disease links.

A Red answer treats the cardiac AP as identical to the skeletal AP or denies the role of Cav1.2 in the plateau.

Common misconceptions:

- "The plateau exists just to delay repolarisation.": Correction: it enforces refractoriness, triggers CICR, is rate-adaptive, and is the locus of autonomic and pharmacological modulation — multiple coordinated functions in one waveform feature.
- "The cardiac AP shape is fixed.": Correction: AP duration shortens at fast heart rates, modulated by IKs and Ito kinetics — a rate-adaptive feature important for preserving diastolic filling.

QUESTION 7
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 68-year-old man with chronic systolic heart failure (LVEF 25%) is treated with carvedilol, lisinopril, spironolactone, and furosemide. Decompose each drug's mechanism in terms of cardiac and vascular smooth-muscle physiology.

Model answer: Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) is characterised by reduced contractility, neurohumoral over-activation (sympathetic, RAAS, ADH), and progressive remodelling. Each drug targets a specific pathophysiological node. (1) Carvedilol — non-selective β-blocker with α1-blocking activity. Counterintuitively, β-blockers improve outcome in chronic HFrEF, despite producing acute negative inotropy. Mechanisms: reduces chronic sympathetic toxicity to cardiomyocytes (cAMP/PKA over-activation → Ca²⁺ overload, apoptosis, arrhythmias); slows heart rate → improved diastolic filling and oxygen economy; up-regulates β-receptors over weeks (down-regulated in chronic HF); reduces renin release; carvedilol's α1-blockade adds afterload reduction; has antioxidant and antiproliferative effects. Must be started at low dose and titrated slowly — initial worsening is possible. (2) Lisinopril — ACE inhibitor. Blocks angiotensin-converting enzyme: lowers angiotensin II → reduced vasoconstriction (afterload reduction), reduced aldosterone (preload reduction via reduced Na⁺ retention), reduced direct cardiac and vascular trophic stimulation (reduced remodelling); raises bradykinin → more NO release → vasodilation; this also explains the cough side-effect (5–20%); reduces myocyte apoptosis and fibroblast proliferation in long-term use. ARBs (losartan, valsartan) are alternatives if cough is intolerable. (3) Spironolactone — mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist. Aldosterone causes Na⁺ retention, K⁺ excretion, cardiac fibrosis, and endothelial dysfunction. Blocking the mineralocorticoid receptor: mild diuretic effect; importantly reduces myocardial fibrosis and remodelling — independent of haemodynamic effects; K⁺-sparing — combined with ACEi/ARB requires monitoring of K⁺. Net effect: profound Na⁺ and water excretion → reduced preload → relief of pulmonary congestion and oedema; provides symptomatic relief but does not improve mortality (unlike the other three above); too aggressive use can drop preload below the optimal Frank–Starling point; risk of azotaemia and electrolyte loss (K⁺, Mg²⁺) → arrhythmias. A complete answer relates each drug's mechanism back to the underlying cardiac and vascular smooth-muscle physiology: contractility, autonomic tone, preload (volume / RAAS), afterload (vascular SMC tone), and remodelling [Berne & Levy ch.13–14; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: This four-drug "guideline-directed medical therapy" (GDMT) is the foundation of modern HFrEF management because it targets multiple pathophysiological nodes simultaneously: sympathetic over-activation (β-blocker), RAAS over-activation (ACEi/ARB plus MRA), and volume overload (loop diuretic). The case illustrates how a complex syndrome is treated by understanding the pathophysiology and matching each drug to a specific node.

Hints:

1. Four drugs, four pathophysiological nodes (sympathetic, RAAS, aldosterone, volume).
2. β-blockers improve outcome despite acute negative inotropy.
3. Loop diuretic gives symptom relief but does not change mortality.

Stem: A 16-year-old previously well girl collapses while playing football. ECG documented during a brief recurrent episode shows bidirectional ventricular tachycardia. Genetic testing reveals a heterozygous RYR2 missense mutation. Decompose the pathophysiology.

Model answer: This is catecholaminergic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (CPVT) — an inherited arrhythmia syndrome caused by gain-of-function mutations in RYR2 (autosomal dominant; recessive forms with CASQ2 mutations also exist). (1) Molecular lesion — RYR2 mutations destabilise the channel's closed state, making it more prone to spontaneous "diastolic" Ca²⁺ release, especially when SR Ca²⁺ load rises. The defect is silent under quiet conditions; it is unmasked by sympathetic activation. (2) Why exercise/emotion triggers events — sympathetic activation: β1 → cAMP → PKA → phosphorylates Cav1.2 (more Ca²⁺ entry per beat) and phospholamban (more SR Ca²⁺ uptake) → SR Ca²⁺ load rises. The mutant RyR2 leaks Ca²⁺ during diastole, well before the next AP. The released Ca²⁺ activates NCX in reverse mode → inward depolarising current → delayed afterdepolarisations (DADs). If a DAD reaches threshold, it triggers a premature ventricular complex; with sufficient burden, this triggers polymorphic or bidirectional VT. (3) Bidirectional VT — alternating beat-to-beat morphology reflecting alternation between two ectopic foci, classically in the right and left posterior fascicles. Polymorphic VT and ventricular fibrillation can also occur. (4) Clinical features — exercise- or emotion-triggered syncope, often misdiagnosed as vasovagal or epilepsy; sudden cardiac death in young people, first event can be fatal; resting ECG and structural heart imaging are normal; diagnosis often via exercise testing (PVCs, bigeminy, bidirectional VT at increasing workloads) and genetic testing of RYR2 (or CASQ2 in recessive cases). (5) Treatment — β-blockers (especially nadolol or propranolol — non-selective): first-line; reduce sympathetic SR loading and DAD generation. Flecainide — Class Ic antiarrhythmic with a direct effect on RyR2 leak; useful adjunct in residual events. Left cardiac sympathetic denervation (LCSD) — surgical removal of left stellate ganglion and upper thoracic ganglia in refractory cases. ICD — for documented ventricular fibrillation or refractory VT despite medical therapy. Avoidance of competitive sports, β-agonists, and digoxin. Cascade family screening — RYR2 testing of first-degree relatives. (6) Pedagogical insight — CPVT illustrates how a normally beneficial physiological signal (sympathetic SR loading for stronger contractions) can be lethal when an upstream channel is mutated. It also shows that arrhythmias are not always the result of structural heart disease — the heart can be macroscopically perfectly normal yet electrically unstable at the channel level. The lessons generalise to other channelopathies (long-QT syndromes, Brugada syndrome, short-QT syndrome) [Berne & Levy ch.13; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: CPVT is one of the cleanest "channelopathy with stress trigger" syndromes — a young, structurally normal heart that becomes electrically unstable only when sympathetic drive loads the SR. The therapeutic strategy is a beautiful inversion of the disease mechanism: β-blockade reduces the SR load that the mutant RyR2 cannot tolerate. Flecainide adds a direct RyR2 effect, complementing β-blockade. The case illustrates how genetic counselling and family screening are integral to management — silent gene carriers benefit from prophylactic β-blockade and lifestyle modification.

1. RYR2 mutation makes the channel leaky during diastole.
2. Sympathetic SR loading unmasks the defect.
3. Bidirectional VT is the pathognomonic morphology.

Stem: A 54-year-old woman with rate-controlled atrial fibrillation on digoxin presents with nausea, "yellow-green halos" around lights, and her ECG shows new junctional rhythm with downsloping ST segments. She is also on furosemide for hypertension. Explain her presentation in terms of cardiac muscle physiology.

Model answer: This is digoxin toxicity, classically precipitated by furosemide-induced hypokalaemia. (1) Digoxin's normal mechanism — inhibits sarcolemmal Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. Intracellular Na⁺ rises slightly. NCX (3 Na⁺ : 1 Ca²⁺) is less effective at extruding Ca²⁺ (or runs in reverse), raising cytosolic and SR Ca²⁺ → bigger transient → positive inotropy. In atrial fibrillation, it is also used for rate control: increased vagal tone slows AV nodal conduction. (2) Why hypokalaemia precipitates toxicity — K⁺ and digoxin compete for the same site on the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. Low extracellular K⁺ exposes more digoxin-binding sites → more enzyme inhibition → more cellular Ca²⁺ overload → more arrhythmogenicity. This is why even therapeutic digoxin doses become toxic in hypokalaemic patients. Furosemide's renal K⁺ wasting is a classic precipitant. (3) Cellular consequences of toxicity — massive Ca²⁺ overload → spontaneous SR Ca²⁺ leak → reverse-mode NCX → DADs → triggered arrhythmias. Common patterns: PVCs, bigeminy, junctional rhythms (digoxin's vagal effect on AV node + atrial automaticity changes), AV blocks, atrial tachycardia with AV block (the "PAT with block" pattern), accelerated junctional rhythm, ventricular arrhythmias including bidirectional VT. The "downsloping ST" or "Salvador Dalí moustache" is a digoxin effect on repolarisation, not necessarily toxic by itself. (4) Non-cardiac manifestations — visual: xanthopsia (yellow-green vision), halos, blurred vision (digoxin alters retinal Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) — classical and pathognomonic; GI: nausea, vomiting, anorexia (chemoreceptor trigger zone); neurological: confusion, weakness, headache. (5) Predisposing factors beyond hypokalaemia: hypomagnesaemia (also depletes intracellular K⁺); hypercalcaemia (worsens Ca²⁺ overload); renal failure (digoxin is renally cleared); drug interactions (amiodarone, verapamil, quinidine, clarithromycin all raise digoxin levels); hypothyroidism (slows metabolism); older age. (7) Pedagogical anchor — the case crystallises the narrow therapeutic index of digoxin and shows why drug–drug and drug–electrolyte interactions matter so much. It also illustrates the principle that ion-pump inhibition links to cytosolic Ca²⁺ via NCX coupling — a beautiful piece of integrative cardiac physiology [Berne & Levy ch.13; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: Digoxin toxicity is one of the most instructive clinical pharmacology cases because it ties together pump inhibition, ion homeostasis, electrolyte interaction, and arrhythmogenesis. The K⁺-digoxin competition at the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase explains why electrolyte status determines the boundary between therapy and toxicity. The pathognomonic visual symptoms (xanthopsia, halos) reflect the same pump being inhibited in retinal cells. The case is a reminder that drugs do not act in isolation — they interact with electrolytes, with renal function, with co-medications, and with the patient's underlying physiology.

Hints:

1. Hypokalaemia precipitates digoxin toxicity by exposing more binding sites.
2. The toxicity manifests as Ca²⁺ overload-driven arrhythmias and visual symptoms.
3. Digibind/DigiFab is the specific antidote for severe toxicity.

- Identifies furosemide as the K⁺-wasting precipitant.

A Yellow answer covers the mechanism but omits the K⁺-competition or the visual symptoms.

A Red answer attributes toxicity to direct receptor effects rather than pump inhibition or denies the K⁺ interaction.

Reduced nNOS expression in inhibitory enteric neurons → impaired pyloric relaxation, contributing to functional outflow obstruction. Smooth-muscle myopathy with collagen deposition and atrophy. Inhibitory peptide imbalance — variable. Vicious cycle: delayed emptying → erratic post-prandial glucose → swings in glucose worsen autonomic neuropathy → worse gastroparesis. Stem: A 65-year-old man with longstanding hypertension and a recent MI presents with shortness of breath. Pathophysiology centres on impaired diastolic relaxation and increased ventricular stiffness. (1) Normal diastolic function requires active relaxation (energy-dependent: Ca²⁺ removal from cytosol via SERCA and NCX, troponin-Ca²⁺ dissociation, cross-bridge release, and elastic recoil from titin), passive compliance (the heart's ability to fill at low pressure, dependent on chamber geometry, wall thickness, and ECM composition), adequate filling time (dependent on heart rate and AV synchrony), and atrial booster pump (~25% of LV filling at rest, more at higher rates). (2) What goes wrong in HFpEF: concentric hypertrophy (chronic pressure overload from hypertension) → thicker walls → reduced cavity volume at any pressure → reduced compliance; cellular hypertrophy with re-expression of "fetal" gene programmes (β-MyHC, ANP, BNP); interstitial fibrosis — collagen accumulation in ECM driven by angiotensin II, aldosterone, TGF-β; this raises passive stiffness dramatically; titin isoform shift toward the stiffer N2B isoform; impaired Ca²⁺ handling — slower SERCA reuptake, increased PLB inhibition, and impaired CICR architecture (T-tubule disorganisation in some cases) → slower relaxation (negative lusitropy); endothelial dysfunction — reduced NO availability → coronary microvascular dysfunction → impaired metabolic supply to active relaxation; atrial fibrillation is common — loss of atrial booster pump worsens filling acutely. (6) Pedagogical insight — HFpEF illustrates that "ejection fraction" alone does not capture cardiac dysfunction. Diastole is an active, energy-consuming process at the cellular level (SERCA-mediated Ca²⁺ reuptake) and a passive elastic process at the tissue level (titin and ECM compliance). Both can fail [Berne & Levy ch.13; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: HFpEF is a fascinating clinical syndrome that has only recently received effective therapy (SGLT2 inhibitors) and is increasingly recognised as the dominant heart failure phenotype in elderly women with hypertension, diabetes, and obesity. The pathophysiology is multifactorial — cellular hypertrophy, interstitial fibrosis, titin remodelling, microvascular dysfunction — all converging on diastolic dysfunction with preserved EF. The contrast with HFrEF reminds us that "heart failure" is a clinical syndrome with at least two distinct pathophysiologies that respond to different therapies.

(1) Pregnancy-induced quiescence — progesterone dominance: high circulating progesterone maintains low contractility, in part by inhibiting prostaglandin synthesis, suppressing oxytocin receptor expression, and stabilising the membrane potential; NO/cGMP signalling is enhanced — keeps the myometrium relaxed; gap junction (connexin-43) expression is suppressed — the syncytium is functionally fragmented, preventing coordinated contractions; oxytocin receptor density is low; β2 adrenergic receptor activity maintains relaxation via cAMP. (2) Transition to labour (the "switch") — withdrawal of progesterone effect: at term, although total progesterone may not fall dramatically, the progesterone-receptor signalling shifts (e.g., balance of PR-A/PR-B isoforms, recruitment of repressive coregulators); functional progesterone withdrawal removes the molecular brakes; rise in oestrogen-driven gene programmes — increased connexin-43 → restored gap-junction coupling, increased oxytocin receptors, increased prostaglandin synthase expression; increased CRH (placental) — drives cortisol rise and prostaglandin synthesis; cervical ripening — collagen is enzymatically remodelled; cervix softens and effaces under prostaglandin and relaxin influence; mechanical stretch of the uterus at term itself induces contractile gene expression (mechanotransduction). (3) Cellular logic of contraction during labour — oxytocin (released by posterior pituitary, with placenta-derived inputs) → OXT receptor (Gq-coupled GPCR on myometrium) → PLC → IP₃ → SR Ca²⁺ release; also Cav1.2 opening → cytosolic Ca²⁺ rises → Ca²⁺/CaM/MLCK → MLC phosphorylation → contraction. Oxytocin also enhances local prostaglandin synthesis, amplifying its own effect (positive feedback). Prostaglandins (PGF2α, PGE2) — produced locally by myometrium and decidua; act on FP/EP receptors (Gq) → similar PLC-IP₃-Ca²⁺ pathway. Add Ca²⁺-sensitisation through Rho-kinase activation. Coordinated propagation — gap junctions allow waves of depolarisation to spread through the myometrium, producing the coordinated, propagating contractions that progressively dilate the cervix and expel the fetus. The Ferguson reflex — cervical stretch → afferent signal → posterior pituitary → more oxytocin → more contraction → more stretch (positive feedback). (4) Postpartum logic — sustained contraction of the myometrium after delivery compresses uterine spiral arteries and prevents post-partum haemorrhage. Oxytocin (clinically given as IV oxytocin, IM ergometrine, or carboprost) is essential for uterine tone post-delivery. (5) Pharmacology rooted in the physiology — tocolysis (slow contractions in preterm labour): β2 agonists (terbutaline) raise cAMP; magnesium sulphate (Ca²⁺ channel modulation); calcium channel blockers (nifedipine); oxytocin receptor antagonists (atosiban). Induction/augmentation of labour: oxytocin (IV titrated infusion), prostaglandin E2 (cervical), misoprostol (PGE1). Postpartum haemorrhage: oxytocin, ergometrine (5-HT2 + α1 actions), carboprost (PGF2α analogue), tranexamic acid. (6) Pedagogical insight — the myometrium illustrates the entire repertoire of smooth-muscle regulation in one organ: hormonal modulation of contractility, gap-junction-dependent syncytium formation, GPCR-driven Ca²⁺ mobilisation, Ca²⁺-sensitisation, mechanotransduction, positive-feedback signalling. It also shows that smooth-muscle behaviour is plastic over weeks-to-months: the same tissue that contracts forcefully in labour was largely silent throughout pregnancy [Berne & Levy ch.14; Costanzo; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: Uterine smooth muscle is the most dramatically plastic muscle in the body — it grows from ~50 g to ~1 kg during pregnancy, transitions from quiescent to forcefully contractile within hours, and returns to baseline in weeks postpartum. The transition is orchestrated by hormonal switches (progesterone withdrawal, oestrogen activation, CRH rise) that set up the cellular machinery for coordinated contraction (gap junction expression, oxytocin receptor up-regulation, prostaglandin synthesis). Modern obstetric pharmacology directly exploits this physiology — inducing labour with oxytocin and PGE2, suppressing it with β2 agonists and Ca²⁺ channel blockers, and managing PPH with uterotonic combinations.

1. Progesterone dominance maintains pregnancy quiescence.
2. The "switch" involves Cx43 up-regulation, oxytocin receptor expression, and prostaglandin synthesis.
3. Both oxytocin and prostaglandins use Gq-coupled GPCRs.

Stem: Why does the heart need to be a syncytium electrically while skeletal muscle does not? Use the differing functional demands to explain the architectural differences.

Model answer: The heart must contract synchronously and rhythmically as a single integrated pump, whereas skeletal muscle must contract in selectively addressable units to produce graded, voluntary movement. This single functional difference drives the entire architectural divergence. (1) Cardiac functional demand — the heart must eject blood as a coordinated mechanical unit (asynchronous activation produces inefficient contraction — the basis of the haemodynamic deterioration with bundle-branch block); originate its rhythm intrinsically (from the SA node) and propagate that rhythm reliably from atria to ventricles; recover diastolic filling time after each contraction; adapt rate and force continuously to circulatory demand. (2) Architectural solutions in cardiac muscle — gap-junction-coupled cells via intercalated discs (connexin-43 channels electrically couple every myocyte to its neighbours; a single AP from the SA node propagates throughout the syncytium without relying on dedicated motor nerves to every cell); specialised conduction system (His-Purkinje) — transmits the AP rapidly to coordinate ventricular contraction (apex-to-base sequence for efficient ejection); long action potential with refractoriness preventing tetanus; autonomic modulation of pacemaker rate and contractility — fine-tunes heart rate and stroke volume continuously. (3) Skeletal muscle functional demand — produce graded force on voluntary command from the CNS; allow selective recruitment of small (fine) and large (powerful) motor units; remain at rest until commanded; fire briefly and recover quickly to allow tetanus on demand. (4) Architectural solutions in skeletal muscle — no gap junctions between fibres (each fibre is electrically isolated; activity in one fibre does not spread to neighbours); innervation by individual motor neurons at the NMJ (every fibre belongs to exactly one motor unit, and is activated only when its motor neuron fires); brief AP with brief refractory period — fast tetanus possible; volitional CNS control — recruitment is the dominant mechanism for grading force, not autonomic modulation. (5) Smooth muscle as an intermediate case — single-unit smooth muscle (gut, uterus, ureter) is gap-junction-coupled like cardiac, allowing waves of contraction to propagate; multi-unit smooth muscle (iris, vas deferens) is innervated cell by cell like skeletal. The choice depends on the function: visceral organs need coordinated waves; iris needs cell-by-cell precision. (6) Take-home design principle — cell–cell coupling architecture is selected to match function. "More coupling" gives synchrony; "less coupling" gives selective control. Disorders of cardiac coupling (Cx43 mutations, intercalated disc disease) produce arrhythmias and conduction abnormalities — the architecture matters enormously for the integrated function [Berne & Levy ch.13–14; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Costanzo].

Elaborative explanation: The contrast between cardiac syncytium and skeletal individuality is a beautiful example of architecture-fits-function. The same problem (how to produce useful contraction) is solved by opposite strategies: maximal coupling for synchronous output, no coupling for selective recruitment. The smooth muscle world contains both strategies (single-unit vs multi-unit), depending on the organ. The principle generalises across biology — coupled networks (heart, gap-junction-coupled glia, electrical synapses in inferior olive) versus selectively addressable elements (motor neurons, sensory afferents, chemical synapses) reflect different computational demands.

Hints:

1. Synchronous output needs cell coupling; selective control needs isolation.
2. Cardiac uses gap junctions; skeletal uses individual NMJs.
3. Smooth muscle has both architectures — single-unit vs multi-unit.

Stem: Common misconception: "Cardiac muscle is just like skeletal muscle but smaller and slower." Critique this by listing the conserved features and listing the substantive differences with their functional consequences.

Model answer: Cardiac and skeletal muscle share the same conserved mechano-chemical core (sliding filament, cross-bridge cycling, troponin-tropomyosin regulation, Ca²⁺ transient, SR), but the differences are not cosmetic — they are profound and tied to function. Conserved features: sarcomeric ultrastructure with A bands, I bands, Z lines; thick filaments of myosin II, thin filaments of actin with troponin and tropomyosin; cross-bridge cycle: ATP binding → detachment → hydrolysis → re-cocking → re-attachment → power stroke (Pi release) → ADP release → rigor; Ca²⁺ as the proximal regulatory signal binding troponin C; SR-localised Ca²⁺ store, SERCA-mediated Ca²⁺ reuptake. Substantive differences and consequences: (1) Cell shape and connectivity — skeletal: long, multinucleate fibres, no electrical coupling between fibres; cardiac: branched, mononucleate cells coupled via gap junctions. Consequence: the heart is an electrical and mechanical syncytium; skeletal muscle is not. (2) T-tubule architecture — skeletal: triads at A-I junction; cardiac: dyads at Z line. Consequence: different geometric coupling between voltage sensor and Ca²⁺ release channel. (3) ECC mechanism — skeletal: mechanical (DHPR voltage sensor directly opens RyR1); cardiac: CICR (Ca²⁺ entry through Cav1.2 opens RyR2). Consequence: cardiac contraction is graded and modulable by Ca²⁺ entry, e.g., by autonomic stimulation. (4) Action potential — skeletal: short (~5 ms), brief refractory, tetanus possible; cardiac: long Ca²⁺-driven plateau (~200 ms), long refractory, no tetanus. Consequence: heart cannot tetanise → preserved diastolic filling, alternation of contraction and relaxation. (5) Force generation — skeletal: graded by motor-unit recruitment and rate coding (CNS-driven); cardiac: graded by Frank-Starling (preload-driven), Bowditch staircase (rate-driven), and autonomic inotropy (β1/M2-driven). Consequence: completely different control architecture — CNS recruitment vs intrinsic and humoral regulation. (6) Length-tension operating range — skeletal: full ascending and descending limbs accessible; cardiac: constrained by stiff titin and ECM to operate on the ascending limb only. Consequence: the heart's force monotonically rises with preload (Frank-Starling), unlike skeletal muscle which can be over-stretched. (7) Energy metabolism — skeletal: fibre-type-specific (oxidative I, intermediate IIa, glycolytic IIx/IIb); cardiac: almost entirely oxidative, fatty acid-dominated, ~30% mitochondrial volume. Consequence: skeletal muscle can sustain anaerobic work; the heart cannot tolerate ischaemia. (8) Refractoriness — skeletal: very brief; tetanic summation is normal; cardiac: spans most of the contraction; protects against tetany. Consequence: cardiac arrhythmias must be managed without driving the heart into tachy-induced ischaemia. (9) Regeneration — skeletal: satellite cells permit regeneration after injury; cardiac: minimal regenerative capacity; lost myocytes are replaced by fibrosis (scarring). Consequence: post-MI scar formation; chronic remodelling drives progression to HF. (10) Innervation — skeletal: somatic motor neurons at NMJ for every contraction; cardiac: autonomic modulatory innervation (sympathetic and parasympathetic) but heart contracts autonomously even denervated (transplanted heart still beats). (11) Diseases — skeletal: muscular dystrophies, myopathies, NMJ disorders; cardiac: cardiomyopathies (dilated, hypertrophic, restrictive, arrhythmogenic, takotsubo), channelopathies (LQTS, Brugada, CPVT), ischaemic heart disease. The disease landscapes barely overlap. Take-home: "smaller and slower" is wrong. Cardiac muscle is an entirely different design built on a shared molecular core. The architectural and regulatory differences are functionally critical [Berne & Levy ch.12–13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Costanzo].

Elaborative explanation: This question explicitly addresses a common student misconception that obscures the design logic of each muscle type. Saying cardiac muscle is "just" skeletal muscle slowed down is like saying a piano is "just" a guitar with more strings — technically related but missing what each one was built to do. The differences in connectivity (syncytium vs isolated fibres), ECC (CICR vs mechanical), AP shape (plateau vs spike), force grading (intrinsic/humoral vs CNS recruitment), and regeneration (fibrotic scarring vs satellite-cell repair) all reflect deep functional adaptations to different demands.

2. At least 10 substantive differences, each with a functional consequence.
3. The disease landscapes (cardiomyopathy/channelopathy vs dystrophy/myopathy) don't overlap.

Stem: Common misconception: "Smooth muscle is a primitive ancestor of striated muscle." Critique this and discuss what is genuinely distinctive about smooth-muscle design.

Model answer: Smooth muscle is not "primitive" — it is exquisitely adapted to its functional niche. The misconception probably stems from the lack of striations (which are visually striking) and the slower contraction. But every "absent" feature in smooth muscle has been replaced by a different solution that is appropriate to its function. What smooth muscle "lacks" (compared to striated) and why this is appropriate: (1) No sarcomeres — replaced by dense bodies and dense areas. The lack of regular sarcomeres means smooth muscle is not constrained to a fixed length-tension geometry; it can shorten over a much wider range than striated muscle (>50% of resting length, vs ~30% for skeletal). This is essential for organs that change volume drastically (uterus, bladder, gut). (2) No T tubules — replaced by caveolae that bring the sarcolemma close to the SR network. Caveolae are smaller, more numerous, and richer in Ca²⁺-handling and signalling proteins than T tubules. They also house GPCRs, eNOS, and other signalling complexes — making smooth muscle exquisitely receptive to humoral signals. (3) No troponin — replaced by calmodulin-MLCK regulation of the thick filament. This thick-filament regulation has two major advantages: (i) it allows graded force at the same Ca²⁺ via Ca²⁺-sensitisation by RhoA/Rho-kinase pathway; (ii) it enables the latch state. Striated muscle cannot do either of these. (4) No fast twitch capability — replaced by tonic capability. A skeletal muscle in tonic contraction would exhaust its energy supply within minutes; smooth muscle uses 1/10 to 1/300 the ATP. The latch state — slow detachment of dephosphorylated cross-bridges — is unique to smooth muscle and is the key to its energy efficiency. (5) No sarcoplasmic Ca²⁺ dominance — replaced by multiple parallel Ca²⁺ pathways. Smooth muscle uses Cav1.2, store-operated Ca²⁺ entry (STIM/Orai), receptor-operated Ca²⁺ channels, RyR3, and IP₃R. This allows integration of depolarisation, hormonal, paracrine, and mechanical signals. (6) No NMJ-style fast point-to-point innervation — replaced by varicosities and "wide-release" innervation, with neurotransmitters diffusing over a broader region. Multi-unit smooth muscle approaches NMJ-style precision; single-unit smooth muscle uses spread through gap junctions. (7) No fibre type division — replaced by continuous tunability of phasic vs tonic activity by hormonal and paracrine modulation. The same airway smooth-muscle cell can be rapidly bronchoconstricted by histamine and slowly relaxed by salmeterol. What smooth muscle does that striated muscle cannot: stress-relaxation (tolerates slow stretch by yielding tension; essential for storage organs); latch state (sustained tone at very low ATP cost; essential for sphincters and resistance vessels); Ca²⁺-sensitisation (force regulation independent of cytosolic Ca²⁺; essential for autonomic, hormonal, and paracrine control of vascular tone); integration of multiple Ca²⁺ entry pathways (responsiveness to a wide spectrum of signals); plasticity over weeks to months (hypertrophy in pregnancy — uterus 10× heavier — contractile-state shift in chronic hypertension, vascular remodelling; essential for adaptation to disease and physiological change). Take-home: smooth muscle is the only muscle type capable of tonic, energy-economical, hormonally regulated contraction in organs that must hold tone for hours and days. It is not a lesser cousin of striated muscle — it is a different design optimised for a different problem [Berne & Levy ch.14; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Costanzo].

Elaborative explanation: The question is a useful corrective to the common (and pejorative) view of smooth muscle as evolutionarily primitive. In fact, smooth muscle has unique capabilities that striated muscle lacks — latch state, Ca²⁺-sensitisation, stress-relaxation, multi-pathway Ca²⁺ regulation, exquisite hormonal/paracrine responsiveness. Each "lack" of striated-muscle features is replaced by a sophisticated alternative that fits the functional niche. The pharmacology of smooth muscle (vasodilators, bronchodilators, tocolytics, GI prokinetics) draws on this regulatory richness — far more pharmacological "handles" than striated muscle has.

Stem: Discuss how T-tubule remodelling contributes to the progression of heart failure. Why is this a structural problem with mechanical consequences, and how does it relate to the failure of CICR?

Model answer: The T-tubule network in cardiac myocytes is the structural substrate of efficient CICR. Disruption of this architecture is now recognised as a fundamental cellular event in chronic heart failure. (1) Normal T-tubule architecture — cardiac T tubules invaginate from the sarcolemma at the Z line, forming dyads with apposed SR junctional cisternae across a ~12-nm gap. L-type Ca²⁺ channels (Cav1.2) cluster opposite RyR2 with this minimal separation. This geometric fidelity allows synchronous, efficient CICR throughout the cell volume — every part of the cell receives Ca²⁺ release at the same time. (2) What goes wrong in heart failure: T-tubule disorganisation — in failing cardiomyocytes (post-MI, dilated cardiomyopathy, hypertrophy), the T-tubule network becomes irregular: fewer T-tubules, with disordered orientations and gaps in coverage; loss of dyadic apposition — Cav1.2 channels on the sarcolemma are no longer aligned with RyR2 on the SR; the dyadic gap widens or disappears; "orphaned" RyR2 — RyR2 clusters not closely apposed to Cav1.2 fail to be triggered efficiently; their Ca²⁺ release is delayed or absent; asynchronous Ca²⁺ transients — different parts of the cell release Ca²⁺ at different times, producing a slower, lower-amplitude, less-spatially-uniform Ca²⁺ transient; reduced gain of CICR — less Ca²⁺ is released per unit Ca²⁺ entering, requiring more Cav1.2 activity for the same force. (3) Mechanical consequences — negative inotropy (reduced Ca²⁺ transient amplitude → less force per beat); negative lusitropy (spatially heterogeneous Ca²⁺ transients delay relaxation; combined with phospholamban dysfunction, diastolic dysfunction develops); increased Ca²⁺ leak (mis-localised RyR2 may release Ca²⁺ aberrantly during diastole → DADs → arrhythmias); mitochondrial Ca²⁺ uptake disrupted (mitochondria located near dyads receive less Ca²⁺ → reduced ATP production capacity). (4) Why does T-tubule remodelling occur? Multiple mechanisms have been implicated: cellular hypertrophy stretching the network beyond its capacity; loss of structural proteins (junctophilin-2 reduction is well-documented); altered membrane lipid composition; mechanical stress from increased wall stress; cytokines (TNFα) and chronic catecholamine exposure. (5) Therapeutic implications — mechanical unloading (e.g., by LVADs) can partially restore T-tubule architecture over weeks-to-months — a key reason "bridge to transplant" sometimes becomes "bridge to recovery"; β-blocker therapy in HFrEF may protect T-tubule architecture by reducing chronic catecholamine stress; junctophilin-2 gene therapy is in pre-clinical development; understanding T-tubule remodelling has shifted the conceptual framework for heart failure: it is not just a contractile-protein problem; it is a structural-coupling problem with downstream functional consequences. (6) Pedagogical insight — cells are not merely bags of enzymes — they are spatially organised. The dyad is a 12-nanometre architectural feature whose disruption produces clinically meaningful contractile failure. This generalises: cellular geometry matters for function, and its remodelling is reversible. The principle reaches beyond cardiology — neuronal spine remodelling in addiction and depression, β-cell granule reorganisation in diabetes, podocyte foot-process effacement in nephrotic syndrome — all illustrate the same theme that nano-scale architecture has macro-scale consequences [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: T-tubule remodelling is one of the most exciting recent additions to the heart-failure pathophysiology framework. It explains why HF is not just a pump-strength problem but a coupling-fidelity problem: even with intact contractile proteins, mis-aligned Cav1.2 and RyR2 produce inefficient, asynchronous Ca²⁺ transients. The reversibility of the remodelling under mechanical unloading (LVADs) and pharmacological β-blockade gives hope that targeted therapies (junctophilin-2 augmentation, structural protein gene therapy) might restore CICR fidelity in failing hearts.

Stem: Why is the cardiac muscle action potential described as "designed by evolution to prevent the heart from killing itself"? Use the design features (long plateau, rate-dependent shortening, refractoriness, autonomic modulability) to explain the engineering logic.

Model answer: The cardiac AP is not just a means of triggering contraction — it is an elegant evolutionary solution to the problem of how to make a continuously contracting pump that runs for a billion beats without failing. Several design features serve this protective function. (1) The long plateau ensures the heart cannot tetanise. Tetanic contraction in the heart would mean continuous systole — no diastolic filling — instantaneous death. The 200-ms plateau enforces an absolute refractory period that spans most of the contraction; another AP cannot fire until repolarisation is well underway. This is the single most important safety feature: it makes lethal cardiac tetanus impossible by design. Any nervous reflex, hormonal surge, or metabolic disturbance that might otherwise tetanise a tissue cannot tetanise the heart. (2) Rate-dependent AP shortening preserves diastolic filling. As heart rate increases, the AP duration shortens automatically (largely via IKs activation and Ito kinetics). At rest, ~70 bpm, AP is ~300 ms; at 180 bpm, AP is ~200 ms. This preserves diastolic time even at high rates. Without it, fast heart rates would have insufficient filling time and stroke volume would collapse. Mutations that abolish rate-dependent shortening (e.g., some long-QT subtypes) cause arrhythmias precisely at high rates because the AP fails to shorten when needed. (3) CICR couples the protective plateau to contractility. The same plateau that protects from tetany delivers the trigger Ca²⁺ that opens RyR2. Refractoriness and contraction are not separable — they share the same molecular substrate (Cav1.2). This is design economy. (4) Autonomic modulability is layered on top — β1 stimulation increases Cav1.2 activity (more force), HCN/If activity (faster pacemaker), and PLB phosphorylation (faster relaxation). M2 stimulation does the opposite. The autonomic nervous system can rapidly adjust output to demand without compromising the protective architecture. The plateau is preserved; only its modulation is changed. (5) Conduction system geometry ensures synchrony — the His-Purkinje system delivers the AP to the apex first, so contraction begins at the apex and propagates upward (toward the outflow tracts) — squeezing blood out efficiently. The AV node imposes a protective delay (~120 ms) so the atria fill the ventricles before ventricular contraction begins. Without this delay, ventricles would contract synchronously with atria, eliminating atrial booster pumping. (6) Multiple parallel K⁺ currents provide redundancy — IKr, IKs, IK1, Ito: each carries part of the repolarising load. Loss of any single channel (genetic mutation or drug block) is partially compensated by the others. This redundancy makes the system robust to perturbation. (7) Pacemaker hierarchy provides backup — if the SA node fails, the AV node takes over (~40–60 bpm). If the AV node fails, ventricular escape rhythms emerge (~30–40 bpm). This hierarchical failsafe means survival is possible even with primary pacemaker failure. The corollary: when the design fails, the heart kills itself. Long-QT syndromes, Brugada syndrome, CPVT, and acquired QT prolongation by drugs all interfere with one of the design features above and produce sudden cardiac death. The fact that there is a recognisable category of "channelopathy-induced sudden cardiac death" implies that the normal design is what keeps everyone else alive [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Ganong].

Elaborative explanation: This integrative question is a way of synthesising the entire chapter — every feature of the cardiac AP serves a purpose, and the integrated logic (plateau + refractoriness + CICR + rate adaptation + autonomic modulability + redundancy + failsafes) is one of the most beautiful examples of evolutionary engineering in physiology. Anti-arrhythmic drug therapy and channel-targeted gene therapy are essentially attempts to restore the design when it fails. The same design principles (long plateau preventing tetany, redundancy, failsafes) are implicit in many other physiological systems — but the heart's example is uniquely vivid because the consequences of design failure are immediately fatal.

2. Multiple K⁺ currents and pacemaker hierarchy provide redundancy and failsafes.
3. Channelopathies show what happens when the design fails.
