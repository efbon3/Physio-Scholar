Stem: Discuss the organisation of memory systems in the brain. How does the hippocampus participate in declarative memory, and what distinguishes it from procedural memory?

Model answer: Answer key (full discussion):

Memory taxonomy — Modern cognitive neuroscience recognises multiple parallel memory systems with distinct anatomy, neurochemistry, and behavioural characteristics.

Declarative (explicit) memory — Conscious recall:

- Episodic — Personal experiences, events, time, place. "What did you do last weekend?"
- Semantic — General facts, knowledge. "What is the capital of France?"

Anatomy: Hippocampus + medial temporal lobe (entorhinal, perirhinal, parahippocampal cortex) for encoding and early retrieval. Long-term storage gradually transferred to distributed cortical networks ("systems consolidation").

Hippocampal circuit (Papez circuit, simplified):

- Sensory cortex → entorhinal cortex → dentate gyrus → CA3 → CA1 → subiculum → entorhinal cortex → cortex.
- Long-term potentiation (LTP) at multiple synapses encodes new associations.
- Place cells (pyramidal cells) fire when animal is in specific locations — neural map of space.
- Grid cells (entorhinal cortex) fire at periodic intervals — spatial coordinate system.
- Time cells encode temporal sequence within episodes.

Famous case — H.M. (Henry Molaison) (1953-2008):

- Bilateral medial temporal lobectomy for refractory epilepsy at age 27.
- Result: severe anterograde amnesia — could not form new declarative memories.
- Retrograde amnesia for ~2 years preceding surgery (recent memories not yet consolidated).
- Older memories preserved (already consolidated to cortex).
- Procedural memory intact — could learn new motor skills (mirror tracing), even though he had no memory of having done the task before.
- This dissociation (declarative impaired, procedural preserved) revealed that memory is not unitary — different systems for different types of information.

Non-declarative (implicit) memory — Often unconscious:

- Procedural — Skills, habits ("how to ride a bike"). Dependent on basal ganglia + cerebellum.
- Priming — Recognition facilitated by prior exposure.
- Conditioning — Pavlovian (amygdala for fear; cerebellum for motor).
- Habituation/sensitisation.

Why this matters clinically:

Alzheimer disease:

- Hippocampus + entorhinal cortex among earliest structures affected.
- Episodic memory loss prominent (recent events) — recall of distant past relatively preserved early.
- Progresses to involve other declarative memory and other cognitive functions.
- Procedural memory often relatively preserved until late — explains why patients can sometimes do daily tasks better than they can d

Elaborative explanation: A complete answer integrates the structures, mechanisms, and clinical implications named in the source explanation. The framework above provides the scaffolding; learners should add specific molecular detail, named diseases, and pharmacological agents to deepen the answer.

2. Key terms to mention: LTP, Henry Molaison.
3. Synthesise across mechanism, structure, and clinical implications.

Stem: Describe hemispheric specialisation. What functions are typically lateralised, and how do split-brain studies illustrate them?

Model answer: _Hemispheric specialisation_ (lateralisation) - the two cerebral hemispheres have distinct functional emphases despite being anatomically near-mirrors. Most right-handed individuals (~ 95%) and ~ 70% of left-handers have left hemisphere dominance for language. _Left hemisphere_ (typically dominant): language production (Broca), comprehension (Wernicke), sequencing, mathematical/logical reasoning, analytical processing, fine motor control of right side, calculation. _Right hemisphere_: visuospatial processing, face recognition (fusiform face area more bilateral but right slightly dominant), prosody (emotional tone of speech), music processing, spatial attention (neglect from right parietal lesion much more disabling), holistic/integrative processing, recognising emotions in faces. Connections: corpus callosum (~ 200 million axons), anterior commissure transfer information between hemispheres. Split-brain studies (Sperry, Gazzaniga - Nobel 1981): patients with corpus callosotomy (for refractory epilepsy) show striking dissociations: object presented to left visual field (right hemisphere) cannot be named verbally (language is left); patient names what they see in right visual field (left hemisphere); but patient can use _left hand_ (right hemisphere) to point to picture matching the unnamed object - showing right hemisphere can recognise without language. Reveals each hemisphere has its own consciousness in some sense, with the corpus callosum integrating them. Clinical: stroke pattern - left MCA = aphasia + right hemiparesis; right MCA = neglect + left hemiparesis. Aphasia syndromes localise to specific perisylvian areas. Right parietal lesions cause hemispatial neglect (often dramatic - patient ignores left side of world, body, plate of food). Anosognosia - lack of awareness of deficit - more common with right hemisphere lesions [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: Hemispheric specialisation is one of the most robust principles in neuroscience. The corpus callosum integrates the two hemispheres; split-brain experiments dissociate them and reveal hidden specialisations. Clinical lesions produce predictable patterns based on which hemisphere is affected.

Hints:

1. Left: language, sequential, analytical, math.
2. Right: visuospatial, face, prosody, holistic.
3. Split-brain reveals hidden hemispheric independence.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names ≥ 3 left-hemisphere functions and ≥ 3 right-hemisphere functions.
- Identifies corpus callosum as the integrator.
- Mentions split-brain (Sperry/Gazzaniga) findings.
- Maps stroke patterns to hemispheric functions.
- Names hemispatial neglect as a right-parietal sign.

A Yellow answer covers basic lateralisation but misses split-brain or specific clinical correlates.

A Red answer claims complete hemispheric specialisation in all functions.

Common misconceptions:

- "Left brain is logical, right brain is creative.": Correction: Pop-psychology dichotomy oversimplifies; both hemispheres contribute to most cognitive functions.
- "All left-handers have right-hemisphere language.": Correction: ~ 70% of left-handers still have left-hemisphere language; only ~ 30% have right or bilateral.

Stem: Discuss the neurobiology of sleep. What are the stages, neurochemistry, and clinical implications of sleep disorders?

Model answer: Sleep is a regulated brain state with distinct stages, controlled by interacting nuclei. _Stages_ (recorded on PSG with EEG, EOG, EMG): (a) NREM stage 1 - drowsy, theta waves; (b) NREM stage 2 - sleep spindles, K complexes; (c) NREM stage 3 (slow-wave sleep, SWS) - delta waves; restorative; growth hormone secretion; memory consolidation (declarative); (d) REM sleep - low-amplitude mixed-frequency EEG (similar to wakefulness), rapid eye movements, atonia (skeletal muscle paralysis except diaphragm and eyes), dreaming, autonomic instability. Cycle: ~ 90 min; SWS dominates first half of night; REM dominates second half. Total sleep ~ 7-9 h adults; ~ 25% REM. _Wake-promoting systems_: ascending reticular activating system (ARAS) - cholinergic (PPT/LDT), noradrenergic (locus coeruleus), serotonergic (raphe), histaminergic (tuberomammillary nucleus, TMN), dopaminergic (VTA), orexin/hypocretin (lateral hypothalamus - critical for stabilising wake). _Sleep-promoting systems_: GABAergic VLPO (ventrolateral preoptic nucleus) - inhibits wake systems; adenosine - accumulates during wake; basal forebrain. _Flip-flop switch_: VLPO and wake systems mutually inhibit -> bistable; orexin stabilises wake; loss of orexin neurons = narcolepsy with cataplexy. Circadian regulation: SCN drives ~ 24-h rhythm, entrained by light via melanopsin RGCs -> SCN -> melatonin (pineal). Sleep need is regulated homeostatically (sleep pressure rises with wake) and circadianly (timing). Functions of sleep: memory consolidation (SWS for declarative; REM for procedural and emotional); synaptic homeostasis (downscaling); glymphatic clearance of metabolic waste (60% greater during sleep); immune function; metabolic regulation. Disorders: (1) _Insomnia_ - common; CBT-I first-line; pharmacotherapy second; (2) _Obstructive sleep apnoea_ (OSA) - upper airway collapse; risk factors obesity, neck circumference; presentation with snoring, witnessed apnoeas, daytime sleepiness; cardiovascular complications; treat with CPAP, weight loss, surgery (UPPP), mandibular advancement, hypoglossal nerve stimulation; (3) _Narcolepsy_ - excessive daytime sleepiness, sleep attacks, cataplexy (sudden muscle weakness with emotion - from REM atonia intruding into wake), sleep paralysis, hypnagogic hallucinations; type 1 (with cataplexy) - low CSF orexin; HLA-DQB1*06:02 association; treat with modafinil/pitolisant (wakefulness), sodium oxybate (improves SWS, reduces cataplexy), antidepressants for cataplexy; (4) *REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD)* - loss of REM atonia -> acting out dreams; strong association with prodromal alpha-synucleinopathy (PD, DLB, MSA - up to 80% convert over 10-20 years); treat with melatonin (preferred) or clonazepam; (5) *Restless legs syndrome* - urge to move legs at rest, worse evening; iron deficiency; treat with iron replacement, dopamine agonists (with augmentation risk), gabapentinoids; (6) *Parasomnias* - sleepwalking, sleep terrors (NREM), nightmares (REM); usually benign in childhood; (7) *Circadian disorders\* - shift-work, delayed/advanced sleep phase, jet lag; treat with timed light, melatonin. Pharmacology: benzodiazepines, Z-drugs (zolpidem, zopiclone) - GABA-A enhancement; dual orexin receptor antagonists (suvorexant, lemborexant, daridorexant); melatonin agonists (ramelteon, tasimelteon); antihistamines [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: Sleep neurobiology illustrates how a discrete brain state (with distinct EEG, behavioural, and physiological signatures) is generated by mutually inhibitory wake-promoting and sleep-promoting nuclei. The flip-flop switch model and orexin discovery transformed our understanding of narcolepsy. Sleep disorders span common (insomnia, OSA) to rare (narcolepsy) but are major causes of QoL impact and cardiovascular morbidity.

Hints:

1. NREM (3 stages) + REM cycle ~ 90 min.
2. Wake-sleep flip-flop switch; orexin stabilises wake.
3. OSA most common; narcolepsy = orexin loss; RBD predicts synucleinopathy.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names sleep stages and their EEG signatures.
- Identifies wake-promoting (ARAS, orexin) and sleep-promoting (VLPO, adenosine) systems.
- Explains narcolepsy as orexin loss.
- Names OSA features and CPAP as treatment.
- Mentions ≥ 2 functions of sleep (memory, glymphatic clearance, immune).

A Yellow answer covers stages but misses the neurochemistry or specific disorders.

A Red answer treats sleep as passive shutdown.

Common misconceptions:

- "Sleep is just rest.": Correction: It is an active brain state with specific functions: memory consolidation, glymphatic clearance, synaptic homeostasis, immune support.
- "REM is when we dream; NREM is dreamless.": Correction: Most vivid dreaming occurs in REM, but cognitive activity ("sleep mentation") occurs throughout sleep including NREM.

Stem: Describe the neurobiology of consciousness. What are the necessary components, and how do disorders of consciousness inform our understanding?

Model answer: Consciousness is the awareness of self and environment; broadly two components: (1) _Wakefulness_ (level/arousal) - generated by ascending reticular activating system (ARAS) in upper brainstem and thalamus; (2) _Awareness_ (content) - generated by cerebral cortex and corticothalamic loops. Both are necessary; their dissociation produces clinical syndromes. Mechanisms (active research; competing theories): (a) _Global workspace theory_ (Baars, Dehaene) - information becomes conscious when broadcast to widespread cortical networks; (b) _Integrated information theory_ (Tononi) - consciousness reflects integrated information (Φ); (c) _Higher-order theories_ - consciousness requires representations of one's own mental states. Anatomy: thalamocortical system; default mode network (DMN - PCC, mPFC, angular gyrus) active at rest; lateral frontoparietal network active during attention. Disorders of consciousness: (1) _Coma_ - eyes closed, no arousal, no awareness; lasts hours-weeks; reflects bilateral cortical dysfunction or brainstem ARAS damage. Causes: structural (stroke, haemorrhage, mass), metabolic (uraemia, hypoglycaemia, hepatic, hyponatraemia), toxic (drug overdose, alcohol), hypoxic-ischaemic, hypothermia, infection, seizure (post-ictal). Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS, /15) standardises level. (2) _Vegetative state / unresponsive wakefulness syndrome (UWS)_ - eyes open, sleep-wake cycles preserved, no awareness; brainstem intact but cortex dysfunctional. Persistent (> 1 month); permanent (>3 months non-traumatic, >12 months traumatic). (3) _Minimally conscious state (MCS)_ - inconsistent but reproducible signs of awareness (responds to commands, visual pursuit, simple verbalisations). Better prognosis than VS. Distinguishing VS vs MCS difficult; ~ 40% misdiagnosed; functional MRI/EEG paradigms improve detection (Owen tennis-house imagery task). (4) _Locked-in syndrome_ - intact consciousness, paralysis (typically all motor except eye/eyelid movements); pontine lesion (basis pontis stroke from basilar thrombosis); patients can communicate via vertical eye movement and blinks; communicate suffering distinct from coma. Crucial NOT to confuse with VS. (5) _Brainstem death_ - irreversible cessation of brainstem function; legal definition of death in many jurisdictions; tested with: deep coma + no brainstem reflexes (pupils fixed, no corneal, no oculocephalic, no oculovestibular, no gag, no respiratory drive on apnoea test) + known irreversible cause + no confounders (drugs, hypothermia, electrolyte imbalance). (6) _Delirium_ - acute disturbance of attention/awareness with cognitive change; common in hospitalised elderly (~ 20-30%); fluctuating; often reversible if cause treated; high mortality and morbidity; multifactorial (medications - especially anticholinergic, benzodiazepines, opioids; infection, dehydration, environment, sleep disruption); prevention with HELP/hospital sleep protocols, minimising tethering, reorientation. (7) _Anaesthesia and sedation_ - reversible loss of consciousness; mechanism partly via thalamocortical decoupling; intraoperative awareness rare but devastating; depth of anaesthesia monitors (BIS, entropy) approximate. Clinical and ethical implications: (1) Recognising MCS vs VS affects prognosis and ethical decisions; (2) Locked-in syndrome demands recognition (avoid premature withdrawal of care); (3) Brainstem death testing must be rigorous; (4) Delirium prevention/management is high-yield; (5) Surrogate decision-making, advance directives, palliative care are essential when consciousness is lost. Future: brain-computer interfaces for locked-in patients; predictive imaging in disorders of consciousness; pharmacological approaches (zolpidem, amantadine) for severe brain injury [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: Consciousness disorders span the spectrum from coma to delirium, and each reveals different aspects of how the brain produces awareness. Recognising MCS vs VS, locked-in syndrome, and brainstem death has profound clinical and ethical consequences.

Hints:

1. Wakefulness (brainstem) + awareness (cortex).
2. Coma vs VS vs MCS vs locked-in - all clinically distinct.
3. Brainstem death testing is rigorous.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Distinguishes wakefulness from awareness with anatomical correlates.
- Names ≥ 3 disorders of consciousness with distinguishing features.
- Identifies locked-in syndrome and the importance of not missing it.
- Names brainstem death testing components.
- Mentions delirium as common, reversible, prevention-amenable.

A Yellow answer covers some disorders but misses the key distinctions.

A Red answer conflates all disorders of consciousness.

Common misconceptions:

- "Locked-in syndrome is a vegetative state.": Correction: Critical distinction - locked-in patients are _fully conscious_; vegetative patients are not aware. Misdiagnosis is devastating.
- "Coma always means dead brain.": Correction: Coma can be reversible (drug overdose, metabolic) or due to structural injury; brainstem death is the irreversible end-state.

Type: clinical
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 68-year-old presents with progressive memory loss and difficulty with names and tasks over 2 years. Decompose Alzheimer disease.

Model answer: _Alzheimer disease (AD)_ - the most common cause of dementia (~ 60-70% of dementias). Pathophysiology: extracellular β-amyloid (Aβ) plaques (from APP cleavage by β- and γ-secretases; Aβ42 most amyloidogenic) and intracellular neurofibrillary tangles of hyperphosphorylated tau (from microtubule destabilisation); selective neuronal loss in hippocampus, entorhinal cortex, association cortices; cholinergic neuron loss in nucleus basalis of Meynert -> reduced cortical ACh. Genetics: familial early-onset (< 65 years; ~ 5%) - APP, PSEN1, PSEN2 mutations all increase Aβ42. Sporadic late-onset - APOE ε4 allele is the strongest genetic risk factor (3x risk per copy; ~ 50% lifetime risk in ε4/ε4); other risk variants identified by GWAS. Risk factors: age (most important - prevalence doubles every 5 years after 65), family history, APOE ε4, cardiovascular risk factors, traumatic brain injury, low education, mid-life hypertension, diabetes, depression, social isolation. Clinical features: (1) Insidious progressive memory loss (recent more than remote, episodic memory affected first); (2) Word-finding difficulty (anomic aphasia); (3) Visuospatial difficulties (getting lost in familiar places); (4) Executive dysfunction (planning, abstract reasoning, judgement); (5) Behavioural and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD) - agitation, depression, psychosis, apathy, sleep disturbance, wandering - often most distressing for caregivers; (6) Late-stage: complete dependency, mutism, immobility, swallowing difficulties; (7) Death typically from aspiration pneumonia, infection, or comorbidities; median survival 3-10 years from diagnosis. Diagnosis: clinical (NIA-AA criteria); cognitive testing (MMSE, MoCA - sensitive to mild cognitive impairment); MRI (hippocampal/medial temporal atrophy; vs vascular - white matter disease/lacunes; vs frontotemporal - frontal atrophy); CSF biomarkers (low Aβ42, high tau and phospho-tau ratio; AT(N) framework); amyloid PET (florbetapir, florbetaben), tau PET; new blood biomarkers (plasma p-tau217 - emerging). Management: (1) Cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine) - modest symptomatic benefit in mild-moderate AD; side effects bradycardia, GI; (2) NMDA antagonist (memantine) - moderate-severe AD; (3) Disease-modifying therapy (recent landmark): anti-amyloid antibodies _aducanumab_, _lecanemab_, _donanemab_ - clear amyloid plaques; lecanemab (FDA 2023) and donanemab (FDA 2024) modestly slow cognitive decline in early AD; risk of ARIA (amyloid-related imaging abnormalities - oedema, microhaemorrhage); APOE ε4/ε4 highest risk; expensive, infusion-based, restricted use. (4) Manage cardiovascular risk; (5) BPSD - non-pharmacological first (environmental, music, reminiscence); cautious atypical antipsychotics if needed (mortality risk in dementia); SSRIs for depression; (6) Multidisciplinary care - OT, dementia advisor, support for carers, advance care planning, social services; (7) Supportive - hearing aids, glasses (sensory deficits worsen cognition), exercise, social engagement, cognitive stimulation. Prevention: management of mid-life cardiovascular risk factors; physical and cognitive activity; sleep; possibly Mediterranean diet; hearing/vision optimisation. The amyloid hypothesis dominated for decades; recent failures of some anti-amyloid trials and modest success of others have refocused attention on tau, neuroinflammation, vascular health, and combined approaches [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: AD illustrates the convergence of genetic vulnerability, age-related decline, and modifiable risk factors. The recent disease-modifying anti-amyloid antibodies are the first therapies to alter disease course - though benefits are modest, with significant ARIA risk. Mid-life cardiovascular risk and sensory health may be more impactful preventive targets at population level.

Hints:

1. Aβ plaques + tau tangles + hippocampal/cortical atrophy.
2. Cholinesterase inhibitors symptomatic; anti-amyloid antibodies modify.
3. BPSD management often more impactful for QoL.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names AD with the amyloid+tau pathology.
- Identifies APOE ε4 as the major genetic risk factor.
- Names cholinesterase inhibitors and memantine as symptomatic; anti-amyloid antibodies as disease-modifying.
- Mentions ARIA risk with anti-amyloid therapy.
- Discusses BPSD as a major management target.

A Yellow answer covers AD but misses the new therapies or BPSD.

A Red answer treats AD as just memory loss.

Common misconceptions:

- "AD is just memory loss.": Correction: BPSD (agitation, psychosis, apathy, sleep) is often the most distressing aspect for patients and carers; multidisciplinary management is essential.
- "Anti-amyloid antibodies cure AD.": Correction: They modestly slow decline in early AD; do not reverse it; have significant ARIA risk; access remains limited.

Type: integration
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the language network and aphasia syndromes. Localise Broca, Wernicke, conduction, transcortical, and global aphasia.

Model answer: Language is processed by a _perisylvian network_ in the dominant (typically left) hemisphere. Major regions: (1) _Broca area_ - inferior frontal gyrus, BA 44/45 - speech production, syntax; (2) _Wernicke area_ - posterior superior temporal gyrus, BA 22 - language comprehension; (3) _Arcuate fasciculus_ - white matter tract connecting Wernicke to Broca; (4) _Angular gyrus_ - reading, writing, calculation; (5) _Supramarginal gyrus_ - phonological processing; (6) _Insula_ - articulation; (7) Motor cortex face/mouth area - articulation; (8) Heschl gyrus (primary auditory cortex). Vascular: most via dominant MCA (left in 95% right-handers); Broca by superior division MCA, Wernicke by inferior division MCA. Aphasia syndromes (lesion -> deficit): (1) _Broca aphasia_ (expressive/non-fluent) - inferior frontal lesion -> halting effortful agrammatic speech, telegraphic, content words preserved, function words missing; comprehension _relatively preserved_ (especially for simple commands); repetition impaired; _aware of deficit_ (frustrated); often with right hemiparesis (face/arm > leg). "Tan tan tan" classic. (2) _Wernicke aphasia_ (receptive/fluent) - superior temporal lesion -> fluent prosodic but meaningless speech ("word salad", neologisms, paraphasic errors), poor comprehension, poor repetition; _unaware of deficit_ (jargon aphasia); typically no hemiparesis; behavioural disturbance possible. Sometimes mistaken for psychosis. (3) _Conduction aphasia_ - arcuate fasciculus lesion -> fluent speech, good comprehension, but _poor repetition_; phonemic paraphasias; aware and frustrated. (4) _Global aphasia_ - large MCA lesion affecting both Broca and Wernicke -> non-fluent, poor comprehension, poor repetition; severe motor deficit; very poor functional outcome. (5) _Transcortical motor aphasia_ - lesion in dominant frontal lobe anterior to Broca (watershed between MCA and ACA) -> non-fluent, good comprehension, _good repetition_ (intact perisylvian); limited speech initiation; "echolalic". (6) _Transcortical sensory aphasia_ - posterior watershed lesion (MCA-PCA) -> fluent, poor comprehension, _good repetition_. (7) _Transcortical mixed aphasia (isolation of speech area)_ - large watershed lesion -> non-fluent, poor comprehension, _good repetition_ only (echolalia). (8) _Anomic aphasia_ - mild aphasia with only word-finding difficulty; occurs in many lesions; common in early dementia. Other related deficits: (a) _Alexia_ (reading impairment) - alexia without agraphia (PCA infarct of dominant calcarine + splenium of corpus callosum); (b) _Agraphia_ (writing impairment); (c) _Agnosia_ - inability to recognise; (d) _Apraxia_ - inability to perform learned movements despite intact motor; ideomotor (problem with command movement), ideational (sequence), buccofacial. Clinical assessment: (1) Spontaneous speech (fluent vs non-fluent; content); (2) Comprehension (yes/no questions, simple commands, complex commands); (3) Repetition ("no ifs ands or buts"); (4) Naming (objects); (5) Reading; (6) Writing. Recovery: most aphasia improves over months; speech and language therapy is the mainstay; constraint-induced aphasia therapy intensive; melodic intonation therapy (right hemisphere) for non-fluent aphasia. Aphasia is the most disabling stroke complication for many patients - language is so central to identity [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: Aphasia syndromes are among the most rewarding clinical-anatomical correlations in neurology. Each syndrome localises to a specific lesion, and the bedside assessment (fluency, comprehension, repetition) immediately classifies the syndrome. The Wernicke-Lichtheim model, though simplistic, remains the foundation for clinical aphasia evaluation.

Hints:

1. Broca: non-fluent, comprehension preserved, no repetition.
2. Wernicke: fluent, comprehension impaired, no repetition.
3. Conduction: fluent, comprehension good, no repetition.
4. Transcortical: repetition spared (watershed).

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies Broca and Wernicke areas and the arcuate fasciculus.
- Distinguishes ≥ 4 aphasia syndromes by fluency/comprehension/repetition.
- Names alexia without agraphia and its lesion.
- Connects aphasia to MCA stroke patterns.
- Mentions the assessment scheme (spontaneous speech, comprehension, repetition, naming).

A Yellow answer covers Broca and Wernicke but misses the other syndromes.

A Red answer treats all aphasia as one entity.

Common misconceptions:

- "All aphasia is global.": Correction: Specific subtypes (Broca, Wernicke, conduction, transcortical) localise to specific networks and have different prognoses and rehabilitation strategies.
- "Aphasic patients are intellectually impaired.": Correction: Language and intelligence are dissociable; many aphasic patients have preserved cognition but cannot communicate effectively - tragic for them.

Type: integration
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the limbic system and its role in emotion and memory. How do limbic disorders manifest clinically?

Model answer: _Limbic system_ - heterogeneous group of structures involved in emotion, motivation, memory, and autonomic-behavioural integration. Major components: (1) _Hippocampus_ - declarative (episodic, semantic) memory consolidation; spatial navigation; CA1 vulnerable to ischaemia. (2) _Amygdala_ - fear, threat detection, emotional memory, social cognition. Lesion: Klüver-Bucy syndrome (hyperorality, hypersexuality, placidity, visual agnosia, hyperphagia). Stimulation produces fear, autonomic activation. (3) _Cingulate cortex_ - anterior (emotion, motivation, error detection, conflict monitoring); posterior (default mode, autobiographical memory). (4) _Thalamic nuclei_ - anterior thalamic group (Papez circuit; memory). (5) _Hypothalamus_ - autonomic and endocrine coordination, sleep, appetite, sexual behaviour, defence. (6) _Olfactory cortex_ - smell, with strong limbic projections. (7) _Mammillary bodies_ - memory; lesion in Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome. (8) _Fornix_ - white matter tract connecting hippocampus to mammillary bodies and septum. (9) _Septal nuclei_ - reward, pleasure. (10) _Nucleus accumbens / ventral striatum_ - reward processing, addiction. Functions: emotional salience, fear conditioning, threat assessment, attachment, reward, motivation, autobiographical memory, social cognition. Major circuits: (a) _Papez circuit_ (hippocampus -> fornix -> mammillary -> anterior thalamus -> cingulate -> entorhinal -> hippocampus); (b) _Amygdala-prefrontal_ (emotional regulation); (c) _Reward circuit_ (VTA dopamine -> NAc -> PFC). Clinical syndromes: (1) _Anxiety disorders_ - amygdala hyperactivity (PTSD, GAD, panic); SSRI/SNRI/CBT effective. (2) _Depression_ - reduced hippocampal volume, altered amygdala-PFC connectivity, reduced reward responsiveness; SSRIs + CBT, ECT for severe; ketamine emerging. (3) _PTSD_ - re-experiencing, hyperarousal, avoidance; trauma-focused therapy + SSRIs. (4) _Anterograde amnesia_ - bilateral hippocampal damage (HSV encephalitis, anoxic injury, surgery as in patient HM); preserves remote memory but cannot form new episodic memories. (5) _Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome_ - thiamine deficiency in alcoholism; Wernicke (acute) - confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia (CN VI); reversible with IV thiamine. The limbic system is not a unitary system but rather a network of interacting structures whose collective function gives emotion, memory, and motivation [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: The limbic system illustrates how anatomically distributed structures form functional networks for emotion, memory, and motivation. Stem: Discuss the EEG. What does it measure, what are normal patterns, and how do epileptic discharges manifest?

Model answer: _Electroencephalography (EEG)_ - non-invasive recording of summed cortical electrical activity (mainly cortical pyramidal neuron postsynaptic potentials) from scalp electrodes; reflects synchronous activity of millions of neurons. Setup: 19 electrodes (international 10-20 system); each pair gives a derivation (montage - bipolar or referential). Frequency bands: (a) Delta 0.5-4 Hz - slow-wave sleep, pathological in awake (focal lesion, encephalopathy); (b) Theta 4-8 Hz - drowsiness, mild encephalopathy in awake adult; (c) Alpha 8-13 Hz - posterior dominant rhythm in awake relaxed eyes-closed adult; attenuates with eye opening, attention; (d) Beta 13-30 Hz - awake activity, especially with benzodiazepines or barbiturates; (e) Gamma 30-100 Hz - cognitive processing. Normal: alpha posterior with eye closure; mu rhythm centrally with limb rest; beta in frontal regions. Sleep stages: NREM 1 (theta), NREM 2 (sleep spindles, K complexes), NREM 3 (delta), REM (low-amplitude mixed - paradoxical). Activation procedures: hyperventilation (3-5 min - induces slowing, may trigger absence seizures); photic stimulation (flashing lights - photoparoxysmal response in some idiopathic generalised epilepsies); sleep deprivation (sensitises). Epileptic discharges: (1) _Spike_ - sharp transient < 70 ms; (2) _Sharp wave_ 70-200 ms; (3) _Spike-wave complex_ - spike followed by slow wave; classic 3-Hz spike-wave for absence (childhood absence epilepsy); (4) _Polyspike-wave_ - juvenile myoclonic epilepsy; (5) _Hypsarrhythmia_ - chaotic high-amplitude background with multifocal spikes; West syndrome (infantile spasms). Seizure types - by EEG and clinical: (a) Focal seizures - localised onset; aware vs impaired awareness; motor vs non-motor (sensory, autonomic, dyscognitive, emotional); may evolve to bilateral tonic-clonic; (b) Generalised seizures - bilateral synchronous from onset; tonic-clonic, absence, myoclonic, atonic, clonic, tonic. Specific epilepsy syndromes: childhood absence epilepsy (CAE - 3-Hz SW, ethosuximide), juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (JME - polyspike, valproate / levetiracetam / lamotrigine), Dravet syndrome (SCN1A, refractory, special drugs), Lennox-Gastaut (slow SW, multiple seizure types), West syndrome (infantile spasms - hypsarrhythmia, ACTH/vigabatrin/steroids), benign rolandic (centrotemporal spikes, age-limited, often no treatment). Other EEG uses: (a) Encephalopathy (generalised slowing, triphasic waves in metabolic encephalopathy - especially hepatic); (b) Brain death assessment (electrocerebral silence after exclusions); (c) Ischaemia/stroke; (d) Status epilepticus monitoring; (e) Sleep disorders; (f) Anaesthesia depth (BIS, entropy derived from EEG). Limitations: spatial resolution poor (cm scale); deep structures (hippocampus, brainstem) hard to capture; volume conduction; artefacts (muscle, eye, electrode). Magnetoencephalography (MEG) - related modality with better spatial resolution. Intracranial EEG (subdural strips, depth electrodes) for epilepsy surgery localisation [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: EEG is the workhorse of clinical neurophysiology for epilepsy and encephalopathy. The pattern recognition (3-Hz spike-wave for absence; hypsarrhythmia for West; epileptiform discharges for focal epilepsy) directly guides diagnosis and treatment. Newer tools (high-density EEG, source localisation, intracranial recording) extend its reach.

Hints:

1. Frequency bands: delta, theta, alpha, beta, gamma.
2. Awake adult: posterior alpha, attenuates with eyes open.
3. Epileptic discharges: spikes, sharp waves, spike-wave complexes.

Stem: Describe focal seizure semiology and classification. How does the clinical pattern localise the seizure focus?

Model answer: Focal (formerly partial) seizures arise from a localised cortical region; semiology reflects the function of that region. Classification (ILAE 2017): (1) Onset - focal vs generalised vs unknown; (2) Awareness - focal aware (FAS, formerly simple partial) vs focal impaired awareness (FIAS, formerly complex partial); (3) Motor or non-motor onset; (4) Whether focal evolves to bilateral tonic-clonic. Focal aware seizures (FAS): patient retains awareness; reflects activity in the focus before propagation. Subtypes by region: (1) _Frontal lobe_ - motor (Jacksonian march - somatotopic spread; e.g., starts in face/thumb spreads to arm); supplementary motor (proximal asymmetric posturing - "fencer's posture"); frontal eye field (forced gaze deviation contralateral); orbitofrontal (autonomic, behavioural); medial frontal (urinary/genital sensations). (2) _Temporal lobe_ - mesial temporal (most common focal epilepsy in adults) - olfactory hallucinations (uncinate seizures - often unpleasant), gustatory, fear, déjà vu, jamais vu, depersonalisation, autonomic (epigastric rising sensation), oroalimentary automatisms (lip smacking, chewing), upper limb dystonic posturing contralateral; lateral temporal - auditory hallucinations, vertigo. Mesial temporal sclerosis on MRI; Ammon horn sclerosis; often refractory; surgically curable. (3) _Parietal lobe_ - somatosensory (numbness, paraesthesiae, electric sensation, march - somatotopic); body schema disturbance; visual phenomena (lateral); apraxia. (4) _Occipital lobe_ - visual phenomena: flashes, colours, scintillations (simple), formed objects (more complex); contralateral hemianopia/illusions; eye movements; nausea; postictal blindness or migraine. (5) _Insula_ - autonomic, somatosensory, viscerosensory; can mimic temporal. Focal impaired awareness seizures (FIAS): involve bilateral or larger network -> awareness compromised; automatisms (oral, manual); often arise from temporal lobe; "complex partial" in older terminology. Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic (BTC): focal seizure spreads bilaterally with classic generalised tonic-clonic phase. Postictal: confusion, drowsiness, headache, focal weakness (Todd paralysis - temporary post-ictal hemiparesis localising to focus); aphasia if dominant temporal; psychotic features. Diagnostic workup: (1) MRI brain - structural lesion (mesial temporal sclerosis, malformation of cortical development, cavernoma, tumour); (2) EEG - interictal epileptiform discharges localising; ictal recording (video-EEG if surgical evaluation); (3) Functional imaging (PET interictal hypometabolism; SPECT ictal hyperperfusion); (4) Magnetoencephalography for localisation; (5) Neuropsychological testing for memory/language lateralisation. Treatment: (1) Antiepileptic drugs - first-line for focal epilepsy: lamotrigine, levetiracetam, lacosamide, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, eslicarbazepine; many others; choice based on patient factors (sex, age, comorbidities); (2) Surgery for refractory focal epilepsy: most effective for mesial temporal sclerosis - selective amygdalohippocampectomy or temporal lobectomy; ~ 60-70% become seizure-free; outcomes worse for extratemporal; (3) Vagal nerve stimulation, responsive neurostimulation, deep brain stimulation for refractory; (4) Ketogenic diet (selected cases); (5) Cannabidiol (Dravet, Lennox-Gastaut). Status epilepticus: prolonged seizure (> 5 min) or repeated without recovery; medical emergency - benzodiazepines first-line (lorazepam IV, diazepam, midazolam), then second-line (phenytoin/fosphenytoin, valproate, levetiracetam), then refractory protocols (induced anaesthesia with midazolam, propofol, ketamine, pentobarbital infusions); EEG monitoring; treat underlying cause [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: Focal seizures localise beautifully to cortical function. Each region produces characteristic semiology; pattern recognition guides anatomy. Mesial temporal sclerosis is the prototype surgically remediable epilepsy. Status epilepticus is a true emergency requiring stepwise pharmacological intervention.

2. Mesial temporal sclerosis - most common, often surgically curable.
3. Status epilepticus - benzodiazepines first, then anticonvulsants.

Stem: Describe the neurobiology of major depressive disorder. How do current and emerging treatments work?

Model answer: _Major depressive disorder (MDD)_ - common, recurrent, debilitating illness; lifetime prevalence ~ 15-20%; suicide a major cause of mortality. Diagnosis (DSM-5): ≥ 5 symptoms for ≥ 2 weeks, including depressed mood OR anhedonia: weight/appetite change, sleep disturbance, psychomotor agitation/retardation, fatigue, worthlessness/guilt, concentration deficit, suicidal ideation. Pathophysiology (multifactorial): (1) _Monoamine hypothesis_ (classic) - reduced synaptic 5-HT, NE, DA in mood-relevant circuits; supported by efficacy of SSRIs and noradrenergic antidepressants. Limitations: drugs work over weeks despite immediate synaptic effect; treatment-resistant patients exist. (2) _Neuroplasticity hypothesis_ - reduced BDNF, hippocampal neurogenesis, dendritic spine density in depressed brains; antidepressants enhance neuroplasticity; ketamine acts rapidly via this pathway. (3) _Neuroinflammation_ - elevated cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, CRP) in some patients; sickness-behaviour overlap. (4) _HPA axis hyperactivity_ - elevated cortisol; abnormal dexamethasone suppression; chronic stress contribution. (5) _Genetic + environmental_ - heritability ~ 30-40%; gene-environment interactions (childhood adversity); polygenic risk. Imaging: reduced hippocampal volume, increased amygdala reactivity, altered prefrontal-limbic connectivity, reduced reward responsiveness in ventral striatum. Treatment hierarchy: (1) _Mild-moderate_ - psychotherapy first-line (CBT, IPT, behavioural activation); SSRI alternative; mindfulness-based cognitive therapy for relapse prevention. (2) _Moderate-severe_ - SSRI (sertraline, fluoxetine, escitalopram), SNRI (venlafaxine, duloxetine), atypical (mirtazapine - improves sleep/appetite via 5-HT2/H1; bupropion - DA/NE reuptake, useful in fatigue/low motivation, smoking cessation, no sexual side effects; vortioxetine - multimodal); 4-6 weeks for full effect; treat for ≥ 6 months after remission for first episode, longer for recurrent. (3) _Treatment-resistant depression_ (failure of ≥ 2 adequate antidepressant trials) - combination/augmentation: lithium, second antipsychotic (aripiprazole, quetiapine, olanzapine), thyroid hormone, SAMe, omega-3, exercise. (4) _Severe with psychosis or suicidality_ - antidepressant + antipsychotic; ECT highly effective for severe melancholic and psychotic depression. (5) _Rapid-acting agents_ - ketamine (NMDA antagonist; IV sub-anaesthetic; rapid onset hours; effects 1-2 weeks); esketamine (intranasal; FDA-approved for TRD); psychedelic-assisted therapy (psilocybin, MDMA - in late-stage trials). (6) _Brain stimulation_ - ECT (gold standard for severe, refractory, psychotic, pregnant); rTMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation - left DLPFC; effective for moderate); vagus nerve stimulation (chronic refractory); deep brain stimulation (subcallosal cingulate or NAc - investigational). (7) _Lifestyle_ - exercise (effect size approaching pharmacotherapy in mild-moderate), sleep hygiene, social engagement, light therapy (especially seasonal affective). (8) _Psychotherapy_ - CBT, IPT, mindfulness-based therapy; combined with medication superior to either alone in moderate-severe. Suicide prevention: assess in every depressed patient; means restriction; safety planning; crisis lines; pharmacotherapy (lithium reduces suicide; some evidence for clozapine in schizophrenia); rapid-acting agents (ketamine, ECT) for acute crisis. Prognosis: ~ 50% remit with first treatment; ~ 50% recurrence within 5 years after first episode; recurrence risk increases with each episode. Subtypes: melancholic (severe, anhedonic, response to ECT), atypical (mood reactivity, hyperphagia, hypersomnia), seasonal, postpartum, with psychosis [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: MDD is one of the most common and disabling conditions in medicine. The neurobiology has expanded from monoamine deficiency to encompass plasticity, inflammation, HPA axis, and circuit dysfunction. Rapid-acting agents (ketamine, ECT) and emerging psychedelics challenge the traditional 6-week-onset model and offer hope for treatment-resistant patients.

Stem: Describe the neurobiology and treatment of schizophrenia. How does the dopamine hypothesis fit modern understanding?

Model answer: _Schizophrenia_ - chronic psychotic disorder; ~ 1% lifetime prevalence; onset typically late adolescence/early adulthood; substantial morbidity and mortality (life expectancy reduced ~ 15-20 years from cardiovascular, suicide, substance use). Diagnosis (DSM-5): ≥ 2 symptoms for ≥ 1 month, with at least one of: delusions, hallucinations, disorganised speech; plus negative symptoms or grossly disorganised behaviour; functional decline; ≥ 6 months total. Symptom dimensions: (1) _Positive_ - hallucinations (auditory most common - voices in third person, commenting, command), delusions (persecutory, grandiose, of reference, thought insertion/withdrawal/broadcasting), thought disorder (loose associations, tangentiality, neologisms), bizarre behaviour. (2) _Negative_ - blunted affect, alogia, avolition, anhedonia, asociality. (3) _Cognitive_ - impaired attention, working memory, executive function, social cognition. Pathophysiology (multifactorial): (1) _Dopamine hypothesis_ (classic) - excess dopamine in mesolimbic pathway -> positive symptoms (DA blockade reduces); reduced dopamine in mesocortical pathway -> negative symptoms; supported by D2 antagonism efficacy and amphetamine-induced psychosis. (2) _Glutamate (NMDA) hypothesis_ - NMDA receptor hypofunction; PCP/ketamine (NMDA antagonists) produce schizophrenia-like symptoms; GRIN gene variants; explains negative and cognitive symptoms better than DA. (3) _Neurodevelopmental_ - dysmaturation of brain (synaptic pruning excessive in adolescence); risk factors include obstetric complications, prenatal infections, advanced paternal age. (4) _Genetic_ - heritability ~ 80%; polygenic - hundreds of common variants of small effect, plus rare large-effect CNVs (22q11.2 deletion - DiGeorge - 25-30% develop schizophrenia); SETD1A, GRIN2A among emerging risk genes. (5) _Inflammation_ - elevated cytokines, complement (C4 - synaptic pruning); some autoimmune cases (anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis can mimic). (6) _Circuit dysfunction_ - prefrontal hypofrontality, hippocampal dysfunction, default mode disruption. Imaging: enlarged ventricles, reduced grey matter (especially frontal/temporal), altered connectivity. Treatment: (1) _Antipsychotics_ (D2 receptor antagonists or partial agonists; varying degrees of 5-HT2A, M, H1, alpha-1 antagonism): (a) _First-generation/typical_ (haloperidol, chlorpromazine) - effective for positive symptoms; high EPS (parkinsonism, dystonia, akathisia, tardive dyskinesia); raised prolactin. (b) _Second-generation/atypical_ (risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, ziprasidone, aripiprazole, paliperidone, lurasidone, brexpiprazole, cariprazine) - 5-HT2A + D2 antagonism; less EPS; metabolic side effects (weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidaemia - especially olanzapine, clozapine, quetiapine); cardiovascular risk. (c) _Clozapine_ - super-atypical; highly effective for treatment-resistant schizophrenia; reduces suicide; risk of agranulocytosis (mandatory monitoring), myocarditis, seizures, severe constipation, weight gain, sialorrhoea; under-used relative to evidence. (d) _Long-acting injectables_ (LAI) - paliperidone, risperidone, aripiprazole, olanzapine; improve adherence in some. (2) _Psychosocial_ - assertive community treatment, cognitive remediation, supported employment, family interventions, CBT for psychosis, social skills training, smoking cessation. (3) _Coordinated specialty care_ (RAISE, EASA models) - first-episode programmes - improve long-term outcomes. (4) _Physical health_ - aggressive cardiovascular risk management; ~ 60% of mortality is cardiovascular; smoking ~ 70%. (5) _Cariprazine_ and _lumateperone_ - newer agents with novel mechanisms (D3 partial agonist; 5-HT2A inverse agonist + low-affinity D2). (6) Future: TAAR1 agonists (ulotaront), KarXT (xanomeline + trospium - M1/M4 muscarinic; FDA submission 2024), genetic precision medicine. Prognosis: ~ 25% recover, ~ 50% intermittent course, ~ 25% chronic disability; outcomes better in low-income countries (multifactorial). Recovery model emphasises functional outcomes beyond symptoms [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: The dopamine hypothesis launched modern psychiatry but is incomplete. Modern models integrate dopamine, glutamate, neurodevelopmental, genetic, and immune mechanisms. Clozapine remains the gold-standard for treatment-resistant disease but is under-utilised. Coordinated specialty care for first-episode psychosis substantially improves trajectories.

1. Positive (hallucinations, delusions), negative (avolition, blunted affect), cognitive symptoms.
2. Dopamine + glutamate + neurodevelopmental + genetic + immune contributions.
3. Antipsychotics + psychosocial + cardiovascular care.

Stem: Describe headache classification. How are migraine, tension-type, and cluster headache distinguished and managed?

Model answer: Headache classification (ICHD-3): _primary_ (migraine, tension-type, cluster, other primary) vs _secondary_ (caused by underlying disorder - meningitis, SAH, tumour, GCA, dissection, idiopathic intracranial hypertension, medication overuse, post-traumatic). Phases: prodrome (hours-days; mood, food cravings, neck pain), aura (~ 30%; visual scintillations, scotoma; sensory; rarely motor or speech; lasts < 60 min), headache (4-72h; unilateral, throbbing, moderate-severe, worsened by activity, photophobia, phonophobia, nausea/vomiting), postdrome ("migraine hangover"). Management: simple analgesia; preventive amitriptyline if frequent; lifestyle and physical therapy. (3) _Cluster headache_ - severe unilateral periorbital pain with autonomic features (lacrimation, conjunctival injection, ptosis, miosis, nasal congestion - same side as pain; "trigeminal autonomic cephalalgia"). Comprehensive headache history: "OPQRST" - onset, palliating/provoking, quality, region/radiation, severity, time course [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Distinguishing primary from secondary is critical (red flags); within primary, migraine vs tension vs cluster have specific clinical patterns and treatments. CGRP-targeting therapies are revolutionising migraine management. Cluster headache is the "suicide headache" - severe but treatable with oxygen and sumatriptan.

3. Cluster: severe periorbital + ipsilateral autonomic features.

Stem: Discuss multiple sclerosis. What is the pathophysiology, and how have treatments evolved?

Model answer: _Multiple sclerosis (MS)_ - chronic demyelinating disease of the CNS; T-cell-mediated autoimmunity targeting myelin; characterised by lesions disseminated in space and time. Epidemiology: peak onset 20-40 years; F:M 2-3:1; latitude effect (more common in higher latitudes; Vitamin D and EBV implicated); HLA-DRB1*15:01 strongest genetic risk; childhood EBV infection is a strong environmental risk factor (recent population data: EBV infection 32-fold increased risk of MS). Subtypes: (a) *Clinically isolated syndrome (CIS)* - first attack; some convert to MS; (b) *Relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS)* - 85% at onset; relapses with remission; (c) *Secondary progressive MS (SPMS)* - RRMS evolves to gradual progression after years; (d) *Primary progressive MS (PPMS)* - 10-15%; gradual progression from onset, no clear relapses; older age, M = F. Clinical features: (1) Optic neuritis (often first; pain on eye movement, vision loss, RAPD); (2) Sensory disturbances (paraesthesiae, numbness); (3) Motor weakness (UMN signs, often legs); (4) Cerebellar (ataxia, intention tremor, dysarthria); (5) Brainstem (diplopia from internuclear ophthalmoplegia - bilateral suggests MS in young person; vertigo); (6) Spinal cord (transverse myelitis, Lhermitte sign - electric shock down spine on neck flexion, bladder/bowel dysfunction); (7) Cognitive (information processing speed, executive function); (8) Fatigue (~ 80%); (9) Heat intolerance (Uhthoff phenomenon - symptoms worsen with heat). Diagnosis (McDonald criteria 2017): clinical + MRI + CSF; MRI shows T2/FLAIR lesions (oval, periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord; "Dawson fingers"); CSF oligoclonal bands (positive in ~ 95%, supportive); aquaporin-4 IgG (NMOSD - distinct disease) and MOG IgG (MOGAD - distinct) must be excluded. Management has been transformed: (1) *Acute relapse* - high-dose IV methylprednisolone (1 g/day x 3-5 days); plasma exchange for refractory; (2) *Disease-modifying therapy (DMT)* - large arsenal: (a) *Injectable* - interferon-β (Avonex, Rebif, Betaseron), glatiramer acetate (immunomodulator); modest efficacy, well-tolerated; (b) *Oral* - dimethyl fumarate, teriflunomide, fingolimod (S1P modulator), siponimod, ozanimod, ponesimod, cladribine; (c) *Infusion biologics* - natalizumab (anti-α4 integrin; very effective; PML risk in JC virus positive patients), alemtuzumab (anti-CD52; highly effective; autoimmune sequelae), ocrelizumab (anti-CD20 B-cell depletion; first DMT proven effective in *PPMS\*), ofatumumab (anti-CD20, subcutaneous), rituximab (off-label CD20). Treatment paradigm shifted toward early high-efficacy therapy (HET) over escalation - earlier intervention reduces long-term disability. (3) Symptomatic - fatigue (modafinil, amantadine; cognitive behavioural therapy; exercise), spasticity (baclofen, gabapentin, tizanidine; intrathecal baclofen, BTX), bladder (antimuscarinics, intermittent catheterisation), pain (gabapentinoids, TCAs), cognition (rehabilitation), depression (SSRIs); fampridine (4-aminopyridine) for walking. (4) Multidisciplinary care - neurologist, MS nurse, OT, PT, SLT, neuropsychology, urology. (5) Lifestyle - vitamin D, smoking cessation, exercise (improves outcomes), comorbidity management. (6) Pregnancy - DMTs require careful planning (most stopped pre-conception); relapse risk falls in pregnancy, rises post-partum. Prognosis: highly variable; with modern HET, many patients have minimal disability progression. NMOSD (anti-AQP4) and MOGAD are distinct demyelinating diseases requiring different treatment (e.g., eculizumab, satralizumab, inebilizumab for NMOSD) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: MS is one of the most active fields in neurology. The DMT landscape has expanded dramatically; the EBV link reframes pathogenesis; high-efficacy early therapy is the new paradigm. Distinguishing MS from NMOSD and MOGAD has become essential because treatments differ.

Hints:

1. Demyelinating + axonal injury; CD4 Th17 + CD8 + B cell.
2. Lesions disseminated in space and time on MRI + CSF OCB.
3. DMT arsenal expanded; early high-efficacy therapy paradigm.

Stem: Describe stroke pathophysiology and the principles of acute stroke management. How has hyperacute care changed outcomes?

Model answer: _Stroke_ - acute neurological deficit due to cerebrovascular event; ~ 87% ischaemic, ~ 10% intracerebral haemorrhage (ICH), ~ 3% subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH). Globally a leading cause of death and disability. Ischaemic stroke pathophysiology: (1) Sudden reduction in cerebral blood flow to a vascular territory; (2) Core (CBF < 10 mL/100 g/min) - infarcts within minutes; (3) Penumbra (CBF 10-20) - electrically silent but viable tissue; (4) Cellular cascade - ATP depletion -> Na/K ATPase failure -> cellular swelling -> glutamate release -> NMDA receptor activation -> Ca²⁺ overload -> mitochondrial failure -> apoptosis/necrosis. Stroke aetiology (TOAST classification): (a) _Large-artery atherosclerosis_ (carotid, vertebrobasilar, intracranial) - 30%; (b) _Cardioembolic_ - 25% (AF most common; also valvular, MI, cardiomyopathy, PFO/septal aneurysm); (c) _Small-vessel disease (lacunar)_ - 20% (lipohyalinosis, microatheroma in penetrators); hypertension, diabetes; (d) _Cryptogenic_ - 25%; ~ 30% may be embolic ("ESUS"); (e) _Other determined_ (5%) - dissection (carotid, vertebral), vasculitis, hypercoagulable, hypoperfusion, illicit drugs (cocaine), genetic (CADASIL, Fabry, MELAS). Common syndromes: (i) MCA - contralateral hemiparesis (face, arm > leg), hemisensory loss, hemianopia, aphasia (dominant), neglect (non-dominant); (ii) ACA - contralateral leg weakness, urinary incontinence, frontal cognitive features; (iii) PCA - contralateral homonymous hemianopia (often macula-spared), alexia without agraphia (dominant); (iv) Lacunar - pure motor, pure sensory, ataxic hemiparesis, dysarthria-clumsy hand, sensorimotor stroke (no cortical signs); (v) Brainstem - cranial nerve + contralateral long tract, crossed signs (Wallenberg, Weber, Millard-Gubler, Foville, basis pontis); (vi) Cerebellar - ataxia, vertigo, nausea, headache; (vii) Watershed - between major territories; "man-in-the-barrel" with bilateral arm weakness from ACA-MCA watershed. Acute management: _Time is brain_ (~ 1.9 million neurons lost per minute). (1) _Initial assessment_ - ABC, glucose (rule out hypoglycaemia mimicker), NIHSS (severity); call stroke team. (2) _Imaging_ - non-contrast CT (rule out haemorrhage); CT angiography (large vessel occlusion); CT perfusion or DWI/PWI MRI (core/penumbra mismatch for late-window thrombectomy). (3) _Reperfusion therapy_: (a) IV alteplase (tPA) - eligible if < 4.5 h, no contraindications (recent surgery, anticoagulation, severe HTN, large infarct, intracranial haemorrhage); (b) Tenecteplase - alternative to alteplase, single bolus; (c) Mechanical thrombectomy - large vessel occlusion (proximal MCA, ICA, basilar) - within 6 h standard; up to 24 h with imaging mismatch (DAWN, DEFUSE 3 trials) - has revolutionised stroke care. (4) _Blood pressure management_ - permissive hypertension (allow SBP < 220 in non-thrombolysed; < 185/110 if thrombolysing); avoid hypotension. (5) _Glucose control_ (75-180 mg/dL); (6) _Temperature control_ (treat hyperthermia). (7) _Antithrombotics_ - aspirin within 24-48 h (after exclusion of haemorrhage); dual antiplatelet for minor stroke/TIA (CHANCE, POINT trials - 21 days); long-term anticoagulation if AF (DOACs preferred in non-valvular). (8) _Statin_ high-dose (LDL < 70). (9) _Stroke unit care_ - reduces mortality; multidisciplinary; prevent complications (DVT, infection, dysphagia/aspiration, decubitus). (10) Carotid endarterectomy (CEA) or stenting (CAS) for symptomatic high-grade carotid stenosis. (11) Rehabilitation - early, intensive, multidisciplinary. ICH management: (1) BP control (target SBP 140-160 in INTERACT2; lower in haemorrhagic vs ischaemic); (2) Reverse anticoagulation (PCC, vitamin K, idarucizumab/andexanet); (3) Surgical evacuation in selected (cerebellar > 3 cm, large lobar with deterioration, hydrocephalus); (4) ICP management; (5) Treat seizures. SAH management: (1) Aneurysm clipping or coiling within 24-72 h; (2) Nimodipine for vasospasm prevention; (3) Triple-H therapy (now refined); (4) ICP management; (5) Hydrocephalus (EVD). Secondary prevention (after any stroke): risk factor management, antiplatelet/anticoagulation as appropriate, statin, lifestyle, smoking cessation [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: Stroke management has been transformed - thrombolysis (1995) and thrombectomy (2015 trials) have moved many patients from devastating disability to good outcomes. Early systems (telestroke networks, mobile stroke units, hub-and-spoke models) directly translate this physiology into clinical outcomes. Secondary prevention is essential.

Hints:

1. Time is brain - reperfusion within hours.
2. tPA ≤ 4.5 h; thrombectomy ≤ 6-24 h with imaging.
3. Aspirin + statin + RF management for secondary prevention.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names ischaemic vs haemorrhagic stroke.
- Identifies the penumbra concept.
- Distinguishes major vascular syndromes (MCA, ACA, PCA, lacunar, brainstem).
- Names tPA and thrombectomy with their windows.
- Mentions secondary prevention components.

A Yellow answer covers stroke but misses the reperfusion windows or syndromes.

A Red answer treats all strokes the same.

Common misconceptions:

- "Aggressive BP lowering helps acute stroke.": Correction: Acute ischaemic stroke - permissive hypertension preserves penumbra; ICH - modest control. Aggressive lowering risks ischaemia.
- "Thrombectomy is only for first 6 h.": Correction: Late-window (up to 24 h) thrombectomy is now established for selected patients with imaging mismatch (DAWN, DEFUSE 3).

Stem: Describe Parkinson disease motor and non-motor features and treatment in depth.

Model answer: _Parkinson disease (PD)_ - second most common neurodegenerative disorder. Pathology: alpha-synuclein aggregation (Lewy bodies, neurites) -> selective loss of dopaminergic SNc neurons; widespread Lewy pathology in autonomic, olfactory, brainstem, cortex (Braak staging starts in olfactory bulb and dorsal motor nucleus of vagus, ascending). Genetics: ~ 10-15% familial; LRRK2 (most common AD), PARK7/DJ-1, PINK1, parkin (PARK2 - young-onset AR), GBA (most common risk variant; carriers have 5-fold risk and faster progression), SNCA. Risk factors: age, family history, environmental (pesticides, MPTP, well water), male sex, traumatic brain injury. Protective: tobacco use (caffeine), exercise. Cardinal motor features: _TRAP_ - Tremor (resting, 4-6 Hz, pill-rolling, asymmetric), Rigidity (cogwheel, lead-pipe), Akinesia/bradykinesia, Postural instability (later). Plus: hypomimia, hypophonia, micrographia, festinating gait, freezing, stooped posture, reduced arm swing, retropulsion. Non-motor features (often precede motor by years - prodromal PD): (1) Olfactory dysfunction - very early; (2) REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD) - very strong predictor of PD/DLB/MSA; (3) Constipation; (4) Depression/anxiety; (5) Autonomic - orthostatic hypotension, urinary frequency, erectile dysfunction, sweating; (6) Cognitive impairment; PD dementia (PDD) develops in 30-50% within 10 years (different from DLB by timing of cognitive vs motor symptoms - DLB cognitive symptoms within 1 year of motor); (7) Sleep disturbance; (8) Pain (musculoskeletal, dystonic, central). Diagnosis: clinical (UK PDS Brain Bank or MDS criteria); supportive features (asymmetric, L-DOPA response, no atypical features); DAT-SPECT shows reduced striatal dopamine transporter; imaging excludes structural lesions; differential from atypical parkinsonism (PSP, MSA, CBD), drug-induced, vascular, essential tremor (action vs rest tremor). Treatment - motor symptoms: (1) _Symptomatic medications_: (a) _Carbidopa/levodopa_ (most effective; 5-10 year window before motor fluctuations and dyskinesias develop with progressive disease); (b) _Dopamine agonists_ (pramipexole, ropinirole, rotigotine patch) - useful in younger patients; risks impulse control disorders (gambling, hypersexuality, eating); (c) _MAO-B inhibitors_ (selegiline, rasagiline, safinamide) - mild benefit; rasagiline may have neuroprotective effect (debated); (d) _COMT inhibitors_ (entacapone, opicapone) - prolong L-DOPA effect, reduce off-time; (e) _Amantadine_ - for dyskinesias and tremor; (f) _Anticholinergics_ (trihexyphenidyl) - tremor in young patients; cognitive side effects in elderly. (2) _Advanced disease_: (a) Continuous dopaminergic delivery - apomorphine subcutaneous infusion or pen; intrajejunal L-DOPA gel (Duodopa); (b) _Deep brain stimulation_ (DBS) - subthalamic nucleus or GPi; excellent for refractory motor fluctuations and dyskinesias in well-selected patients (good L-DOPA response, no significant cognitive impairment, no significant axial features); (c) Focused ultrasound thalamotomy (tremor); pallidotomy (rare); (d) Continuous subcutaneous foslevodopa (recently approved). Non-motor management: (1) RBD - melatonin (preferred) or clonazepam; (2) Orthostatic hypotension - non-pharmacological + fludrocortisone, midodrine, droxidopa; (3) Constipation - lifestyle, polyethylene glycol; (4) Depression - SSRIs, SNRIs, mirtazapine; (5) Cognitive impairment / PDD - cholinesterase inhibitors (rivastigmine FDA-approved); (6) Hallucinations / psychosis - reduce dopaminergic medications, pimavanserin (5-HT2A inverse agonist), quetiapine (low dose, avoid haloperidol/risperidone); (7) Pain management; (8) Gait freezing - cueing, behaviour therapy, focused PT; (9) Speech and swallowing - SLT, LSVT-LOUD; (10) Exercise - improves outcomes, possibly disease-modifying. Disease-modifying therapy is unmet need; trials targeting alpha-synuclein (immunotherapy - prasinezumab, cinpanemab, others - mixed results), GBA pathway, kinase inhibitors. Prognosis: progressive disability over decades; survival reduced (related to comorbidities, falls, infections). Multidisciplinary care improves outcomes [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: PD is the prototype movement disorder and one of the most studied neurodegenerative diseases. Modern management is multifaceted - motor symptoms (L-DOPA core; DBS for advanced), non-motor symptoms (often more disabling than motor), and the search for disease modification. Pre-motor features (anosmia, RBD, constipation) suggest opportunities for early intervention.

Hints:

1. TRAP motor features + many non-motor features.
2. L-DOPA + carbidopa core symptomatic; DBS for advanced.
3. Non-motor (RBD, autonomic, depression) often most disabling.

Stem: Describe the neurobiology of addiction. How do mesolimbic dopamine and prefrontal control interact in development and treatment of substance use disorders?

Model answer: _Addiction_ (substance use disorder) - chronic, relapsing brain disorder characterised by compulsive substance use despite harm. Neurobiology: (1) _Mesolimbic dopamine reward pathway_ - VTA dopamine projections to nucleus accumbens (NAc, ventral striatum), prefrontal cortex, amygdala, hippocampus. All addictive drugs increase NAc dopamine acutely (directly or indirectly): cocaine/amphetamines (block reuptake or trigger release); opioids (disinhibit VTA via μ receptors on GABA interneurons); nicotine (nAChR on VTA neurons); ethanol (multiple mechanisms); cannabis (CB1); benzodiazepines (GABA-A); psychedelics (5-HT2A but reward less); food, sex, gambling also activate this circuit. (2) _Tolerance and physical dependence_ - neuroadaptation; receptor downregulation, intracellular signalling changes; withdrawal on cessation due to system imbalance. (3) _Sensitisation_ - paradoxically increased responses (e.g., cue-induced craving) develop with repeated use. (4) _Stress and craving_ - extended amygdala (CeA, BNST), HPA axis; CRF signalling drives stress-induced relapse. (5) _Habit/compulsion_ - repeated use shifts control from ventral (NAc, reward) to dorsal striatum (habit), and from prefrontal (deliberative) to subcortical (automatic). (6) _Prefrontal hypofunction_ - reduced cognitive control, impaired decision-making, impulsivity. Stages of addiction (Koob and Volkow): (a) Binge/intoxication (positive reinforcement; ventral striatum, dopamine); (b) Withdrawal/negative affect (negative reinforcement; extended amygdala, CRF); (c) Preoccupation/anticipation (PFC dysfunction, executive). Risk factors: genetics (heritability ~ 50%), age (adolescence vulnerability), psychiatric comorbidity, trauma/adverse childhood experiences, social environment. Specific substance use disorders: (1) _Alcohol_ - GABA-A enhancer + NMDA antagonist + opioid system + dopamine; chronic effects (Wernicke-Korsakoff, peripheral neuropathy, cardiomyopathy, cirrhosis, withdrawal seizures, delirium tremens). Treatments: psychosocial (AA, CBT); naltrexone (μ antagonist; reduces reward), acamprosate (modulates glutamate), disulfiram (aldehyde dehydrogenase inhibitor; aversive). (2) _Opioids_ - μ receptor agonism; high overdose risk; pharmacology: methadone (full μ agonist; long half-life), buprenorphine (partial agonist; ceiling effect on respiratory depression; combination with naloxone), naltrexone (μ antagonist; long-acting injectable); MOUD (medications for opioid use disorder) reduces mortality. Naloxone for overdose reversal (intranasal, IM); harm reduction (needle exchange, supervised consumption). Fentanyl crisis - synthetic opioid 50-100x more potent than morphine. (3) _Tobacco_ - nicotine on α4β2 nAChR -> VTA dopamine; treatment: NRT, varenicline (partial agonist of α4β2 - high efficacy), bupropion (DA/NE reuptake inhibitor; helps cravings); cessation reduces all-cause mortality. (4) _Stimulants_ (cocaine, amphetamines, methamphetamine) - reuptake inhibition + release; no FDA-approved pharmacotherapy; psychosocial mainstay; emerging contingency management. (5) _Cannabis_ - CB1 agonism; cannabis use disorder underrecognised; CBT; no approved pharmacotherapy. (6) _Benzodiazepines_ - dependence common with chronic use; tapering essential; severe withdrawal (seizures); flumazenil for overdose (caution in chronic use). Treatment principles: (1) Chronic disease model - long-term care like other chronic illnesses; relapse not failure; (2) Pharmacotherapy + psychosocial; (3) Treat psychiatric comorbidity; (4) Harm reduction; (5) Family/community support; (6) Address social determinants. Modern advances: psychedelic-assisted therapy (psilocybin for tobacco/alcohol/depression - phase 3), digital therapeutics, deep brain stimulation (refractory cases - investigational), GLP-1 agonists (early signal of reduced alcohol/nicotine use). The opioid epidemic has reframed addiction as a public health crisis; medication for OUD substantially reduces mortality but is underused. Stigma remains a barrier to care [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: Addiction is a brain disease with social and behavioural dimensions. The neurobiology of dopamine reward, withdrawal, and prefrontal control explains the cycle of compulsive use. MOUD, harm reduction, and psychosocial support form the modern care model. The opioid crisis shows the consequences of treating addiction as a moral failing rather than a medical condition.

1. Mesolimbic DA reward + extended amygdala stress + PFC control.
2. Three stages: binge, withdrawal, preoccupation.
3. MOUD (methadone, buprenorphine, naltrexone) reduces mortality.

Stem: Describe traumatic brain injury (TBI). What are the primary and secondary injury mechanisms, and how is severe TBI managed?

Model answer: _Traumatic brain injury (TBI)_ - acute insult to the brain from external mechanical force; major cause of death and disability globally. Severity classified by GCS: mild (13-15; concussion), moderate (9-12), severe (3-8). Mechanisms: (1) Closed head injury - blunt trauma (motor vehicle, fall, assault, sport); (2) Penetrating - bullet, knife. Primary injury (occurs at moment of impact): (a) _Focal lesions_ - contusions (especially frontal/temporal poles - coup-contrecoup), epidural haematoma (arterial - middle meningeal artery; biconvex on CT, lucid interval), subdural haematoma (venous - bridging veins; crescent on CT), intracerebral haemorrhage, traumatic SAH; (b) _Diffuse axonal injury (DAI)_ - shearing forces from rotational acceleration -> stretching/disruption of axons especially at grey-white interface and corpus callosum; severity correlates with consciousness duration; MRI shows multifocal hyperintensities. Secondary injury (develops over hours-days, potentially preventable): (a) _Hypoxia, hypotension_ - additive damage; aim PaO2 > 60, MAP > 80; (b) _Cerebral oedema_ - cytotoxic + vasogenic; raises ICP; (c) _Raised ICP_ (> 20-22 mm Hg) - reduces CPP, herniation risk; (d) _Glutamate excitotoxicity_ - massive release; NMDA receptor activation; Ca²⁺ overload; (e) _Free radicals, lipid peroxidation_; (f) _Inflammation_ - microglial activation, cytokines; (g) _Mitochondrial dysfunction_; (h) _Apoptosis_; (i) _Secondary haemorrhage_ - expansion of contusions/haematomas; (j) _Seizures_ - common; raise CMRO2, ICP; (k) _Vasospasm_ (in traumatic SAH); (l) _Hyperglycaemia, hyperthermia_ - worsen outcomes. Acute management of severe TBI: (1) _Pre-hospital and ED_ - ATLS principles; airway protection (intubation if GCS ≤ 8); avoid hypoxia and hypotension. (2) _Imaging_ - non-contrast CT (Marshall classification: focal vs diffuse, midline shift, basal cistern compression); progressive imaging if neurological decline. (3) _ICU care_: head elevation 30°, sedation (propofol, fentanyl), CPP target 60-70 mm Hg (CPP = MAP - ICP); ICP monitoring (intraventricular catheter or parenchymal bolt) for moderate-severe; (4) _ICP management_ - tiered (see Q12 of this chapter): (a) Position, sedation, normocarbia; (b) Osmotherapy (mannitol, hypertonic saline); (c) CSF drainage via EVD; (d) Brief hyperventilation only for impending herniation; (e) Decompressive craniectomy in refractory; (f) Barbiturate coma (pentobarbital) for refractory ICP; (5) _Surgical evacuation_ of mass lesions when indicated (epidural > 30 mL or pupil-involving; subdural > 10 mm or > 5 mm midline shift; large ICH, contusion with mass effect; depressed skull fracture); (6) _Anti-seizure prophylaxis_ - levetiracetam or phenytoin for 7 days post-injury; longer if early seizures; (7) _Avoid_ prolonged hyperventilation (causes ischaemia), corticosteroids (CRASH trial showed harm in TBI), prophylactic hypothermia (mixed evidence); (8) _Multimodal monitoring_ in selected centres - PbtO2, microdialysis, jugular bulb saturation, thermal diffusion CBF. Cervical spine: clear or immobilise. DVT prophylaxis: pneumatic compression initially; LMWH after demonstrated lesion stability (24-48 h). Nutrition early. Outcome: severe TBI mortality ~ 30%; survivors often have lasting deficits (cognitive, behavioural, motor); recovery over months-years. Mild TBI/concussion - typically symptoms resolve over weeks; post-concussive syndrome (cognitive, sleep, mood, headache) prolonged in some; chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) from repetitive injury (boxers, American football) - tau pathology, progressive cognitive/behavioural decline; identified post-mortem currently. Rehabilitation - early, multidisciplinary, vocational. Long-term care. Prevention: helmets, seat belts, fall prevention, gun safety, sport rules [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10].

Elaborative explanation: TBI care has dramatically improved through ICU principles, ICP monitoring, and trauma systems. The distinction between primary (occurs instantly) and secondary (preventable) injury underlies management - we cannot reverse the initial impact, but we can prevent secondary cascades. CTE has reframed concussion in contact sports.

3. ICP-targeted ICU care + selective surgery.

Stem: Synthesise this chapter: how do higher cortical functions arise from neural networks, and what design principles emerge?

Model answer: Higher cortical functions emerge from organisation across multiple scales. Design principles: (1) _Hierarchical processing_ - primary sensory/motor cortices process basic features; association cortices integrate modalities and abstract; prefrontal areas implement executive function. (2) _Network organisation_ - networks of distant regions connected by white matter tracts work together: language network (Broca-Wernicke + arcuate fasciculus + supporting regions); default mode network (PCC, mPFC, angular gyrus - autobiographical, mind-wandering); salience network (anterior insula, dorsal anterior cingulate - attention); central executive network (dorsolateral PFC + parietal - working memory, control). (3) _Lateralisation_ - left dominant for language and analytical processing; right for visuospatial and prosody. (4) _Plasticity_ - networks can reorganise after injury (rehabilitation), learning (musicians, taxi drivers), or developmental shifts. (5) _Modularity_ - specialised regions for face, body, place, scene processing in ventral temporal cortex; prosopagnosia from fusiform face area lesion. (6) _Top-down control_ - prefrontal cortex modulates attention, action selection, emotion regulation, reward valuation. (7) _Memory systems_ - declarative (hippocampus) vs procedural (basal ganglia, cerebellum) vs working memory (PFC); each separable, each clinically relevant. (8) _Default mode network_ - active during rest, mind-wandering, self-referential thought; abnormal in depression, AD, schizophrenia. (9) _Sleep-wake regulation_ - flip-flop switch with orexin stabilisation; lessons in narcolepsy. (10) _Limbic integration_ - emotion influences cognition, memory, and behaviour; clinical implications across psychiatry and neurology. Clinical syndromes localise to specific design failures: aphasia (language network), amnesia (hippocampus), depression (limbic-prefrontal circuits), schizophrenia (DA + glutamate + circuit), Parkinson (basal ganglia), Alzheimer (medial temporal -> cortical), MS (white matter networks), epilepsy (focal cortical dysfunction), stroke (vascular territory + network), TBI (DAI disrupts networks). Pedagogical insight: higher functions are not localised to single areas but emerge from network dynamics. Same lesion in different patients can produce different deficits depending on individual network organisation. The brain is a _network of networks_, and disorders are network failures - which is why connectomics, functional MRI, and network neuroscience are central to modern brain science. Modern therapeutic approaches (DBS, TMS, cognitive rehabilitation, network-targeted pharmacology) increasingly target network function rather than focal pathology. Integrated understanding of higher functions - language, memory, emotion, cognition, consciousness - requires holding multiple levels simultaneously: molecular (neurotransmitter), cellular (neuron, synapse), circuit (microcircuit, loop), network (large-scale), and behavioural [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].
