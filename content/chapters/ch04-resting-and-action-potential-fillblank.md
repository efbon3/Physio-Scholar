## Pass 1 — Numeric values

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The resting membrane potential of a typical neuron is approximately \_\_\_ mV (cell interior negative).

Canonical answer: −70 mV

Accepted variants:

- −70 mV
- −70
- -70 mV
- approximately −70 mV
- −70 to −80 mV
- around −75 mV

Tolerance: ±10 mV (i.e., −60 to −80 mV all grade Green)

Yellow conditions:

- Numeric value matches but unit (mV) missing → "Right value — include the units (mV)."
- Value 0 or +30 → "RMP is negative inside the cell. Re-check the sign."
- Value −95 → "That is the K⁺ equilibrium potential. RMP is _less_ negative than EK."

Explanation: The resting membrane potential of most excitable cells lies between −70 and −80 mV [Costanzo ch.1]. RMP sits close to but slightly less negative than the K⁺ equilibrium potential (~−95 mV in skeletal muscle) because small Na⁺ and Cl⁻ permeabilities pull it away from EK. The value is conventionally negative because by convention, membrane potential is expressed as intracellular relative to extracellular.

Hints:

1. The cell interior is negative.
2. The value is more negative than the threshold (~−60 mV) and less negative than EK (~−95 mV).
3. The number is in the seventies.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The threshold potential at which a typical neuronal action potential becomes inevitable is approximately \_\_\_ mV.

Canonical answer: −60 mV

Accepted variants:

- −60 mV
- −60
- approximately −60 mV
- around −55 mV

Tolerance: ±10 mV

Yellow conditions:

- Numeric value matches but unit missing → "Right value — include the units (mV)."
- Value −90 or 0 → "Threshold sits between RMP and the action-potential peak. Re-check the value."

Explanation: Threshold for the typical neuronal action potential is approximately −60 mV — about 10–15 mV less negative than the RMP [Costanzo ch.1]. At threshold, the inward Na⁺ current through opening voltage-gated Na⁺ channels just exceeds the outward K⁺ current, and depolarisation becomes self-sustaining. Below threshold, depolarisation dies away as the cell repolarises; at or above threshold, a full-amplitude action potential is triggered (the "all-or-none" property).

Hints:

1. Threshold is _less_ negative than the resting potential.
2. The difference between RMP and threshold is about 10–15 mV.
3. The number is in the negative-fifties to negative-sixties.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: In skeletal muscle, the equilibrium potential for Na⁺ (ENa) is approximately \_\_\_ mV.

Canonical answer: +65 mV

Accepted variants:

- +65 mV
- +65
- 65 mV
- approximately +65 mV
- about +60 mV

Tolerance: ±10 mV

Yellow conditions:

- Sign missing → "Almost — the sign is positive (the cell interior would be positive at Na⁺ equilibrium because Na⁺ enters the cell)."
- Value −95 or −90 → "That is EK or ECl. ENa is _positive_ because Na⁺ is more concentrated outside than inside; at equilibrium, the cell interior must be positive to oppose further Na⁺ influx."

Explanation: ENa in skeletal muscle is approximately +65 mV [Costanzo ch.1]. This is the membrane potential at which the chemical and electrical driving forces on Na⁺ are equal and opposite — Na⁺ is more concentrated outside (~140 mmol/L) than inside (~14 mmol/L), so it tends to enter the cell; the inside must therefore be positive to oppose further entry. The peak of the neuronal action potential approaches but does not quite reach this value (typically peaking around +30 to +40 mV) because K⁺ permeability is also rising as the action potential progresses.

Hints:

1. ENa has the sign opposite to the typical RMP.
2. Na⁺ tends to flow into the cell (down its concentration gradient), so equilibrium needs the interior to be positive.
3. The number is in the positive sixties.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: In skeletal muscle, the equilibrium potential for K⁺ (EK) is approximately \_\_\_ mV.

Canonical answer: −95 mV

Accepted variants:

- −95 mV
- −95
- approximately −95 mV
- −90 mV
- around −90 to −95 mV

Tolerance: ±10 mV

Yellow conditions:

- Sign missing → "EK is negative inside the cell."
- Value +65 → "That is ENa. EK is _negative_ because K⁺ is more concentrated inside than outside; at equilibrium, the cell interior is negative."

Explanation: EK in skeletal muscle is approximately −95 mV [Costanzo ch.1]. K⁺ is much more concentrated inside the cell (~120 mmol/L) than outside (~4 mmol/L); it therefore tends to diffuse outward. Because K⁺ is positively charged, its outward diffusion leaves the cell interior negative until the negative voltage stops further net K⁺ outflow. The RMP sits close to EK because K⁺ permeability dominates at rest, but RMP is slightly less negative because of small Na⁺ and Cl⁻ contributions.

Hints:

1. EK has the same sign as the RMP, but is more extreme.
2. K⁺ leaves the cell at rest (more concentrated inside than outside), making the inside more negative.
3. The number is in the negative nineties.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: At body temperature (37 °C), the constant 2.3RT/F in the simplified Nernst equation equals approximately \_\_\_ mV.

Canonical answer: 60 mV

Accepted variants:

- 60 mV
- 60
- approximately 60 mV
- around 61 mV

Tolerance: ±5 mV

Yellow conditions:

- Numeric value matches but unit missing → "Right value — include the units (mV)."
- Value 25 → "That is 2.3RT/F at 25 °C, not body temperature. The body-temperature value is higher because T (Kelvin) is higher."

Explanation: The Nernst equation at body temperature (310 K) simplifies to Ex = (60/z) × log₁₀(Ce/Ci), where z is the ion's charge and the factor 60 mV represents 2.3RT/F at 37 °C [Costanzo ch.1]. This value is what allows quick mental calculation of equilibrium potentials: a 10-fold concentration ratio for a monovalent ion produces 60 mV. (At 25 °C the constant is about 58 mV; at 37 °C it is about 60–61 mV; many texts round to 60 for simplicity.)

Hints:

1. The factor depends on temperature (R, T, F are all in the formula).
2. At body temperature, the value is about half the RMP magnitude.
3. The number is a round figure — sixty.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: For each ATP molecule hydrolysed, the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase produces a net movement of \_\_\_ positive charge(s) out of the cell — making the pump electrogenic.

Canonical answer: 1

Accepted variants:

- 1
- one
- 1 positive charge
- 1 net positive charge

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "0" → "If the pump moved no net charge it would be electroneutral. The 3:2 stoichiometry means a net charge moves per cycle."
- "5" → "That is the _total_ number of ions moved per cycle (3 + 2). The _net_ charge difference is 3 − 2 = 1."

Explanation: The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase moves 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per ATP hydrolysed [Costanzo ch.1; Berne ch.1]. Both Na⁺ and K⁺ are monovalent cations, so the _net_ charge movement per cycle is 3 − 2 = 1 positive charge out of the cell. This makes the pump _electrogenic_ — it contributes a small (a few mV) outward current that adds to the negative resting membrane potential. The pump's electrogenic contribution is small compared to the K⁺ diffusion potential (~−95 mV in skeletal muscle), but is functionally relevant: pump inhibition (e.g., by ouabain) produces a small immediate depolarisation before ionic gradients have changed.

Hints:

1. Three Na⁺ out, two K⁺ in. Both are positively charged.
2. The net charge is the difference between the two.
3. The pump produces a small outward current per cycle.

## Pass 2 — Channels, structures, and named entities

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The voltage-gated ion channel responsible for the rapid upstroke (depolarisation) phase of the neuronal action potential is the voltage-gated \_\_\_ channel.

Canonical answer: Na⁺ (sodium)

Accepted variants:

- Na+
- sodium
- Na
- Na⁺
- voltage-gated sodium

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "K+" or "potassium" → "K⁺ channels open _during_ the action potential, but their role is _repolarisation_ (returning the membrane potential to negative). The upstroke is driven by inward current through a different ion channel."
- "Ca2+" or "calcium" → "Ca²⁺ channels drive the upstroke in some cells (e.g., cardiac slow-response action potentials, smooth muscle), but the _neuronal_ upstroke is driven by a different ion."
- "Cl-" or "chloride" → "Cl⁻ channels are typically inhibitory (hyperpolarising). The upstroke is depolarising, requiring an inward cation current."

Explanation: The neuronal action potential upstroke is generated by inward Na⁺ current through voltage-gated Na⁺ channels [Costanzo ch.1]. When the membrane is depolarised to threshold, the activation gates of Na⁺ channels open rapidly; Na⁺ rushes in down its electrochemical gradient (Em is far from ENa = +65 mV); the inward current causes further depolarisation, opening more Na⁺ channels — a regenerative loop that produces the rapid upstroke. The membrane potential approaches but doesn't reach ENa because K⁺ channels begin to open and Na⁺ channels begin to inactivate before equilibrium is reached.

Hints:

1. The upstroke is depolarising (less negative). Which ion's equilibrium potential is positive?
2. The ion enters the cell during the upstroke.
3. The relevant channel is named after the ion that flows through it.

QUESTION 8
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The voltage-gated ion channel responsible for the repolarisation phase of the neuronal action potential is the voltage-gated \_\_\_ channel.

Canonical answer: K⁺ (potassium)

Accepted variants:

- K+
- potassium
- K
- K⁺
- voltage-gated potassium

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "Na+" or "sodium" → "Na⁺ channels drive the _upstroke_ (depolarisation) of the action potential. Repolarisation is driven by a different ion."
- "Ca2+" or "calcium" → "Ca²⁺ has a different role (e.g., in cardiac plateau, neurotransmitter release). The neuronal repolarisation is driven by a different ion."

Explanation: Repolarisation is driven by outward K⁺ current through voltage-gated K⁺ channels (combined with closure of Na⁺ channel inactivation gates) [Costanzo ch.1]. Voltage-gated K⁺ channels open with a delay during depolarisation; once open, they conduct K⁺ outward (down its electrochemical gradient), which carries positive charge out of the cell and drives the membrane back toward EK (~−95 mV). The K⁺ conductance remains higher than the resting level for a brief period after repolarisation, producing the hyperpolarising afterpotential (undershoot). The combination of slow Na⁺ inactivation and delayed K⁺ activation is what makes the action potential brief and self-terminating rather than self-sustaining.

Hints:

1. Repolarisation drives the membrane back toward EK (the most negative equilibrium potential).
2. The relevant ion leaves the cell during repolarisation.
3. The relevant channel is named after that ion.

Canonical answer: 4

Accepted variants:

- 4
- four
- 4 (I, II, III, IV)
- four domains

Tolerance: not applicable

Explanation: The voltage-gated Na⁺ channel has a complex transmembrane architecture [Costanzo ch.1]. The pore-forming α subunit has 4 homologous domains, each containing 6 transmembrane α-helices. QUESTION 10
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The lipid insulator wrapped around vertebrate axons that increases conduction velocity is called \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: myelin

Accepted variants:

- myelin
- myelin sheath
- myelin (sheath)

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "axoplasm" or "cytoplasm" → "Those are intracellular constituents, not the insulating sheath surrounding the axon."
- "neurolemma" → "Neurolemma (the cell membrane of the Schwann cell) is part of the structure but the insulating component itself has a different name."

Explanation: Myelin is a lipid-rich insulator wrapped around axons by Schwann cells (in the peripheral nervous system) and oligodendrocytes (in the central nervous system) [Costanzo ch.1]. Myelin increases membrane resistance and decreases membrane capacitance, both of which dramatically increase conduction velocity. Action potentials are regenerated only at gaps in the myelin sheath called _nodes of Ranvier_ (where Na⁺ and K⁺ channels are concentrated), with the depolarisation effectively "jumping" from node to node — _saltatory conduction_. The most common demyelinating disease of the CNS is multiple sclerosis; in the PNS, Guillain-Barré syndrome is a classical demyelinating polyneuropathy.

Hints:

1. The substance has high lipid content — it's an electrical insulator.
2. It is produced by Schwann cells in the PNS and oligodendrocytes in the CNS.
3. The word starts with "M" and is six letters.

QUESTION 11
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The periodic gaps in the myelin sheath, where voltage-gated Na⁺ and K⁺ channels are concentrated and where action potentials are regenerated, are called the \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: nodes of Ranvier

Accepted variants:

- nodes of Ranvier
- node of Ranvier
- Ranvier nodes
- Ranvier's nodes

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "Schmidt-Lanterman incisures" → "Schmidt-Lanterman incisures are a different feature of myelinated axons — small clefts in the myelin sheath, not gaps where action potentials regenerate. The structures named after a French anatomist are the answer."
- "synaptic clefts" → "Synaptic clefts are the spaces between presynaptic and postsynaptic membranes at synapses, not gaps in myelin."

Explanation: Nodes of Ranvier are gaps (~1 µm wide) in the myelin sheath at regular intervals along myelinated axons [Costanzo ch.1]. At each node, voltage-gated Na⁺ and K⁺ channels are densely concentrated; between nodes, the axonal membrane is heavily insulated by myelin. Local currents from an action potential at one node spread rapidly through the high-resistance internodal region (because little current escapes through the high-resistance membrane) and depolarise the next node to threshold, where a new action potential is generated. This _saltatory conduction_ between nodes is much faster than continuous conduction in unmyelinated axons. Demyelination (e.g., in multiple sclerosis) disrupts this architecture, slowing or blocking conduction.

Hints:

1. The structure is named after the 19th-century French anatomist who first described them.
2. They are gaps in the myelin sheath, exposing the axonal membrane.
3. The eponym begins with "R" and is French.

QUESTION 12
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The mode of action potential conduction in myelinated nerves, in which depolarisation appears to "jump" from one node of Ranvier to the next, is called \_\_\_ conduction.

Canonical answer: saltatory

Accepted variants:

- saltatory
- saltatory conduction
- jumping conduction (informal)

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "continuous" → "Continuous conduction is what happens in _unmyelinated_ axons — the AP propagates point-by-point along the membrane. Myelinated axons use a different, faster mode."
- "axoplasmic" → "Axoplasmic flow is a different concept — bulk movement of cytoplasm along the axon, not action potential conduction."

Explanation: Saltatory conduction (from Latin _saltare_, "to leap") is the mode of action potential conduction in myelinated axons [Costanzo ch.1]. Local currents from an action potential at one node of Ranvier spread rapidly through the high-resistance, low-capacitance internodal region to depolarise the next node to threshold; the action potential is regenerated there, and the process continues. The action potential effectively "jumps" from node to node, achieving conduction velocities of up to ~100 m/s in the largest myelinated motor axons — far higher than the ~1 m/s typical of unmyelinated C fibres of similar diameter. The metabolic economy is also significant: ion fluxes occur only at the small node area, not the entire axonal membrane, reducing the energetic cost of restoring gradients between action potentials.

Hints:

1. The Latin root means "to leap."
2. The name describes the visual appearance of the action potential — jumping between nodes.
3. The word is nine letters and starts with "S."

## Pass 3 — Pharmacology and clinical anchors

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The toxin from the Japanese pufferfish that selectively blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels and abolishes nerve conduction is called \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: tetrodotoxin (TTX)

Accepted variants:

- tetrodotoxin
- TTX
- pufferfish toxin

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "ouabain" → "Ouabain inhibits the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase (the pump), not the voltage-gated Na⁺ channel."
- "tetraethylammonium" or "TEA" → "TEA blocks voltage-gated K⁺ channels, not Na⁺ channels."
- "lidocaine" → "Lidocaine _also_ blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, but it is a synthetic local anaesthetic, not a natural toxin from a pufferfish."

Explanation: Tetrodotoxin (TTX) is a small natural toxin found in pufferfish (and several other organisms including some newts and octopuses) [Costanzo ch.1]. It binds with extraordinary specificity to the extracellular pore of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, blocking Na⁺ entry. Without functional Na⁺ channels, the upstroke of the action potential cannot occur, and neuromuscular paralysis follows — first peripheral numbness (around the lips and tongue, classic in pufferfish poisoning), then ascending paralysis, and ultimately respiratory failure if the dose is sufficient. Pufferfish (fugu) is consumed as a delicacy in Japan, but specially licensed chefs are required because of the lethal risk if the toxin-containing organs are not removed. There is no specific antidote; treatment is supportive (mechanical ventilation until the toxin is metabolised).

Hints:

1. The toxin is named after the family of fish from which it is isolated.
2. Tetrodontidae are the pufferfish family.
3. The drug name begins with "tetro-" and ends with "-toxin."

Stem: The classical experimental drug that blocks voltage-gated K⁺ channels and prolongs the action potential by preventing repolarisation is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: tetraethylammonium (TEA)

Accepted variants:

- tetraethylammonium
- TEA
- tetraethyl ammonium

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "tetrodotoxin" or "TTX" → "TTX blocks voltage-gated _Na⁺_ channels, abolishing the _upstroke_. The drug that blocks K⁺ channels has a different name."
- "lidocaine" → "Lidocaine blocks Na⁺ channels (use-dependently). The K⁺-channel blocker has a different name."

Explanation: Tetraethylammonium (TEA) is a quaternary ammonium compound that blocks voltage-gated K⁺ channels by binding inside the pore [Costanzo ch.1]. With K⁺ channels blocked, repolarisation of the action potential is markedly slowed — the action potential becomes broader and the cell may be unable to recover quickly enough to fire repeated action potentials. TEA was the original tool used to dissect the K⁺ component of action potentials in classical electrophysiology experiments (along with TTX for Na⁺). Clinical drugs that block K⁺ channels include the class III antiarrhythmics (amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide), which prolong cardiac repolarisation and the QT interval; they are used to suppress arrhythmias but carry a risk of torsades de pointes (a polymorphic ventricular tachycardia) when QT prolongation is excessive.

2. The acronym is three letters.
3. The full name has the same prefix as tetrodotoxin (both start with "tetra-").

QUESTION 15
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The most common demyelinating disease of the central nervous system is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: multiple sclerosis (MS)

Accepted variants:

- multiple sclerosis
- MS

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "Guillain-Barré syndrome" → "GBS is a demyelinating polyneuropathy of the _peripheral_ nervous system (PNS), not the CNS. The CNS demyelinating disease has a different name."
- "amyotrophic lateral sclerosis" or "ALS" → "ALS is a motor neuron disease — degeneration of upper and lower motor neurons — not a demyelinating disease."

Explanation: Multiple sclerosis (MS) is the most common demyelinating disease of the central nervous system [Costanzo ch.1, Box 1.4]. An autoimmune attack on oligodendrocyte-derived myelin produces demyelinating lesions ("plaques") scattered in time and space throughout the brain and spinal cord white matter. Loss of myelin reduces membrane resistance, current "leaks out" across the demyelinated axon during conduction, and local currents may decay below the threshold needed to depolarise the next node of Ranvier. Clinical features depend on lesion location: optic neuritis (blurred vision, eye pain), brainstem lesions (diplopia, vertigo, dysarthria), spinal cord lesions (weakness, paraesthesiae, sphincter dysfunction). Diagnosis combines clinical history, MRI demonstration of multiple white-matter lesions, and CSF studies. Treatment includes acute corticosteroids for relapses and disease-modifying agents (interferon-beta, glatiramer acetate, monoclonal antibodies, etc.) to reduce relapse frequency.

Hints:

1. The CNS counterpart of Guillain-Barré syndrome (which affects PNS).
2. Lesions are scattered ("multiple") in the white matter.
3. The disease name has two words.

QUESTION 16
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The refractory period during which _no_ action potential can be elicited regardless of stimulus strength — caused by closed inactivation gates of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels — is called the \_\_\_ refractory period.

Canonical answer: absolute

Accepted variants:

- absolute
- absolute refractory
- ARP

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "relative" → "The relative refractory period allows an AP to be elicited with a stronger-than-usual stimulus, because Na⁺ channels are recovering. The refractory period during which _no_ AP can fire has the opposite name."
- "effective" → "Effective refractory period is a term used in cardiac physiology — close to but not synonymous with absolute. The general physiology answer for nerve cells is different."

Explanation: The absolute refractory period overlaps with most of the action potential's duration [Costanzo ch.1]. During this time, the inactivation gates of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are closed (they closed during the upstroke), so essentially no Na⁺ channels are available to fire. No matter how depolarising the stimulus, no action potential can be generated. The Na⁺ channels recover from inactivation only after the cell has repolarised back to RMP. The _relative_ refractory period follows: Na⁺ channels are recovering, but K⁺ conductance is still elevated (afterhyperpolarisation), so a stronger-than-normal stimulus is needed to bring the cell to threshold. The two refractory periods together limit the maximum firing rate of a neuron and ensure that action potentials propagate unidirectionally down an axon.

Hints:

1. Two refractory periods exist: one cannot be overcome by any stimulus; the other can be overcome by a stronger stimulus.
2. The name reflects the absoluteness of the prohibition.
3. The word starts with "A" and means "complete" or "without exception."

QUESTION 17
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: When a nerve or muscle membrane is depolarised slowly or held at a depolarised level, it may fail to fire an action potential even when threshold is crossed — because Na⁺ channels close their inactivation gates before activation can occur. This phenomenon is called \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: accommodation

Accepted variants:

- accommodation
- Accommodation

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "summation" → "Summation is the additive integration of multiple stimuli — the opposite of accommodation. The phenomenon described, where slow depolarisation fails to trigger an AP, has a different name."
- "facilitation" → "Facilitation is enhancement of response with repeated stimulation — also the opposite. Slow depolarisation that fails to fire an AP is the phenomenon called accommodation."

Explanation: Accommodation occurs when a stimulus depolarises the membrane slowly enough that the inactivation gates of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels close as quickly as the activation gates open [Costanzo ch.1, Box 1.3]. Net inward Na⁺ current is therefore small, and even though the membrane potential may eventually pass the usual threshold, no action potential is fired. Accommodation is clinically relevant in _hyperkalaemia_: high ECF K⁺ depolarises RMP gradually toward the threshold, but the slow depolarisation produces accommodation rather than firing — Na⁺ channels become inactivated, the cell becomes inexcitable, and muscle weakness or cardiac arrhythmias develop. The same phenomenon explains why a slow-rising stimulus is less effective than a brief sharp stimulus of the same final magnitude.

Hints:

1. The word implies "adaptation" or "fitting in" — the cell adapts to the slow depolarisation.
2. Slow depolarisation closes Na⁺ channel inactivation gates before activation can occur.
3. The word has 13 letters and is shared with eye-related anatomy (lens accommodation).

Stem: The class IB antiarrhythmic drug commonly used as a local anaesthetic for minor procedures, which blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in a _use-dependent_ manner, is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: lidocaine

Accepted variants:

- lidocaine
- Lidocaine
- lignocaine (UK spelling)

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "tetrodotoxin" or "TTX" → "TTX blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels but is not a clinically used drug — it is a natural toxin. The clinical drug used as both a local anaesthetic and an antiarrhythmic is different."
- "procaine" or "novocaine" → "Procaine is also a local anaesthetic but is not the classical class IB antiarrhythmic. The agent that bridges both indications is different."
- "amiodarone" → "Amiodarone is a class III antiarrhythmic (K⁺-channel blocker), not a Na⁺-channel blocker."

Explanation: Lidocaine (lignocaine in British/Indian usage) is a small-molecule local anaesthetic and class IB antiarrhythmic [Costanzo ch.1; Berne ch.1]. It blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in a _use-dependent_ (state-dependent) manner — preferentially binding channels in their open or inactivated states. This selectivity gives lidocaine clinically valuable specificity: in local anaesthesia, it preferentially blocks actively firing pain fibres (small-diameter A-delta and C fibres) over quiescent ones; in antiarrhythmic use, it preferentially suppresses ectopic firing in ischaemic cardiac myocytes (where channels are partially depolarised and inactivated) over normally-paced ones. The drug is given by injection (subcutaneously, perineurally, intravenously); systemic toxicity can produce CNS effects (perioral numbness, dizziness, seizures) and cardiovascular effects (hypotension, bradycardia).

1. The drug is one of the most widely used local anaesthetics in clinical practice.
2. Its UK / Indian spelling differs slightly from the American.
3. The name begins with "L" and ends in "-caine" (the suffix typical of -caine local anaesthetics).
