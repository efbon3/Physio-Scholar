Stem: The three main components of haemostasis are:

Correct answer: Vascular phase (vasoconstriction), primary haemostasis (platelet plug formation), secondary haemostasis (coagulation cascade → fibrin clot); plus fibrinolysis to dissolve clots

- "Lymphatic system" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Haemostasis** maintains a balance between bleeding and excessive clotting:

1. **Vascular phase** — Endothelial damage → vasoconstriction (myogenic + endothelin) → reduces flow to area.

2. **Primary haemostasis** — Platelet plug formation:
   - **Adhesion** — vWF on exposed subendothelium binds platelet GPIb.
   - **Activation** — Platelets release ADP, TXA₂.
   - **Aggregation** — GPIIb/IIIa binds fibrinogen, linking platelets.

3. **Secondary haemostasis** — Coagulation cascade:
   - **Extrinsic** (TF/VIIa) initiates.
   - **Intrinsic** (XII, XI, IX, VIII) amplifies.
   - **Common pathway** (X, V, II, fibrinogen → fibrin).

4. **Fibrinolysis** — Plasmin breaks down fibrin → D-dimers.

**Anti-thrombotic mechanisms** (constitutive):

- Endothelial-derived NO, prostacyclin (PGI₂) — inhibit platelets.
- Heparan sulphate on endothelium → activates antithrombin.
- Thrombomodulin → activates protein C.
- Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA).

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.18; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.37.)

Correct answer: Von Willebrand factor (vWF) bridging platelet GPIb-IX-V receptor and collagen-bound vWF; particularly important under high shear (arterioles)

- "Fibrinogen" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Platelet adhesion**:

- Endothelial damage exposes collagen.
- vWF (released from endothelium and platelets) binds collagen.
- Platelet **GPIb-IX-V receptor** binds vWF.
- Especially important at **high shear** (arterial circulation).

**Von Willebrand disease (vWD)** — Most common inherited bleeding disorder:

- **Type 1** (most common, ~75%) — Quantitative reduction of vWF.
- **Type 2** — Qualitative defects (subtypes 2A, 2B, 2M, 2N).
- **Type 3** (severe) — Absent vWF.

**Clinical features**: Mucocutaneous bleeding (epistaxis, menorrhagia, gum bleeding, easy bruising). Less severe than haemophilia but more common.

**Lab**: Prolonged bleeding time, prolonged aPTT (because vWF protects FVIII from degradation; reduced vWF → reduced FVIII), reduced ristocetin cofactor activity.

**Treatment**:

- **Desmopressin (DDAVP)** — Releases vWF stores from endothelium. Useful for type 1.
- **vWF concentrate** (with factor VIII) — For severe cases or before surgery.

**Bernard-Soulier syndrome** — Rare; deficiency of GPIb. Severe bleeding.

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.18.)

Correct answer: Fibrinogen bridging adjacent platelets via GPIIb/IIIa receptors (activated by ADP, TXA₂); fibrinogen has multiple GPIIb/IIIa binding sites

- "Calcium alone" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "RBC stacking" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Platelet aggregation pathway**:

1. Activated platelets (by collagen, thrombin, ADP, TXA₂) undergo shape change (discoid → spheroid with pseudopodia).
2. **GPIIb/IIIa receptors** become activated (inside-out signalling) → high-affinity for fibrinogen.
3. Fibrinogen has multiple GPIIb/IIIa binding sites → bridges adjacent platelets.
4. Aggregation amplified by released ADP (binds P2Y12) and TXA₂.

**Platelet activation pathways**:

- **Collagen + vWF** → primary activation.
- **Thrombin** → most potent activator (PAR-1, PAR-4 receptors).
- **ADP** (released from dense granules) → P2Y12 receptor.
- **Thromboxane A₂** (synthesised from arachidonic acid via COX-1) → TP receptor.

**Antiplatelet drugs**:

- **Aspirin** — Irreversible COX-1 inhibition → reduced TXA₂. Effect lasts platelet lifespan (~7 days).
- **P2Y12 inhibitors** (clopidogrel, prasugrel, ticagrelor, cangrelor) — Block ADP receptor.
- **GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors** (abciximab, eptifibatide, tirofiban) — Block fibrinogen binding. IV use in PCI.
- **PAR-1 antagonist** (vorapaxar) — Newer.

**Glanzmann thrombasthenia** — Rare; deficiency of GPIIb/IIIa. Severe bleeding.

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.18.)

Correct answer: 7–10 days; produced from megakaryocytes in bone marrow; thrombopoietin (TPO) is the main growth factor

- "120 days" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Platelet biology**:

- Lifespan ~7–10 days.
- ~150–400 × 10⁹/L circulating.
- ~30% in spleen (sequestered).
- Production: HSC → megakaryoblast → megakaryocyte → cytoplasmic fragmentation → platelets (each megakaryocyte produces 2,000–5,000 platelets).
- **Thrombopoietin (TPO)** — Liver-derived growth factor; stimulates megakaryocyte production. TPO mimetics (romiplostim, eltrombopag) treat ITP and aplastic anaemia.

**Platelet disorders**:

**Quantitative**:

- **Thrombocytopenia**:
  - **Production**: aplastic anaemia, MDS, leukaemia, B12/folate deficiency, drugs (chemotherapy).
  - **Destruction**: ITP (immune-mediated), TTP, HUS, DIC, drugs (heparin → HIT), HIV, autoimmune.
  - **Sequestration**: hypersplenism.
  - **Dilution**: massive transfusion.
- **Thrombocytosis**:
  - Reactive (inflammation, iron deficiency, infection, malignancy, post-splenectomy).
  - Primary: essential thrombocythaemia (JAK2/CALR/MPL mutations) — risk of thrombosis and bleeding.

**Qualitative** (normal count, dysfunction):

- Inherited: Glanzmann (GPIIb/IIIa), Bernard-Soulier (GPIb), storage pool disease.
- Acquired: aspirin/clopidogrel, uraemia, liver disease.

**ITP (immune thrombocytopenia)**:

- Anti-platelet antibodies → splenic destruction.
- Often post-viral in children (acute, self-limiting); chronic in adults.
- Treatment: corticosteroids (first-line), IVIG, anti-D, rituximab, splenectomy, TPO agonists, fostamatinib.

**HIT (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia)**:

- Type II HIT — IgG vs PF4-heparin complex → platelet activation, thrombosis (paradoxically, despite low platelets).
- Treatment: stop heparin, alternative anticoagulant (argatroban, fondaparinux, DOAC).
- Test: HIT antibody, serotonin release assay.

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.18.)

Stem: The "extrinsic pathway" of coagulation is initiated by:

Correct answer: Tissue factor (TF, factor III) released from damaged tissue → binds factor VIIa → activates X (TF/VIIa-Ca²⁺-PL complex); main initiator of physiological coagulation

Distractors:

- "Activated factor XII" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Calcium alone" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Platelets" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Modern view of coagulation cascade**:

The traditional "intrinsic" + "extrinsic" pathway model has been refined to a **cell-based model**:

**1. Initiation** (extrinsic; on TF-bearing cells):

- Vessel injury exposes **tissue factor (TF)** on subendothelial cells.
- TF binds plasma **factor VIIa** (small amount circulates pre-activated).
- **TF/VIIa complex** activates X (and IX).
- Xa + Va → small thrombin generation.

**2. Amplification** (on platelet surface):

- Initial thrombin activates platelets, factors V, VIII, XI.
- Activated platelets provide phospholipid surface.

**3. Propagation** (intrinsic on platelet surface):

- IXa + VIIIa (tenase complex) → activates X massively.
- Xa + Va (prothrombinase complex) → activates thrombin (II → IIa).
- **Thrombin burst** — large quantities.
- Thrombin cleaves fibrinogen → fibrin monomers → polymerise.
- Thrombin activates XIII → XIIIa cross-links fibrin → stable clot.

**Factors involved**:

- **Vitamin K-dependent factors**: II (prothrombin), VII, IX, X, plus protein C and protein S. Require vitamin K for γ-carboxylation of glutamate residues.
- **Common pathway**: X, V, II (prothrombin → thrombin), I (fibrinogen → fibrin).

**Calcium** — Required throughout (Factor IV).

**Phospholipid surface** — Provided by activated platelets.

**Lab tests**:

- **PT/INR** — Tests extrinsic + common (II, V, VII, X, fibrinogen). Sensitive to vitamin K antagonists (warfarin) and liver disease.
- **aPTT** — Tests intrinsic + common (XII, XI, IX, VIII, X, V, II, fibrinogen). Used to monitor heparin.
- **Thrombin time (TT)** — Tests fibrinogen → fibrin step.
- **Fibrinogen** — Direct measurement.
- **D-dimer** — Fibrin breakdown product (raised in thrombosis, DIC, recent surgery).
- **Mixing studies** — Distinguish factor deficiency from inhibitor.
- **Specific factor assays**.

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.18; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.37.)

Explanation: **Haemophilias** — X-linked recessive bleeding disorders.

**Haemophilia A** (factor VIII deficiency, ~80%):

- 1 in 5,000 male births.
- Classification: severe (<1% activity), moderate (1–5%), mild (5–40%).
- **Spontaneous bleeding** in severe disease (joints — chronic haemophilic arthropathy if untreated; muscles; intracranial — leading cause of death).
- **Lab**: prolonged aPTT, normal PT, normal vWF, low factor VIII activity.

**Haemophilia B** (factor IX deficiency, "Christmas disease", ~20%):

- Same X-linked inheritance, same phenotype, lab same except factor IX low.

**Haemophilia C** (factor XI deficiency):

- Autosomal recessive.
- Common in Ashkenazi Jews.
- Milder bleeding (often only post-surgical).

**Treatment**:

- **Factor concentrate** — Prophylactic for severe, on-demand for mild-moderate.
- **Recombinant factor VIII or IX** — Standard of care in developed countries.

- **DDAVP** (desmopressin) — Releases stored factor VIII from endothelium; useful for mild haemophilia A only.
- **Antifibrinolytics** (tranexamic acid, aminocaproic acid) — For mucosal bleeding.

**Inhibitor development** — ~30% of severe haemophilia A patients develop neutralising antibodies (alloantibodies) to factor VIII. Treated with FVIII bypassing agents (FEIBA, factor VIIa) or immune tolerance induction; emicizumab effective despite inhibitors.

**Hereditary bleeding disorder differential**:

- **vWD** — Mucocutaneous bleeding, common, autosomal dominant typically.
- **Haemophilia A/B** — Deep tissue bleeding, X-linked, deep tissue/joint bleeding.
- **Other rare factor deficiencies** (FVII, FXI, FXIII).

Explanation: **Vitamin K cycle**:

- Reduced vitamin K (KH₂) is cofactor for γ-glutamyl carboxylase → adds γ-carboxyl groups to specific glutamate residues in factors II, VII, IX, X, and proteins C, S, Z.
- Carboxylated factors can bind Ca²⁺ → bind to phospholipid membranes (essential for tenase, prothrombinase complexes).
- During reaction, vitamin K oxidised to KO (epoxide).
- **Vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR)** regenerates KH₂.

**Warfarin mechanism**:

- Inhibits VKOR → vitamin K stays oxidised → cannot carboxylate factors → "PIVKA" (proteins induced by vitamin K absence) — non-functional factors.
- Factors II, VII, IX, X, protein C, S all reduced.
- **Half-lives matter**:
  - VII shortest (~6 h).
  - IX (~24 h).
  - X (~36 h).
  - II longest (~60 h).
  - Protein C (~6 h, similar to VII).
- **Initial paradox**: Both procoagulants (VII, X — reduced first) and anticoagulants (protein C — reduced first) decline. If protein C declines faster than X and II, can have transient prothrombotic state ("warfarin-induced skin necrosis" classically with protein C deficiency).
- **Bridging with heparin** initially when starting warfarin in high-risk patients.

**Reversal of warfarin**:

- **Vitamin K** (oral 5–10 mg) — Slow (12–48 h).
- **4-factor PCC (prothrombin complex concentrate)** — Rapid; for major bleeding or urgent surgery.
- **FFP (fresh frozen plasma)** — Slower; volume issues.

**INR (international normalised ratio)**:

- Standardises PT across labs.
- Therapeutic range 2.0–3.0 for most indications (3.0–4.0 for mechanical mitral valve).
- INR <2 → underdosed, thrombosis risk.
- INR >3 → overdosed, bleeding risk.
- Multiple drug and food interactions.

**DOACs** (direct oral anticoagulants) — Largely replaced warfarin for non-valvular AF, VTE:

- **Direct thrombin inhibitor**: dabigatran. Reversal: idarucizumab.
- **Factor Xa inhibitors**: rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban. Reversal: andexanet alfa.

Explanation: **Endogenous anticoagulant systems**:

- Serpin family. Inhibits thrombin (IIa), Xa, IXa, XIa, XIIa.
- Slow alone; **heparin** binds AT → conformational change → 1000× faster inhibition of thrombin/Xa.

- Inhibits TF/VIIa/Xa complex.
- Endothelial.

- **Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS)** — Acquired; lupus anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-β2-glycoprotein. Recurrent thrombosis + miscarriages. Chronic anticoagulation.

Explanation: **Fibrinolysis**:

- **Plasminogen** (zymogen) → **plasmin** (active enzyme).
- **Activators**:
  - **tPA (tissue plasminogen activator)** — From endothelium. Most active when bound to fibrin (specificity for clots).
  - **Urokinase (u-PA)** — From kidney, urine.
  - **Streptokinase** — Bacterial; therapeutic thrombolytic.
- **Plasmin** cleaves fibrin (and fibrinogen) → fibrin degradation products (FDPs), including **D-dimers** (cross-linked fibrin breakdown, indicating prior clot).

**Inhibitors of fibrinolysis**:

- **PAI-1 (plasminogen activator inhibitor-1)** — Inhibits tPA, u-PA. Raised in metabolic syndrome (prothrombotic).
- **α2-antiplasmin** — Inhibits free plasmin.
- **TAFI** (thrombin-activatable fibrinolysis inhibitor) — Reduces plasmin generation on fibrin.

**Thrombolytic drugs**:

- **Alteplase (recombinant tPA)** — For acute ischaemic stroke (within 4.5 h), STEMI (if PCI unavailable), massive PE.
- **Tenecteplase** — Improved alteplase; longer half-life, more fibrin-specific. Increasingly used.
- **Streptokinase** — Older; less specific; allergic risk.

**Antifibrinolytic drugs** (used to reduce bleeding):

- **Tranexamic acid (TXA)** — Lysine analogue; binds plasminogen → prevents binding to fibrin. Used in heavy menstrual bleeding, surgery, trauma (CRASH-2 trial showed mortality benefit in trauma haemorrhage), postpartum haemorrhage.
- **Aminocaproic acid** — Similar.

**D-dimer clinical use**:

- **High sensitivity, low specificity** for thrombosis.
- **Negative D-dimer** — Useful to **rule out** DVT/PE in low-pretest-probability patients.
- **Positive** — Non-specific (raised in surgery, trauma, infection, pregnancy, malignancy, advanced age).
- Always interpret with clinical context.
