# Chapter 6 — The Neuron and Synaptic Transmission — MCQs

---

QUESTION 1

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Which of the following best characterises an electrical synapse compared with a chemical synapse?

Correct answer: It is bidirectional, has essentially no synaptic delay, and acts as a low-pass filter

Distractors:

- "It is unidirectional and shows a long synaptic delay" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "It uses voltage-gated Ca²⁺ entry to trigger transmitter release" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Each channel passes only ions and is impermeable to small metabolites" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Electrical synapses (gap junctions) couple cells through connexon channels that permit current flow in either direction with no detectable delay. Slow voltage changes are transmitted more faithfully than fast ones (low-pass filtering), so they are well suited for synchronising network activity. The pore is large (~1–2 nm) and passes ions plus small molecules up to ~1 kDa (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Electrical Synapses; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46 — Electrical synapse section).

QUESTION 2

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The structural unit of a gap junction channel consists of:

Correct answer: Two hemichannels (connexons), each made of six connexin subunits, contributed one by each cell

Distractors:

- "A hexamer of α-subunits forming a single pore through one cell membrane" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "A pentamer of subunits arranged similarly to the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "A single tetraspan protein spanning both apposed membranes" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Each gap junction channel is built from two connexons; each connexon is a hexamer of connexin subunits, with one connexon contributed by each apposed cell. The intercellular space narrows to ~3 nm at the junction. At least 21 connexin genes exist in mammals; connexin 36 is the major neuronal connexin in the adult CNS (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — At the Cellular Level box; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.12).

QUESTION 3

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The trigger that links a presynaptic action potential to neurotransmitter release is:

Correct answer: Ca²⁺ influx through voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in the active zone

Distractors:

- "K⁺ efflux through delayed rectifier channels" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Cl⁻ entry through ligand-gated GABA-A channels" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Na⁺ influx through voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in the terminal membrane" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Depolarisation of the presynaptic terminal opens voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels clustered at the active zone. The local rise in cytoplasmic [Ca²⁺] (a "microdomain") binds to synaptotagmin and triggers SNARE-mediated vesicle fusion. The amount of transmitter released is steeply dependent on Ca²⁺ entry. Although Na⁺ and K⁺ currents shape the action potential itself, only Ca²⁺ entry triggers fusion (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46 — Transmitter Release).

QUESTION 4

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The Ca²⁺ sensor that binds Ca²⁺ at the active zone and triggers fast vesicle fusion is best identified as:

Correct answer: Synaptotagmin

Distractors:

- "Synaptobrevin (a v-SNARE)" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Syntaxin (a t-SNARE)" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "SNAP-25 (a t-SNARE)" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Synaptotagmin contains two cytoplasmic C2 domains that bind Ca²⁺; binding triggers the final fusion step of docked vesicles. The SNARE complex (synaptobrevin on the vesicle, syntaxin and SNAP-25 on the plasma membrane) zippers the membranes close together, but SNAREs do not bind Ca²⁺ themselves. Botulinum and tetanus neurotoxins are zinc proteases that cleave SNARE proteins and abolish release (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Molecular Apparatus; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

QUESTION 5

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Spontaneous miniature end-plate potentials (mEPPs) recorded at the neuromuscular junction in the absence of nerve stimulation are best explained by:

Correct answer: Spontaneous fusion of single synaptic vesicles, each releasing a quantum of transmitter

Distractors:

- "Random opening of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in the muscle fibre" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Leakage of choline through the postsynaptic membrane" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Spontaneous activation of acetylcholinesterase" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: mEPPs (~0.4 mV in the human end-plate, ≤1 mV in classical frog NMJ recordings) are produced by the spontaneous, random fusion of single synaptic vesicles. Evoked end-plate potentials are integer multiples of the mEPP amplitude — the cornerstone of Katz's quantal hypothesis. Each vesicle contains roughly 2,000–10,000 ACh molecules (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Quantal Nature; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46; Costanzo, 7e — Synaptic Transmission).

QUESTION 7

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The reversal potential of a typical fast EPSP at the neuromuscular junction is approximately:

Correct answer: 0 mV

Distractors:

- "−90 mV" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "−70 mV" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "+60 mV" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: The nicotinic ACh receptor is a non-selective cation channel that conducts Na⁺ and K⁺ with similar conductance. The reversal potential lies between the Nernst potentials of Na⁺ (~+60 mV) and K⁺ (~−90 mV) and is therefore close to 0 mV (typically 0 ± 10 mV depending on the synapse). For excitatory synapses generally, the EPSP reversal potential is similarly close to 0 mV (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — EPSPs; Costanzo, 7e — NMJ).

QUESTION 8

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: An IPSP that opens Cl⁻ channels at a postsynaptic membrane usually inhibits the cell because:

Correct answer: The Nernst potential for Cl⁻ is slightly negative to the resting potential, so Cl⁻ entry hyperpolarises the cell, and the open channels also create a shunt that reduces EPSP amplitude

Distractors:

- "Cl⁻ entry depolarises the cell beyond threshold" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Cl⁻ binds intracellular GABA receptors directly" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Cl⁻ activates voltage-gated Na⁺ channel inactivation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: In typical mature CNS neurons, ECl is roughly −70 mV — slightly negative to the resting potential of −65 mV — so opening GABA-A channels drives Cl⁻ into the cell and produces a small hyperpolarisation. Equally important, the open conductance "shunts" subsequent EPSCs by lowering input resistance, attenuating their depolarising effect even when ECl is at the resting potential (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — IPSPs; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

QUESTION 9

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The action potential of the postsynaptic neuron is preferentially initiated at the axon initial segment because:

Correct answer: The initial segment has roughly seven times the density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels found in the soma, giving it the lowest threshold for spike initiation

Distractors:

- "The initial segment has the lowest resistance" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The soma cannot conduct current" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Dendrites lack passive cable properties" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Although depolarising synaptic currents spread electrotonically into the soma, the soma itself has relatively few voltage-gated Na⁺ channels. The axon initial segment carries roughly seven times the somal density and therefore has the lowest spike threshold; the EPSP at the initial segment that triggers a spike is only ~+10 to +20 mV above resting, compared with ~+30–40 mV at the soma (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46 — Threshold for Excitation; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Synaptic Integration).

QUESTION 10

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: EPSPs from two synapses on widely separated dendrites can sum to bring the soma to threshold even though either alone is sub-threshold. This is best classified as:

Correct answer: Pure spatial summation

Distractors:

- "Pure temporal summation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Presynaptic facilitation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Decremental conduction" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Spatial summation refers to the algebraic addition at the axon hillock of EPSPs generated by different synapses active at the same time. Temporal summation, by contrast, refers to the addition of successive EPSPs generated at the same synapse before each has decayed (typical EPSP duration ~15 ms, so EPSPs separated by less than this can sum). Most central synapses have safety factors below 1 and therefore depend on summation to reach threshold (Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6).

QUESTION 11

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A typical EPSP at a central neuron lasts about 15 ms — far longer than the underlying excitatory postsynaptic current (EPSC), which lasts only 1–2 ms. The slow decay of the EPSP relative to the EPSC is determined by:

Correct answer: The passive membrane (RC) time constant of the postsynaptic cell

Distractors:

- "The time required for transmitter to diffuse across the cleft" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The duration that ligand-gated channels remain open" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The kinetics of acetylcholinesterase" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Channel kinetics determine the EPSC duration; the slower tail of the EPSP reflects the membrane's passive resistance × capacitance time constant — i.e. how quickly the change in charge bleeds away. This long decay is functionally crucial because it permits temporal summation of successive inputs (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Postsynaptic Potentials; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

QUESTION 12

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: An IPSP whose reversal potential equals the resting membrane potential can still inhibit the postsynaptic neuron because:

Correct answer: The opening of conductance produces a shunt that reduces the depolarising effect of concurrent EPSPs

Distractors:

- "It activates voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "It directly closes Na⁺ channels" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "It depletes intracellular ATP" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Even when ECl matches Vrest (so that opening Cl⁻ channels produces no net change in membrane potential), the increase in conductance lowers input resistance. Subsequent excitatory currents therefore generate smaller depolarisations (Ohm's law: ΔV = I × R). This "shunting inhibition" is a powerful mechanism distinct from algebraic hyperpolarisation (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Synaptic Integration; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

QUESTION 13

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The principal difference between an ionotropic and a metabotropic neurotransmitter receptor is that:

Correct answer: The ionotropic receptor is an integral ion channel itself, while the metabotropic receptor signals through a G protein and second messengers to indirectly alter ion channels and other targets

Distractors:

- "Ionotropic receptors are always inhibitory; metabotropic receptors are always excitatory" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Ionotropic receptors require ATP, whereas metabotropic receptors do not" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Metabotropic receptors generate faster postsynaptic responses than ionotropic ones" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Ionotropic receptors (e.g., nicotinic ACh receptor, GABA-A, AMPA, NMDA, glycine) contain the ion channel as an integral part of the protein and produce fast (millisecond-scale) postsynaptic potentials. Metabotropic receptors (e.g., muscarinic ACh, GABA-B, mGluRs, adrenoceptors) couple to G proteins, which act through second-messenger cascades to modulate ion channels, enzymes, or transcription — slower in onset (tens of ms to seconds) but with longer-lasting effects (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Receptors; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

QUESTION 15

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The NMDA receptor differs from AMPA receptors in that, at resting membrane potentials, the NMDA channel is largely blocked by:

Correct answer: Mg²⁺ ions, with relief of block requiring postsynaptic depolarisation

Distractors:

- "Ca²⁺ ions binding to the pore" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "K⁺ ions blocking the channel from the outside" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Zn²⁺ from the extracellular fluid" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: The NMDA channel is uniquely both ligand- and voltage-gated. At rest, extracellular Mg²⁺ blocks the pore; only when the postsynaptic membrane is sufficiently depolarised (typically by AMPA-mediated current) does Mg²⁺ leave, and the channel — when glutamate (and the co-agonist glycine) are bound — opens to pass Na⁺, K⁺, and importantly Ca²⁺. This coincidence detection underlies long-term potentiation (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — LTP).

QUESTION 17

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Glutamate released into the synaptic cleft is removed primarily by:

Correct answer: Excitatory amino-acid transporter (EAAT) reuptake into glial cells (mainly astrocytes) and neurons, with conversion to glutamine in glia

Distractors:

- "Enzymatic hydrolysis at the postsynaptic membrane" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Diffusion alone" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Active pumping into the bloodstream by capillary endothelium" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: There is no extracellular enzyme that degrades glutamate. Clearance depends on Na⁺/K⁺-coupled EAAT transporters; astrocytic uptake (mainly EAAT2/GLT-1 and EAAT1/GLAST) dominates. Glia convert glutamate to glutamine, which is shuttled back to neurons, reconverted to glutamate by glutaminase, and repackaged into vesicles by vesicular glutamate transporters (vGLUTs) — the glutamate-glutamine cycle (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Glutamate Transport Cycle; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).

QUESTION 18

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The classical exception to "neurotransmitter clearance by reuptake" is acetylcholine, which is terminated primarily by:

Correct answer: Hydrolysis in the synaptic cleft by acetylcholinesterase, releasing choline that is then taken back up by the presynaptic terminal

Distractors:

- "Reuptake into the presynaptic terminal as intact ACh" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Endocytosis by glial cells" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Conversion to acetate in the postsynaptic cell" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Acetylcholinesterase, concentrated in the synaptic cleft and basal lamina, hydrolyses ACh to choline and acetate within milliseconds. Choline is recovered by an Na⁺-coupled high-affinity choline transporter on the presynaptic membrane and reused for ACh resynthesis by choline acetyltransferase. Anticholinesterase drugs (e.g., neostigmine, pyridostigmine) prolong ACh action and are therapeutic in myasthenia gravis (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46; Costanzo, 7e — NMJ).

QUESTION 19

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Repeated high-frequency stimulation of certain hippocampal synapses produces a persistent (hours to days) increase in synaptic strength known as long-term potentiation (LTP). The early phase of NMDA-receptor-dependent LTP is initiated by:

Correct answer: Influx of Ca²⁺ through the NMDA receptor into the postsynaptic spine

Distractors:

- "Release of nitric oxide from the presynaptic terminal" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Closure of postsynaptic K⁺ channels" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Direct activation of CREB by glutamate" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: During strong/coincident activity, the postsynaptic membrane depolarises enough to relieve the Mg²⁺ block of NMDA receptors. The resulting Ca²⁺ influx activates Ca²⁺/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II (CaMKII), which phosphorylates AMPA receptors and promotes their insertion into the postsynaptic membrane, enhancing synaptic responses. A retrograde messenger (nitric oxide has been proposed) may also enhance presynaptic release (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — Long-Term Changes; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13 Plasticity).

QUESTION 20

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The synaptic delay at a single chemical synapse is approximately:

Correct answer: 0.5 ms

Distractors:

- "0.05 ms" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "5 ms" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "50 ms" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Synaptic delay — the interval between the arrival of the action potential at the presynaptic terminal and the onset of the postsynaptic potential — is about 0.5 ms. The delay reflects the time for Ca²⁺ entry and SNARE-driven vesicle fusion (~0.2 ms is fusion alone), diffusion of transmitter across the cleft, receptor binding, and channel opening. QUESTION 21

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A classical anatomical feature of the spinal motor neuron is that of the 10,000–200,000 presynaptic terminals on its surface, the great majority terminate on:

Correct answer: Dendrites (~80–95%)

Distractors:

- "The axon hillock" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The soma" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 22

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Presynaptic GABA-A receptors on a sensory afferent terminal in the spinal cord open Cl⁻ channels and produce a small depolarisation of the terminal. Despite the depolarisation, transmitter release is reduced because:

Correct answer: The shunt and partial inactivation of Na⁺ channels reduce the size of the action potential reaching the active zone, so less Ca²⁺ enters

Distractors:

- "The depolarisation directly closes Ca²⁺ channels" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "GABA inhibits acetylcholinesterase" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Cl⁻ entry directly displaces transmitter from vesicles" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: This is presynaptic inhibition, classically demonstrated in the spinal dorsal horn. Although ECl is more positive than the resting potential in the terminal (so Cl⁻ flows out and produces a small depolarisation), the increased conductance shunts the action potential, partially inactivates Na⁺ channels, and lessens the depolarisation reaching the active zone. Less voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channel opening means less Ca²⁺ entry and reduced quantal release — even though no postsynaptic effect is required (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.46).

QUESTION 23

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Posttetanic potentiation (PTP) of a chemical synapse — a transient (seconds to minutes) increase in postsynaptic response after a brief train of high-frequency presynaptic stimuli — is most directly attributable to:

Correct answer: Residual Ca²⁺ left in the presynaptic terminal that potentiates subsequent release

Distractors:

- "Receptor up-regulation in the postsynaptic membrane" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "New synapse formation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 25

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: In myasthenia gravis, repeated voluntary activation of a muscle produces increasing weakness ("decremental response") because:

Correct answer: Antibodies reduce the number of functional postsynaptic nicotinic ACh receptors at the end-plate, lowering the safety factor so that the normal frequency-related decline in quantal release leads to subthreshold EPPs

Distractors:

- "Acetylcholine synthesis is irreversibly blocked" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Calcium channels in the presynaptic terminal are progressively destroyed" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Acetylcholinesterase is over-active" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Autoantibodies against the nicotinic ACh receptor reduce the number of functional postsynaptic receptors. Because the normal NMJ has a high safety factor, the modest decline in vesicle release that occurs during repetitive firing is normally invisible — but with fewer receptors, the EPP falls below the threshold needed to trigger a muscle action potential, producing fatigable weakness. Anticholinesterases (e.g., pyridostigmine) ameliorate symptoms by prolonging ACh action. (Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome, by contrast, is presynaptic — antibodies target voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in the terminal; Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.6 — In the Clinic; Costanzo, 7e — Box 1.5; Ganong, 26e — NMJ disorders.)

Correct answer: Its light chain is a zinc protease that cleaves SNARE proteins (e.g., SNAP-25, synaptobrevin) in cholinergic terminals, abolishing exocytotic ACh release

- "It blocks postsynaptic nicotinic ACh receptors" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: SSRIs raise extracellular serotonin within hours by blocking the serotonin transporter (SERT), but the antidepressant effect typically takes 2–6 weeks. The initial elevation of 5-HT activates inhibitory 5-HT₁A autoreceptors on raphe neurons, transiently dampening firing. Slow desensitisation of these autoreceptors, coupled with downstream changes in postsynaptic receptor expression, BDNF signalling, and gene transcription, parallel the clinical response. This illustrates a general principle: chronic drug effects on monoaminergic systems often involve receptor adaptation rather than the acute pharmacological action alone (Ganong, 26e — Synaptic & Junctional Transmission; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.13).
