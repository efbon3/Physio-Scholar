## Pass 1 — Neuron architecture, synapse types, presynaptic Ca²⁺ → vesicle release, EPSP/IPSP fundamentals

QUESTION 1
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Which of the following best characterises an electrical synapse compared with a chemical synapse?

Correct answer: It is bidirectional, has essentially no synaptic delay, and acts as a low-pass filter for slow voltage changes.

Distractors:

- "It is unidirectional and shows a long synaptic delay." — Reveals misconception: student conflates electrical with chemical transmission. Correction: chemical synapses are unidirectional with a delay of ~0.5–1 ms (because Ca²⁺-triggered vesicle fusion takes time); electrical synapses lack this delay because current flows directly through gap-junction channels.
- "It uses voltage-gated Ca²⁺ entry to trigger transmitter release." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a chemical-synapse mechanism to an electrical one. Correction: electrical synapses do not release transmitter — they couple cells through connexon channels and propagate current directly; Ca²⁺-triggered vesicle fusion is the chemical-synapse mechanism.
- "Each channel passes only ions and is impermeable to small metabolites." — Reveals misconception: student assumes connexons behave like ligand-gated ion channels. Correction: gap-junction pores (~1–2 nm) permit not only ions but also small molecules up to ~1 kDa (cAMP, IP₃, ATP, peptides), supporting metabolic coupling between cells.

Explanation: Electrical synapses (gap junctions) couple cells through connexon channels that permit current flow in either direction with essentially no detectable delay. Slow voltage changes are transmitted more faithfully than fast ones (low-pass filtering), so electrical synapses are well suited for synchronising network activity (e.g., interneuron networks, retinal coupling) [Berne & Levy ch.6 — Electrical Synapses; Guyton & Hall ch.46]. The pore is large (~1–2 nm) and passes ions plus small molecules up to ~1 kDa. By contrast, chemical synapses are unidirectional, exhibit a synaptic delay (~0.5–1 ms reflecting Ca²⁺-triggered fusion), and provide signal amplification and modulation that is impossible with direct electrical coupling.

Hints:

1. One key feature is the direction of signal flow.
2. A second is the time taken between presynaptic and postsynaptic events.
3. Think about whether a slow voltage change passes faithfully across the junction.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The structural unit of a gap junction channel consists of:

Correct answer: Two hemichannels (connexons), each made of six connexin subunits, contributed one by each apposed cell.

Distractors:

- "A hexamer of α-subunits forming a single pore through one cell membrane." — Reveals misconception: student forgets that a gap junction spans both apposed membranes. Correction: each cell contributes its own hexameric connexon; the two connexons dock end-to-end across the intercellular space (~3 nm) to form the complete channel.
- "A pentamer of subunits arranged similarly to the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor." — Reveals misconception: student conflates gap-junction architecture with that of pentameric ligand-gated ion channels (Cys-loop family). Correction: gap junctions are hexameric in each cell; pentameric architecture is characteristic of nicotinic ACh, GABA-A, and glycine receptors.
- "A single tetraspan protein spanning both apposed membranes." — Reveals misconception: student imagines one polypeptide bridging both cells. Correction: gap junctions are built from two separate hexameric structures (one per cell), each composed of four-transmembrane-domain connexins.

Explanation: Each gap junction channel is built from two connexons; each connexon is a hexamer of connexin subunits, with one connexon contributed by each apposed cell. The intercellular space narrows to ~3 nm at the junction. At least 21 connexin genes exist in mammals; connexin 36 is the major neuronal connexin in the adult CNS, while connexin 43 dominates in cardiac muscle [Berne & Levy ch.6; Boron & Boulpaep ch.12]. Mutations in connexins underlie various clinical disorders — connexin 26 in non-syndromic congenital deafness; connexin 32 in Charcot-Marie-Tooth X-linked.

Hints:

1. Think about whether the channel spans one cell or two.
2. How many subunits does each cell contribute?
3. Each subunit is a member of the connexin family.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The trigger that links a presynaptic action potential to neurotransmitter release is:

Correct answer: Ca²⁺ influx through voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in the active zone.

Distractors:

- "K⁺ efflux through delayed rectifier channels." — Reveals misconception: student confuses repolarisation with the release trigger. Correction: K⁺ efflux through delayed rectifiers terminates the presynaptic action potential and closes Ca²⁺ channels; it does not itself trigger fusion.
- "Cl⁻ entry through ligand-gated GABA-A channels." — Reveals misconception: student conflates a postsynaptic inhibitory current with a presynaptic release trigger. Correction: GABA-A activation is a postsynaptic IPSP mechanism; vesicle fusion is triggered by presynaptic Ca²⁺ entry.
- "Na⁺ influx through voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in the terminal membrane." — Reveals misconception: student equates the upstroke of the action potential with the release trigger. Correction: Na⁺ entry depolarises the terminal and opens Ca²⁺ channels; it is the resulting Ca²⁺ entry — not the Na⁺ entry itself — that binds synaptotagmin and triggers fusion.

Explanation: Depolarisation of the presynaptic terminal opens voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels (Cav2.1 — P/Q-type, and Cav2.2 — N-type) clustered at the active zone. The local rise in cytoplasmic [Ca²⁺] within microdomains (~10–100 µM transiently) binds to synaptotagmin and triggers SNARE-mediated vesicle fusion within ~0.5 ms [Berne & Levy ch.6; Guyton & Hall ch.46]. The amount of transmitter released is a fourth-power function of presynaptic [Ca²⁺] — small changes produce large effects. Although Na⁺ and K⁺ currents shape the action potential itself, only Ca²⁺ entry triggers fusion. Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome is caused by autoantibodies against presynaptic P/Q-type Ca²⁺ channels, reducing release.

Hints:

1. Which ion is the messenger between membrane depolarisation and vesicle fusion?
2. The presynaptic channels involved are voltage-gated.
3. The Ca²⁺ sensor at the vesicle is synaptotagmin.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The Ca²⁺ sensor that binds Ca²⁺ at the active zone and triggers fast vesicle fusion is best identified as:

Correct answer: Synaptotagmin.

Distractors:

- "Synaptobrevin (a v-SNARE)." — Reveals misconception: student confuses a SNARE protein with the Ca²⁺ sensor. Correction: synaptobrevin (VAMP) is the vesicle-side SNARE that engages with t-SNAREs to drive membrane fusion, but it does not bind Ca²⁺ directly; Ca²⁺ binding occurs at synaptotagmin's C2 domains.
- "Syntaxin (a t-SNARE)." — Reveals misconception: same confusion as above. Correction: syntaxin is a target-membrane SNARE; it cooperates with SNAP-25 and synaptobrevin to zipper membranes together but lacks Ca²⁺-binding C2 domains.
- "SNAP-25 (a t-SNARE)." — Reveals misconception: student again conflates SNARE machinery with Ca²⁺ sensing. Correction: SNAP-25 anchors to the plasma membrane via palmitoyl groups and contributes two helices to the SNARE four-helix bundle, but does not bind Ca²⁺.

Explanation: Synaptotagmin contains two cytoplasmic C2 domains (C2A, C2B) that bind Ca²⁺ ions; binding triggers the final fusion-pore opening of docked vesicles within ~0.5 ms [Berne & Levy ch.6 — Molecular Apparatus; Boron & Boulpaep ch.13]. The SNARE complex (synaptobrevin on the vesicle, syntaxin and SNAP-25 on the plasma membrane) zippers the membranes close together to a primed state, but is not itself the Ca²⁺ sensor. Botulinum and tetanus neurotoxins are zinc proteases that cleave SNARE proteins and abolish release: BoNT/A and BoNT/E cleave SNAP-25, BoNT/B/D/F/G and TeNT cleave synaptobrevin, BoNT/C cleaves syntaxin.

Hints:

1. The sensor has C2 domains that bind Ca²⁺.
2. It is a vesicle-membrane protein (not a SNARE).
3. Loss of function would prevent fast synchronous release.

QUESTION 5
Type: application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Spontaneous miniature end-plate potentials (mEPPs) recorded at the neuromuscular junction in the absence of nerve stimulation are best explained by:

Correct answer: Spontaneous fusion of single synaptic vesicles, each releasing a quantum of transmitter.

Distractors:

- "Random opening of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in the muscle fibre." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a presynaptic phenomenon to the postsynaptic membrane. Correction: mEPPs originate from the presynaptic terminal — single vesicle fusion events release a packet of ACh that activates postsynaptic nicotinic receptors; they are abolished by curare and by botulinum toxin.
- "Leakage of choline through the postsynaptic membrane." — Reveals misconception: student misunderstands the molecular basis of mEPPs. Correction: choline alone is not the ligand at the nicotinic receptor (ACh is), and mEPPs reflect packet-release of intact ACh, not slow leakage of breakdown products.
- "Spontaneous activation of acetylcholinesterase." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the role of AChE. Correction: AChE terminates transmitter action by hydrolysing ACh — its activation would reduce, not produce, postsynaptic potentials.

Explanation: mEPPs (~0.4 mV in the human end-plate, ≤1 mV in classical frog NMJ recordings) are produced by the spontaneous, random fusion of single synaptic vesicles. Evoked end-plate potentials are integer multiples of the mEPP amplitude — the cornerstone of Katz's quantal hypothesis [Berne & Levy ch.6 — Quantal Nature; Guyton & Hall ch.46; Costanzo — Synaptic Transmission]. Each vesicle contains roughly 2,000–10,000 ACh molecules and constitutes one quantum. Quantal analysis allows measurement of three parameters: the number of release sites (n), the probability of release per site (p), and the quantum size (q); evoked release amplitude = n × p × q.

Hints:

1. mEPPs occur in the absence of nerve stimulation.
2. Their amplitude is fixed and small; evoked EPPs are integer multiples of this.
3. The phenomenon is the basis of Katz's quantal hypothesis.

QUESTION 6
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: SNARE proteins required for synaptic vesicle fusion are cleaved by which class of toxins, leading to flaccid paralysis?

Correct answer: Botulinum and tetanus neurotoxins (zinc-dependent metalloproteases).

Distractors:

- "Cholera toxin." — Reveals misconception: student confuses a presynaptic SNARE-cleaving toxin with a Gαs-modifying toxin. Correction: cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gαs, locking it in an active state — a mechanism affecting cAMP signalling in intestinal epithelium, not SNARE proteins.
- "Pertussis toxin." — Reveals misconception: similar conflation with a G-protein-modifying toxin. Correction: pertussis toxin ADP-ribosylates Gαi (locking it inactive), affecting cAMP-mediated signalling — it does not cleave SNAREs.
- "α-Bungarotoxin." — Reveals misconception: student confuses a presynaptic SNARE-targeting toxin with a postsynaptic receptor blocker. Correction: α-bungarotoxin (from Bungarus snake venom) is an irreversible competitive antagonist at postsynaptic muscle nicotinic ACh receptors; it does not act presynaptically.

Explanation: Botulinum (BoNT) and tetanus (TeNT) toxins are zinc proteases that cleave specific SNARE proteins. BoNT acts predominantly at peripheral cholinergic terminals (causing flaccid paralysis) — BoNT/A and BoNT/E cleave SNAP-25; BoNT/B, D, F, G cleave synaptobrevin; BoNT/C cleaves syntaxin and SNAP-25. TeNT preferentially cleaves synaptobrevin in spinal inhibitory interneurons (Renshaw cells), abolishing glycine and GABA release and producing spastic paralysis (lockjaw, opisthotonus) [Berne & Levy ch.6; Boron & Boulpaep ch.13]. Therapeutic botulinum toxin (Botox) is used in dystonia, blepharospasm, hemifacial spasm, hyperhidrosis, chronic migraine, cosmetic indications, and overactive bladder.

Hints:

1. Two clinically relevant toxins cleave SNAREs.
2. One produces flaccid paralysis; the other paradoxically produces spasticity (by disinhibition).
3. They are zinc proteases.

QUESTION 7
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: An excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP) at a typical glutamatergic synapse is generated by:

Correct answer: Opening of postsynaptic ligand-gated cation channels (AMPA receptors) → net influx of Na⁺ (and some K⁺ efflux) → depolarisation toward 0 mV.

Distractors:

- "Closure of postsynaptic K⁺ channels by glutamate." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a slow modulatory mechanism to the dominant fast EPSP. Correction: closure of K⁺ channels does occur in some metabotropic responses (e.g., M-current closure by ACh at M1) but the fast glutamatergic EPSP is a Na⁺/K⁺ cation-channel current, not a K⁺-channel-closure phenomenon.
- "Influx of Cl⁻ through ligand-gated GABA-A channels." — Reveals misconception: student conflates IPSP with EPSP. Correction: Cl⁻ influx through GABA-A is the inhibitory postsynaptic mechanism (IPSP), not an EPSP.
- "Direct activation of postsynaptic Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase by glutamate." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a postsynaptic potential to a pump. Correction: the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase maintains gradients but does not produce a fast postsynaptic potential; EPSPs reflect channel opening, not pump activity.

Explanation: A fast EPSP at a glutamatergic synapse is generated mainly by AMPA receptors — non-selective cation channels that pass both Na⁺ (inward, depolarising) and K⁺ (outward) when bound by glutamate. The reversal potential (~0 mV) lies between ENa and EK, so opening drives the membrane potential from rest toward 0 mV — a depolarisation [Berne & Levy ch.6; Boron & Boulpaep ch.13]. NMDA receptors are also glutamate-gated but require simultaneous glutamate binding plus depolarisation (to relieve Mg²⁺ block) and pass Ca²⁺ in addition — they contribute to slower components of the EPSP and to plasticity. A typical single quantum produces an EPSP of ~0.1–1 mV at the cell body; spatial and temporal summation of many EPSPs is required to reach threshold.

Hints:

1. Think about which ion species moves and in which direction.
2. The reversal potential lies near 0 mV.
3. The receptor is a non-selective cation channel.

QUESTION 8
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: An inhibitory postsynaptic potential (IPSP) in adult mammalian CNS is most commonly generated by:

Correct answer: Cl⁻ influx through ligand-gated GABA-A or glycine receptors → hyperpolarisation toward ECl (~−70 mV), or "shunting" inhibition near rest.

Distractors:

- "Influx of Ca²⁺ through L-type voltage-gated channels." — Reveals misconception: student associates an inward Ca²⁺ current with inhibition. Correction: Ca²⁺ influx through L-type channels in postsynaptic dendrites tends to depolarise and contribute to plateau potentials and CICR — not to inhibition.
- "Closure of postsynaptic Cl⁻ channels by GABA." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the channel state. Correction: GABA opens GABA-A Cl⁻ channels (it does not close them), allowing Cl⁻ to flow according to its electrochemical gradient.
- "Influx of Na⁺ through ligand-gated AMPA channels." — Reveals misconception: student conflates IPSP with EPSP. Correction: AMPA-mediated Na⁺ influx produces a depolarising EPSP, not an inhibitory current.

Explanation: Adult CNS IPSPs are predominantly generated by Cl⁻-permeable channels: GABA-A (throughout the brain) and glycine receptors (mainly spinal cord and brainstem). With ECl typically near −70 mV — slightly more negative than the resting potential — channel opening produces hyperpolarising Cl⁻ influx [Berne & Levy ch.6; Costanzo — Synaptic Transmission]. When ECl is near rest, the dominant effect is "shunting" inhibition: opening Cl⁻ channels reduces input resistance, attenuating EPSPs without much voltage change. Importantly, in immature neurons (and in some dorsal root afferents) intracellular [Cl⁻] is high (because of the NKCC1 transporter), so ECl is more positive than rest and GABA-A activation is depolarising rather than hyperpolarising — relevant to neonatal seizure pharmacology. GABA-B and metabotropic glutamate group II/III receptors produce slower inhibitory effects via K⁺ channel opening.

Hints:

1. Two ion species commonly mediate fast inhibition.
2. The transmitters involved are GABA and glycine.
3. The relevant equilibrium potential lies just below the resting potential in mature neurons.

QUESTION 9
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The synaptic delay between presynaptic action potential arrival and postsynaptic current onset at a fast chemical synapse (e.g., NMJ) is approximately:

Correct answer: 0.5–1 ms.

Distractors:

- "5–10 µs (essentially instantaneous)." — Reveals misconception: student confuses chemical with electrical synaptic timing. Correction: electrical synapses have negligible delay; chemical synapses must complete Ca²⁺ entry, vesicle fusion, transmitter diffusion, and receptor binding — these collectively take ~0.5–1 ms.
- "10–50 ms." — Reveals misconception: student overestimates synaptic delay or confuses it with the EPSP rise time. Correction: although the postsynaptic response can take tens of ms to peak, the _delay_ from presynaptic AP to onset of postsynaptic current is much shorter (~0.5–1 ms).
- "100 ms or more." — Reveals misconception: student confuses synaptic delay with reaction time of the whole reflex. Correction: a monosynaptic spinal reflex (knee jerk) takes ~30 ms total — most of which is conduction; only ~1 ms is synaptic delay.

Explanation: Synaptic delay at a fast chemical synapse is ~0.5–1 ms and is dominated by the time required for voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels to open at the active zone, for Ca²⁺ to reach the sensor (synaptotagmin), and for SNARE-mediated fusion-pore opening; transmitter diffusion across the cleft (~20 nm) and receptor binding are very fast [Berne & Levy ch.6 — Synaptic Delay; Guyton & Hall ch.46]. At cooler temperatures or when Ca²⁺ entry is reduced, the delay increases. Multiple chemical synapses in series (e.g., polysynaptic reflexes) add up the synaptic delay, contributing to longer reflex latencies. Electrical synapses, by comparison, have essentially no delay.

Hints:

1. Most of the delay reflects presynaptic events, not postsynaptic ones.
2. The figure is sub-millisecond at body temperature.
3. The dominant rate-limiting step involves vesicle fusion.

## Pass 2 — Synaptic integration, plasticity, ionotropic vs metabotropic receptors, major small-molecule neurotransmitters

QUESTION 10
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: At the soma of a typical CNS neuron, an action potential is initiated when the summed postsynaptic potentials reach threshold at:

Correct answer: The axon hillock / initial segment, where voltage-gated Na⁺ channel density is highest and threshold is lowest.

Distractors:

- "The dendritic spine where the EPSP is largest." — Reveals misconception: student equates the site of input with the site of integration. Correction: although individual EPSPs are largest at their generation site, each EPSP attenuates as it spreads passively to the soma; the action potential is initiated where Na⁺ channels are most densely packed (the initial segment), not at the dendrite.
- "The presynaptic terminal of the next neuron in the circuit." — Reveals misconception: student confuses the postsynaptic action potential site with the next presynaptic terminal. Correction: action potential initiation occurs in the postsynaptic neuron's own initial segment, not in the next downstream cell.
- "The soma uniformly across its surface." — Reveals misconception: student assumes uniform excitability. Correction: Na⁺ channel density varies across the neuron — the axon initial segment has ~50× the density of the soma.

Explanation: Spatial and temporal summation of EPSPs and IPSPs occurs along dendrites and at the soma; the resulting voltage at the **axon hillock / initial segment** determines whether threshold is reached. The initial segment has the highest density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (Nav1.6) and therefore the lowest voltage threshold for action potential initiation (~−55 mV) [Berne & Levy ch.6; Costanzo — Synaptic Transmission]. The action potential then propagates orthodromically along the axon and antidromically into the soma/dendrites (somatic backpropagation, important for plasticity). Inhibitory inputs at or near the initial segment (axoaxonic synapses) are particularly powerful because they intercept output before it can be initiated.

Hints:

1. The site has the highest density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels.
2. It is where the axon emerges from the cell body.
3. Threshold is lowest there.

QUESTION 11
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Spatial vs temporal summation of postsynaptic potentials differ in that:

Correct answer: Spatial summation combines simultaneous inputs from multiple presynaptic neurons; temporal summation combines successive inputs from a single (or few) presynaptic neurons close together in time.

Distractors:

- "Spatial summation requires backpropagation; temporal summation does not." — Reveals misconception: student conflates plasticity mechanisms with simple summation. Correction: backpropagation is a mechanism for action-potential travel from soma to dendrites and is implicated in plasticity, not in passive summation of EPSPs.
- "Spatial summation depends on the membrane time constant; temporal summation does not." — Reveals misconception: student inverts which constant matters. Correction: spatial summation is governed by the length constant (λ); temporal summation is governed by the time constant (τ). Both are passive cable properties.
- "Spatial summation is excitatory only; temporal summation is inhibitory only." — Reveals misconception: student introduces an artificial sign restriction. Correction: both spatial and temporal summation can combine excitatory and/or inhibitory potentials.

Explanation: Spatial summation combines inputs from different presynaptic neurons arriving simultaneously at different sites on the postsynaptic membrane — its effectiveness depends on the membrane length constant (λ), which determines how far a passive potential decays before reaching the soma [Berne & Levy ch.6 — Synaptic Integration]. Temporal summation combines successive inputs at the same site arriving close together in time — its effectiveness depends on the membrane time constant (τ), which determines how long a postsynaptic potential persists before decay. Together they allow a single neuron to integrate thousands of inputs and decide whether to fire. Pathological loss of inhibition (e.g., strychnine — glycine receptor blockade in spinal cord) magnifies summation toward unrestrained excitation.

Hints:

1. Spatial = where; temporal = when.
2. The relevant cable parameters are λ and τ.
3. Both can combine EPSPs and IPSPs.

QUESTION 12
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Ionotropic and metabotropic receptors differ principally in that:

Correct answer: Ionotropic receptors are themselves ligand-gated ion channels (fast, ms timescale); metabotropic receptors are G-protein-coupled and signal indirectly through second messengers (slow, seconds to minutes).

Distractors:

- "Ionotropic receptors are intracellular; metabotropic receptors are membrane-bound." — Reveals misconception: student confuses ionotropic/metabotropic with nuclear/membrane receptors. Correction: both ionotropic and metabotropic receptors are membrane-bound; intracellular receptors are a separate class for lipophilic ligands (steroids, thyroid).
- "Both receptor classes have identical kinetics but differ in tissue distribution." — Reveals misconception: student dismisses functional distinction. Correction: the kinetic difference is fundamental — ionotropic produce ms-timescale responses, metabotropic produce s-to-min responses with amplification.
- "Ionotropic receptors are exclusively excitatory; metabotropic exclusively inhibitory." — Reveals misconception: student introduces an artificial sign restriction. Correction: both classes include excitatory and inhibitory examples (e.g., AMPA — ionotropic excitatory; GABA-A — ionotropic inhibitory; mGluR1/5 — metabotropic excitatory via Gq; GABA-B — metabotropic inhibitory via Gi/o).

Explanation: Ionotropic receptors integrate ligand binding and ion permeation into a single protein — opening produces direct, fast (ms timescale) postsynaptic currents (e.g., nicotinic ACh, AMPA, NMDA, GABA-A, glycine, 5HT3) [Berne & Levy ch.6; Boron & Boulpaep ch.13]. Metabotropic receptors are seven-transmembrane G-protein-coupled receptors that signal indirectly via heterotrimeric G proteins → second messengers (cAMP, IP3/DAG, Ca²⁺) → effector proteins (PKA, PKC, ion channel modulation) on a seconds-to-minutes timescale, with substantial amplification (e.g., muscarinic M1–M5, mGluR1–8, GABA-B, dopamine D1–D5, all adrenergic, all opioid, most peptide receptors). Ionotropic mediates fast neurotransmission; metabotropic mediates neuromodulation, synaptic plasticity, and longer-term changes.

Hints:

1. The two classes differ in mechanism and timescale.
2. One is itself a channel; the other works through second messengers.
3. ms vs seconds.

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Long-term potentiation (LTP) at hippocampal CA3-CA1 synapses canonically requires:

Correct answer: Coincidence of presynaptic glutamate release with postsynaptic depolarisation sufficient to relieve Mg²⁺ block of NMDA receptors, allowing Ca²⁺ entry and downstream kinase activation (CaMKII) → AMPA receptor insertion / phosphorylation.

Distractors:

- "Sustained presynaptic firing alone, without postsynaptic depolarisation." — Reveals misconception: student ignores the postsynaptic requirement and the Mg²⁺-block of NMDA. Correction: LTP is associative — presynaptic release alone (e.g., low-frequency stimulation) does not produce LTP; postsynaptic depolarisation is required to remove Mg²⁺ from NMDA channels.
- "Activation of postsynaptic GABA-A receptors and Cl⁻ entry." — Reveals misconception: student conflates an inhibitory mechanism with potentiation. Correction: GABA-A activation is inhibitory; LTP is mediated by glutamate / NMDA receptors and increased AMPA receptor function.
- "Inhibition of presynaptic Ca²⁺ entry." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the direction of plasticity. Correction: LTP is associated with increased synaptic efficacy; inhibition of Ca²⁺ entry would reduce, not increase, transmission and is more consistent with LTD-type changes.

Explanation: NMDA receptors are coincidence detectors: they open only when glutamate binds AND the postsynaptic membrane is sufficiently depolarised to expel the voltage-dependent Mg²⁺ block from the pore. This permits Ca²⁺ entry through NMDA receptors → activation of CaMKII (and other kinases including PKC, PKA, MAPK, ERK) → phosphorylation of AMPA GluA1 subunits and insertion of additional AMPA receptors at the postsynaptic density [Berne & Levy ch.6 — Synaptic Plasticity]. The result is a long-lasting (hours to days, with protein synthesis required for late-phase) increase in EPSP amplitude. LTP is the cellular correlate of associative learning and memory formation in the hippocampus. Long-term depression (LTD) involves modest, prolonged Ca²⁺ rises activating phosphatases (calcineurin → PP1) and AMPA receptor internalisation.

Hints:

1. The "coincidence detector" receptor is critical.
2. Mg²⁺ acts as a voltage-dependent gatekeeper.
3. The downstream messenger is Ca²⁺, the kinase is CaMKII.

QUESTION 14
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The principal excitatory neurotransmitter in the adult mammalian CNS is:

Correct answer: Glutamate.

Distractors:

- "Acetylcholine." — Reveals misconception: student names a neurotransmitter important at the neuromuscular junction and in autonomic ganglia / postganglionic parasympathetic / select CNS pathways but not the dominant CNS excitatory transmitter. Correction: ACh has important CNS roles (basal forebrain → cortex for attention/memory; brainstem; striatal interneurons) but glutamate is the principal fast excitatory transmitter in the adult CNS.
- "Dopamine." — Reveals misconception: student elevates a neuromodulator to the role of principal excitatory transmitter. Correction: dopamine acts mainly through metabotropic D1–D5 receptors as a neuromodulator in select pathways (nigrostriatal, mesolimbic, mesocortical, tuberoinfundibular), not as the dominant fast excitatory transmitter.
- "GABA." — Reveals misconception: student inverts excitation and inhibition. Correction: GABA is the principal _inhibitory_ neurotransmitter in adult CNS.

Explanation: Glutamate is the principal fast excitatory neurotransmitter throughout the adult mammalian CNS, acting on ionotropic AMPA, NMDA, and kainate receptors and on metabotropic mGluR1–8 [Berne & Levy ch.6; Costanzo — Synaptic Transmission]. Most cortical pyramidal cells, cerebellar granule cells, and many subcortical projection neurons are glutamatergic. Glutamate is recycled via the glutamate-glutamine cycle: synaptic glutamate is taken up by astrocyte EAATs, converted to glutamine by glutamine synthetase, returned to neurons, and reconverted to glutamate by glutaminase. 3. Excessive signalling causes excitotoxicity in stroke and other CNS injury.

Explanation: **Myasthenia gravis** is an autoimmune disorder with antibodies against the postsynaptic nicotinic ACh receptor (~85% of cases — anti-AChR), MuSK (~10%), or LRP4 (~5%). The reduced number of functional receptors causes a smaller endplate potential — sometimes failing to reach threshold, especially at higher firing frequencies → fluctuating fatigable weakness, classically affecting extraocular muscles (ptosis, diplopia), bulbar muscles, proximal limbs [Berne & Levy ch.6; Costanzo]. **Pyridostigmine** reversibly inhibits AChE, prolonging the life of synaptic ACh and increasing the chance that available receptors are activated. It improves symptoms but does not modify the autoimmune disease. **Definitive immunomodulatory therapy**: corticosteroids, azathioprine, mycophenolate, IVIG, plasmapheresis (in crisis), rituximab, eculizumab (in refractory anti-AChR-positive disease), efgartigimod (FcRn antagonist — reduces IgG including pathogenic antibodies). **Thymectomy** improves outcomes (especially in young patients with thymic hyperplasia or thymoma). **Myasthenic crisis** (severe weakness with respiratory failure) requires intubation, IVIG/plasmapheresis, treatment of precipitant. **Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome** (LEMS) is a related but distinct disorder — antibodies against presynaptic P/Q-type Ca²⁺ channels; weakness paradoxically improves with use; associated with small cell lung cancer; treated with 3,4-diaminopyridine.

Hints:

1. The drug acts at the cleft, not at the receptor.
2. It enhances signalling through _available_ receptors rather than restoring lost receptors.
3. The same mechanism (anticholinesterase) is also therapeutic in Alzheimer disease (donepezil, rivastigmine).

QUESTION 27
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: Which of the following synapses or junctions exemplifies the principle that loss of inhibition can produce a clinical picture indistinguishable from excessive excitation?

Correct answer: Strychnine (glycine receptor antagonist) blockade in the spinal cord — produces hyperreflexia, opisthotonus, and convulsions clinically indistinguishable from excessive excitation despite acting on inhibitory receptors.

Distractors:

- "Curare blockade at the NMJ." — Reveals misconception: student confuses an excitatory blockade with a disinhibition mechanism. Correction: curare blocks postsynaptic nicotinic ACh receptors at the _excitatory_ NMJ — producing flaccid paralysis. Disinhibition is not the mechanism.
- "Sildenafil action in the corpus cavernosum." — Reveals misconception: student introduces a vascular smooth muscle pharmacological mechanism unrelated to disinhibition. Correction: sildenafil prolongs cGMP signalling downstream of NO; it is not a disinhibition mechanism.
- "Atropine block of muscarinic receptors." — Reveals misconception: student confuses muscarinic blockade with disinhibition. Correction: atropine blocks muscarinic receptors postsynaptically, producing parasympathetic blockade (tachycardia, dry mouth, mydriasis) — not disinhibition.

Explanation: Strychnine and tetanus toxin both produce spastic paralysis by removing glycinergic and (for TeNT) GABAergic inhibition in the spinal cord, allowing α-motor neurons to fire unchecked. **Strychnine** is a competitive glycine receptor antagonist (and weakly active at GABA-A) — symptoms include hyperreflexia, painful muscle spasms, opisthotonus, trismus, and convulsions in the setting of preserved consciousness; the clinical picture mimics severe excitation despite the underlying mechanism being loss of inhibition [Berne & Levy ch.6; Costanzo]. The same principle underlies tetanus (TeNT cleaves synaptobrevin in spinal inhibitory interneurons) and explains why clinical features alone cannot distinguish "excess excitation" from "loss of inhibition" without knowing the responsible agent. The same conceptual point applies in higher CNS: epileptogenesis often reflects a shift in the excitation/inhibition balance — many anti-epileptic drugs work by enhancing GABAergic inhibition (benzodiazepines, barbiturates, vigabatrin) rather than by blocking excitatory transmission. Other examples: PCP and ketamine (NMDA antagonists — produce dissociation despite blocking an excitatory receptor, partly through disinhibition of cortical circuits); the "GABAergic disinhibition" of cortex also contributes to anaesthesia and to certain epileptic phenotypes.

Hints:

1. The drug or toxin in question is itself an antagonist of an _inhibitory_ receptor.
2. The clinical picture mimics excitation despite the mechanism being loss of inhibition.
3. Strychnine and tetanus illustrate the same principle.
