Stem: Why is the α-motor neuron called the "final common pathway"? Describe the convergent inputs and how the cell integrates them.

Model answer: Sherrington coined the term "final common pathway" because the α-motor neuron is the obligate target through which all motor commands — voluntary, reflex, autonomic via somatic muscles — must pass. There is no alternative route from CNS to skeletal muscle.

Convergent inputs:

- 10,000–200,000 synaptic terminals per α-motor neuron.
- Afferents:
  - Ia afferents (muscle spindle primary) — monosynaptic excitation of homonymous α-motor neuron (stretch reflex). Synergists also activated; antagonists inhibited via Ia inhibitory interneurons (reciprocal inhibition).
  - Ib afferents (GTO) — inhibition via interneurons (autogenic inhibition, inverse stretch reflex).
  - II afferents (muscle spindle secondary) — excitation, mostly via interneurons.
  - Cutaneous afferents — flexor withdrawal reflex.
  - Visceral afferents — visceromotor reflexes.
- Descending pathways:
  - Lateral corticospinal — Direct (~30%) on α-motor neurons + indirect via interneurons. Voluntary fine motor control, especially distal limbs.
  - Anterior corticospinal — Proximal trunk muscles.
  - Rubrospinal — Influences flexor tone.
  - Vestibulospinal — Postural reflexes.
  - Reticulospinal — Postural control, locomotion modulation.
  - Tectospinal — Head/eye orientation.
- Local interneurons:
  - Renshaw cells — Recurrent inhibition of α-motor neuron itself.
  - Ia inhibitory interneurons — Reciprocal inhibition of antagonist motor neurons.
  - Ib inhibitory interneurons — Autogenic inhibition.
  - Propriospinal interneurons — Coordinate intersegmental movements.
- Other inputs:
  - Modulatory (5-HT, NA, dopamine) from descending systems → modulate excitability.

How the cell integrates input:

The α-motor neuron is a "leaky integrator":

- Spatial summation — multiple synapses active at the same time → EPSPs sum.
- Temporal summation — successive EPSPs from same synapse before each decays → sum.
- Inhibitory inputs subtract; shunting inhibition reduces gain.
- Modulatory inputs (5-HT, NA) shift threshold and excitability.
- Output: AP frequency (Hz) coded for force and timing.

Why is this design important:

1. Single point of integration — All motor decisions converge on α-motor neuron output. This means descending pathways can't independently activate muscle without α-motor neuron compliance.

2. Force grading — Both recruitment (more α-motor neurons fire) and rate coding (each fires faster) grade muscle force.

3. Reflex protection — Spinal reflexes (stretch, GTO, withdrawal) automatical

Stem: Compare upper motor neuron (UMN) and lower motor neuron (LMN) lesions on every clinically relevant feature. How does the location of the lesion determine the clinical pattern?

Model answer: The distinction between UMN and LMN syndromes is one of the most clinically important in neurology. It immediately localises the lesion and dictates investigation and management.

Definitions:

- UMN — Neurons in cortex (Betz cells) and brainstem that project to spinal cord α-motor neurons. The "upper" because they're upstream.
- LMN — α-motor neurons in spinal cord ventral horn (and brainstem motor cranial nerve nuclei) and their axons. The "lower" because they're downstream.

The Comparison:

| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Tone                     | Spastic (clasp-knife)                                                                            | Flaccid                                          |
| Reflexes                 | Hyperreflexia, clonus                                                                            | Areflexia, hyporeflexia                          |
| Plantar response         | Babinski (extensor)                                                                              | Absent or normal (downgoing)                     |
| Atrophy                  | None initially; may develop later from disuse                                                    | Severe, from denervation                         |
| Fasciculations           | Absent                                                                                           | Present                                          |
| Fibrillations on EMG     | Absent                                                                                           | Present                                          |
| Sphincter                | Often spared (cortical control)                                                                  | Often involved (lower segments)                  |
| Distribution of weakness | "Pyramidal pattern" (extensors weaker than flexors in arm; flexors weaker than extensors in leg) | Confined to muscles supplied by affected neurons |
| Onset                    | Variable; often acute (stroke)                                                                   | Variable; often chronic (motor neuron disease)   |
| Sensation                | Variable (depending on lesion)                                                                   | Often spared (separate pathways)                 |

Why each feature differs:

1. Tone (spasticity vs flaccidity):

- UMN: Loss of cortical inhibition over spinal stretch reflex circuits → exaggerated stretch reflex → spasticity.
- LMN: α-motor neuron unable to fire → muscle has no tonic activity → flaccidity.

2. Reflexes:

- UMN: Stretch reflex circuit is intact in cord but lacks normal cortical inhibition → exaggerated.
- LMN: α-motor neuron destroyed → no efferent arm of reflex → absent.

3. Babinski sign:

- UMN: Loss of corticospinal modulation of plantar reflex; stimulus → toe extension. Pathognomonic of UMN lesion in adults. Normal in infants <1 year (immature corticospinal).
- LMN: Reflex arc broken; no response or normal flexor response.

4. Atrophy:

- UMN: α-motor neuron intact → muscle innervation maintained → minimal atrophy initially.
- LMN: Denervation → muscle fibres atrophy rapidly (significant within weeks).

5. Fasciculations:

- UMN: α-motor neuron firing controlled (even if dysregulated) → no spontaneous twitches.
- LMN: Denervation supersensitivity in muscle → spontaneous depolarisations of remaining motor units → visible twitches under skin.

Stem: Describe the basal ganglia direct and indirect pathways. How does dopamine modulate them, and how does loss of dopamine produce Parkinson disease?

Model answer: The basal ganglia (BG) are a set of subcortical nuclei central to motor selection, scaling, and adjustment. Their main circuit comprises two parallel pathways — direct (movement-promoting) and indirect (movement-suppressing) — modulated by dopamine.

The basal ganglia nuclei:

- Striatum = caudate + putamen.
- Globus pallidus = external segment (GPe) + internal segment (GPi).
- Subthalamic nucleus (STN).
- Substantia nigra = pars compacta (SNc, dopaminergic) + pars reticulata (SNpr, GABAergic, equivalent to GPi for face/eye movements).

Direct pathway (movement-promoting):

Cortex → Striatum → GPi (and SNpr) → Thalamus → Cortex.

Logic:

- Striatum projects GABAergic (inhibitory) onto GPi.
- GPi normally tonically inhibits thalamus.
- When striatum fires, it inhibits GPi.
- Reduced GPi firing → less inhibition of thalamus → thalamus more active → cortical motor areas more activated → movement promoted.

Indirect pathway (movement-suppressing):

Cortex → Striatum → GPe → STN → GPi → Thalamus → Cortex.

Logic:

- Striatum inhibits GPe (GABA).
- GPe inhibits STN (GABA).
- STN excites GPi (glutamate).
- When striatum fires, it inhibits GPe → STN released from inhibition → STN excites GPi → more inhibition of thalamus → less movement.

Dopamine modulation:

SNc neurons release dopamine onto striatum:

- D1 receptors (on striatal neurons projecting to direct pathway) — Excitatory effect → activates direct pathway.
- D2 receptors (on striatal neurons projecting to indirect pathway) — Inhibitory effect → suppresses indirect pathway.

Net effect of dopamine: promotes movement (activates direct + suppresses indirect).

Tonic vs phasic dopamine:

- Tonic dopamine maintains baseline muscle tone and movement readiness.
- Phasic dopamine (in response to reward, novel stimuli) reinforces actions and learning.

Hyperdirect pathway:

- Cortex → STN directly (faster than indirect pathway).
- Provides rapid braking signal to suppress competing movements ("stop now").
- Important for action selection.

Parkinson disease — what happens:

Loss of SNc dopaminergic neurons → reduced dopamine in striatum → reduced D1 stimulation → reduced direct pathway activity AND increased D2-mediated indirect pathway activity → net result: increased GPi activity → increased thalamic inhibition → reduced cortical motor activity → bradykinesia, rigidity.

- Likely involves abnormal oscillatory activity in basal ganglia-thalamocortical circuits.

Stem: The cerebellum is described as comparing "intended" vs "actual" movement. Describe its functional architecture and how it produces coordinated, accurate movement.

Model answer: The cerebellum has been called a "motor calibrator" — receiving copies of motor commands ("efference copies" or "corollary discharge") + actual movement feedback (proprioceptive, vestibular, visual) → comparing them → adjusting output to minimise error.

Functional divisions (based on input-output connections):

1. Vestibulocerebellum (flocculonodular lobe + posterior vermis):
   - Receives vestibular afferents.
   - Outputs to vestibular nuclei.
   - Functions: balance, postural reflexes, VOR calibration.
   - Lesions: truncal ataxia, falls, nystagmus.

2. Spinocerebellum (vermis + paravermis):
   - Receives spinal afferents (proprioception via DSCT, VSCT, CSCT).
   - Receives trigeminal, visual, auditory.
   - Outputs to fastigial and interposed nuclei → brainstem motor pathways.
   - Functions: postural and gait control, ongoing limb coordination.
   - Lesions: ataxia of gait (vermis), appendicular ataxia (paravermis).

3. Cerebrocerebellum (lateral hemispheres):
   - Receives from cerebral cortex via pontine nuclei.
   - Outputs via dentate → contralateral red nucleus, thalamus → motor cortex.
   - Functions: motor planning, fine coordination, motor learning.
   - Lesions: appendicular ataxia, dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesia, intention tremor.

Cerebellar microcircuit (uniform across regions):

Inputs come via two pathways:

1. Mossy fibres (from spinal cord, brainstem, pontine nuclei):

- Synapse on granule cells (most numerous neurons in CNS!) in granular layer.
- Granule cells project up through Purkinje cell layer → form parallel fibres in molecular layer.
- Parallel fibres synapse on Purkinje cell dendrites.

2. Climbing fibres (from inferior olive only):

- One climbing fibre per Purkinje cell (1:1 monogamous synapse).
- Powerful excitatory input — single climbing fibre AP triggers complex spike in Purkinje.
- Believed to provide "error" or "teaching" signal.

Purkinje cells (the only output of cerebellar cortex):

- Located in single layer between granular and molecular layers.
- Use GABA — inhibitory.
- Project to deep cerebellar nuclei (fastigial, interposed, dentate).
- Deep cerebellar nuclei use glutamate — excitatory output to brainstem and thalamus.

So the cerebellar cortex inhibits the deep nuclei. Purkinje firing rate determines deep nuclear output. Climbing fibres modulate Purkinje activity (LTD with climbing + parallel fibre coactivation — the substrate of motor learning).

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the cerebellar functional anatomy and its role in motor coordination. How do cerebellar lesions present?

Model answer: _Cerebellum_ - the "little brain" controlling motor coordination, balance, and motor learning. Functional divisions: (1) _Spinocerebellum_ (vermis + paravermis) - receives spinal afferents (proprioception, exteroception); coordinates posture (vermis -> trunk) and limb movements (paravermis -> limbs); projects via fastigial and interposed nuclei to red nucleus and motor cortex. (2) _Cerebrocerebellum_ (lateral hemispheres) - receives massive corticopontocerebellar input; coordinates planning and timing of skilled voluntary movements; projects via dentate -> ventrolateral thalamus -> motor cortex. (3) _Vestibulocerebellum_ (flocculonodular lobe) - receives vestibular input; controls balance, posture, eye movements (smooth pursuit, VOR); projects via fastigial to vestibular nuclei. Microcircuit: input (mossy fibres from spinocerebellar tracts and pontine nuclei; climbing fibres from inferior olive) -> granule cells -> parallel fibres -> Purkinje cells (sole output of cortex) -> deep cerebellar nuclei -> motor system. Climbing fibres deliver "error signals" that drive motor learning via long-term depression at parallel fibre-Purkinje cell synapses. Lesions: ipsilateral signs (cerebellar output crosses twice). (1) Vermis lesion - truncal ataxia, gait ataxia (alcoholic cerebellar degeneration). (2) Hemispheric lesion - ipsilateral limb ataxia (intention tremor, dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesia, decomposition of movement), dysarthria, hypotonia. (3) Flocculonodular lobe - vestibular ataxia, nystagmus, gait disturbance (medulloblastoma in children). Causes: stroke (PICA, AICA, SCA territories), tumour, MS, alcohol, paraneoplastic, Friedreich ataxia, hereditary ataxias. Tests: heel-shin, finger-nose, rapid alternating movements, tandem gait, Romberg [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: The cerebellum is a comparator: it compares the motor command (efference copy from cortex) with the actual movement (sensory feedback) and corrects the discrepancy. Its lesions produce _coordination_ deficits, not weakness - movements are made but inaccurately, with overshoot, oscillation, and poor timing.

Hints:

1. Three divisions: spino, cerebro, vestibulocerebellum.
2. Climbing fibres = error signals; Purkinje cells = output.
3. Lesions are ipsilateral; coordination deficit not weakness.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the three functional divisions.
- Identifies Purkinje cells as cortical output and inferior olive as climbing fibre source.
- Describes ≥ 3 cerebellar lesion signs (ataxia, intention tremor, dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesia).
- Notes that signs are ipsilateral.
- Mentions ≥ 2 causes (stroke, alcohol, tumour, MS).

A Yellow answer covers anatomy but misses the comparator/error-signal logic.

A Red answer confuses cerebellar with motor cortex or basal ganglia signs.

- "Cerebellar lesions cause weakness": Correction: Cerebellar lesions cause _coordination_ deficits, not weakness; movements occur but are dysmetric and tremulous.
- "Cerebellar signs are contralateral": Correction: Cerebellar signs are _ipsilateral_ because cerebellar output crosses twice (decussation in superior peduncle, then thalamocortical and corticospinal recrossing).

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the descending motor pathways: corticospinal, rubrospinal, vestibulospinal, and reticulospinal. What does each control?

Model answer: _Corticospinal tract_ - fine voluntary movement, especially of distal limbs (hands, fingers). Origin: motor cortex (M1, premotor, supplementary motor) and somatosensory cortex; ~85-90% decussate at the pyramidal decussation in lower medulla -> _lateral corticospinal tract_ (controls contralateral distal limbs); ~10-15% remain ipsilateral as _anterior corticospinal tract_ and decussate near the level of innervation (controls bilateral axial/proximal muscles). Synapse on alpha motor neurons in anterior horn. _Rubrospinal tract_ - facilitates upper limb flexion. Origin: red nucleus in midbrain; decussates immediately; descends in lateral cord. In humans, smaller and less important than in quadrupeds; largely subsumed by corticospinal. _Vestibulospinal tract_ - postural control, antigravity muscles. Origin: vestibular nuclei (medial nucleus -> medial vestibulospinal -> cervical/thoracic axial muscles for head-neck stability; lateral nucleus -> lateral vestibulospinal -> ipsilateral antigravity extensor muscles for balance). Activated by vestibular and proprioceptive input. _Reticulospinal tracts_ - postural and locomotor control, autonomic, reflex modulation. Origin: pontine reticular formation -> _medial pontine reticulospinal_ (facilitates extensors); medullary reticular formation -> _lateral medullary reticulospinal_ (inhibits extensors). Bilateral; under cortical influence. Modulates reflexes, rhythm of locomotion (central pattern generators), respiratory and cardiovascular function. Clinical: corticospinal lesions -> contralateral spastic hemiparesis (UMN: weakness, hyperreflexia, Babinski, spasticity); brainstem lesions disrupt rubrospinal/reticulospinal -> _decerebrate posturing_ (lesion below red nucleus -> extensor posturing) vs _decorticate posturing_ (lesion above red nucleus -> flexor posturing in arms, extensor in legs - because rubrospinal flexor influence is preserved) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: The descending motor systems are organised hierarchically: corticospinal for fine voluntary distal movements; rubrospinal as a parallel flexor pathway (much reduced in humans); vestibulospinal for posture and balance; reticulospinal for proximal/axial control, locomotor rhythm, and reflex modulation. Decerebrate vs decorticate posturing reveals which descending tracts are spared.

Hints:

1. Corticospinal: fine distal voluntary; pyramidal decussation.
2. Rubrospinal: flexor; minor in humans.
3. Vestibulospinal: posture, antigravity.
4. Reticulospinal: proximal/axial, locomotor rhythm.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names all four tracts and their primary functions.
- Identifies the pyramidal decussation in lower medulla.
- Describes corticospinal control of distal limbs vs vestibulospinal/reticulospinal of proximal/axial.
- Notes the difference between decerebrate and decorticate posturing.
- Names UMN signs (hyperreflexia, spasticity, Babinski).

A Yellow answer covers corticospinal but misses the brainstem tracts or posturing.

A Red answer treats all motor pathways as equivalent.

Common misconceptions:

- "All motor signals come from corticospinal tract": Correction: Corticospinal handles fine distal voluntary movement; brainstem tracts (vestibulospinal, reticulospinal) handle posture and proximal/axial control.
- "Decerebrate and decorticate posturing are interchangeable": Correction: Decerebrate (extensor) vs decorticate (flexor arms, extensor legs) reflects different lesion levels - decerebrate is below the red nucleus.

Stem: A 65-year-old presents with a resting tremor of the right hand, bradykinesia, and rigidity. Decompose Parkinson disease.

Model answer: _Parkinson disease (PD)_ - neurodegenerative disorder of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc), producing motor and non-motor features. Pathophysiology: alpha-synuclein aggregation (Lewy bodies, Lewy neurites) -> SNc neuron loss -> reduced dopaminergic innervation of striatum -> imbalance of basal ganglia direct/indirect pathways. Direct pathway underactive (D1 receptors -> reduced disinhibition of thalamus -> reduced movement initiation); indirect pathway overactive (D2 receptors normally inhibit indirect pathway; without D2, pathway facilitates STN-GPi excessive output -> excessive thalamic inhibition -> bradykinesia, rigidity). Clinical features (cardinal): (1) Resting tremor (3-5 Hz, "pill-rolling", asymmetric onset, decreases with action); (2) Bradykinesia/akinesia (slowness; reduced amplitude; freezing); (3) Rigidity (cogwheel - tremor superimposed on lead-pipe; affects neck, limbs); (4) Postural instability (later feature). Non-motor: anosmia (often precedes motor by years), REM sleep behaviour disorder, constipation, depression/anxiety, autonomic dysfunction, cognitive decline (PD dementia in 30-50% over time). Other features: masked facies, soft hypophonic speech, micrographia, festinating gait, stooped posture. Diagnosis: clinical (UK PDS Brain Bank criteria); supportive - response to L-DOPA; DAT-SPECT shows reduced striatal dopamine transporter binding. Differential: essential tremor (action, bilateral, no other features), atypical parkinsonism (PSP - vertical gaze palsy and falls; MSA - autonomic + cerebellar; corticobasal - asymmetric apraxia and alien limb), drug-induced (haloperidol, metoclopramide), vascular parkinsonism, dementia with Lewy bodies (DLB - cognitive prominent, fluctuations, visual hallucinations). Treatment: (1) Symptomatic - L-DOPA + carbidopa (most effective; 5-10 year window before motor fluctuations and dyskinesias); dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole - useful in younger, but impulse control disorders); MAO-B inhibitors (selegiline, rasagiline); COMT inhibitors (entacapone) prolong L-DOPA effect; amantadine for dyskinesias; (2) Non-motor - antidepressants, treatment of constipation, sleep, autonomic; (3) Advanced disease - apomorphine pump, intrajejunal L-DOPA gel, deep brain stimulation (subthalamic nucleus or GPi - excellent for refractory motor symptoms in well-selected patients). Disease-modifying therapy is an unmet need; current trials target alpha-synuclein, GBA pathway [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: PD illustrates how loss of a single neurotransmitter system (dopaminergic SNc -> striatum) produces a stereotyped clinical syndrome explainable by direct/indirect pathway dysfunction. L-DOPA was a triumph of physiology-driven therapy - giving back the missing neurotransmitter precursor. The motor fluctuations after years of L-DOPA reflect progressive loss of dopaminergic terminals to buffer the pulsatile delivery.

Hints:

1. Cardinal: tremor, bradykinesia, rigidity, postural instability.
2. Mechanism: SNc dopaminergic loss -> direct/indirect imbalance.
3. L-DOPA + carbidopa is most effective; DBS for refractory.

- Names L-DOPA + carbidopa as primary therapy and DBS for advanced disease.
- Mentions ≥ 2 non-motor features (anosmia, RBD, constipation, depression).

A Yellow answer covers PD motor features but misses the non-motor features or pathway logic.

- "PD is purely a motor disorder": Correction: PD has prominent non-motor features (anosmia, RBD, constipation, autonomic dysfunction, dementia) often preceding motor onset by years.
- "L-DOPA cures PD": Correction: L-DOPA is symptomatic; the disease progresses; after 5-10 years motor fluctuations and dyskinesias develop; DBS or apomorphine help advanced disease.

Stem: A 50-year-old develops chorea, behavioural changes, and cognitive decline. Family history of similar illness in father at age 50. Decompose Huntington disease.

Model answer: _Huntington disease_ - autosomal dominant trinucleotide (CAG) repeat expansion in the HTT gene on chromosome 4, producing a polyglutamine-expanded huntingtin protein that aggregates and causes selective neurodegeneration (most severely in striatum - medium spiny neurons of caudate and putamen). Genetics: > 35 CAG repeats - pathogenic; > 39 - fully penetrant; longer repeats -> earlier onset; _anticipation_ (paternal transmission tends to expand further -> earlier onset in successive generations - juvenile HD with paternal inheritance). Pathophysiology: striatal medium spiny neurons die preferentially; _indirect pathway_ affected first (D2 receptor MSNs project from striatum to GPe) -> disinhibition of thalamic motor relay -> excessive thalamic-cortical drive -> hyperkinetic chorea. Later, direct pathway also lost -> bradykinesia and rigidity emerge in late disease. Clinical features: (1) Motor - chorea (involuntary, irregular, dance-like movements), dystonia, parkinsonism in late disease, dysarthria, dysphagia; (2) Cognitive - executive dysfunction, attention, memory (subcortical dementia pattern); progresses to global dementia; (3) Psychiatric - depression (high suicide risk), anxiety, irritability, apathy, OCD, psychosis; (4) Other - weight loss, sleep disturbance. Onset typically 30-50 years; juvenile-onset (< 20 years, paternal inheritance) presents with Westphal variant - bradykinesia, rigidity, seizures, faster decline. Diagnosis: clinical features + family history + genetic testing for CAG repeat. Imaging: caudate atrophy with ex-vacuo dilatation of frontal horns ("box-shaped" lateral ventricles). Management: no disease-modifying therapy currently; (1) Chorea - tetrabenazine, deutetrabenazine (VMAT2 inhibitors deplete dopamine); antipsychotics (olanzapine, risperidone) when comorbid psychiatric symptoms; (2) Depression - SSRIs; (3) Parkinsonism in late disease - cautious L-DOPA may worsen chorea; (4) Multidisciplinary care - PT, OT, SLT, dietitian, social work, genetic counselling for family; (5) Predictive testing for at-risk family members - careful counselling required. Stem: Why is the cerebellum often called a "comparator" or "forward model"? Discuss the role of cerebellar internal models in motor control and learning.

Model answer: The cerebellum is widely conceptualised as housing internal models for motor control. (1) _Forward model_ - given a motor command (efference copy from cortex), predict the _expected_ sensory consequences. (2) _Inverse model_ - given a desired outcome, compute the motor command needed. The cerebellum implements both. Mechanism: (1) Mossy fibres carry context (current state, motor intent) from spinal cord and pontine nuclei to granule cells; (2) Climbing fibres from inferior olive deliver _error signals_ - actual minus expected sensory feedback - to Purkinje cells; (3) Long-term depression at parallel fibre - Purkinje cell synapses adjusts the model to reduce future errors; (4) Output via deep cerebellar nuclei refines motor commands sent back via thalamus to cortex. Functional benefits: (a) _Motor accuracy_ - online correction of ongoing movements before sensory feedback could arrive (sensory feedback is slow; predictions allow real-time correction); (b) _Motor learning_ - errors drive plasticity, refining motor programmes (e.g., learning to throw a ball, play piano); (c) _Anticipatory postural adjustments_ - prepare for predictable perturbations (e.g., stabilising trunk before lifting an arm); (d) _Vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR) calibration_ - adjusts gain as growing/aging changes; (e) _Cognitive cerebellum_ - extending the same model concept beyond motor to cognitive and emotional domains (Schmahmann syndrome - cerebellar cognitive-affective syndrome). Evidence: (1) Lesion studies - patients show dysmetria (overshoot/undershoot from imperfect prediction), tremor (oscillatory correction), dysdiadochokinesia (poor timing); cannot adapt to new motor demands. (2) Imaging - cerebellum activates during motor learning, error correction, prediction. (3) Behavioural - prism adaptation experiments show cerebellar role in recalibrating sensorimotor maps. (4) Computational neuroscience - models match cerebellar architecture to error-driven adaptive control. Clinical: cerebellar lesions disrupt prediction -> poorly timed/scaled movements; rehabilitation exploits residual learning capacity through motor practice. The same forward-model framework explains why athletes, musicians, and surgeons rely on long cerebellar training - they are building accurate internal models. The Marr-Albus-Ito theory (1969-1971) was a foundational proposal of cerebellar function as a learning machine; experimental work continues to refine it [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13; Berne & Levy ch.7].

Elaborative explanation: The cerebellar comparator concept unifies its role across motor learning, prediction, error correction, and cognitive functions. Internal models are a powerful explanatory framework - the cerebellum is essentially a learning machine that minimises prediction error. Modern neuroscience extends this to cerebellar contributions to language, attention, and emotion.

3. Climbing fibre error signals drive plasticity (LTD).

- Defines forward and inverse models.

- Notes LTD at parallel fibre - Purkinje synapses as the learning rule.

A Red answer treats cerebellum as a passive coordinator.

Stem: A 50-year-old has a stroke with right-sided weakness that initially shows flaccidity and hyporeflexia, then progresses to spasticity and hyperreflexia over weeks. Explain the time-course of UMN lesion features.

Mechanism: sudden loss of descending input - including both excitatory (corticospinal direct/indirect) and inhibitory (descending modulation of stretch reflex circuits) components - leaves spinal circuits in an _under-driven_ state. The motor neurons hyperpolarise and become less excitable; reflex arcs are temporarily "silenced". Subacute phase (days to weeks): gradual return of reflexes, often initially normal, then _hyperactive_. Mechanism: (1) Loss of _tonic descending inhibition_ normally provided by reticulospinal/corticobulbar tracts; (2) Compensatory plasticity in spinal circuits - increased motor neuron excitability, sprouting of intact afferent terminals; (3) Adaptive changes in interneuron circuits that mediate stretch reflexes. Chronic phase (weeks to months): full UMN syndrome - (1) _Spasticity_ - velocity-dependent increase in tone (clasp-knife); (2) _Hyperreflexia_ with clonus; (3) _Babinski sign_ - abnormal extensor plantar response; (4) Pyramidal weakness pattern (extensors weaker than flexors in arms; flexors weaker than extensors in legs); (5) No fasciculations or atrophy (unlike LMN). Recovery: (1) _Spontaneous recovery_ via reorganisation of intact pathways (corticospinal tracts can reorganise to use anterior corticospinal pathway, alternative routes); (2) _Plasticity-driven recovery_ in early weeks-months; some recovery continues for years; (3) _Rehabilitation_ exploits this plasticity through task-specific training, constraint-induced movement therapy, mirror therapy. Clinical implications: (1) Acute flaccidity does not exclude UMN lesion - the temporal evolution is the giveaway; (2) Spasticity is not just "paralysis with stiffness" - it reflects positive UMN signs from disinhibition; (3) Treatment of spasticity (baclofen, tizanidine, botulinum toxin, intrathecal baclofen pump for severe cases) addresses excessive tone but does not improve underlying weakness; (4) Rehabilitation works best when started early. In spinal cord injury, the duration of spinal shock is variable (days-weeks) and correlates with severity [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: The temporal evolution of UMN signs is a beautiful example of how loss of one balanced neural input system gradually reveals adaptations in the spinal circuit. Clinically, recognising this evolution is essential - early flaccidity does not mean LMN lesion; spasticity is a _positive_ sign, not just persistent weakness.

2. Subacute-chronic: spastic + hyperreflexive (full UMN).
3. Mechanism: loss of descending inhibition + circuit plasticity.

Stem: Synthesise the basal ganglia direct and indirect pathways. How does the model explain hyperkinetic and hypokinetic movement disorders, and what are its limitations?

Model answer: The classic basal ganglia model (Albin-Young-Penney 1989, refined since) explains movement disorders via two opposing pathways. _Direct pathway_: cortex -> striatum (D1-expressing MSNs) -> internal globus pallidus / substantia nigra reticulata (GPi/SNr) (inhibitory) -> thalamus (disinhibited) -> cortex (excited) -> _facilitates movement_. _Indirect pathway_: cortex -> striatum (D2-expressing MSNs) -> external globus pallidus (GPe) (inhibitory) -> subthalamic nucleus (STN) (disinhibited - GPe normally inhibits STN; reduced GPe inhibition -> increased STN activity) -> GPi/SNr (excited) -> thalamus (more inhibited) -> cortex (less excited) -> _suppresses unwanted movement_. _Dopamine modulation_: dopamine from SNc has _opposite_ effects on the two pathways - excites D1 (direct, facilitatory) and inhibits D2 (indirect, less inhibition of thalamic motor relay). Net effect of dopamine: facilitates movement. Hypokinetic disorders (Parkinson disease): SNc dopaminergic loss -> reduced D1 excitation (direct path underactive) and reduced D2 inhibition of indirect path (indirect path overactive) -> excessive thalamic inhibition -> bradykinesia, rigidity. Hyperkinetic disorders: (1) _Huntington disease_ - early loss of D2 striatal MSNs (indirect pathway) -> reduced indirect pathway suppression of unwanted movement -> chorea; later loss of direct pathway as well -> bradykinesia in late HD; (2) _Hemiballismus_ - subthalamic nucleus lesion -> reduced indirect pathway output -> hyperkinetic large-amplitude flinging movements; (3) _Tardive dyskinesia_ - chronic D2 antagonist exposure -> D2 receptor upregulation/sensitisation -> indirect pathway dysfunction -> hyperkinetic movements. Therapeutic implications: (1) L-DOPA in PD restores dopaminergic input; (2) Deep brain stimulation of STN or GPi reduces excessive output to thalamus, improving PD; (3) Dopamine antagonists worsen PD but help chorea; (4) Anticholinergics (trihexyphenidyl) help PD tremor by reducing striatal cholinergic interneuron activity. Limitations of the classic model: (1) Oversimplified - direct/indirect pathways are not strictly anatomically segregated; (2) Hyperdirect pathway (cortex -> STN bypassing striatum) is important for action cancellation; (3) Striatal interneurons (cholinergic, GABAergic) play significant roles; (4) DBS works in ways not fully predicted by the model (does not simply silence STN); (5) Dopamine has receptor diversity (D1-D5) and complex temporal dynamics; (6) Cognitive and affective basal ganglia loops parallel the motor loop; (7) The model does not fully explain tics, dystonia, or essential tremor; (8) Recent work emphasises basal ganglia in _reinforcement learning_ (dopamine as reward prediction error) - extending function beyond motor control. Despite limitations, the direct/indirect framework remains pedagogically powerful and clinically useful [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13].

Elaborative explanation: The basal ganglia direct/indirect model is one of the most successful pedagogical frameworks in neurology. Its core claim - that movement disorders reflect imbalance between facilitatory and suppressive pathways - explains a huge range of clinical phenomena. Modern refinements (hyperdirect pathway, reinforcement learning, complex circuitry) build on rather than replace the classic model.

Hints:

1. Direct: facilitates movement; indirect: suppresses unwanted.
2. Dopamine excites D1 (direct), inhibits D2 (indirect) - net facilitation.
3. PD = both pathways unfavourable; HD = indirect lost first.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Diagrams the direct and indirect pathways with anatomical detail.
- Identifies dopamine's opposite effects on D1 and D2 receptors.
- Maps PD, HD, hemiballismus, and tardive dyskinesia to the model.
- Mentions DBS targets (STN, GPi) and their rationale.
- Acknowledges ≥ 2 limitations (hyperdirect pathway, reinforcement learning, segregation).

A Yellow answer covers the model but misses the limitations or specific clinical mappings.

A Red answer treats movement disorders as unrelated entities.

Common misconceptions:

- "Direct and indirect pathways are anatomically separate.": Correction: There is overlap; modern data show D1/D2 MSNs can co-express receptors; the segregation is functional more than absolute.
- "DBS works by silencing the stimulated nucleus.": Correction: DBS works by complex network effects (depolarisation block, stimulus-induced gamma oscillations, modulation of pathological synchrony) - not simple inhibition.

QUESTION 17
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 6-year-old presents with sudden-onset weakness and absent reflexes after a recent viral illness. Decompose Guillain-Barré syndrome.

Model answer: _Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS)_ - acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy (AIDP, the most common Western form), an autoimmune attack on peripheral nerve myelin or axons triggered by molecular mimicry following infection (Campylobacter jejuni - 30%; Mycoplasma; CMV; EBV; influenza; SARS-CoV-2; rarely vaccinations). Other variants: AMAN (acute motor axonal neuropathy), AMSAN (motor + sensory axonal), Miller-Fisher syndrome (ophthalmoplegia + ataxia + areflexia), pharyngeal-cervical-brachial. Pathophysiology: antibodies against gangliosides (GM1, GD1a, GQ1b in MFS) cross-react with peripheral myelin or nodes of Ranvier -> demyelination or axonal injury -> conduction block -> weakness. Clinical features: (1) Ascending symmetric weakness over hours-days, classically starting in legs; (2) _Areflexia_ - hallmark; (3) Mild distal sensory symptoms (paraesthesiae, pain); (4) Cranial nerve involvement (facial palsy bilateral, bulbar); (5) Autonomic dysfunction (arrhythmias, BP fluctuation, ileus); (6) Respiratory failure in ~25% (FVC < 20 mL/kg or rapidly declining triggers ICU admission). Diagnosis: (1) Clinical features (Brighton criteria); (2) CSF - _albumino-cytological dissociation_ (raised protein > 0.45 g/L with normal cell count, present in ~80% by week 1-2; may be normal early); (3) Nerve conduction studies - demyelinating features in AIDP (slowed velocities, prolonged distal latencies, conduction block, absent F waves); axonal loss in AMAN; (4) Anti-ganglioside antibodies in selected variants (GQ1b in MFS). Management: (1) ICU monitoring - vital capacity hourly, ECG monitoring, autonomic surveillance; (2) Mechanical ventilation when indicated; (3) IV immunoglobulin (IVIG) 0.4 g/kg/day for 5 days OR plasma exchange (5 sessions) - both equally effective; speeds recovery, reduces residual disability; _steroids alone do not work_; (4) Supportive - DVT prophylaxis, pain control (neuropathic), nutrition, physiotherapy from acute phase, psychological support; (5) Rehabilitation. Prognosis: ~85% recover fully or near-fully, often over months; ~15% have residual weakness or fatigue; mortality ~3-5% (respiratory failure, autonomic, complications); recurrence rare; CIDP (chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy) is the chronic counterpart [Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: GBS is one of the great teaching cases for medical neurology - a treatable post-infectious autoimmune neuropathy with a stereotyped clinical course. The areflexia, ascending pattern, and CSF dissociation are classic; the urgency lies in detecting respiratory and autonomic complications early. IVIG and plasma exchange exemplify how immunomodulation can change course.

Hints:

1. Ascending symmetric weakness + areflexia post-infection.
2. CSF: albumino-cytological dissociation.
3. IVIG or plasma exchange; monitor FVC + autonomic.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names GBS and the post-infectious autoimmune mechanism.
- Identifies areflexia and ascending weakness as hallmarks.
- Names the CSF finding (albumino-cytological dissociation).
- Names IVIG or plasma exchange as treatment; rejects steroids alone.
- Mentions vital capacity monitoring and ICU criteria.

A Yellow answer covers GBS but misses the autonomic or respiratory complications.

A Red answer treats GBS as a primary muscle disease.

Common misconceptions:

- "Steroids treat GBS": Correction: Steroids alone are _ineffective_; IVIG and plasma exchange are the evidence-based treatments.
- "All ascending weakness is GBS": Correction: Differential includes transverse myelitis, MS, polio, tick paralysis, heavy metal poisoning, porphyria; clinical context and CSF/NCS distinguish.

QUESTION 18
Type: synthesis
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Synthesise the chapter: how do motor system components (motor cortex, basal ganglia, cerebellum, brainstem, spinal cord, motor unit) collaborate to produce voluntary movement, and what design principles emerge?

Model answer: Voluntary movement requires hierarchical and parallel collaboration across multiple levels. (1) _Motor planning_ (premotor cortex, supplementary motor area, parietal cortex) - intentions and motor programmes selected based on context, sensory input, and goals. (2) _Motor command generation_ (primary motor cortex, M1) - selected programmes drive corticospinal output to spinal motor neurons; somatotopic homunculus organises body representation. (3) _Modulation_ (basal ganglia, cerebellum) - basal ganglia gate movement through direct/indirect pathways (action selection, suppress unwanted), cerebellum refines via internal models (timing, accuracy, motor learning). (4) _Brainstem descending tracts_ (rubrospinal, vestibulospinal, reticulospinal) - postural control, antigravity, locomotor rhythm. (5) _Spinal cord_ - motor neuron pools, central pattern generators (locomotion), reflex arcs, interneuronal integration. (6) _Motor unit and neuromuscular junction_ - final common pathway; force grading via recruitment (size principle) and rate coding. (7) _Sensory feedback_ - proprioception (muscle spindles, GTOs, joint receptors) returns via spinocerebellar tracts and dorsal columns; visual and vestibular input integrated. (8) _Autonomic and metabolic support_ - cardiovascular, respiratory, thermoregulatory adjustments scaled to motor demand. Design principles: (a) _Hierarchical control_ - high-level goals -> low-level execution; each level can act locally without higher input (spinal reflexes work after decerebration); (b) _Parallel processing_ - basal ganglia and cerebellum loop in parallel with descending pathways, each contributing; (c) _Feedforward + feedback_ - predictive (cerebellar internal models) plus reactive (sensory feedback) control; (d) _Specialisation_ - corticospinal for fine distal voluntary movement; brainstem for posture/locomotor rhythm; basal ganglia for action selection; cerebellum for accuracy and learning; (e) _Motor learning_ - cerebellar LTD, basal ganglia reinforcement learning, cortical plasticity (motor map reorganisation with practice); (f) _Redundancy and robustness_ - movements can be partly preserved after lesions through alternative pathways; rehabilitation exploits this; (g) _Coupled to autonomic and cardiorespiratory systems_ - exercise pressor reflex, central command. Clinical synthesis: each disorder reveals a specific component. UMN lesion (cortex, internal capsule, brainstem, spinal cord) -> spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski. LMN lesion (anterior horn, peripheral nerve, NMJ, muscle) -> weakness, atrophy, fasciculations, hyporeflexia. Cerebellar -> ataxia, dysmetria, intention tremor (ipsilateral). Basal ganglia -> hypo- or hyperkinetic movement disorders (PD, HD, dystonia, tardive dyskinesia). Sensory loss -> sensory ataxia, Romberg-positive. Combined patterns (eg, ALS = UMN + LMN; Friedreich = sensory ataxia + corticospinal + cerebellar) localise to specific disease processes. The chapter integrates these to support precise neurological localisation - the cornerstone of clinical neurology [Boron & Boulpaep ch.13; Berne & Levy ch.7].

Elaborative explanation: The motor system is not a single hierarchical pyramid but a richly interconnected network of parallel loops, each with specialised contributions. Movement is the integrated product of cortex, basal ganglia, cerebellum, brainstem, spinal cord, and the motor unit, modulated by sensory feedback and autonomic support. Clinical neurology of motor disorders is essentially applied physiology of these loops.

Hints:

1. Hierarchical + parallel control across motor cortex, BG, cerebellum, brainstem, cord, motor unit.
2. Each lesion pattern (UMN, LMN, cerebellar, BG, sensory) localises to a specific component.
3. Motor learning and recovery exploit network plasticity.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the major motor system components and their functions.
- Articulates ≥ 4 design principles (hierarchy, parallel, feedforward+feedback, specialisation, learning, redundancy).
- Maps lesion patterns to components.
- Connects motor learning to specific neural substrates (cerebellar LTD, BG reinforcement learning).
- Synthesises across the chapter rather than listing facts.

A Yellow answer covers components but misses the integrative framework.

A Red answer treats motor system as a single linear pathway.

Common misconceptions:

- "Movement comes from the motor cortex alone.": Correction: Motor cortex provides the command but BG, cerebellum, brainstem, cord, and sensory feedback all contribute to producing accurate movement.
- "Brain controls voluntary movement; spinal cord just relays.": Correction: Spinal circuits include central pattern generators (locomotion) and reflexes that are _autonomous_ below cortical input.
