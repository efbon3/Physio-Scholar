## Pass 1 — Values

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The total volume of cerebrospinal fluid in an adult is approximately \_\_\_ mL.

Canonical answer: ~150 mL

Accepted variants:

- 150
- 150 mL
- ~150
- 100–160 mL

Tolerance: ±20 mL (130–170 mL graded Green; 100–160 mL accepted)

Yellow conditions:

- "25" → "That is the _ventricular_ volume only; total CSF (ventricles + subarachnoid space + cisterns) is ~150 mL."
- "500" → "That is the _daily production_ of CSF; the resident pool is ~150 mL — replaced 3–4× per day."

Explanation: ~25 mL in ventricles, ~125 mL in subarachnoid space and cisterns. The entire pool is replaced ~3–4× per day at the production rate of ~500 mL/day [Guyton & Hall ch.62; Boron & Boulpaep ch.10].

Hints:

1. Ventricles + subarachnoid space + cisterns combined.
2. Less than a quarter of a litre.
3. ~150 mL.

Type: recall

Stem: The rate of CSF production is approximately \_\_\_ mL/day.

Canonical answer: ~500 mL/day (~0.35 mL/min)

Accepted variants:

- 500
- 500 mL/day
- ~500
- 0.35 mL/min

Tolerance: ±50 mL/day (450–550 graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "150" → "That is the resident _pool_ volume; production is ~500 mL/day — meaning the pool is replaced 3–4× per day."
- "50" → "Far too low; CSF is produced at ~500 mL/day."

Explanation: Production is largely independent of ICP within physiological range — driven by active Na⁺ secretion by choroid plexus epithelium (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase on the apical/CSF side). Acetazolamide (carbonic anhydrase inhibitor) reduces CSF production by ~50% [Guyton & Hall ch.62].

Hints:

1. ~3–4× the resident CSF volume per day.
2. ~0.35 mL/min.
3. ~500 mL/day.

QUESTION 8

Stem: The lower limit of mean arterial pressure for normal cerebral autoregulation in a healthy adult is approximately \_\_\_ mm Hg.

Canonical answer: ~60 mm Hg

Accepted variants:

- 60
- ~60
- 60 mm Hg
- 50–60

Tolerance: ±10 mm Hg (50–70 graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "100" → "That is closer to the _upper_ limit (~150) — the normal autoregulatory range is 60–150 mm Hg MAP."
- "30" → "Too low — at MAP ~30 the brain is already severely ischaemic; the autoregulatory floor is ~60."

Explanation: Range 60–150 mm Hg in normotensive adults. Below 60, CBF becomes pressure-dependent and ischaemic injury risk rises. The curve shifts right in chronic hypertension (e.g., 100–200) — these patients tolerate higher pressures but become symptomatic at "normal" ones [Guyton & Hall ch.62].

Hints:

1. Lower bound of the autoregulatory plateau.
2. Below this, CBF becomes pressure-dependent.
3. ~60 mm Hg.

Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: CBF rises by approximately \_\_\_ % for each 1 mm Hg rise in PaCO₂ above the normal of 40 mm Hg.

Canonical answer: ~3–4 % per mm Hg

Accepted variants:

- 3
- 4
- 3–4
- 3 to 4%

Tolerance: ±1 (2–5% accepted)

Yellow conditions:

- "1" → "Too low — CBF rises ~3–4% per 1 mm Hg PaCO₂ change; doubling PaCO₂ roughly doubles CBF."
- "10" → "Too steep — the actual sensitivity is ~3–4% per mm Hg of PaCO₂."

Explanation: Doubling PaCO₂ roughly doubles CBF; halving it roughly halves CBF. Mechanism: H⁺ from CO₂ hydration → cerebral arteriolar vasodilation. Therapeutic hyperventilation lowers ICP transiently in head injury via this mechanism [Guyton & Hall ch.62].

Hints:

1. Doubling PaCO₂ approximately doubles CBF.
2. Mediated by perivascular H⁺.
3. ~3–4 % per mm Hg.

Stem: The selective barrier formed by tight junctions between cerebral capillary endothelial cells, supported by pericytes and astrocyte end-feet, is the \_\_\_.

Accepted variants:

- blood-brain barrier
- BBB
- blood–brain barrier

3. Blood–brain barrier.

Stem: Cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP) is calculated as MAP minus \_\_\_.

Canonical answer: ICP (intracranial pressure)

Accepted variants:

- ICP
- intracranial pressure

Tolerance: synonyms only

Yellow conditions:

- "CVP" -> "CVP is right-atrial pressure; the cerebral driving pressure is MAP - _ICP_."
- "PCWP" -> "PCWP is left-atrial pressure surrogate; the cerebral driving pressure is MAP - ICP."

Explanation: CPP = MAP - ICP. Target > 70 mm Hg in head-injury management. Both lowering ICP (head elevation, mannitol/hypertonic saline, CSF drainage, decompressive craniectomy) and maintaining MAP (cautious pressors, fluids) are essential [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10].

Hints:

1. The "back-pressure" against which the brain is perfused.
2. Subtracted from MAP to give CPP.
3. ICP.

## Summary

This 50-question fill-blank set covers CSF physiology (volume, production, pressure, composition), cerebral blood flow regulation (CBF, CMRO2, autoregulation, CO2 and O2 responsiveness), the BBB (structure, permeability rules, transporters), neuroglial cells (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, ependyma, tanycytes), CSF circulation pathways (foramina of Monro, Sylvius, Luschka, Magendie), and clinical applications (raised ICP, Cushing reflex, herniation syndromes, hydrocephalus, NPH, post-LP headache, mannitol, acetazolamide). Numerical anchors: CSF volume ~150 mL; production ~500 mL/day; lumbar opening pressure ~130 mm H2O; CBF ~50 mL/100 g/min; CMRO2 ~3.5; brain ~2% of body mass but 15% CO and 20% O2 consumption; autoregulation 60-150 mm Hg; CO2 sensitivity ~3-4% per mm Hg; PaO2 threshold ~50 mm Hg; glucose ~120 g/day; CSF/plasma glucose ~0.6; CSF protein < 0.45 g/L; CPP ~70-90; CBF infarction threshold ~10. Disease anchors: Cushing reflex (ICP), uncal herniation (CN III blown pupil), NPH triad, bacterial vs viral CSF profile, IIH, GLUT1 deficiency, GBS albumino-cytological dissociation, Dandy-Walker, aqueductal stenosis. Pharmacology anchors: acetazolamide -> carbonic anhydrase; mannitol -> osmotic diuretic via intact BBB; L-DOPA -> LAT-1 transporter; hyperventilation -> transient ICP reduction via H+ -> vasoconstriction.
