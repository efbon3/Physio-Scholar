## Pass 1 — The plasma membrane

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The plasma membrane of a typical eukaryotic cell is a lipid bilayer with associated proteins. What is its approximate thickness?

Correct answer: About 5 nm.

Distractors:

- "About 50 nm" — Reveals misconception: student inflates the membrane thickness by an order of magnitude, perhaps confusing it with the diameter of a small organelle. Correction: the lipid bilayer itself is only ~5 nm; 50 nm is closer to the size of a small vesicle.
- "About 0.5 µm" — Reveals misconception: student confuses membrane thickness with cell-scale dimensions. Correction: 0.5 µm is roughly 100× the actual bilayer thickness.
- "About 1 nm" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception (it underestimates rather than overestimates).

Explanation: The plasma membrane consists of a 5-nm-thick lipid bilayer with associated proteins [Berne ch.1]. The thickness is set by two opposing layers of phospholipid molecules (each fatty acyl chain is 14–20 carbons long), with cholesterol intercalated between them. This thinness is functionally important: it allows transmembrane proteins to be modest in size (a single α-helical span of ~20 amino acids is enough to cross), and it limits diffusion distances for lipid-soluble substances crossing by simple diffusion.

Hints:

1. The dimension is in nanometres, not micrometres.
2. Two layers of phospholipid stacked head-to-tail set the thickness.
3. The number is a single digit.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A first-year medical student describes phospholipids as "amphipathic." Which statement best captures what amphipathic means in the context of cell membranes?

Correct answer: Each phospholipid molecule contains a polar (hydrophilic) head and non-polar (hydrophobic) fatty acyl tails — it has both water-loving and water-avoiding regions.

Distractors:

- "The molecule is electrically neutral overall, with no charged groups." — Reveals misconception: student equates "amphipathic" with "neutral," missing that amphipathic refers to _spatial_ segregation of hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions, not net charge. Correction: phospholipids do have charged head groups (e.g., phosphatidylcholine carries net charges); the key is that polar and non-polar regions are _separated_.
- "The molecule has two identical regions that mirror each other across a central axis." — Reveals misconception: student conflates "amphipathic" with "amphi-" meaning two-fold symmetry. Correction: amphipathic means having two _different_ affinities (water-loving and water-avoiding), not having two identical halves.
- "The molecule can rotate freely in three dimensions within the membrane." — Reveals misconception: student conflates amphipathic structure with fluidity-related properties.

Explanation: A phospholipid is amphipathic because it has a hydrophilic (polar) head — the phosphorylated alcohol-glycerol portion — and hydrophobic (non-polar) fatty acyl tails [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. This dual nature is what drives bilayer formation: in an aqueous environment, phospholipids spontaneously orient so that the hydrophobic tails face each other, sequestered from water, while the hydrophilic heads contact the aqueous solutions of ICF and ECF. The amphipathic geometry is therefore the molecular basis for the entire architecture of the plasma membrane.

Hints:

1. The prefix _amphi-_ means "both" — both _what_?
2. Think about the head and tails of the phospholipid as having different relationships with water.
3. One end loves water; the other end avoids it.

QUESTION 3
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Cholesterol is a major lipid component of the plasma membrane. Up to approximately what fraction of the total lipid in the membrane can it represent, and what is its principal role?

Correct answer: Up to about 50% of the membrane lipids — cholesterol stabilises the membrane at body temperature and modulates its fluidity.

Distractors:

- "Less than 5%; it serves only as a metabolic precursor for steroid hormones." — Reveals misconception: student dramatically underestimates cholesterol's structural abundance in the membrane. Correction: cholesterol is a quantitatively major lipid component, not a minor one; its precursor role is separate from its membrane structural role.
- "About 50%; its principal role is to anchor membrane proteins to the bilayer." — Reveals misconception: student gets the percentage right but mis-assigns the function. Correction: cholesterol does not anchor proteins; it intercalates between phospholipids and modulates fluidity.
- "About 80%; it is the dominant lipid in the bilayer." — Reveals misconception: student overestimates cholesterol's share — phospholipids remain the most abundant single class.

Explanation: As much as 50% of the lipids found in the plasma membrane can be cholesterol [Berne ch.1]. Cholesterol intercalates between phospholipid molecules in both leaflets of the bilayer. At body temperature (37°C), it stabilises the membrane: it dampens phospholipid motion above the phase-transition temperature (reducing excessive fluidity) and prevents tight packing below it (preventing the membrane from becoming too rigid). Cholesterol is thus a "fluidity buffer." It is also a defining component of lipid rafts — specialised membrane domains where sphingomyelin and cholesterol aggregate together with specific signalling proteins.

Hints:

1. The fraction is much higher than students typically guess on a first reading.
2. Cholesterol is a planar, rigid molecule — what does inserting it between flexible phospholipids do to membrane behaviour?
3. The role relates to fluidity at physiological temperature.

QUESTION 4
Type: prediction
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Two cell membranes are compared. Membrane A contains phospholipids with predominantly _saturated_ fatty acyl tails. Membrane B contains phospholipids with a higher proportion of _unsaturated_ fatty acyl tails. Which prediction is most likely correct?

Correct answer: Membrane B is more fluid than Membrane A at the same temperature.

Distractors:

- "Membrane A is more fluid because saturated chains pack more loosely." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the relationship. Correction: saturated chains pack _tightly_ (they are straight); unsaturated chains have a kink that prevents tight packing, increasing fluidity.
- "The two membranes have identical fluidity because temperature is the only determinant." — Reveals misconception: student treats temperature as the sole determinant. Correction: lipid composition is a major independent determinant.
- "Membrane B is more rigid because the kinked tails interlock." — Reveals misconception: student gets the kink right but the consequence wrong. Correction: kinks _prevent_ tight packing rather than promote interlocking.

Explanation: Membrane fluidity is determined by both temperature and lipid composition [Berne ch.1]. A double bond (unsaturation) in a fatty acyl chain produces a "kink" in the molecule, which prevents the molecule from associating closely with surrounding lipids. The result is that membranes with more unsaturated fatty acids pack less tightly and are more fluid at a given temperature. This is why cold-water fish, for example, have plasma membranes enriched in unsaturated fatty acids — to maintain fluidity at low temperatures.

Hints:

1. Saturated chains are straight; unsaturated chains have a kink.
2. Kinked chains can or cannot pack tightly?
3. Looser packing means more or less fluid?

QUESTION 5
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The phospholipid composition of the plasma membrane is asymmetric between the two leaflets. Which group of phospholipids is found _predominantly in the inner (cytoplasmic) leaflet_ of the erythrocyte plasma membrane?

Correct answer: Phosphatidylethanolamine, phosphatidylserine, and phosphatidylinositol.

Distractors:

- "Phosphatidylcholine and sphingomyelin." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the asymmetry. Correction: PC and sphingomyelin are predominantly _outer_-leaflet lipids; PE, PS, and PI are predominantly _inner_-leaflet lipids.
- "Cholesterol and glycolipids." — Reveals misconception: student conflates the leaflet asymmetry of phospholipids with that of cholesterol/glycolipids. Correction: cholesterol distributes in both leaflets; glycolipids are predominantly outer-leaflet.
- "All phospholipids are distributed equally between the two leaflets." — Reveals misconception: student denies the asymmetry exists.

Explanation: The phospholipid composition of the membrane is asymmetric between the inner and outer leaflets [Berne ch.1]. In the erythrocyte plasma membrane, phosphatidylcholine and sphingomyelin are found predominantly in the outer leaflet; phosphatidylethanolamine, phosphatidylserine, and phosphatidylinositol are found predominantly in the inner leaflet. This asymmetry is functionally important — phosphatidylinositol on the inner leaflet is well-positioned for its role in signal transduction, and phosphatidylserine exposure on the outer leaflet (when this asymmetry is broken) serves as an apoptotic "eat-me" signal for phagocytes.

Hints:

1. Two phospholipids dominate the outer leaflet; three dominate the inner.
2. Phosphatidylinositol's position relates to its role in signal transduction inside the cell.
3. The "PE / PS / PI" trio sits on the cytoplasmic side.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Membrane proteins are classified into three main categories based on how they associate with the lipid bilayer. Which categorisation is correct?

Correct answer: Integral (transmembrane), peripheral, and lipid-anchored.

Distractors:

- "Channel, carrier, and pump." — Reveals misconception: student categorises by _function_ rather than _structural association_ with the bilayer. Correction: channels/carriers/pumps are functional categories that all sit within "integral protein"; the question asks about structural classification.
- "Cytoplasmic, extracellular, and transmembrane." — Reveals misconception: student categorises by _which side_ of the membrane the protein lies on. Correction: this conflates location with attachment mode.
- "Embedded, glycosylated, and phosphorylated." — Reveals misconception: student lists post-translational modifications instead of structural categories.

Explanation: Plasma membrane proteins are classified as integral, lipid-anchored, or peripheral [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. _Integral_ proteins are embedded in the bilayer through hydrophobic interactions; many span the membrane (transmembrane proteins), often with α-helical hydrophobic regions. _Lipid-anchored_ proteins are covalently attached to a lipid molecule (e.g., glycosylphosphatidylinositol — GPI — anchors a protein to the outer leaflet; fatty-acid or prenyl anchors attach to the inner leaflet). _Peripheral_ proteins are not embedded; they associate with polar head groups or with integral/lipid-anchored proteins. This structural classification is independent of function — channels, receptors, enzymes can all be in the integral category.

Hints:

1. The classification is by how the protein _attaches_ to the bilayer, not by what it does.
2. Three modes of attachment exist: through the bilayer, anchored to a lipid, or loosely associated.
3. The "through-the-bilayer" category is called _integral_.

QUESTION 7
Type: misconception-targeted
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A student reads about lipid rafts and asks: "What is a lipid raft, and why does it matter?" Which response is most accurate?

Correct answer: Lipid rafts are membrane microdomains enriched in sphingomyelin and cholesterol that often contain specific signalling proteins, allowing those proteins to diffuse together as a discrete unit and segregating signalling complexes from the rest of the membrane.

Distractors:

- "A lipid raft is the entire outer leaflet of the membrane that floats on the cytoskeleton." — Reveals misconception: student treats the whole leaflet as a raft. Correction: lipid rafts are _microdomains_ — small, defined regions — not whole leaflets.
- "A lipid raft is a region of the membrane completely devoid of cholesterol." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the lipid composition of rafts. Correction: rafts are _enriched_ in cholesterol (and sphingomyelin), not depleted of it.
- "A lipid raft is a structure that floats freely in the cytoplasm and delivers cargo between organelles." — Reveals misconception: student confuses lipid rafts with vesicles or inclusion bodies.

Explanation: Some regions of the plasma membrane contain lipids — specifically sphingomyelin and cholesterol — that aggregate together to form _lipid rafts_ [Berne ch.1]. These microdomains often associate with specific proteins, and the lipids and proteins within a raft diffuse together as a discrete unit within the plane of the membrane. One important function of lipid rafts is to _segregate signalling molecules_: by clustering related receptors, transducers, and effectors into the same membrane microdomain, the cell increases the efficiency of signal transduction. Lipid rafts also have roles in membrane trafficking and pathogen entry (e.g., several viruses exploit rafts for cellular invasion).

Hints:

1. Rafts are special regions of the membrane, not the whole membrane.
2. Their composition is _enriched_ in two specific lipids — which two?
3. They cluster signalling proteins together.

QUESTION 8
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The plasma membrane is described by the _fluid mosaic model_. Which feature does this model emphasise?

Correct answer: That membrane proteins can move laterally within the plane of the lipid bilayer, much like icebergs floating in a sea of lipid.

Distractors:

- "That the membrane is a static, rigid structure with proteins fixed in place." — Reveals misconception: student misreads "mosaic" as "fixed" rather than "patterned but movable." Correction: the model's central insight is the _fluidity_ — lateral mobility of components within the bilayer.
- "That phospholipids freely flip between the two leaflets at a high rate." — Reveals misconception: student over-extends fluidity to include trans-leaflet movement. Correction: lateral diffusion within a leaflet is fast, but flip-flop between leaflets is rare and usually requires specific enzymes (flippases).
- "That the membrane is exclusively composed of phospholipids in a tightly packed crystalline lattice." — Reveals misconception: student treats the membrane as a crystal rather than a fluid.

Explanation: The fluid mosaic model, originally described by Singer and Nicolson, captures two key features: (1) the membrane is a _fluid_ — phospholipids and many proteins can diffuse laterally within the plane of the bilayer; and (2) it is a _mosaic_ — proteins, lipids, and carbohydrates are heterogeneously distributed within the bilayer, with multiple types intermixed [Costanzo ch.1; Rapid Review ch.1]. Within this model, lateral diffusion of components is rapid; trans-leaflet flip-flop is slow; and certain proteins are constrained by anchoring to the cytoskeleton or by inclusion in lipid rafts.

Hints:

1. "Fluid" and "mosaic" describe two complementary properties.
2. The "fluid" part means components are not fixed in place.
3. Think about whether proteins can move _within_ a leaflet versus _between_ leaflets.

## Pass 2 — Membrane-enclosed organelles

QUESTION 9
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Rough endoplasmic reticulum (rER) and smooth endoplasmic reticulum (sER) differ structurally and functionally. Which difference is correctly stated?

Correct answer: rER bears ribosomes and is the site of synthesis of proteins destined for secretion or for membranes; sER lacks ribosomes and is involved in lipid synthesis and drug detoxification.

Distractors:

- "rER lacks ribosomes; sER bears ribosomes." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the structural distinction. Correction: the names describe the appearance — rough = ribosome-studded (granular under EM); smooth = no ribosomes.
- "Both contain ribosomes; they differ only in location within the cell." — Reveals misconception: student denies the structural difference altogether.
- "rER synthesises lipids; sER synthesises proteins." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the functional distinction.

Explanation: Both forms of the endoplasmic reticulum are vesicular networks continuous with the nuclear envelope, but they differ in the presence of ribosomes [Rapid Review ch.1; Berne ch.1]. _Rough ER_ has ribosomes attached to its cytoplasmic surface and is responsible for synthesis of proteins destined for the secretory pathway (export from the cell), the plasma membrane, or the lumen of organelles. _Smooth ER_ lacks ribosomes and serves multiple roles: synthesis of lipids (including steroid hormones in steroid-producing cells), detoxification of drugs (especially in hepatocytes via the cytochrome P450 system), and storage and release of calcium (especially in muscle, where it is called sarcoplasmic reticulum).

Hints:

1. The names tell you the structural difference at first glance.
2. One synthesises proteins; the other synthesises lipids and detoxifies.
3. Hepatocytes induce more of one type of ER when exposed to drugs — which one?

QUESTION 10
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A neonate is diagnosed with I-cell disease — a rare lysosomal disorder in which lysosomal enzymes are _secreted_ from cells instead of being delivered to lysosomes. Which step in protein trafficking is defective?

Correct answer: Post-translational addition of the mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) tag in the Golgi apparatus, which normally targets enzymes to lysosomes.

Distractors:

- "Translation of lysosomal enzyme mRNAs by free cytosolic ribosomes." — Reveals misconception: student misplaces lysosomal-enzyme synthesis on free ribosomes. Correction: lysosomal enzymes are synthesised on rough ER (because they enter the secretory pathway); the trafficking decision is made later, in the Golgi.
- "Acidification of the lysosomal lumen by the H+ pump." — Reveals misconception: student confuses the function-supporting acidic pH (which removes M6P after delivery) with the targeting mechanism itself. Correction: the M6P tag is added in the Golgi; the lysosomal pH only removes it once the enzyme has arrived.
- "Cleavage of the enzyme into its mature form by lysosomal proteases." — Reveals misconception: student conflates targeting with maturation steps inside the lysosome.

Explanation: Lysosomal enzymes are synthesised in the rough ER and processed in the Golgi apparatus, where they receive a _mannose-6-phosphate (M6P)_ tag [Rapid Review ch.1]. The M6P tag is recognised by M6P receptors that direct the enzymes into lysosome-bound vesicles. In I-cell disease, the phosphorylating enzyme that adds the M6P tag is deficient, so the lysosomal enzymes are not tagged and instead enter the constitutive secretory pathway — they are exported from the cell rather than delivered to lysosomes. Cells become filled with undigested material (the characteristic cytoplasmic _inclusions_, which give the disease its name), and the secreted enzymes can be measured at high levels in patient plasma. Severe multiorgan dysfunction follows, with death typically in childhood from cardiopulmonary complications related to inclusion accumulation in heart valves.

Hints:

1. The Golgi adds a specific molecular tag that addresses the enzyme to its destination.
2. The tag uses a phosphorylated sugar.
3. The tag begins with "M" and ends with a number.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The interior of a lysosome is maintained at a pH that is critical for the activity of its hydrolytic enzymes. What is the approximate intra-lysosomal pH?

Correct answer: About 4.8.

Distractors:

- "About 7.4 — the same as plasma." — Reveals misconception: student assumes intracellular compartments share plasma pH. Correction: lysosomes are _acidic_; plasma pH is irrelevant inside them.
- "About 5.5 — the same as the cytosol." — Reveals misconception: student misremembers cytosolic pH. Correction: cytosolic pH is about 7.1–7.2; lysosomal pH is much lower.
- "About 2.0 — the same as gastric lumen." — Reveals misconception: student conflates lysosomal acidity with gastric acidity. Correction: the gastric lumen is much more acidic; lysosomes sit around pH 4.8.

Explanation: The interior of the lysosome is maintained at approximately pH 4.8 by a vacuolar H⁺ pump (V-ATPase) in the lysosomal membrane [Rapid Review ch.1]. Lysosomal hydrolases (proteases, nucleases, lipases, glycosidases) have acidic pH optima — they function efficiently at this low pH and would be inactive in the neutral cytosol. This is also a safety feature: if lysosomal enzymes leak into the cytosol, the higher pH (~7.1) inactivates them and limits damage. The acidic pH also removes M6P tags from incoming lysosomal enzymes (the targeting tag is no longer needed once the enzyme has arrived).

Hints:

1. Lysosomes are _acidic_, not neutral.
2. The pH is well below cytosolic pH.
3. The number is between 4 and 5.

QUESTION 12
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A child presents with neurological deterioration, hepatosplenomegaly, and a deficiency of a single hydrolytic enzyme. Substrates that the missing enzyme normally degrades accumulate within cellular vesicles. Which class of disease is this?

Correct answer: A lysosomal storage disease.

Distractors:

- "A peroxisomal disorder." — Reveals misconception: student conflates lysosomes with peroxisomes (both are vesicular organelles). Correction: peroxisomal disorders involve very-long-chain fatty acid breakdown and other oxidation reactions, not the broad spectrum of substrates accumulated in lysosomal storage diseases.
- "A mitochondrial disorder." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a vesicular accumulation to mitochondrial dysfunction. Correction: mitochondrial disorders typically present with energy-related deficits, not vesicular substrate accumulation.
- "An autoimmune disorder of the macrophage system." — Reveals misconception: student attributes accumulation to immune dysfunction rather than enzyme deficiency.

Explanation: Lysosomal storage diseases are a group of more than 45 inherited disorders in which a deficiency of a single lysosomal hydrolytic enzyme leads to accumulation of its undigested substrate within the lysosome [Rapid Review ch.1]. Examples include Tay-Sachs disease (hexosaminidase A deficiency, GM2 ganglioside accumulation), Niemann-Pick disease (sphingomyelinase deficiency, sphingomyelin accumulation), Gaucher disease (β-glucocerebrosidase deficiency), Hurler syndrome (α-L-iduronidase deficiency), and Fabry disease (α-galactosidase A deficiency). Clinical features depend on the substrate and the tissues that normally turn it over most rapidly: many present in infancy or early childhood with hepatosplenomegaly, neurological deterioration, and short life expectancy. Recognising the architectural pattern — _missing enzyme → substrate accumulation within lysosomes_ — is the unifying concept across the group.

Hints:

1. The accumulation is within a specific organelle — which one?
2. The cause is a missing degradative enzyme.
3. The disease class is named after the organelle whose function fails.

QUESTION 13
Type: misconception-targeted
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A student says: "Mitochondria are inherited from both parents equally, since both contribute genetic material at fertilisation." Identify the correction.

Correct answer: Mitochondria (and their DNA) are inherited _exclusively_ from the mother — the egg cell contributes the mitochondria; sperm mitochondria are excluded shortly after fertilisation.

Distractors:

- "The student is correct — mitochondria are inherited equally from both parents because both gametes contain mitochondria." — Reveals misconception: student treats mtDNA inheritance as Mendelian (biparental). Correction: mtDNA is _non-Mendelian_; only the maternal line transmits it.
- "Mitochondria are inherited from the father only — sperm deliver them at fertilisation." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the maternal/paternal pattern.
- "Mitochondria are not inherited at all — they form _de novo_ in each cell." — Reveals misconception: student denies the existence of organelle inheritance entirely.

Explanation: Mitochondria are inherited maternally — they are received only from the egg, not from the sperm [Rapid Review ch.1]. Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) therefore follows a non-Mendelian inheritance pattern: an affected mother passes the disease to all her offspring, but only female offspring pass it on; an affected father does not transmit the disease through mtDNA. (Note: nuclear-encoded mitochondrial proteins do follow Mendelian inheritance — disorders caused by mutations in _nuclear_ genes encoding mitochondrial proteins, such as some forms of optic neuropathy, can be inherited from either parent.) Recognising the maternal pattern is diagnostically important: a disease with strict maternal transmission strongly suggests a mitochondrial-DNA mutation.

Hints:

1. mtDNA inheritance is _not_ Mendelian — only one parent contributes it.
2. The egg cell is much larger than the sperm cell — which contributes most of the cytoplasm at fertilisation?
3. The pattern is one-parent transmission.

Stem: The nucleus contains the cell's DNA complexed with histone proteins, packaged into structures called chromatin. Within the nucleus, what is the principal function of the nucleolus?

Correct answer: Synthesis of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and assembly of ribosomal subunits.

Distractors:

- "Synthesis of messenger RNA (mRNA)." — Reveals misconception: student conflates the function of the nucleolus with general nuclear transcription. Correction: mRNA is transcribed throughout the nucleoplasm; the nucleolus has a specialised role in rRNA synthesis.
- "Storage of mature proteins before export to the cytoplasm." — Reveals misconception: student treats the nucleolus as a protein-storage site.
- "Replication of mitochondrial DNA." — Reveals misconception: student conflates nuclear and mitochondrial genomes; mtDNA replication occurs in mitochondria, not in the nucleus.

Explanation: The nucleolus is a prominent, RNA-rich, dense body within the nucleus whose principal function is the synthesis of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and the assembly of ribosomal subunits [Rapid Review ch.1]. Ribosomal subunits are then exported to the cytoplasm where they associate with mRNA to perform translation. The size of the nucleolus reflects ribosomal-synthesis activity — cells with high protein-synthetic demand (e.g., growing or actively secreting cells) typically have large, prominent nucleoli; quiescent cells have smaller ones. mRNA synthesis (transcription of protein-coding genes) occurs in the rest of the nucleoplasm.

Hints:

1. The nucleolus has a focused role in producing one specific kind of RNA.
2. That RNA is the structural backbone of the ribosome.
3. The first letter of the relevant RNA matches the first letter of "ribosomal."

QUESTION 15
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Within the nucleus, chromatin exists in two visually and functionally distinct forms. Which pairing is correct?

Correct answer: Heterochromatin is highly compacted and transcriptionally inactive; euchromatin is less compact and contains transcriptionally active DNA sequences.

Distractors:

- "Heterochromatin is loose and transcriptionally active; euchromatin is compact and transcriptionally inactive." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the relationship between condensation and activity.
- "Heterochromatin contains DNA only; euchromatin contains DNA and histones." — Reveals misconception: student misidentifies the structural distinction. Correction: both contain DNA and histones; the difference is _condensation_, not composition.
- "Heterochromatin is found only in dividing cells; euchromatin only in non-dividing cells." — Reveals misconception: student treats the distinction as cell-cycle-dependent rather than functional.

Explanation: Chromatin appears in two forms in interphase nuclei [Rapid Review ch.1]. _Heterochromatin_ is highly compacted, electron-dense in micrographs, and transcriptionally inactive — DNA in this state is largely silenced. _Euchromatin_ is less compact, lighter in micrographs, and contains the transcriptionally active DNA sequences. The relative proportions vary by cell type and metabolic state: a metabolically quiet cell may have more heterochromatin, while an actively transcribing cell has more euchromatin. The condensation state is regulated by histone modifications (acetylation, methylation, etc.) — the molecular basis of epigenetic gene regulation.

Hints:

1. One form of chromatin is more compact; the other is more open.
2. Transcription requires the DNA to be accessible to RNA polymerase.
3. Which form is more accessible — open or compact?

QUESTION 16
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Free ribosomes and ribosomes attached to the rough endoplasmic reticulum perform the same catalytic function (translation), but they handle different protein populations. Which statement correctly describes the division of labour?

Correct answer: Free ribosomes synthesise proteins destined for the cytosol or for organelles imported post-translationally; rough-ER-bound ribosomes synthesise proteins destined for secretion, the plasma membrane, or the lumen of the secretory pathway.

Distractors:

- "Free ribosomes synthesise mRNA; rough-ER ribosomes synthesise proteins." — Reveals misconception: student confuses transcription with translation. Correction: ribosomes do not synthesise mRNA at all; they translate it. Both free and rough-ER ribosomes do translation.
- "Free ribosomes are functionally inactive; only rough-ER ribosomes synthesise proteins." — Reveals misconception: student denies the productive role of free ribosomes.
- "Free ribosomes synthesise lipids; rough-ER ribosomes synthesise proteins." — Reveals misconception: student conflates free ribosomes with smooth ER lipid synthesis.

Explanation: Both free and rough-ER ribosomes carry out translation, but the destination of their products differs [Berne ch.1; Rapid Review ch.1]. Free cytosolic ribosomes synthesise proteins that remain in the cytosol or are imported into organelles (e.g., the nucleus, mitochondria, peroxisomes) post-translationally via specific targeting sequences. Rough-ER-bound ribosomes synthesise proteins that enter the secretory pathway: secreted proteins, plasma-membrane proteins, and proteins of the ER, Golgi, and lysosomes. The decision is made early during translation: an N-terminal signal sequence on the nascent polypeptide is recognised by the signal recognition particle, which docks the ribosome onto the rough ER membrane.

Hints:

1. Both populations of ribosomes do the same job — translation.
2. They differ in _where the product goes_.
3. Proteins destined for export from the cell are made on which ribosomes?

## Pass 3 — Cytoskeleton, motility, and clinical integration

QUESTION 17
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The cytoskeleton provides mechanical support, cell shape, intracellular transport, and motility. It comprises three principal classes of filaments. Which set correctly identifies them?

Correct answer: Microfilaments (composed of actin), intermediate filaments, and microtubules (composed of tubulin).

Distractors:

- "Phospholipid filaments, glycoprotein filaments, and cholesterol filaments." — Reveals misconception: student lists membrane components rather than cytoskeletal proteins.
- "DNA filaments, RNA filaments, and ribosomal filaments." — Reveals misconception: student conflates cytoskeleton with nucleic acids.
- "Myosin filaments, tropomyosin filaments, and actin filaments." — Reveals misconception: student lists muscle-specific contractile proteins as cytoskeletal classes. Correction: myosin and tropomyosin are muscle proteins; the three universal cytoskeletal classes are microfilaments (actin), intermediate filaments, and microtubules.

Explanation: The cytoskeleton has three principal filament classes [Rapid Review ch.1; Berne ch.1]. _Microfilaments_ are the smallest (5–9 nm), flexible, helical polymers of G-actin; they form the cortex layer just under the plasma membrane and function in cell shape, motility, organelle transport, cytokinesis, and (in muscle) contraction. _Microtubules_ are the largest (25 nm), rigid hollow cylinders of tubulin polymers; they extend from the centrosome, serve as scaffolding for organelle and chromosome transport, and form the structural core of cilia and flagella. _Intermediate filaments_ are 10 nm in diameter, formed from a heterogeneous family of proteins, and provide mechanical stability — particularly to epithelial cells, where they form desmosomes and hemidesmosomes.

Hints:

1. The three classes are named by _size_: small, medium, large.
2. The smallest is named for its main protein — actin.
3. The largest forms cilia and flagella.

QUESTION 18
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Colchicine and the _vinca_ alkaloids (vincristine, vinblastine) are used as antimitotic drugs in the treatment of gout and certain cancers. Which cytoskeletal target accounts for their effect on cell division?

Correct answer: Microtubules — these drugs disrupt microtubule polymerisation, preventing the formation of the mitotic spindle.

Distractors:

- "Microfilaments — they prevent actin polymerisation, halting cytokinesis." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the antimitotic effect to actin disruption. Correction: actin is involved in cytokinesis (the final division step), but colchicine and vinca alkaloids act on microtubules, which form the spindle that separates chromosomes.
- "Intermediate filaments — they destabilise the nuclear lamina." — Reveals misconception: student picks intermediate filaments because of their structural role; these drugs do not act on intermediate filaments.
- "Phospholipids — they disrupt membrane integrity during division." — Reveals misconception: student treats antimitotic action as a membrane phenomenon.

Explanation: The mitotic spindle that separates chromosomes during mitosis is composed of microtubules [Rapid Review ch.1]. Antimitotic drugs that target microtubules — colchicine (used in gout for its anti-inflammatory effect on neutrophil migration; toxic at higher doses), vincristine, and vinblastine (used in chemotherapy) — bind tubulin and disrupt microtubule polymerisation, preventing assembly of a functional spindle. Cells cannot complete mitosis and arrest in metaphase. Beyond their antimitotic effect, microtubule dysfunction can also produce _immotile cilia syndrome_ (Kartagener syndrome) and male infertility, since cilia and flagella have microtubule cores.

Hints:

1. Mitosis depends on a spindle. What cytoskeletal element forms it?
2. The drugs in the question all act on the same cytoskeletal class.
3. Colchicine binds tubulin.

QUESTION 19
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A patient presents with the clinical triad of bronchiectasis, chronic sinusitis, and situs inversus (organs reversed left-to-right). The underlying defect is in microtubule function within cilia. What is this syndrome called?

Correct answer: Kartagener syndrome (a form of immotile cilia syndrome).

Distractors:

- "Hereditary spherocytosis" — Reveals misconception: student picks a different inherited red-cell disorder. Correction: hereditary spherocytosis is a defect in cytoskeletal proteins of red cells (ankyrin/spectrin), not ciliary microtubules.
- "Cystic fibrosis" — Reveals misconception: student associates bronchiectasis with CF, the most common chronic lung disorder. Correction: CF is a chloride-channel (CFTR) defect, not a ciliary microtubule defect — though it can also produce bronchiectasis.
- "Tay-Sachs disease" — Reveals misconception: student picks a lysosomal storage disorder; clinically irrelevant to the picture.

Explanation: Kartagener syndrome is caused by ciliary dysmotility — the dynein arms in the microtubule core of cilia are absent or defective, so cilia cannot beat normally [Rapid Review ch.1]. The clinical consequences include impaired mucociliary clearance (causing recurrent sinusitis and bronchiectasis), male infertility (sperm flagellar dysfunction), and _situs inversus_ (because normal embryonic visceral rotation depends on the leftward flow generated by motile cilia at the embryonic node). The combination of chronic respiratory infection, sinusitis, and situs inversus is the classical clinical triad. Recognising the architectural commonality — defective cilia in airways, sperm, and embryonic node — is the key to the diagnosis.

Hints:

1. The defect is in the same cytoskeletal element that forms the mitotic spindle, but here in cilia.
2. The triad includes a structural anomaly that develops in embryogenesis.
3. The syndrome is named after a German physician.

QUESTION 20
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A patient has hereditary spherocytosis — a hemolytic anaemia in which red blood cells are spherical, mechanically unstable, and lodge in the splenic cords. The molecular defect involves cytoskeletal proteins of the red-cell membrane. Which proteins are most commonly affected?

Correct answer: Ankyrin and spectrin — proteins that anchor the cytoskeleton to the inner face of the red-cell plasma membrane.

Distractors:

- "Tubulin and dynein — proteins of microtubule motors." — Reveals misconception: student associates the defect with microtubules. Correction: red-cell membrane integrity depends on the actin-spectrin-ankyrin cortical cytoskeleton, not microtubules.
- "Histones and lamin — proteins of the nuclear envelope." — Reveals misconception: red blood cells lack a nucleus; nuclear-envelope proteins are not involved.
- "Collagen and elastin — extracellular matrix proteins." — Reveals misconception: student picks structural ECM proteins rather than red-cell membrane cytoskeleton.

Explanation: Hereditary spherocytosis is caused most commonly by mutations in the _ankyrin_ gene (other affected proteins include spectrin, band 3, and protein 4.2) [Rapid Review ch.1]. Ankyrin and spectrin together form the cortical cytoskeletal network on the inner face of the red-cell plasma membrane; this network is what gives the normal biconcave disc its mechanical resilience and deformability. When ankyrin is defective, the spectrin network detaches from the membrane in patches, the membrane buds off as small vesicles, and the cell becomes spherical (lower surface-to-volume ratio) and rigid. Spherical, inflexible cells cannot navigate the splenic microvasculature and are scavenged by splenic macrophages — producing extravascular haemolysis, jaundice, splenomegaly, and anaemia. Splenectomy is curative because it removes the site of red-cell destruction.

Hints:

1. The red-cell shape depends on a cortical protein network on the inner membrane face.
2. The two proteins are linked: one anchors the other to the membrane.
3. The names of both proteins begin with vowels (A and S).

QUESTION 21
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Microvilli, cilia, and flagella are surface projections of cells. Which combination correctly identifies their internal cytoskeletal core?

Correct answer: Microvilli have an actin-microfilament core; cilia and flagella have a microtubule core.

Distractors:

- "Microvilli have a microtubule core; cilia and flagella have an actin core." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the cytoskeletal cores.
- "All three structures have an actin core." — Reveals misconception: student over-generalises actin's role to all surface projections.
- "All three structures have a microtubule core." — Reveals misconception: student over-generalises microtubules.

Explanation: Surface projections have different cytoskeletal cores [Rapid Review ch.1]. _Microvilli_ are small fingerlike projections that increase surface area for absorption (e.g., intestinal brush border, renal proximal convoluted tubule); they have an actin-microfilament core. _Cilia_ and _flagella_ are longer projections supported by a microtubule core arranged in the classical 9 + 2 axoneme; they generate movement (cilia move fluid over the cell surface; flagella move the cell itself, as in sperm — the only human cell with a flagellum). The functional and architectural distinction explains why microtubule-targeting drugs (colchicine, vinca alkaloids) and Kartagener syndrome affect cilia/flagella but not microvilli.

Hints:

1. Three projections, two cytoskeletal cores.
2. Two of the three projections move (or move things); they share the same cytoskeletal element.
3. The third projection is for surface-area amplification, with a different cytoskeleton.

QUESTION 22
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Across the lysosomal storage diseases as a group, the unifying architectural pattern is the same despite very different clinical presentations (Tay-Sachs, Niemann-Pick, Gaucher, Fabry, Hurler). What is the architectural commonality?

Correct answer: A deficiency of a specific lysosomal hydrolytic enzyme leads to accumulation of its substrate within lysosomes, eventually disrupting the function of cells in tissues that turn that substrate over most rapidly.

Distractors:

- "All are caused by mutations in the same gene; the variable presentation reflects environmental factors." — Reveals misconception: student treats the group as a single disease with variable expressivity.
- "All result from over-activity of a lysosomal enzyme that destroys cellular substrates." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the direction of the defect (loss-of-function, not gain-of-function).
- "All are autoimmune disorders in which the body produces antibodies against lysosomal contents." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the disorders to immune dysfunction.

Explanation: Lysosomal storage diseases share a common architectural pattern despite their clinical heterogeneity [Rapid Review ch.1]. Each disease is caused by a deficiency of a specific lysosomal hydrolytic enzyme; the substrate that the missing enzyme normally degrades therefore accumulates within lysosomes; over time the accumulation disrupts the function of cells in tissues that depend most heavily on turnover of that substrate. Tay-Sachs (hexosaminidase A → GM2 ganglioside accumulates in neurons) presents with neurological deterioration. Gaucher (β-glucocerebrosidase → glucocerebroside accumulates in macrophages) presents with hepatosplenomegaly, bone disease, and the "crinkled paper" appearance of macrophages. Fabry (α-galactosidase A → ceramide trihexoside accumulates in vascular endothelium) presents with renal failure, cardiac disease, and acroparaesthesiae. Recognising the unifying architectural principle — _missing enzyme → substrate accumulation in lysosomes_ — turns a long list of unrelated-looking conditions into a single conceptual class.

Hints:

1. Each disease has a different missing enzyme and a different accumulated substrate.
2. The location of accumulation is the same in every case.
3. The pattern is loss-of-function with downstream substrate buildup.
