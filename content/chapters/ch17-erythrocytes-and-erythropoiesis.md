## Pass 1 — RBC structure, haemoglobin, and major haemoglobinopathies

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: The erythrocyte's biconcave disc shape:

Correct answer: Maximises surface area for gas exchange (~30% greater than an equivalent sphere), enables flexibility through capillaries narrower than the cell diameter, and arises from a spectrin–ankyrin–protein 4.1–actin membrane skeleton; defects in this skeleton cause hereditary spherocytosis with splenic sequestration and chronic haemolysis.

Distractors:

- "Limits cellular function." — Reveals misconception: student treats the shape as restrictive. Correction: the biconcave disc is functionally optimised — high surface-to-volume ratio for gas exchange and deformability for capillary transit.
- "Has no functional benefit." — Reveals misconception: student denies the adaptive value. Correction: the shape is highly adaptive; it maximises gas-exchange surface and allows passage through 3 µm splenic sinusoids.
- "Reduces lifespan." — Reveals misconception: student misattributes the cause of senescence. Correction: lifespan (~120 days) is limited by accumulated oxidative damage in an anucleate cell, not by the biconcave shape.

Explanation: The mature erythrocyte is a biconcave disc ~7 µm in diameter and ~2 µm thick, with a surface area ~30% greater than an equivalent sphere — optimising gas diffusion [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Guyton & Hall ch.33]. The membrane skeleton, composed of spectrin tetramers anchored to the membrane through ankyrin (band 3) and through protein 4.1 (glycophorin C) plus actin, maintains the shape and gives the cell extraordinary flexibility — RBCs squeeze through 3 µm splenic sinusoids and pulmonary capillaries. Defects in this skeleton cause hereditary spherocytosis (ankyrin or spectrin defects → spherical fragile cells → splenic sequestration → chronic haemolysis). Sickle cell disease distorts shape via HbS polymerisation rather than skeletal defect. The biconcave disc is also reversibly deformable, allowing rapid recovery to normal shape after capillary transit.

Hints:

1. The shape provides much more surface area than a sphere of equal volume.
2. The cytoskeletal scaffold is built from spectrin and ankyrin.
3. Hereditary spherocytosis is the membrane skeleton disorder.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The mature erythrocyte lacks:

Correct answer: A nucleus, mitochondria, and ribosomes (extruded during reticulocyte maturation), so it relies on glycolysis for ATP and cannot synthesise new proteins; ~90% of ATP comes from the Embden–Meyerhof pathway, with ~10% from the pentose phosphate pathway producing NADPH for glutathione reduction; 2,3-bisphosphoglycerate from the Rapoport–Luebering shunt right-shifts the O₂ dissociation curve.

Distractors:

- "Haemoglobin." — Reveals misconception: student denies haemoglobin in RBCs. Correction: haemoglobin is the _defining_ RBC protein; what the cell lacks is nucleus, mitochondria, and ribosomes.
- "Membrane." — Reveals misconception: student denies the plasma membrane. Correction: the RBC has a complex plasma membrane with spectrin–ankyrin skeleton; what it lacks is intracellular organelles.
- "ATP." — Reveals misconception: student denies ATP entirely. Correction: RBCs make ATP by glycolysis (no mitochondria); they have ATP, just no oxidative phosphorylation.

Explanation: The mature RBC is anucleate and lacks mitochondria and ribosomes [Guyton & Hall ch.33]. Without mitochondria it cannot perform oxidative phosphorylation, so glycolysis (Embden–Meyerhof) is its sole ATP source (~90%), with the pentose phosphate pathway (~10%) generating NADPH to keep glutathione reduced — protecting against oxidative damage. G6PD deficiency impairs NADPH production → oxidative stress → haemolysis on exposure to oxidants (sulfa drugs, antimalarials, fava beans), with bite cells and Heinz bodies on smear. 2,3-bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG) is produced from a glycolytic intermediate via the Rapoport–Luebering shunt; it binds Hb β-chains and lowers O₂ affinity (right shift of the dissociation curve), helping match O₂ delivery to tissue demand. Lack of protein synthesis explains why oxidative damage and metabolic decline are the limits of RBC lifespan.

Hints:

1. The cell has no nucleus and no mitochondria.
2. ATP comes from glycolysis only.
3. NADPH from the pentose pathway protects against oxidative damage.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Each haem group can bind:

Correct answer: One O₂ molecule — at the ferrous iron (Fe²⁺); the Hb tetramer (α₂β₂) thus binds 4 O₂ molecules in total, with positive cooperativity (Hill coefficient ~2.7) producing a sigmoidal oxyhaemoglobin dissociation curve that optimises lung loading and tissue release; oxidation to Fe³⁺ produces methaemoglobin which cannot bind O₂.

Distractors:

- "4 O₂." — Reveals misconception: student picks the tetramer total per haem. Correction: each haem binds 1 O₂; the _tetramer_ binds 4 O₂.
- "2 O₂." — Reveals misconception: student picks an intermediate value. Correction: each haem binds exactly 1 O₂; cooperativity affects affinity but not stoichiometry.
- "8 O₂." — Reveals misconception: student doubles the tetramer. Correction: the tetramer has 4 haems and binds 4 O₂; 8 has no physiological correspondence.

Explanation: Each haem has one Fe²⁺ binding site for O₂; the Hb tetramer has 4 haems → 4 O₂ binding sites [Boron & Boulpaep ch.29; Guyton & Hall ch.41]. Positive cooperativity: the first O₂ binding causes a T (tense) → R (relaxed) conformational change, increasing affinity for subsequent O₂ binding (Hill coefficient ~2.7). This produces the sigmoidal O₂–Hb dissociation curve: a steep middle portion (~20–60 mm Hg PO₂) — small PO₂ changes give large saturation changes, optimising O₂ release in tissues — and a plateau at high PO₂ — full saturation in the lungs despite some PO₂ variability. P50 is ~26.7 mm Hg in adults (the PO₂ at which Hb is 50% saturated). Carboxyhaemoglobin: CO binds with ~250× greater affinity than O₂ and shifts the curve left (impaired O₂ release); pulse oximetry does not detect CO poisoning; treatment is 100% O₂, with hyperbaric in severe cases. Methaemoglobin (Fe³⁺): cannot bind O₂; chocolate-brown blood; caused by oxidising agents (nitrates, dapsone, lidocaine); treated with methylene blue.

Hints:

1. Each haem binds exactly one O₂.
2. The tetramer has four haems.
3. Cooperative binding produces the sigmoidal curve and the P50.

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Right shift of the oxyhaemoglobin dissociation curve (decreased O₂ affinity, enhanced tissue O₂ delivery) is caused by:

Correct answer: Increased PCO₂, increased temperature, decreased pH (the Bohr effect), and increased 2,3-BPG — the "CADET" mnemonic (CO₂↑, Acid↑, DPG↑, Exercise↑, Temperature↑); left shift is the opposite (alkalosis, hypothermia, low 2,3-BPG, COHb, MetHb, HbF).

Distractors:

- "Decreased temperature." — Reveals misconception: student picks left-shift conditions. Correction: hypothermia _left-shifts_ the curve (increased affinity); right shift requires _increased_ temperature.
- "Decreased 2,3-BPG." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong direction. Correction: low 2,3-BPG _left-shifts_; high 2,3-BPG right-shifts and improves tissue O₂ release.
- "Alkalosis." — Reveals misconception: student picks a left-shift condition. Correction: alkalosis (high pH) _left-shifts_; acidosis (low pH) right-shifts via the Bohr effect.

Explanation: The Bohr effect describes how increased PCO₂ and decreased pH (acidic conditions) decrease Hb O₂ affinity, enhancing tissue O₂ delivery [Costanzo ch.5; Guyton & Hall ch.41]. Active tissues (high CO₂ and acid from metabolism) get more O₂ — a physiological adaptation. In the lungs, the opposite applies: low CO₂ and high pH increase Hb O₂ affinity, optimising O₂ loading. Right-shift conditions (decreased affinity, P50 increases): increased PCO₂, decreased pH, increased temperature (fever, exercise), and increased 2,3-BPG (chronic hypoxia, anaemia, high altitude). Left-shift conditions (increased affinity, P50 decreases): decreased PCO₂, increased pH, decreased temperature, decreased 2,3-BPG, carboxyhaemoglobin, methaemoglobin, and fetal haemoglobin (HbF, α₂γ₂ — higher O₂ affinity than HbA because γ-chain binds 2,3-BPG poorly, allowing the fetus to extract O₂ from maternal blood across the placenta).

Hints:

1. The mnemonic CADET captures the right-shift conditions.
2. Bohr effect is acidosis and high CO₂ promoting O₂ release.
3. Fetal Hb is left-shifted because γ-chains do not bind 2,3-BPG.

QUESTION 5
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Oxygen content of arterial blood (CaO₂) is approximately:

Correct answer: ~20 mL O₂/dL at Hb 15 g/dL, SaO₂ 100%, and PaO₂ 100 mm Hg — calculated as CaO₂ = 1.34 × Hb × SaO₂ + 0.003 × PaO₂; bound O₂ (~20 mL/dL) dominates over dissolved O₂ (~0.3 mL/dL); anaemia reduces CaO₂ even with normal SaO₂.

Distractors:

- "5 mL/dL." — Reveals misconception: student grossly underestimates. Correction: arterial CaO₂ is ~20 mL/dL, not 5; this is one of the most clinically useful numbers in respiratory physiology.
- "50 mL/dL." — Reveals misconception: student overestimates. Correction: ~20 mL/dL is normal; 50 would be incompatible with normal physiology.
- "100 mL/dL." — Reveals misconception: student grossly overestimates. Correction: ~20 mL/dL is correct; 100 has no physiological correlate.

Explanation: Arterial O₂ content (CaO₂) is the sum of bound and dissolved O₂ [Boron & Boulpaep ch.29]. Bound = 1.34 × Hb × SaO₂ (each gram of Hb carries 1.34 mL O₂ at full saturation). Dissolved = 0.003 × PaO₂ (Henry's law). At Hb 15 g/dL, SaO₂ 100%, PaO₂ 100 mm Hg: bound = 1.34 × 15 × 1.0 = 20.1 mL/dL; dissolved = 0.003 × 100 = 0.3 mL/dL; total ≈ 20 mL/dL. Bound O₂ dominates ~67-fold; dissolved O₂ matters only at very high PaO₂ (hyperbaric oxygen therapy raises dissolved O₂ enough to deliver O₂ without Hb — used for CO poisoning, decompression sickness, gas gangrene). Anaemia (low Hb) reduces CaO₂ even with normal SaO₂ — compensation includes increased cardiac output, raised 2,3-BPG, and peripheral vasodilation. Polycythaemia (high Hb) raises CaO₂ but increases viscosity and thrombotic risk. O₂ delivery (DO₂) = CO × CaO₂ — a critical clinical parameter in shock and resuscitation.

Hints:

1. Arterial O₂ content is ~20 mL/dL at normal Hb and saturation.
2. Bound O₂ vastly outweighs dissolved O₂ at physiological PaO₂.
3. Anaemia reduces CaO₂ even when SaO₂ is normal.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Haemoglobin types in adults are predominantly:

Correct answer: HbA (α₂β₂) ~97%, HbA2 (α₂δ₂) ~2.5%, and HbF (α₂γ₂) <1%; HbF dominates in fetal life (γ-chain does not bind 2,3-BPG efficiently → higher O₂ affinity → placental O₂ extraction) until the γ→β switch around 6 months postnatal completes the transition to adult HbA dominance.

Distractors:

- "HbF only." — Reveals misconception: student picks the fetal pattern. Correction: HbF is <1% in adults; HbA dominates after 6 months postnatal.
- "HbS." — Reveals misconception: student picks an abnormal Hb as normal. Correction: HbS is the sickle variant (Glu→Val on β-chain); it is _not_ normal adult Hb.
- "HbC." — Reveals misconception: student picks another abnormal Hb. Correction: HbC (Glu→Lys on β-chain) is also abnormal; normal adult Hb is HbA.

Explanation: Globin genes are clustered on chromosomes 16 (α-cluster: ζ embryonic, α fetal/adult — two α genes per haploid, four total) and 11 (β-cluster: ε embryonic, γ fetal, δ and β adult) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Developmental transitions: embryonic forms (ζ₂ε₂ Gower 1, α₂ε₂ Gower 2, ζ₂γ₂ Portland) → fetal HbF (α₂γ₂) dominant from ~10 weeks → adult γ→β switch beginning late gestation, with HbA dominant by ~6 months postnatal. HbF advantages in fetus: higher O₂ affinity than HbA (because γ-chain binds 2,3-BPG poorly) → placental O₂ extraction. Hereditary persistence of HbF (HPFH): some adults retain high HbF; usually asymptomatic but ameliorates sickle cell disease and β-thalassaemia (HbF does not sickle and does not require β-globin). Hydroxyurea increases HbF via fetal globin reactivation, reducing sickling and crises. β-thalassaemia compensates with HbF and HbA2 elevation, both of which rise on HPLC; severe β⁰/β⁰ remains transfusion-dependent.

Hints:

1. Adult HbA is α₂β₂ and accounts for ~97% of haemoglobin.
2. HbA2 is α₂δ₂ at ~2.5%.
3. HbF persists at trace levels in normal adults.

Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The reticulocyte is best described as:

Correct answer: The immediate post-erythroblast precursor (anucleate, with residual ribosomes appearing as bluish reticulum on supravital staining); ~0.5–1.5% of circulating RBCs normally; _raised_ in haemolysis or recovery from anaemia (responsive marrow) and _low_ in iron/B12/folate deficiency, marrow suppression, or aplastic states.

Distractors:

- "A fully mature RBC." — Reveals misconception: student denies the immature stage. Correction: reticulocytes are _immature_ — they retain residual ribosomes for 1–2 days before maturing.
- "Has a nucleus." — Reveals misconception: student denies the anucleate state. Correction: reticulocytes are anucleate (the nucleus is extruded during erythroblast maturation); residual organelles are RNA, not nucleus.
- "Is found in lymph nodes." — Reveals misconception: student misplaces the reticulocyte. Correction: reticulocytes are in bone marrow and circulating blood; lymph nodes contain lymphocytes, not reticulocytes.

Explanation: The reticulocyte is the immediate post-erythroblast precursor — anucleate with residual ribosomes and organelles being cleared, released from marrow and maturing over 1–2 days [Berne & Levy; Guyton & Hall]. Normal reticulocyte count ~0.5–1.5% of RBCs (or 50–150 × 10⁹/L). The reticulocyte index (corrected reticulocyte percentage) = reticulocyte % × (patient Hct / normal Hct), adjusting for severity of anaemia. Raised reticulocytes (marrow responsive): haemolysis, acute blood loss, recovery from anaemia after treatment, and marrow response to iron/B12/folate replacement (within 5–7 days). Low reticulocytes (marrow unresponsive): untreated iron/B12/folate deficiency, aplastic anaemia, pure red cell aplasia (parvovirus B19 in chronic haemolytic states), marrow infiltration (leukaemia, metastasis), CKD anaemia (low EPO), and anaemia of chronic disease. Reticulocyte count is one of the most useful tests in evaluating anaemia, distinguishing "production problem" (low reticulocytes) from "destruction or loss problem" (high reticulocytes).

Hints:

1. Reticulocytes are the immature anucleate RBC stage.
2. They appear as a bluish reticulum on supravital staining.
3. High reticulocyte count points to haemolysis or marrow recovery.

Stem: Sickle cell disease pathophysiology:

Correct answer: A single-base substitution in the β-globin gene (Glu→Val at position 6) producing HbS; on deoxygenation HbS polymerises into rigid linear fibres → distorted "sickle" RBC shape → vaso-occlusion in microcirculation, chronic haemolytic anaemia (RBC lifespan ~10–20 days), splenic infarction over time → autosplenectomy → susceptibility to encapsulated organisms; treatments include hydroxyurea (raises HbF), L-glutamine, voxelotor, crizanlizumab, vaccination, prophylactic penicillin in children, transfusion, allogeneic HSCT (curative), and gene therapy.

Distractors:

- "Iron deficiency." — Reveals misconception: student picks a nutritional cause. Correction: sickle cell disease is a haemoglobinopathy with normal iron stores; iron deficiency is a separate, common but distinct anaemia.
- "Excess HbF." — Reveals misconception: student picks the protective state. Correction: high HbF _protects_ from sickling (HbF does not sickle); SCD pathology is HbS polymerisation, and hereditary persistence of HbF ameliorates the disease.
- "Plasma protein deficiency." — Reveals misconception: student picks a hypoproteinaemia. Correction: SCD is an intrinsic RBC disorder (β-globin point mutation), not a plasma protein deficiency.

Explanation: Sickle cell disease is autosomal recessive — heterozygotes have sickle trait (generally asymptomatic; confer ~10× resistance to severe falciparum malaria, explaining the gene's persistence in malaria-endemic regions) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Berne & Levy]. Pathophysiology: HbS polymerises on deoxygenation → rigid linear fibres distort RBC shape → cells are rigid (vaso-occlusion), adherent (bind endothelium), and short-lived (chronic haemolytic anaemia, Hb ~7–9 g/dL). Triggers: hypoxia, dehydration, infection, acidosis, cold. Clinical: vaso-occlusive crises (severe bone/abdominal/chest pain), acute chest syndrome (pulmonary infiltrate plus hypoxaemia — major cause of death), stroke (especially in children — TCD screening, chronic transfusion to prevent), splenic sequestration (life-threatening in young), functional asplenia (autosplenectomy from infarcts → encapsulated organism susceptibility), avascular necrosis (femoral/humeral heads), priapism, leg ulcers, retinopathy, renal medullary disease and CKD, and pigment gallstones from haemolysis. Treatment: hydroxyurea (raises HbF — standard of care), L-glutamine (anti-oxidative), voxelotor (HbS polymerisation inhibitor, raises Hb), crizanlizumab (anti-P-selectin), folic acid, hydration and analgesia for crises, exchange transfusion for severe events, vaccination plus prophylactic penicillin in children, allogeneic HSCT (curative in young matched-donor patients), and gene therapy (lovo-cel and exa-cel — FDA-approved 2023).

Hints:

1. The mutation is a single Glu→Val substitution on β-globin position 6.
2. HbS polymerises on deoxygenation.
3. Hydroxyurea raises HbF and reduces crises.

Stem: The thalassaemias α and β are best distinguished by:

Correct answer: α-thalassaemia involves _deletions_ of α-globin genes (4 copies total; severity depends on number deleted: 1 silent, 2 trait, 3 HbH disease, 4 Hb Bart's hydrops fetalis — fatal); β-thalassaemia involves _point mutations_ affecting β-globin transcription/processing (β⁺ reduced, β⁰ absent), with β-thal trait (heterozygous) usually mild and homozygous β-thal major requiring transfusion; HbA2 is raised in β-thal trait, distinguishing it from iron deficiency.

Distractors:

- "Same disease." — Reveals misconception: student denies any difference. Correction: α-thal and β-thal involve different chromosomes (16 vs 11), different molecular mechanisms (deletions vs point mutations), and different inheritance patterns/severity profiles.
- "Both due to iron deficiency." — Reveals misconception: student picks a nutritional cause. Correction: thalassaemias are _genetic_ defects of globin synthesis with normal iron stores (iron may even be elevated from transfusions); iron deficiency is a separate, distinguishable cause of microcytic anaemia.
- "Both autosomal dominant." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong inheritance. Correction: thalassaemias are typically autosomal recessive, with severity depending on the number of affected genes (4 α-genes; 2 β-genes).

Explanation: Thalassaemias are quantitative haemoglobinopathies — reduced or absent globin chain synthesis [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. **α-thalassaemia**: 4 α-genes (2 per chromosome 16); most cases are deletions. --/αα (2 deleted in cis — common Asian populations) and -α/-α (2 deleted in trans — common African populations) → mild trait; --/-α (3 deleted) → HbH disease (β₄ tetramers form because α-chains are lacking; precipitate → haemolysis, splenomegaly, jaundice); --/-- (4 deleted) → Hb Bart's hydrops fetalis (γ₄ tetramers, very high O₂ affinity but no tissue release — fatal in utero or shortly after birth). **β-thalassaemia**: 2 β-globin genes; most cases are point mutations affecting transcription, splicing, or translation; β⁰ = absent expression, β⁺ = reduced expression. β-thal minor/trait (β/β⁺ or β/β⁰): mild microcytic anaemia, often misdiagnosed as iron deficiency; HbA2 raised. β-thal major (Cooley anaemia: β⁺/β⁺, β⁺/β⁰, or β⁰/β⁰): severe transfusion-dependent anaemia from infancy; ineffective erythropoiesis (excess α-chains precipitate), marrow expansion (frontal bossing, "chipmunk facies", "hair-on-end" skull X-ray), hepatosplenomegaly, and iron overload from transfusions and increased gut absorption (suppressed hepcidin). Treatment: regular transfusions, iron chelation (deferoxamine, deferasirox, deferiprone), allogeneic HSCT (curative), and gene therapy (beti-cel, exa-cel). Lab features distinguishing thalassaemia from IDA: normal iron studies; raised HbA2 (β-thal trait); Mentzer index <13 (MCV/RBC count) suggests thalassaemia.

Hints:

1. α-thal is mostly _deletions_; β-thal is mostly _point mutations_.
2. There are 4 α-genes and 2 β-genes; severity scales with the number affected.
3. β-thal trait raises HbA2 and is distinguished from iron deficiency by normal iron studies.

Stem: Hereditary spherocytosis is characterised by:

Correct answer: Spherical RBCs (loss of biconcave shape) due to membrane skeleton defects (most commonly ankyrin or spectrin), with increased osmotic fragility and splenic sequestration → chronic haemolysis; usually autosomal dominant; presents with anaemia, jaundice, splenomegaly, pigment gallstones, and aplastic crises with parvovirus B19; treated with folic acid, splenectomy in severe cases (after immune system maturation), cholecystectomy if symptomatic gallstones.

Distractors:

- "Sickle-shaped cells." — Reveals misconception: student picks the SCD morphology. Correction: HS produces _spherical_ cells without central pallor; sickle cells are crescent-shaped from HbS polymerisation.
- "Microcytic RBCs." — Reveals misconception: student picks the iron deficiency morphology. Correction: HS RBCs are typically _normocytic_ with raised MCHC (very dense cells), not microcytic.
- "Megaloblastic RBCs." — Reveals misconception: student picks the B12/folate morphology. Correction: HS cells are spherocytes; megaloblastic cells are large oval macrocytes from B12 or folate deficiency.

Explanation: Hereditary spherocytosis (HS) is the most common inherited haemolytic anaemia in Northern European populations [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Pathology: defects in ankyrin (most common, ~50%), spectrin, band 3, or protein 4.2 → membrane skeleton instability → membrane microvesicles released → progressive surface area loss → spherical shape. Spherocytes are rigid (cannot navigate spleen) and have decreased osmotic resistance (lyse in mildly hypotonic solutions). Splenic sequestration → chronic haemolysis. Clinical: anaemia (variable), jaundice (haemolytic, raised unconjugated bilirubin), splenomegaly, pigment gallstones from chronic high bilirubin, and aplastic crisis with parvovirus B19 (transient erythropoiesis arrest). Diagnosis: CBC (normocytic anaemia with raised MCHC — denser RBCs), smear (spherocytes — small dense round cells without central pallor), reticulocytosis (compensatory), eosin-5'-maleimide (EMA) flow cytometry (reduced binding — current gold-standard test), osmotic fragility test (positive). Treatment: folic acid (high erythropoiesis demand), splenectomy (curative for haemolysis — corrects anaemia and bilirubin even though spherocytes persist; usually deferred until age >5 to allow immune maturation; lifelong vaccination and selected antibiotic prophylaxis), cholecystectomy if symptomatic gallstones, transfusion in severe cases or aplastic crises. Other RBC membrane disorders: hereditary elliptocytosis (spectrin self-association defect — usually mild), hereditary pyropoikilocytosis (severe variant), hereditary stomatocytosis, and paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH — acquired clonal stem cell disorder with PIGA mutation → loss of GPI-anchored CD55/CD59 → complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis; treated with eculizumab).

Hints:

1. The cells are spherical due to membrane skeleton defects.
2. Splenic sequestration drives the chronic haemolysis.
3. Splenectomy is curative for the haemolysis even though the cells remain spherocytic.

QUESTION 11
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: The stages of erythropoiesis from haematopoietic stem cell to mature RBC are:

Correct answer: HSC → CMP (common myeloid progenitor) → MEP (megakaryocyte–erythroid progenitor) → BFU-E → CFU-E → proerythroblast → basophilic, polychromatic, and orthochromatic erythroblasts → reticulocyte (nucleus extruded) → mature RBC; the entire process takes ~7 days, with EPO acting principally on CFU-E and early erythroblasts.

Distractors:

- "HSC → reticulocyte → RBC, with no intermediate stages." — Reveals misconception: student collapses the lineage. Correction: erythropoiesis passes through several committed progenitor and morphologically distinct erythroblast stages before the reticulocyte.
- "RBC → reticulocyte → erythroblast → HSC." — Reveals misconception: student reverses the developmental direction. Correction: differentiation flows _from_ HSC _to_ mature RBC, not the reverse.
- "HSC → lymphoid lineage → RBC." — Reveals misconception: student routes RBCs through the lymphoid lineage. Correction: RBCs derive from the _myeloid_ lineage (CMP → MEP), not from CLP.

Explanation: Erythropoiesis follows a defined hierarchy: HSC (self-renewing, multipotent) → CMP (common myeloid progenitor) → MEP (megakaryocyte–erythroid progenitor) → BFU-E (burst-forming unit–erythroid; large colonies on culture) → CFU-E (colony-forming unit–erythroid; small colonies) → proerythroblast → basophilic erythroblast (haemoglobin synthesis begins) → polychromatic erythroblast → orthochromatic erythroblast (nucleus condensed, ready for extrusion) → reticulocyte (anucleate, residual ribosomes) → mature RBC [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Total time from CFU-E to circulating RBC is ~7 days. EPO acts mainly on CFU-E and early erythroblasts, preventing apoptosis and driving proliferation and differentiation. Iron, B12, and folate are required for adequate erythropoiesis — deficiencies produce ineffective erythropoiesis with characteristic morphological abnormalities (microcytic in iron deficiency, megaloblastic in B12/folate deficiency). The marrow can expand erythropoiesis 6- to 8-fold in response to severe anaemia, provided substrates are available.

Hints:

1. The lineage starts at HSC and ends at the mature RBC.
2. EPO acts mainly on CFU-E and early erythroblasts.
3. The total time from CFU-E to circulating RBC is about a week.

- "Reduced reticulocytes and high haptoglobin." — Reveals misconception: student inverts both markers. Correction: haemolysis raises reticulocytes (marrow response) and _reduces_ haptoglobin (consumed binding free Hb).
- "Conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong bilirubin fraction. Correction: haemolysis raises _unconjugated_ bilirubin (excess haem catabolism); conjugated rises in cholestasis or post-hepatic obstruction.
- "Microcytic hypochromic RBCs." — Reveals misconception: student picks the iron deficiency morphology. Correction: haemolytic anaemia is typically normocytic (with reticulocytosis pushing MCV slightly up); microcytic hypochromic suggests iron deficiency.

Explanation: Haemolytic anaemia features are largely shared across causes [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Markers of cell destruction: raised LDH (released from lysed cells), unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia (haem catabolism), low haptoglobin (consumed binding free Hb in plasma; absent or undetectable in significant haemolysis). Markers of marrow response: reticulocytosis (often >5%); polychromasia on smear (young RBCs appear bluish). Intravascular haemolysis adds: haemoglobinaemia (free Hb in plasma), haemoglobinuria (Hb in urine if haptoglobin saturated), urinary haemosiderin (Prussian blue staining of sloughed renal tubular cells; chronic), reduced complement levels (in some immune-mediated forms). Smear features point to specific causes: spherocytes (HS, warm AIHA), sickle cells (SCD), bite cells/Heinz bodies (G6PD), schistocytes (MAHA — TTP, HUS, DIC, mechanical valves, malignant hypertension, eclampsia/HELLP), target cells (thalassaemia, liver disease), elliptocytes (HE), agglutinates (cold AIHA). Tests of cause include direct antiglobulin (Coombs) test (positive in AIHA), Hb electrophoresis (SCD, thalassaemia), G6PD assay, EMA flow cytometry (HS), and ADAMTS13 (TTP).

Hints:

1. Reticulocytosis and raised LDH go together with low haptoglobin.
2. Bilirubin rise is _unconjugated_, not conjugated.
3. The smear morphology points to the specific cause.

Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The direct and indirect Coombs tests differ in that:

Correct answer: The direct antiglobulin test (DAT) detects antibody or complement _already bound to the patient's RBCs_ (used to diagnose autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, drug-induced immune haemolysis, HDFN, transfusion reactions); the indirect antiglobulin test (IAT) detects _free_ antibodies in the patient's serum by adding test RBCs and looking for binding (used for cross-matching and prenatal antibody screening).

Distractors:

- "DAT and IAT detect the same thing." — Reveals misconception: student denies any difference. Correction: DAT detects antibody on RBCs; IAT detects antibody in serum; the distinction matters for diagnosis vs cross-matching.
- "DAT is performed in the patient's serum without RBCs." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the methods. Correction: DAT uses the _patient's RBCs_; IAT uses the patient's _serum_ tested against added RBCs.
- "IAT is used to diagnose AIHA." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong test for AIHA. Correction: AIHA is diagnosed by _DAT_ (positive when antibody is on RBCs); IAT is used in cross-matching.

Explanation: The Coombs (antiglobulin) tests detect immune-mediated RBC destruction [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Direct antiglobulin test (DAT): patient's RBCs are washed and exposed to anti-human-globulin (Coombs reagent); agglutination indicates antibody (IgG) or complement (C3d) bound to RBC surface. Used for autoimmune haemolytic anaemia (warm AIHA — IgG positive; cold AIHA — C3d positive), drug-induced immune haemolytic anaemia (e.g., methyldopa, penicillin, cefotetan), haemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn (maternal anti-D crosses placenta), and acute or delayed haemolytic transfusion reactions. Indirect antiglobulin test (IAT): patient serum is incubated with reagent RBCs of known antigens; if patient has free antibodies, they bind to test cells; addition of Coombs reagent produces agglutination. Used for cross-matching before transfusion, prenatal antibody screening (anti-D and other red-cell antibodies), antibody identification panels, and blood typing. Both tests share the use of anti-human-globulin reagent — the difference is whether antibodies are pre-bound to the patient's own RBCs (DAT) or free in serum (IAT).

Hints:

1. DAT looks for antibody _on_ the patient's RBCs.
2. IAT looks for antibody _in_ the patient's serum.
3. AIHA is diagnosed by DAT; cross-matching uses IAT.

Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia (AIHA) is best classified into:

Correct answer: Warm AIHA (IgG, optimal binding at 37 °C, extravascular haemolysis in the spleen) — idiopathic, drug-induced (methyldopa, penicillin, cefotetan), lymphoma, SLE — treated with corticosteroids first-line, then rituximab and splenectomy; and cold AIHA (IgM, agglutinates in cold extremities, complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis) — Mycoplasma pneumoniae, EBV (transient), Waldenström macroglobulinaemia (chronic) — treated with cold avoidance, rituximab, and complement inhibition (e.g., sutimlimab) for chronic forms.

Distractors:

- "Identical pathophysiology and treatment in warm and cold AIHA." — Reveals misconception: student denies the distinction. Correction: warm AIHA is IgG-mediated (steroid-responsive), cold AIHA is IgM-mediated (steroid-unresponsive, requires rituximab/complement-targeted therapy).
- "Always intravascular haemolysis." — Reveals misconception: student picks one mechanism for all. Correction: warm AIHA is _extravascular_ (splenic macrophage clearance); cold AIHA can be intravascular (complement-mediated lysis).
- "Treated identically to iron deficiency anaemia." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong therapy. Correction: AIHA requires immunosuppression (corticosteroids, rituximab) and sometimes splenectomy; iron is irrelevant unless concurrent deficiency.

Explanation: AIHA is divided by the thermal optimum of the responsible antibody [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Warm AIHA (~80% of cases): IgG (with or without complement) optimal binding at 37 °C; extravascular haemolysis in spleen (macrophages with Fc receptors recognise IgG-coated RBCs); causes — idiopathic (most), lymphoma/CLL, SLE, drugs (methyldopa — classical, now historic; penicillin and cefotetan via different immune mechanisms), infections; presentation — anaemia, jaundice, splenomegaly, raised LDH, low haptoglobin, unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia; positive DAT (IgG ± C3d); spherocytes on smear. Treatment: corticosteroids (prednisolone 1 mg/kg) first-line; rituximab second-line; splenectomy in refractory cases; transfusion in severe symptomatic anaemia (cross-match difficult — give "least incompatible" units). Cold AIHA (~20%): IgM agglutinates RBCs at lower temperatures (cold extremities), fixes complement, and produces complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis; raised cold agglutinin titre; causes — Mycoplasma pneumoniae and EBV (post-infectious, transient), Waldenström macroglobulinaemia and B-cell lymphomas (chronic), idiopathic. Treatment: cold avoidance (warm clothing, warm fluids), treat underlying disorder, rituximab for chronic; sutimlimab (anti-C1s antibody) — newly approved (2022) for chronic cold AIHA. Steroids are _less effective_ in cold AIHA. Mixed AIHA and paroxysmal cold haemoglobinuria (Donath–Landsteiner antibody — biphasic IgG, post-viral in children) are rarer variants.

Hints:

1. Warm AIHA is IgG-mediated and extravascular.
2. Cold AIHA is IgM-mediated, complement-driven, and intravascular.
3. Steroids work in warm AIHA but are less useful in cold AIHA.

Stem: Microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (MAHA) is best characterised by:

Correct answer: RBC fragmentation as cells traverse fibrin strands or abnormal endothelium → schistocytes on smear, raised LDH, low haptoglobin, unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia, often thrombocytopenia, and end-organ injury; causes include thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP — ADAMTS13 deficiency), haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS — Shiga-toxin or complement-mediated), DIC, malignant hypertension, mechanical heart valves, and HELLP syndrome; TTP is treated urgently with plasma exchange, corticosteroids, and caplacizumab.

Distractors:

- "Spherocytes on smear." — Reveals misconception: student picks the HS/AIHA morphology. Correction: MAHA shows _schistocytes_ (RBC fragments) from mechanical fragmentation; spherocytes belong to HS and AIHA.
- "Treated with iron supplementation." — Reveals misconception: student picks an iron deficiency therapy. Correction: MAHA requires treatment of the underlying cause (plasma exchange for TTP, antibiotics and supportive care for HUS, treat underlying DIC trigger, BP control for malignant hypertension).
- "Always benign and self-limited." — Reveals misconception: student dismisses MAHA. Correction: MAHA is potentially life-threatening — TTP without prompt plasma exchange has high mortality; HUS may produce AKI requiring dialysis.

Explanation: MAHA is a syndrome of RBC fragmentation by abnormal microvasculature [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Pathophysiology: small vessels develop fibrin strands, platelet microthrombi, or abnormal endothelium; passing RBCs are sliced, producing characteristic schistocytes (helmet cells, fragments) on blood smear plus markers of haemolysis. Causes: TTP (ADAMTS13 deficiency — autoimmune in adults, congenital in some; ADAMTS13 normally cleaves ultra-large von Willebrand factor multimers, and its deficiency allows VWF-platelet microthrombi to form; classical pentad — fever, thrombocytopenia, MAHA, AKI, and neurological signs — though all five are rarely seen together; treatment is urgent plasma exchange, corticosteroids, and caplacizumab (anti-VWF nanobody, FDA-approved 2019)); HUS (typical post-Shiga toxin from E. coli O157:H7 — bloody diarrhoea, AKI, MAHA, thrombocytopenia, supportive care; atypical HUS — complement dysregulation, treated with eculizumab/ravulizumab); DIC (consumption coagulopathy from sepsis, malignancy, obstetric emergencies, trauma — schistocytes plus prolonged PT/aPTT, low fibrinogen, raised D-dimer); malignant hypertension; mechanical heart valves (intracardiac haemolysis); HELLP syndrome (haemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, low platelets — pre-eclampsia complication, immediate delivery). Immediate workup includes urgent CBC, smear (schistocytes), LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin, renal function, ADAMTS13 activity, and coagulation studies; treatment depends on cause but TTP requires immediate plasma exchange.

Hints:

1. The key smear feature is schistocytes from RBC fragmentation.
2. TTP, HUS, DIC, and HELLP are major causes.
3. TTP requires urgent plasma exchange.

Stem: Aplastic anaemia is best characterised by:

Correct answer: Pancytopenia (anaemia, leukopenia/neutropenia, thrombocytopenia) from bone marrow failure with hypocellular marrow on biopsy; causes include idiopathic (autoimmune destruction of HSCs — most common), drugs (chloramphenicol, sulfa, NSAIDs, anticonvulsants), radiation, viruses (parvovirus B19 — pure red cell aplasia; hepatitis), and inherited bone marrow failure syndromes (Fanconi anaemia); treated with allogeneic HSCT (curative in young matched-donor patients), immunosuppression (ATG + cyclosporine ± eltrombopag), and supportive care.

Distractors:

- "Hyperplastic marrow with leukocytosis." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the marrow picture. Correction: aplastic anaemia features _hypocellular_ marrow with pancytopenia; hypercellular marrow with leukocytosis suggests leukaemia or reactive states.
- "Isolated thrombocytosis." — Reveals misconception: student picks single-lineage abnormality. Correction: aplastic anaemia is _pancytopenia_ — all three lineages reduced.
- "Reversed by iron and B12 supplementation." — Reveals misconception: student picks nutritional therapy. Correction: aplastic anaemia is marrow failure; treatment is HSCT or immunosuppression, not nutritional supplementation.

Explanation: Aplastic anaemia is bone marrow failure producing pancytopenia with hypocellular marrow on biopsy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Causes: idiopathic (most common, autoimmune destruction of HSCs by cytotoxic T cells), drug-induced (chloramphenicol — historical; sulfonamides; NSAIDs; anticonvulsants — phenytoin, carbamazepine; gold; some chemotherapy), radiation, viral (parvovirus B19 — selective red cell aplasia, especially in chronic haemolytic anaemias; hepatitis viruses; HIV; EBV), pregnancy (rare), and inherited bone marrow failure syndromes (Fanconi anaemia — autosomal recessive, short stature, skeletal anomalies including absent radii, predisposition to AML and solid tumours; dyskeratosis congenita — telomere maintenance defect with skin and nail abnormalities; Diamond–Blackfan — pure red cell aplasia from infancy; Shwachman–Diamond — pancreatic exocrine insufficiency plus marrow failure). Diagnosis: bone marrow biopsy (hypocellular <25% of normal, with relative preservation of stromal elements) and exclusion of myelodysplastic syndromes, hypoplastic AML, and other causes of pancytopenia. Treatment: allogeneic HSCT (curative in young patients <40 with matched sibling donor — first-line); immunosuppression (anti-thymocyte globulin + cyclosporine ± eltrombopag, a TPO mimetic — first-line if no transplant donor or >40 years); supportive care (RBC and platelet transfusions, prophylactic antimicrobials, growth factors). Untreated severe aplastic anaemia has high mortality from infection and bleeding. Pure red cell aplasia (selective erythroid hypoplasia) is a related entity treated similarly.

Hints:

1. The hallmark is pancytopenia with hypocellular marrow.
2. Idiopathic, drug-induced, viral, and inherited causes are the main categories.
3. HSCT is curative; immunosuppression is the alternative.

Stem: Pure red cell aplasia (PRCA) is best characterised by:

Correct answer: Selective failure of erythroid progenitors with profound reticulocytopenia and normocytic normochromic anaemia, but preserved leukocytes and platelets; causes include parvovirus B19 (especially in patients with chronic haemolytic anaemias such as sickle cell disease and HS, where it causes a transient aplastic crisis), thymoma association, drugs, anti-EPO antibodies, autoimmune disease, and Diamond–Blackfan anaemia (congenital); treatment depends on cause — IVIG for chronic parvovirus, thymectomy if thymoma, immunosuppression for autoimmune.

Distractors:

- "Pancytopenia with hypocellular marrow." — Reveals misconception: student picks aplastic anaemia. Correction: PRCA is _selective_ erythroid failure — leukocytes and platelets are preserved; aplastic anaemia is pancytopenic.
- "Macrocytic megaloblastic anaemia from B12 deficiency." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong mechanism. Correction: PRCA shows normocytic anaemia with profound reticulocytopenia; megaloblastic anaemia from B12 has hypersegmented neutrophils and macrocytosis.
- "Self-limiting in all cases." — Reveals misconception: student dismisses chronic forms. Correction: parvovirus B19 PRCA in immunocompetent patients with chronic haemolytic anaemia is usually self-limited (~1–2 weeks) but persistent forms (e.g., immunocompromised) need IVIG; other causes (thymoma, autoimmune) require specific therapy.

Explanation: PRCA is selective erythroid progenitor failure, distinguishing it from aplastic anaemia (pancytopenia with hypocellular marrow) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Lab: normocytic normochromic anaemia with profound reticulocytopenia (often <0.1%) and preserved leukocytes and platelets; bone marrow shows absent erythroid precursors with normal myeloid and megakaryocyte lineages. Causes: parvovirus B19 (binds P antigen on erythroid progenitors; in chronic haemolytic anaemias such as SCD, HS, and thalassaemia, the brief erythroid arrest precipitates an _aplastic crisis_ with severe Hb drop — transient because RBCs are short-lived in these conditions; in immunocompromised patients, persistent infection causes ongoing PRCA), thymoma association (~10% of PRCA — thymectomy can resolve it), drugs (phenytoin, isoniazid, EPO-induced anti-EPO antibodies — rare with current EPO formulations), autoimmune (often associated with CLL or other lymphoproliferative disorders, SLE, RA), large granular lymphocyte leukaemia, ABO-incompatible bone marrow transplantation, and congenital (Diamond–Blackfan anaemia — autosomal dominant, ribosomal protein gene mutations, presents in infancy with macrocytic anaemia, often with congenital anomalies). Treatment: IVIG for chronic parvovirus PRCA; thymectomy for thymoma-associated; immunosuppression (corticosteroids, cyclosporine, rituximab) for autoimmune/idiopathic; corticosteroids for Diamond–Blackfan; transfusion support.

Hints:

1. The defect is selective — only erythroid lineage fails.
2. Parvovirus B19 is the classical infectious cause.
3. Thymoma association is an important clinical clue.

Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Anaemia of chronic kidney disease (CKD) is best characterised by:

Correct answer: Normocytic normochromic anaemia from reduced renal EPO production by peritubular interstitial fibroblasts (HIF-α suppression in failing kidneys), with reticulocyte response blunted; raised hepcidin from inflammation contributes to functional iron deficiency; treated with recombinant EPO (epoetin α/β, darbepoetin) and concurrent iron supplementation, with newer oral HIF stabilisers (roxadustat, daprodustat, vadadustat) increasingly used.

Distractors:

- "Macrocytic megaloblastic anaemia from B12 deficiency." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong morphology and cause. Correction: CKD anaemia is normocytic with low reticulocytes from EPO deficiency; B12 deficiency is macrocytic megaloblastic.
- "High EPO and reticulocytosis." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the EPO picture. Correction: CKD reduces EPO production → low EPO and blunted reticulocyte response.
- "Treated by oral B12 alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong therapy. Correction: anaemia of CKD is treated with recombinant EPO (or HIF stabilisers) and iron; oral B12 alone is irrelevant unless concurrent deficiency.

Explanation: Anaemia of CKD is a normocytic normochromic anaemia with low reticulocytes due to reduced renal EPO production [Boron & Boulpaep ch.34]. Mechanism: peritubular interstitial fibroblasts in the renal cortex normally produce EPO in response to hypoxia (via HIF stabilisation); with renal failure, fewer functional fibroblasts and impaired HIF responses reduce EPO; concurrent inflammation raises hepcidin (CKD has high IL-6) which traps iron in stores, producing functional iron deficiency despite adequate stores; uraemia also shortens RBC lifespan modestly. Diagnosis is clinical (low Hb in setting of reduced eGFR, low reticulocytes, raised ferritin from inflammation, low transferrin saturation) — exclude other causes (iron, B12, folate, blood loss, MDS, haemolysis). Treatment: recombinant EPO (epoetin α/β, darbepoetin α — longer half-life) starts when Hb drops below ~100 g/L, targeting Hb 100–120 g/L; concurrent iron supplementation (often IV iron in dialysis patients to overcome hepcidin-mediated absorption block); avoid over-treatment (Hb >130) which paradoxically increases cardiovascular events and mortality (TREAT, CHOIR trials); newer oral HIF prolyl hydroxylase inhibitors (roxadustat, daprodustat, vadadustat) stabilise HIF-α and induce endogenous EPO and reduce hepcidin — convenient oral alternatives now in clinical use.

Hints:

1. The dominant mechanism is reduced renal EPO production.
2. Treatment is recombinant EPO plus iron, with HIF stabilisers as a newer oral option.
3. Over-treatment to high Hb targets paradoxically harms outcomes.

Type: application

Stem: Pregnancy anaemia is best characterised by:

Correct answer: Plasma volume expansion ~50% with RBC mass expansion ~25% → relative haemodilution and modest fall in Hb (lower limit ~110 g/L in pregnancy by WHO definition); iron supplementation often needed because of markedly increased iron demand (~1000 mg total — maternal RBC mass, fetus, placenta, delivery loss); folate supplementation universal (400 µg/day pre-conception and during the first trimester) to reduce neural-tube-defect risk; pregnancy is also a hypercoagulable state with increased VTE risk requiring prophylactic LMWH in selected patients.

Distractors:

- "RBC mass falls and Hb rises." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the haematological changes. Correction: RBC mass _expands_ but plasma expands more — producing relative haemodilution and a modest Hb fall, not a rise.
- "Iron requirements fall during pregnancy." — Reveals misconception: student inverts iron balance. Correction: iron requirements _rise dramatically_ — supplementation is often needed.
- "Folate is contraindicated in pregnancy." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong direction. Correction: folate supplementation (400 µg/day pre-conception and during the first trimester) is universally recommended to reduce neural-tube-defect risk.

Explanation: Pregnancy haematology features physiological remodelling [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Plasma volume expands ~50% (~1500 mL increase by term) with RBC mass expanding ~25% — producing relative haemodilution and a modest Hb fall (WHO defines anaemia in pregnancy as Hb <110 g/L). Iron requirements rise dramatically (~1000 mg total: ~500 for maternal RBC mass expansion, ~300 for fetus and placenta, ~200 for blood loss at delivery); supplementation is often needed (oral iron, IV iron in intolerant patients). Folate requirements rise; supplementation (400 µg/day pre-conception and during the first trimester) reduces neural tube defect risk and is universal in many settings (with food fortification in some countries). The pregnant state is hypercoagulable: increased fibrinogen and factors VII, VIII, IX, X; reduced protein S; increased PAI-1 and PAI-2; reduced fibrinolysis; venous stasis from gravid uterus compression — together producing a 4–5-fold increase in VTE risk during pregnancy and ~20-fold in the puerperium. Prophylactic LMWH is given in selected patients with risk factors (previous VTE, thrombophilia, prolonged immobilisation, post-Caesarean, pre-eclampsia). Postpartum haemorrhage and pre-eclampsia/HELLP syndrome are major causes of maternal morbidity and mortality. Sickle cell disease, HS, and thalassaemia have particular pregnancy considerations (transfusion thresholds, increased crisis risk in SCD, iron management).

Hints:

1. Plasma expands more than RBC mass, so Hb falls relatively.
2. Iron and folate requirements rise — supplementation is routine.
3. Pregnancy is hypercoagulable with increased VTE risk.

## Summary

Pass 1 (Q1–10) develops RBC structure and haemoglobin biology — the biconcave disc and spectrin–ankyrin skeleton, anucleate metabolism (glycolysis, pentose phosphate, 2,3-BPG), haem–O₂ binding and the sigmoidal dissociation curve, the Bohr effect and right/left shifts, arterial O₂ content and the dominance of bound over dissolved O₂, adult and fetal Hb types and developmental switching, the reticulocyte and its diagnostic value in anaemia, sickle cell disease, the α/β thalassaemias, and hereditary spherocytosis.
