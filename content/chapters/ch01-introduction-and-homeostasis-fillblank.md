## Pass 1 — Body fluids and composition

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: In a healthy adult, total body water makes up approximately \_\_\_ % of body weight.

Canonical answer: 60%

Accepted variants:

- 60%
- 60
- 0.6
- 50–70% (textbook range)
- 50% to 70%

Tolerance: ±10 percentage points (i.e., 50% to 70% all grade Green)

Yellow conditions:

- Numeric value matches but unit (%) missing → "Right value — make sure to include the percent sign."
- Value within 40–50% or 70–80% (just outside the tolerance window) → "Close — the textbook reference range is 50% to 70%."

Explanation: About 50% to 70% of an adult's body weight is water [Guyton ch.1; Costanzo p.1; Ganong ch.1]. The exact figure depends on body composition: adipose tissue contains very little water, so total body water correlates inversely with body fat. A lean young man is closer to 60%; a person with more adipose tissue is closer to 50%. Ganong's complementary breakdown of an average young adult male — 18% protein, 7% mineral, 15% fat — leaves the residual ≈60% as water [Ganong ch.1].

Hints:

1. The body is mostly water, but how much is "mostly"?
2. The percentage is well over half but below three-quarters.
3. The reference textbooks give a range, not a single number, because adipose tissue affects it.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The dominant cation in _extracellular_ fluid is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: sodium (Na⁺)

Accepted variants:

- sodium
- Na+
- Na⁺
- sodium ion
- Na

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "potassium" or "K+" → "That is the dominant intracellular cation. The ECF is sodium-rich."

Explanation: ECF is rich in sodium, chloride, and bicarbonate ions, with comparatively little potassium [Guyton ch.1; Ganong ch.1]. The asymmetry between ECF (sodium-rich) and ICF (potassium-rich) is maintained by the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, and it underlies electrical excitability, secondary active transport, and cell volume regulation.

Hints:

1. Two ions dominate the cation profile of the body — one in ECF, one in ICF.
2. The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pumps one cation _out_ of cells and one _in_.
3. The ion pumped _out_ dominates the ECF.

## Pass 2 — Normal values from Table 1-1

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The normal arterial pH is approximately \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: 7.4

Accepted variants:

- 7.4
- 7.40
- 7.35–7.45 (clinical range)

Tolerance: ±0.05

Yellow conditions:

- Value within 7.0–7.7 but outside ±0.05 of 7.4 → "Close — the standard reference value is 7.4."
- "7.0" → "That would be neutral water at 25°C. Plasma pH is slightly more alkaline."

Explanation: Normal arterial pH is 7.4, with a normal range of 7.35–7.45 [Guyton ch.1, Table 1-1; Ganong ch.1]. Hydrogen ion activity in plasma is therefore extremely tightly regulated — the ion concentration variations are normally less than 5 nmol/L. Beyond the short-term non-lethal range of 6.9 to 8.0, enzyme function is so disturbed that survival is impossible.

Hints:

1. Plasma pH is slightly more alkaline than neutral.
2. The value is regulated within a very narrow range — typically only ±0.05 from the central value.
3. The number begins with seven.

## Pass 3 — Concepts, terminology, and the gain calculation

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: In 1926, Walter Cannon coined the term \_\_\_ to describe the maintenance of nearly constant conditions in the internal environment.

Canonical answer: homeostasis

Accepted variants:

- homeostasis
- Homeostasis

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "milieu intérieur" or "internal environment" → "That is the _concept_ (introduced by Bernard) — Cannon's term names the _regulation_ of that environment."

Explanation: Walter Cannon, an American physiologist, coined "homeostasis" in 1926 to describe the maintenance of nearly constant conditions in the body's internal environment [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1]. The concept of an internal environment was earlier articulated by Claude Bernard as the _milieu intérieur_; Cannon's contribution was naming and formalising the regulatory framework that defends its constancy.

Hints:

1. The word combines Greek roots meaning "similar" and "standing/state."
2. The term is from the early 20th century and is now the central concept of physiology.
3. The term begins with "homeo-".

QUESTION 14
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Claude Bernard's 19th-century French term for the internal environment in which cells live is the \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: milieu intérieur

Accepted variants:

- milieu intérieur
- milieu interieur (without accent)
- milieu interior
- "internal environment" (English equivalent)

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "homeostasis" → "That is Cannon's term (1926) for the _regulation_ of the internal environment, not Bernard's term for the environment itself."

Explanation: Claude Bernard (1813–1878) recognised that cells of the body do not contact the external world directly but live in an internal fluid environment — the extracellular fluid — whose composition is held within narrow limits [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1; Boron ch.1]. He named this internal environment the _milieu intérieur_. The concept is the philosophical foundation of regulatory physiology: homeostatic mechanisms exist precisely to defend the constancy of the _milieu intérieur_.

Hints:

1. The term is in French.
2. It describes _where_ cells live, not how the body regulates that environment.
3. The two French words mean "environment" and "interior."

QUESTION 15
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A control mechanism in which a deviation in a regulated variable triggers a response that _opposes_ the deviation is called \_\_\_ feedback.

Canonical answer: negative

Accepted variants:

- negative
- Negative

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "positive" → "Positive feedback amplifies the deviation; the control architecture that _opposes_ the deviation is the opposite type."
- "feed-forward" → "Feed-forward acts in anticipation of a deviation, before it occurs. The architecture that responds _to_ a deviation by opposing it is named differently."

Explanation: Negative feedback is the central architectural principle of homeostatic control. A change in a regulated variable triggers a response opposite to the change, returning the variable toward its set point [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1; Ganong ch.1]. Most homeostatic systems — temperature regulation, arterial pressure control, blood glucose, acid–base balance — are negative-feedback systems.

Hints:

1. The word "negative" describes the _direction_ of the response (opposite the disturbance).
2. The response cancels the disturbance, returning the variable to its set point.
3. It is the opposite of an amplifying loop.

QUESTION 16
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The opening of voltage-gated sodium channels during the upstroke of a nerve action potential — where each opened channel promotes the opening of more channels — is an example of \_\_\_ feedback.

Canonical answer: positive

Accepted variants:

- positive
- Positive

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "negative" → "Negative feedback opposes the deviation; here, sodium entry causes more sodium entry — the response amplifies the deviation."

Explanation: The action potential upstroke is the paradigmatic example of useful positive feedback in physiology [Guyton ch.1]. A small depolarisation opens voltage-gated sodium channels; sodium entry depolarises further; further depolarisation opens more channels — a self-amplifying loop. Like all useful positive feedback, it has a built-in termination event: voltage-dependent inactivation of the sodium channels and activation of voltage-gated potassium channels end the cycle. Other useful examples include the clotting cascade and uterine contractions during childbirth.

Hints:

1. The response amplifies the original change rather than opposing it.
2. The architecture is self-reinforcing — each step reinforces the previous one.
3. The opposite of the architecture in Question 15.

QUESTION 17
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: When gut hormones trigger insulin release in anticipation of a glucose load — _before_ blood glucose has risen — this anticipatory mechanism is called \_\_\_ control.

Canonical answer: feed-forward

Accepted variants:

- feed-forward
- feedforward
- feed forward
- anticipatory

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "negative feedback" → "Negative feedback responds to a deviation that has occurred. Here, the response is _before_ the deviation occurs."
- "positive feedback" → "Positive feedback amplifies a deviation. Anticipation is not amplification — it acts before any deviation has begun."
- "adaptive control" → "Adaptive control refines a feed-forward signal across repeated trials. The act of releasing insulin in anticipation, on its own, is called something else."

Explanation: Feed-forward control acts in _anticipation_ of a change rather than in response to one [Sherwood ch.1; Guyton ch.1]. Incretin hormones (e.g., GIP, GLP-1) released by the gut when food is detected stimulate insulin secretion before the glucose load reaches the bloodstream, blunting the post-prandial rise. Feed-forward complements negative feedback by reducing the magnitude of the excursion that negative feedback would otherwise have to correct.

Hints:

1. The release happens _before_ the deviation, not in response to it.
2. The body is acting in anticipation, not reacting.
3. The term begins with "feed-".

QUESTION 18
Type: calculation
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A blood transfusion raises arterial pressure from 100 to 175 mm Hg in a patient whose baroreflex is non-functional, and from 100 to 125 mm Hg in a patient whose baroreflex is intact. Using gain = correction ÷ error, the gain of the baroreceptor pressure-control system is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: −2

Accepted variants:

- −2
- -2
- minus 2
- "−2 (no units)"

Tolerance: ±0.5

Yellow conditions:

- "+2" or "2" → "Right magnitude — include the negative sign. The negative sign indicates the correction is in the _opposite direction_ of the disturbance, which is the defining feature of negative feedback."
- "−0.5" → "Inverted — divide _correction_ by _error_, not the other way round."
- "−1" → "Recompute. Correction is −50 mm Hg (the system pulled the pressure down by 50). Error is +25 mm Hg (the residual rise the system did not correct)."

Explanation: Gain = correction ÷ error [Guyton ch.1]. The system corrected the rise from a hypothetical 175 mm Hg back to 125 mm Hg — a correction of −50 mm Hg. The residual error (the rise the system did not eliminate) is +25 mm Hg. So gain = −50 / +25 = −2. The negative sign reflects that the correction is in the opposite direction of the disturbance — the defining feature of negative feedback. The temperature-control system has a much higher gain (about −33), reflecting tighter regulation than the baroreflex achieves for arterial pressure.

Hints:

1. Gain has two parts — what the system corrected, and what error remained.
2. Correction = pressure-without-baroreflex minus pressure-with-baroreflex (with sign).
3. Compute correction (−50) divided by error (+25); pay attention to the sign.

**Convention used in stems:** book names (Guyton, Costanzo, Ganong, Sherwood) appear only as inline citation markers in the explanation portion — never in the question stem itself. The student-facing stem describes the physiology directly.

**Pedagogical choices worth noting:**

- _Single-blank only._ Per the prompt, no question has more than one blank. Concepts that involve two values (e.g., normal range AND survivable range for pH) were split into two questions (Q7 and Q8).
- _Tolerance and Yellow conditions._ Numeric questions specify a tolerance window for Green grading; for non-numeric (term recall) questions, tolerance is "not applicable" and Yellow conditions name the _predictable wrong term_ a student is most likely to type instead. The Yellow feedback messages are written so the student understands the specific confusion they made.
- _Distribution of types._ 16 of 18 are recall (Type: recall); 1 is conceptual recall edging toward understanding (Q17 — feed-forward); 1 is calculation (Q18). This skew matches the prompt's guidance that fill-blank should be dominated by recall and calculation.
- _Bloom's level skew._ Most questions are at "remember"; Q17 is "understand" (the student must recognise that anticipation = feed-forward, not just recall a number); Q18 is "apply" (the student computes a value).
- _Aspects covered._ Body composition (Q1), compartments and their proportions (Q2, Q3), ionic asymmetry (Q4, Q5), microcirculation (Q6), normal values (Q7–Q12), terminology (Q13–Q17), control gain (Q18). The set illuminates Chapter 1 broadly rather than drilling a single facet.
- _No questions on Bernard's biographical dates, Cannon's nationality, or other low-yield trivia._ The one question on each historical figure (Q13, Q14) tests the _concept_ they are remembered for, not biographical detail.
