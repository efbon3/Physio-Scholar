QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe vascular tone regulation - local, neural, humoral.

Model answer: Vascular tone reflects balance of vasoconstrictors and vasodilators. _Local_: (a) myogenic (Bayliss reflex - smooth muscle contracts when stretched, maintains autoregulation); (b) metabolic (active tissue produces CO2, H+, K+, adenosine, lactate → vasodilation); (c) endothelial (NO via shear stress and ACh; PGI2; EDHF; opposed by ET-1, TXA2). _Neural_: sympathetic α1 (vasoconstriction), β2 (vasodilation in skeletal muscle); parasympathetic limited (sex organs, salivary glands - vasodilation via NO). _Humoral_: NA, adrenaline, AT II, vasopressin/ADH, ANP/BNP. Autoregulation maintains organ flow despite BP changes (cerebral, renal, coronary). Drugs: nitrates (NO donors), CCBs, α1 blockers, endothelin antagonists, PDE5 inhibitors. Endothelial dysfunction underlies atherosclerosis, HTN, DM, smoking [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe Starling forces and capillary fluid exchange.

Model answer: Net filtration = K*f × [(P_c - P_i) - σ(π_c - π_i)]. \_Hydrostatic*: P*c (capillary) and P_i (interstitial) push out; \_Oncotic*: π_c (plasma, mainly albumin) pulls in, π_i (interstitial) pushes out. K_f = capillary filtration coefficient (permeability × area); σ = reflection coefficient. Forces vary along capillary - filtration arteriolar end (P_c high), reabsorption venular end. Net 2-4 L/day filters out, returned by lymphatics. Imbalance → oedema: (1) ↑ P_c (HF, venous obstruction, fluid overload); (2) ↓ π_c (nephrotic syndrome, malnutrition, liver failure); (3) ↑ permeability (inflammation, sepsis, burns, anaphylaxis); (4) lymphatic obstruction (filariasis, post-mastectomy, malignancy). Pulmonary oedema: cardiogenic (high P_c) vs non-cardiogenic ARDS (high permeability). Treat underlying + diuretics + albumin selectively [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Stem: Describe lymphatic system function and disorders.

Model answer: Lymphatic system: parallel network draining excess interstitial fluid + protein + cells. ~ 2-4 L/day total. Initial lymphatics blind-ended, valveless; collect through valved vessels → lymph nodes (filtration + immune surveillance) → main trunks → thoracic duct (most) into left subclavian vein. Functions: (1) Returns interstitial fluid; (2) Protein recycling (filtered macromolecules); (3) Lipid absorption (chylomicrons via intestinal lacteals → cisterna chyli → thoracic duct → systemic); (4) Immune function (lymph nodes - antigen sampling, lymphocyte trafficking); (5) Tumour metastasis pathway. Disorders: (1) _Lymphoedema_ primary (Milroy disease, congenital absence) or secondary (post-mastectomy/radiation, filariasis - W. bancrofti most common globally, malignancy); chronic protein-rich oedema, infection-prone (cellulitis), fibrosis. Treat - compression, manual lymphatic drainage, infection prophylaxis, surgery (lymphovenous bypass) selected. (2) _Chylothorax_ - thoracic duct injury → lymph in pleural space; nutritional + drainage. (3) _Lymphangitis_ - infection. (4) _Hodgkin/non-Hodgkin lymphomas_ - lymphoid malignancies [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Type: recall

Stem: Describe endothelial function in health and disease.

Model answer: Endothelium - single layer of cells lining all vessels (~ 1 kg total in adult); active endocrine, barrier, regulatory organ. Functions: (1) _Vasoregulation_ - NO (eNOS, shear-stress activated; major basal vasodilator), PGI2 (anti-platelet + vasodilator), EDHF (small/medium arteries); opposed by ET-1, TXA2, AT II conversion. (2) _Anticoagulant_ - thrombomodulin/protein C/S system; tissue factor pathway inhibitor; heparan sulfate (antithrombin); contact phase. (3) _Antiplatelet_ - NO + PGI2; CD39/CD73 hydrolyse ADP/ATP. (4) _Immune_ - selectins for leucocyte rolling; ICAM/VCAM for adhesion; cytokine secretion. (5) _Permeability barrier_ - tight junctions (continuous capillaries; brain BBB) vs fenestrated (kidney glomerulus, GI, endocrine) vs sinusoidal (liver, spleen, marrow). (6) _Angiogenesis_ - VEGF, PDGF responsive. _Endothelial dysfunction_: reduced NO bioavailability + procoagulant + proinflammatory + impaired barrier; underlies atherosclerosis, HTN, DM, smoking, ageing, sepsis. Drugs targeting: ACE-I/ARB (preserve), statins (improve), L-arginine (precursor), tetrahydrobiopterin. Therapeutic angiogenesis (regenerative). Anti-VEGF (bevacizumab) for tumours [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Stem: Describe microcirculation in inflammation.

Model answer: Inflammation produces stereotyped microcirculatory changes (Celsus signs): rubor, calor, tumor, dolor, functio laesa. Phases: (1) _Vasoconstriction_ (transient, seconds); (2) _Vasodilation_ (minutes-hours; histamine, kinins, NO, prostaglandins) → ↑ blood flow + warmth + redness; (3) _Increased permeability_ - histamine, bradykinin, leukotrienes (LTC4/D4/E4), VEGF cause endothelial cell contraction (intercellular gaps); also direct injury, leukocyte-dependent injury → protein-rich exudate (transudate vs exudate distinction by protein content); (4) _Stasis_ - blood flow slows from haemoconcentration; (5) _Leukocyte adhesion + extravasation_ - margination → rolling (selectins) → firm adhesion (integrins) → diapedesis through endothelial junctions → chemotaxis to injury site (chemokines like IL-8/CXCL8). Mediators: vasoactive amines (histamine, serotonin), kinins, complement (C3a, C5a anaphylatoxins, MAC), prostaglandins, leukotrienes, cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6), chemokines, NO, ROS. Resolution or progression to chronic inflammation. Anti-inflammatory drugs: NSAIDs (COX inhibition), steroids, biologics (anti-TNF, anti-IL-6, anti-IL-1, anti-IL-17, JAK inhibitors). Failure of resolution → chronic inflammation, fibrosis, autoimmunity [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe atherosclerosis pathogenesis.

Model answer: Atherosclerosis: chronic progressive arterial intimal disease; major cause of MI, stroke, PAD globally. Pathogenesis (response-to-injury hypothesis): (1) _Endothelial dysfunction_ (HTN, smoking, dyslipidaemia, DM, shear stress at branches) - reduced NO, procoagulant, proinflammatory; (2) _LDL retention_ in intima - oxidised LDL is highly atherogenic; (3) _Monocyte recruitment_ via VCAM, MCP-1; differentiate to macrophages → ingest oxLDL via scavenger receptors (CD36, SR-A) → foam cells; (4) _Fatty streak_ (early lesion); (5) _Smooth muscle cell migration_ from media to intima, proliferation, ECM deposition; (6) _Fibrous cap_ formation; (7) _Plaque progression_ - calcification, neovascularisation, lipid core; (8) _Plaque rupture_ (vulnerable plaque - thin cap, large lipid core, inflammation) → thrombosis → acute event. Risk factors: modifiable (smoking, dyslipidaemia, HTN, DM, obesity, sedentary, diet) and non-modifiable (age, male sex, family history, genetic - Lp(a), PCSK9). Inflammation - CRP marker. Statins reduce LDL via HMG-CoA reductase + pleiotropic effects (anti-inflammatory, plaque stabilisation); PCSK9 inhibitors (alirocumab, evolocumab) reduce LDL further; ezetimibe; bempedoic acid; icosapent ethyl. Aggressive secondary prevention reduces events [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

QUESTION 7
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: Describe coronary circulation and ischaemic heart disease.

Model answer: Coronary circulation: RCA + LCA from aortic sinuses just above aortic valve. RCA → posterior descending in ~ 70% (right dominant); supplies SA + AV nodes (95% / 90%), inferior + posterior LV. LCA → left main → LAD (anterior LV, septum, apex) + circumflex (lateral LV, often LA, sometimes posterior). Coronary flow ~ 250 mL/min (4-5% CO at rest); can ↑ 3-5x with exercise. Unique: most LV flow during _diastole_ (systolic compression of intramyocardial vessels); RV continuous. Autoregulation maintains flow over MAP 60-130. Metabolic regulation - adenosine, NO, K+, prostaglandins; sympathetic α (constriction) overridden by metabolic vasodilation in exercise. _Ischaemic heart disease_ spectrum: stable angina (atherosclerotic narrowing - O2 supply/demand mismatch on exertion); acute coronary syndromes - unstable angina + NSTEMI + STEMI from plaque rupture/erosion + thrombosis. Diagnosis: history (typical chest pain, radiation, autonomic features), ECG, troponin (high-sensitivity), exercise/stress testing, CT coronary angiography, invasive coronary angiography. Management: secondary prevention quartet (aspirin, statin, ACE-I, β-blocker), nitrates symptomatic, revascularisation (PCI vs CABG by anatomy, comorbidities, patient preference - SYNTAX score for complex), cardiac rehabilitation, lifestyle. Modern outcomes transformed since reperfusion era [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Stem: A 65-year-old with claudication and absent foot pulses. Decompose PAD.

Model answer: PAD: atherosclerotic narrowing of peripheral arteries (most lower limb); same RFs as CAD. Spectrum: asymptomatic (still ↑ MACE risk), claudication (reproducible exercise-induced calf/thigh/buttock pain relieved by rest within minutes), critical limb ischaemia (rest pain, ulcers, gangrene), acute limb ischaemia (sudden 6Ps - pain, pallor, paraesthesia, paralysis, pulselessness, perishing cold). _ABI_ (ankle-brachial index): < 0.9 diagnostic (normal 1.0-1.4); > 1.4 calcified non-compressible (DM, CKD); 0.5-0.9 claudication; < 0.5 critical; toe-brachial index for non-compressible. Investigation: ABI + post-exercise; duplex US, MRA/CTA, conventional angiography. Management: (1) RFs - smoking cessation #1 (most impactful); statin (target LDL < 1.8 mmol/L per ESC); BP control; glycaemic; antiplatelet (aspirin or clopidogrel; rivaroxaban 2.5 BD + aspirin per COMPASS trial reduces MACE in stable PAD); (2) Supervised exercise programme (improves walking distance > drugs for claudication); (3) Cilostazol (PDE3 inhibitor + adenosine reuptake) for claudication; (4) Revascularisation (endovascular angioplasty/stent or surgical bypass) for limiting claudication, CLI, or salvage in ALI; (5) Acute limb ischaemia - vascular surgery emergency (Rutherford classification guides management); (6) Wound care, podiatry, multidisciplinary in DM. PAD = systemic disease - mortality from CV events; aggressive global RF management is essential [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Stem: A 50-year-old with severe headache and BP 230/130. Decompose hypertensive emergency.

Model answer: _Hypertensive emergency_ = severe BP elevation (typically SBP > 180 or DBP > 120) WITH end-organ damage. Distinguish from hypertensive urgency (severe BP without acute end-organ damage). End-organ involvement: (1) Encephalopathy (headache, seizure, altered mental state - PRES on MRI); (2) Retinopathy (papilloedema, haemorrhages, exudates); (3) AKI (acute renal failure with proteinuria); (4) Acute pulmonary oedema (LVF); (5) ACS (MI from ↑ wall stress); (6) Aortic dissection; (7) Eclampsia (pregnancy). Causes: untreated/poorly-controlled essential HTN; secondary - renovascular disease, phaeochromocytoma, primary aldosteronism, cocaine/amphetamines, MAOI + tyramine, drug-related (PPI, NSAIDs in some, sympathomimetics); abrupt cessation of clonidine/β-blockers (rebound). Investigation: ECG, troponin, U+E, FBC, LFT, urinalysis, fundoscopy, CT brain if neurological, CT aortogram if dissection suspected, urine drug screen if relevant, urgent endocrine workup if young/refractory. Management: ICU/HDU; IV agents - labetalol (mixed α/β; 1st-line many), GTN (HF, ACS, dissection), nicardipine, nitroprusside (dissection - reduces afterload; cyanide toxicity in prolonged use), esmolol (dissection - β-block before vasodilator), hydralazine (eclampsia). Lower MAP by 20-25% in first hour, then to 160/100 over 2-6h, then normal over 24-48h (avoid too rapid - risk of organ hypoperfusion - especially watershed strokes in chronic hypertensives whose autoregulation curve has shifted right). Dissection - target SBP < 120 within 20 min. Eclampsia - magnesium + delivery [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Type: clinical

Stem: A 30-year-old with painful blue digits in cold. Decompose Raynaud phenomenon.

Model answer: Raynaud phenomenon: episodic vasospasm of digital arteries → triphasic colour change (white - ischaemia; blue - cyanosis; red - reactive hyperaemia) on cold exposure or stress. _Primary (Raynaud disease)_: idiopathic; bilateral symmetric; often F < 30 years; no underlying disease; no tissue damage typically. _Secondary_: associated with disease - systemic sclerosis (most common; severe; can cause digital ulcers, gangrene, pitting), SLE, dermatomyositis, MCTD, Sjögren syndrome (CTD); occupational - vibration tools (jackhammer); drug-induced (β-blockers, ergot, methysergide, sympathomimetics, chemotherapy - bleomycin, cisplatin, gemcitabine); haematological (cryoglobulinaemia, polycythaemia, paraproteinaemia); arterial disease (atherosclerosis, Buerger). Investigation: history (age, family, symmetry, severity, ulcers, drugs), examination (look for sclerodactyly, telangiectasia, digital ulcers, weak pulses), nailfold capillaroscopy (abnormal in CTD), ANA + scleroderma-specific antibodies (anti-Scl-70, anti-centromere), inflammatory markers, vascular studies if asymmetric. Management: (1) Lifestyle - cold avoidance (gloves, hand warmers), smoking cessation, avoid offending drugs and caffeine, stress reduction; (2) Pharmacological - dihydropyridine CCBs (nifedipine, amlodipine - first-line); nitrates (topical GTN); ARBs/ACE-I; sildenafil/PDE5 inhibitors; α1 blockers; SSRIs (fluoxetine); (3) Severe digital ischaemia - IV iloprost (prostacyclin analogue), bosentan (endothelin antagonist - prevents new ulcers in scleroderma); sympathectomy in selected; treat underlying CTD; debridement if gangrene. Prognosis depends on cause; primary often improves with age; secondary related to underlying disease [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: A 70-year-old with intermittent visual loss and jaw claudication. Decompose GCA.

Model answer: _Giant cell arteritis (GCA, temporal arteritis)_: large + medium-vessel vasculitis; granulomatous; > 50 years; F > M; associated polymyalgia rheumatica (~ 50%). Vessels affected: extracranial branches of carotid (especially temporal artery, ophthalmic - vision), aorta + branches (aortitis, subclavian, brachial), vertebral; can cause aneurysms long-term. Clinical: (1) New headache (often temporal, persistent); (2) Scalp tenderness; (3) Jaw claudication (pathognomonic); (4) Visual symptoms (transient monocular vision loss - amaurosis fugax; _permanent vision loss in 15-25% if untreated_ - emergency); (5) Polymyalgia rheumatica (proximal aching shoulders/hips, morning stiffness > 45 min); (6) Constitutional (fever, weight loss, fatigue); (7) Tongue claudication; (8) Aortic involvement - chest/back pain, large-vessel signs. Investigation: raised ESR (often > 50, can be > 100) + CRP; FBC (anaemia of chronic disease); platelet count often raised; LFT; _temporal artery biopsy_ (gold standard - within 1-2 weeks of starting steroids; granulomatous arteritis with multinucleate giant cells, fragmented internal elastic lamina; skip lesions - need adequate length 2-3 cm; sensitivity 70-80%); ultrasound temporal/axillary arteries (halo sign); MRA/CTA/PET for large vessels. Management - _medical emergency for vision_: (1) Start high-dose steroids immediately on clinical suspicion (do NOT wait for biopsy); 40-60 mg prednisolone; if visual symptoms - IV methylprednisolone 1g/day x 3 days then oral; (2) Aspirin 75 mg daily reduces visual loss/stroke risk; (3) PPI + bisphosphonate + Ca/vitD prophylaxis; (4) _Tocilizumab_ (anti-IL-6) - approved for GCA; reduces relapse and steroid burden; (5) Methotrexate as steroid-sparing in selected; (6) Long taper over 1-2 years with monitoring for relapse; (7) Aortic surveillance with imaging. Modern care has substantially reduced vision loss; tocilizumab transformed maintenance [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].

Stem: A 40-year-old with sudden severe headache and stroke. Decompose intracranial vasculitis.

QUESTION 13
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Discuss the cardiovascular response to exercise.

Model answer: _Cardiovascular response to exercise_: dynamic (running, cycling) vs static (weight). _Dynamic exercise_: (1) Central command - feedforward signal from motor cortex to brainstem CV centres → anticipatory rise in HR before exercise; (2) Exercise pressor reflex - mechanoreceptors (group III) and metaboreceptors (group IV) in working muscle → reinforce sympathetic activation; (3) Baroreflex resets to higher set-point during exercise. Effects: ↑ HR (vagal withdrawal first, then sympathetic activation - HR can reach 200/min in young); ↑ contractility (β1 inotropic); ↑ SV via Frank-Starling + contractility; ↑ CO from 5 L/min to 20-25 L/min in trained athletes; ↑ MAP modestly (SBP up, DBP stable or down, PP widens); SVR ↓ (vasodilation in working muscle from local metabolites override sympathetic, while non-working tissue vasoconstricts to redistribute flow - splanchnic, renal); ↑ skin flow eventually for thermoregulation (cholinergic sympathetic + reflex); ↑ extraction of O2 by working muscle (a-vO2 difference widens); ↑ ventilation matched to CO2 production. _Static exercise_: ↑ MAP markedly (pressor response from muscle compression + sympathetic); modest CO change; less time-limited by O2 delivery. Limits: VO2max - cardiac output × maximal a-vO2 difference; trainable; aerobic capacity. Heart rate recovery after exercise (vagal reactivation) is prognostic - slow recovery → ↑ mortality. Cardiac rehabilitation exploits these principles for IHD, HF, post-bypass; mortality and QoL benefits [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

QUESTION 14
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Synthesise vascular function: how does the system serve tissue perfusion?

Model answer: Vascular function serves tissue perfusion through coordinated mechanisms: (1) _Pressure source_ - heart provides pumping pressure; (2) _Conduit_ - large arteries low resistance, elastic for pressure storage during systole and continuous flow in diastole (Windkessel); pulse pressure widens with age/atherosclerosis (stiffness); (3) _Resistance vessels_ - arterioles set systemic vascular resistance (Poiseuille's law - r⁴ sensitivity); main site of regulation; sympathetic + local + humoral inputs; (4) _Exchange_ - capillaries thin-walled for diffusion (gases, nutrients) + filtration (Starling forces); huge total cross-sectional area minimises velocity for exchange; (5) _Collection_ - venules + veins low pressure capacitance reservoir holding 70% of blood volume; venoconstriction mobilises; valves + skeletal muscle pump aid return; (6) _Return_ - thoracic pump, atrial filling. Regulation across multiple time scales: seconds (baroreflex, autoregulation), minutes (RAAS, ADH, sympathetic), hours-days (kidney pressure-natriuresis), weeks-months (structural remodelling). Auto-regulation of organ flow (cerebral 60-150, coronary 60-130, renal 80-180 mm Hg MAP) allows constant flow despite BP variation. Disease patterns: HTN (↑ SVR + remodelling), atherosclerosis (focal stenosis), HF (poor pump), shock (4 categories), oedema (Starling imbalance), vasculitis (vessel wall inflammation), thrombosis/embolism (occlusion), aneurysm/dissection (wall failure). Therapy targets each: vasodilators, diuretics, lipid-lowering, antiplatelet/anticoagulant, immunosuppressants for vasculitis, surgery/endovascular procedures. Modern vascular medicine integrates all - precision matching of intervention to mechanism [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20].
