## Pass 1 — Blood composition: volume, plasma proteins, cells, electrolytes

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The total blood volume in a 70 kg adult is approximately:

Correct answer: ~5 L (about 7–8% of body weight) — comprising ~3 L plasma (~55%) and ~2 L cellular elements (~45%, mostly erythrocytes); women are slightly lower than men; volume is regulated by RAAS, ADH, and atrial natriuretic peptide; loss of >40% causes class IV haemorrhagic shock.

Distractors:

- "1 L." — Reveals misconception: student grossly underestimates blood volume. Correction: total blood volume is ~5 L in a 70 kg adult; 1 L would be incompatible with normal circulation.
- "10 L." — Reveals misconception: student doubles the actual volume. Correction: 10 L exceeds total blood volume; the figure is ~5 L (about 7–8% of body weight).
- "15 L." — Reveals misconception: student grossly overestimates. Correction: 15 L corresponds to total body water in an average adult, not blood volume; blood volume is ~5 L.

Explanation: Total blood volume in an average adult is ~5 L (women slightly less than men), comprising ~55% plasma (3 L) and ~45% cells (2 L — mostly erythrocytes) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Guyton & Hall ch.33]. The cellular fraction expressed as haematocrit is ~45% in men and ~40% in women. Blood volume is regulated by the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system, antidiuretic hormone, and atrial natriuretic peptide. Acute loss of blood is classified by ATLS into four classes: I (<15%, no signs), II (15–30%, tachycardia), III (30–40%, hypotension and tachycardia), and IV (>40%, life-threatening shock). Chronic blood loss is better tolerated as plasma volume re-expands but RBC mass falls (anaemia).

Hints:

1. Total blood is roughly 7–8% of body weight.
2. Plasma is the larger fraction; cells are the smaller.
3. Haematocrit is the cellular fraction expressed as a percentage.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The major plasma protein, contributing ~75–80% of plasma colloid osmotic pressure, is:

Correct answer: Albumin (66 kDa) — synthesised by the liver, plasma concentration ~3.5–5.0 g/dL, half-life ~20 days; major contributor to plasma oncotic pressure (~25 mm Hg) due to its abundance and strongly negative charge (Donnan effect amplifies effective oncotic pressure); also a carrier for drugs, bilirubin, calcium, fatty acids, and hormones.

Distractors:

- "Globulin." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong major protein. Correction: globulins comprise α1, α2, β, and γ classes (immunoglobulins) and contribute less to oncotic pressure than albumin, which alone accounts for 75–80%.
- "Fibrinogen." — Reveals misconception: student picks a clotting protein. Correction: fibrinogen is a large rod-shaped protein important for clotting and viscosity (and ESR), but contributes little to oncotic pressure compared with albumin.
- "Clotting factors." — Reveals misconception: student picks coagulation proteins. Correction: clotting factors are present in plasma but in smaller concentrations and contribute little to oncotic pressure.

Explanation: Albumin is the most abundant plasma protein, with multiple functions [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Berne & Levy]. Oncotic pressure: maintains intravascular volume; opposes Starling forces driving fluid out of capillaries. Carrier: transports drugs (warfarin, phenytoin), bilirubin, calcium, free fatty acids, and hormones (thyroid, cortisol). Buffering: minor pH buffer. Antioxidant: binds free radicals. Hypoalbuminaemia (<3.5 g/dL) causes oedema (reduced oncotic pressure → Starling fluid shift to interstitium) and impaired drug binding (increased free fraction → toxicity, e.g., warfarin, phenytoin); causes include malnutrition, liver disease (reduced synthesis), nephrotic syndrome (urinary loss), severe burns, and inflammation (negative acute-phase reactant).

Hints:

1. Albumin is by far the most abundant plasma protein.
2. Its negative charge amplifies oncotic effect via Donnan.
3. Loss in urine (nephrotic syndrome) or reduced synthesis (cirrhosis) produces oedema.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Haematocrit measures the percentage of blood volume occupied by:

Correct answer: Erythrocytes (red blood cells) — normal ~45% in men and ~40% in women, raised in polycythaemia, dehydration, and chronic hypoxia (high altitude, COPD, smoking, sleep apnoea), and lowered in anaemia.

Distractors:

- "Plasma." — Reveals misconception: student inverts haematocrit. Correction: haematocrit is the _cellular_ fraction (mainly RBCs); plasma is the remainder (~55%).
- "Leukocytes." — Reveals misconception: student picks white cells. Correction: leukocytes contribute negligibly to haematocrit by volume; haematocrit is dominated by RBCs.
- "Platelets." — Reveals misconception: student picks platelets. Correction: platelets contribute negligibly to haematocrit by volume; the cellular fraction is dominated by RBCs.

Explanation: Haematocrit (Hct) = packed cell volume (PCV). The cellular fraction is dominated by RBCs (>99% by volume); WBCs and platelets contribute negligibly to Hct [Guyton & Hall ch.33; Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Reference ranges: men 41–50%, women 36–44%. High Hct (>50%) is either relative (haemoconcentration from dehydration, vomiting, burns, hypovolaemia) or absolute polycythaemia (primary — polycythaemia vera, JAK2 mutation; secondary — chronic hypoxia from high altitude, COPD, smoking, sleep apnoea, congenital cyanotic heart disease, EPO-secreting tumours). Low Hct (anaemia) results from bleeding (acute or chronic), iron/B12/folate deficiency, bone marrow suppression, haemolysis, chronic disease, or pregnancy (relative — plasma volume expands more than RBC mass).

Hints:

1. The cellular fraction is dominated by red cells.
2. White cells and platelets contribute almost nothing by volume.
3. High Hct may be true (polycythaemia) or relative (dehydration).

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The three major cellular components of blood are:

Correct answer: Erythrocytes (RBCs, ~5 × 10¹²/L), leukocytes (WBCs, ~4–10 × 10⁹/L — neutrophils 50–70%, lymphocytes 20–40%, monocytes 2–10%, eosinophils 1–4%, basophils <1%), and platelets (thrombocytes, ~150–400 × 10⁹/L) — all derived from haematopoietic stem cells in bone marrow under cytokine control (EPO, G-CSF, TPO).

Distractors:

- "Erythrocytes only." — Reveals misconception: student narrows blood cells to RBCs. Correction: blood contains three cellular elements — RBCs, WBCs, and platelets — each with distinct functions.
- "RBCs and WBCs only." — Reveals misconception: student omits platelets. Correction: platelets are the third major cellular element (thrombocytes), critical for primary haemostasis.
- "Platelets only." — Reveals misconception: student narrows blood cells to platelets. Correction: blood contains three cellular elements; platelets alone cannot perform the gas-transport, immune, and haemostatic functions of blood.

Explanation: Three cellular elements [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Erythrocytes (RBCs, 4.5–5.5 × 10¹²/L): anucleate biconcave discs (~7 µm), lifespan ~120 days, carry haemoglobin for O₂ transport. Leukocytes (WBCs, 4–10 × 10⁹/L): neutrophils 50–70% (acute bacterial infection), lymphocytes 20–40% (T, B, NK cells; viral infection, adaptive immunity), monocytes 2–10% (tissue macrophages; chronic infection), eosinophils 1–4% (parasites, allergy), basophils <1% (allergy, mast-cell-related). Platelets (150–400 × 10⁹/L): anucleate cell fragments from megakaryocytes, lifespan ~7–10 days, primary haemostasis. All three lineages derive from haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) in bone marrow with progressive lineage commitment via cytokines (EPO for RBCs, G-CSF for granulocytes, TPO for platelets, M-CSF for monocytes, IL-3 for multilineage, IL-7 for lymphocytes).

Hints:

1. Three classes: red, white, and platelet.
2. White cells subdivide into five named lineages.
3. All derive from a common haematopoietic stem cell in bone marrow.

QUESTION 5
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Plasma is distinguished from serum by the fact that:

Correct answer: Plasma contains all clotting factors (including fibrinogen) and is collected in an anticoagulant tube (EDTA, citrate, heparin); serum is plasma after coagulation has occurred (collected in a tube without anticoagulant) and so lacks fibrinogen and consumed clotting factors.

Distractors:

- "Identical." — Reveals misconception: student denies any difference. Correction: plasma contains fibrinogen and clotting factors; serum lacks them — the distinction matters for laboratory measurement of coagulation parameters.
- "Serum has cells, plasma doesn't." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the cellular content. Correction: both serum and plasma are cell-free; the distinction is in clotting factor content, not cellularity.
- "Plasma is from arteries only." — Reveals misconception: student introduces a vascular distinction. Correction: plasma is the liquid portion of any blood sample (arterial or venous) collected with anticoagulant; serum is the post-coagulation supernatant.

Explanation: Plasma is the liquid component of blood (cells removed) and contains all proteins including fibrinogen and clotting factors II, V, VII, IX, X, XI, XII, and XIII [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Serum is the liquid component after coagulation — the same composition as plasma minus fibrinogen and consumed clotting factors. Most laboratory tests (electrolytes, glucose, lipids, hormones) give similar results in plasma or serum. Collection tubes by colour: red top — no additive, allows clotting → serum; lavender (purple) — EDTA anticoagulant for CBC and blood typing; light blue — citrate for coagulation testing (PT/INR, aPTT — citrate ratio matters); green — heparin for plasma chemistry; grey — sodium fluoride for glucose preservation. Selecting the right tube is essential for valid results.

Hints:

1. The difference is whether clotting has occurred before separation.
2. Plasma keeps fibrinogen; serum loses it.
3. Coagulation tests must use citrate-anticoagulated plasma, not serum.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The plasma proteins responsible for blood viscosity contribution beyond albumin include:

Correct answer: Globulins (α1, α2, β, γ — including immunoglobulins, transport proteins, complement, acute phase reactants) and fibrinogen (a large rod-shaped protein contributing disproportionately to viscosity and to the erythrocyte sedimentation rate via RBC stacking).

Distractors:

- "Albumin alone." — Reveals misconception: student narrows viscosity to albumin. Correction: while albumin is the most abundant plasma protein, globulins and fibrinogen contribute disproportionately to viscosity given their molecular size and shape.
- "Glucose." — Reveals misconception: student picks a small molecule. Correction: glucose contributes negligibly to plasma viscosity; it contributes to osmolality but not to viscosity.
- "Lipids." — Reveals misconception: student picks lipoproteins. Correction: while gross hyperlipidaemia raises plasma viscosity modestly, the dominant protein contributors are globulins and fibrinogen.

Explanation: Plasma proteins are separated electrophoretically into albumin (~50–60% of total), α1-globulins (α1-antitrypsin, α-fetoprotein, transcortin), α2-globulins (haptoglobin, ceruloplasmin, α2-macroglobulin), β-globulins (transferrin, complement C3 and C4, β-microglobulin), γ-globulins (immunoglobulins IgG, IgM, IgA, IgD, IgE), and fibrinogen (migrates between β and γ regions) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Acute phase response: hepatic synthesis of CRP, fibrinogen, haptoglobin, α1-antitrypsin, and ceruloplasmin rises during inflammation; ESR reflects fibrinogen-driven RBC stacking. Albumin and transferrin are _negative_ acute-phase reactants (decrease). Multiple myeloma produces monoclonal gammopathy from plasma cell malignancy; serum protein electrophoresis shows a narrow "M-spike" in the γ region with hyperviscosity syndrome from elevated immunoglobulins.

Hints:

1. Globulins and fibrinogen are larger and more shape-asymmetric than albumin.
2. Fibrinogen drives both viscosity and the ESR.
3. Hyperviscosity syndromes occur in monoclonal gammopathies and polycythaemia.

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Plasma viscosity relative to water is approximately:

Correct answer: 1.6–1.8 × water (water = 1.0); whole blood is 3–4 × water at normal Hct; haemoglobin and Hct are the major determinants of whole-blood viscosity, with the Fahraeus–Lindqvist effect reducing apparent viscosity in small vessels at high shear rates.

Distractors:

- "Equal to water." — Reveals misconception: student denies plasma viscosity. Correction: plasma proteins make plasma 1.6–1.8 times more viscous than water.
- "0.5×." — Reveals misconception: student assumes plasma is less viscous than water. Correction: plasma is _more_ viscous than water due to its protein content.
- "10×." — Reveals misconception: student grossly overestimates plasma viscosity. Correction: plasma is 1.6–1.8 × water; whole blood is 3–4 × water; 10× would not flow through capillaries normally.

Explanation: Plasma viscosity is ~1.6–1.8 × water; whole blood viscosity is ~3–4 × water at normal Hct [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Viscosity rises non-linearly with Hct. The Fahraeus–Lindqvist effect describes reduced apparent blood viscosity at high shear rates and in small-diameter vessels, important for capillary blood flow. Hyperviscosity consequences: polycythaemia (Hct >55%) raises thrombosis and stroke risk — treated with phlebotomy; multiple myeloma and Waldenström macroglobulinaemia raise globulins → hyperviscosity syndrome (headache, visual disturbance, confusion, mucosal bleeding) — treated with plasmapheresis; sickle cell disease produces sickled RBCs that increase microvascular viscosity → ischaemic crises — treated with exchange transfusion. The relationship between viscosity, vessel diameter, and shear rate is captured in the modified Poiseuille equation.

Hints:

1. Plasma alone is ~1.6–1.8 × water.
2. Whole blood is ~3–4 × water at normal Hct.
3. Viscosity rises non-linearly with Hct above ~55%.

QUESTION 8
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Iron is transported in plasma bound to:

Correct answer: Transferrin — a β-globulin that binds 2 Fe³⁺ ions, with normal saturation ~30%; total iron-binding capacity (TIBC) reflects transferrin concentration; cellular storage is in ferritin; release from macrophages and gut absorption are regulated by hepcidin (raised in inflammation).

Distractors:

- "Albumin." — Reveals misconception: student picks the carrier of many other ligands. Correction: although albumin carries many ligands (drugs, bilirubin, calcium, fatty acids), iron is specifically bound by transferrin.
- "Haemoglobin in plasma." — Reveals misconception: student picks an intracellular iron protein. Correction: free haemoglobin in plasma (only seen in haemolysis) is bound by haptoglobin; physiological iron transport uses transferrin.
- "Ferritin." — Reveals misconception: student picks the storage protein. Correction: ferritin is the _cellular storage_ form of iron (mainly liver, marrow); transferrin transports iron in plasma.

Explanation: Transferrin transports plasma iron, carrying Fe³⁺ to bone marrow and other tissues for utilisation [Boron & Boulpaep ch.45; Ganong]. Ferritin is the cellular iron storage form (especially in liver and marrow); plasma ferritin reflects body stores — low in iron deficiency, raised in inflammation (acute-phase reactant). Hepcidin is the liver-derived hormone regulating iron absorption from the gut and release from macrophages; raised in inflammation it traps iron in stores ("anaemia of chronic disease"). Functional iron is in haemoglobin within RBCs. Iron deficiency (the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide) is caused by blood loss (menstrual, GI), poor intake, or malabsorption (coeliac); lab findings include low Hb, microcytic hypochromic RBCs, low ferritin, high TIBC, and low transferrin saturation; treatment is oral iron (ferrous sulphate) or IV iron when intolerant or malabsorbing, plus addressing the underlying cause.

Hints:

1. Plasma iron is bound to a specific β-globulin transport protein.
2. Ferritin stores iron inside cells.
3. Hepcidin regulates iron absorption and release.

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Haemoglobin in healthy adult RBCs is composed of:

Correct answer: A tetramer of two α and two β globin chains (HbA, α₂β₂, ~97%), each binding one haem (iron-protoporphyrin IX with Fe²⁺); minor adult forms include HbA2 (α₂δ₂, ~2.5%) and HbF (α₂γ₂, <1%); α-globin genes are on chromosome 16 and β-globin genes on chromosome 11.

Distractors:

- "Single polypeptide chain." — Reveals misconception: student denies the tetrameric structure. Correction: haemoglobin is a tetramer (2 α + 2 β); allosteric cooperativity between subunits underlies the sigmoidal O₂ binding curve.
- "Single haem only." — Reveals misconception: student denies the four haem groups. Correction: each globin chain binds one haem, giving four haems per tetramer; this enables cooperative O₂ binding.
- "RNA-protein hybrid." — Reveals misconception: student picks a wrong macromolecular class. Correction: haemoglobin is a pure protein (globin) plus a non-protein prosthetic group (haem), not an RNA-protein hybrid.

Explanation: HbA (α₂β₂) is normal adult haemoglobin [Berne & Levy — Blood; Guyton & Hall ch.33]. Each haem contains Fe²⁺ which reversibly binds O₂; oxidation to Fe³⁺ produces methaemoglobin (cannot bind O₂). Haemoglobinopathies: sickle cell disease (β-globin Glu→Val at position 6 → HbS polymerises on deoxygenation → sickling, vaso-occlusive crises, haemolysis; sickle trait confers malaria resistance). Thalassaemia (reduced/absent globin chain synthesis; α-thal involves four genes, β-thal involves two; severity varies; microcytic anaemia). Methaemoglobinaemia (Fe³⁺ form; blue cyanosis; causes — oxidising drugs such as dapsone, lidocaine, and nitrites; congenital from cytochrome b5 reductase deficiency; treatment — methylene blue, vitamin C). Carboxyhaemoglobin (CO bound; tightly held; reduces O₂ carriage; treated with high-flow O₂ ± hyperbaric).

Hints:

1. Haemoglobin is a tetramer of four globin chains.
2. Adult haemoglobin (HbA) has 2 α and 2 β chains.
3. Each chain binds one haem with Fe²⁺.

QUESTION 10
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The principal extracellular cation and the principal extracellular anion are:

Correct answer: Na⁺ (~135–145 mEq/L) and Cl⁻ (~98–106 mEq/L), followed by HCO₃⁻ (~22–28 mEq/L); the principal _intracellular_ cation is K⁺ (~140 mEq/L), with phosphates and proteins as the dominant intracellular anions.

Distractors:

- "K⁺ and Cl⁻." — Reveals misconception: student picks the intracellular cation. Correction: K⁺ is principally _intracellular_; the dominant _extracellular_ cation is Na⁺.
- "Mg²⁺ and SO₄²⁻." — Reveals misconception: student picks minor ions. Correction: Mg²⁺ and SO₄²⁻ are present in low concentrations; the dominant extracellular ions are Na⁺ and Cl⁻.
- "Ca²⁺ and HCO₃⁻." — Reveals misconception: student picks Ca²⁺ as the dominant cation. Correction: Ca²⁺ is present at much lower concentrations than Na⁺ (~2.2–2.6 mmol/L total); HCO₃⁻ is the second-most abundant anion after Cl⁻.

Explanation: Plasma electrolytes (mEq/L): Na⁺ ~135–145, K⁺ ~3.5–5.0, Cl⁻ ~98–106, HCO₃⁻ ~22–28, total Ca²⁺ ~2.2–2.6 mmol/L (free ionised ~1.2), Mg²⁺ ~0.7–1.0 mmol/L, PO₄³⁻ ~0.8–1.5 mmol/L [Costanzo ch.6]. Anion gap = [Na⁺] − ([Cl⁻] + [HCO₃⁻]) ≈ 8–12 mEq/L, representing unmeasured anions (mostly albumin); raised in metabolic acidosis with unmeasured anions (lactate, ketones, salicylate, methanol, ethylene glycol — the MUDPILES and GOLDMARK mnemonics). Plasma osmolality = 2 × [Na⁺] + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8 ≈ 285–295 mOsm/kg; increased in hypernatraemia, hyperglycaemia, uraemia, mannitol, ethanol; decreased in hyponatraemia. The Donnan equilibrium across cell membranes maintains the ionic asymmetry that drives transmembrane potentials.

Hints:

1. Sodium is the dominant extracellular cation.
2. Chloride is the dominant extracellular anion.
3. Potassium dominates inside the cell, opposite to sodium outside.

## Pass 2 — Plasma osmolality, oncotic pressure, haematopoiesis, blood groups, acid–base

QUESTION 11
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Normal plasma osmolality is approximately:

Correct answer: 285–295 mOsm/kg, calculated as 2[Na⁺] + glucose/18 + BUN/2.8; tightly regulated by hypothalamic osmoreceptors (OVLT, subfornical organ — circumventricular), antidiuretic hormone release at osmolality >290, and thirst at osmolality >295.

Distractors:

- "100 mOsm/kg." — Reveals misconception: student grossly underestimates. Correction: 100 mOsm/kg would be severely hypotonic; normal is 285–295.
- "400 mOsm/kg." — Reveals misconception: student picks a hyperosmolar state. Correction: 400 mOsm/kg corresponds to severe hypertonic states (HHS, mannitol overdose), not normal.
- "700 mOsm/kg." — Reveals misconception: student picks a urinary maximum. Correction: maximally concentrated urine reaches ~1200 mOsm/kg; _plasma_ osmolality is tightly maintained at 285–295.

Explanation: Plasma osmolality is tightly maintained at 285–295 mOsm/kg [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Costanzo ch.6]. Set point regulated by hypothalamic osmoreceptors (OVLT, subfornical organ — circumventricular), with ADH release from the posterior pituitary when osmolality rises >290 and thirst activated when osmolality rises >295. Hypotonic states (low osmolality): dilutional hyponatraemia (excess water — SIADH, primary polydipsia, congestive heart failure, cirrhosis); cerebral oedema risk if rapid correction. Hypertonic states (high osmolality): hypernatraemia (water deficit — diabetes insipidus, dehydration); hyperglycaemia (DKA, HHS); uraemia (renal failure); toxic alcohols (methanol, ethylene glycol — also raise the _osmolar gap_ = measured − calculated osmolality, used clinically). Treatment principles: correct slowly (Na⁺ change <8–12 mEq/L per 24 h) — too fast risks central pontine myelinolysis (rapid hyponatraemia correction) or cerebral oedema (rapid hypernatraemia correction).

Hints:

1. The narrow normal range is 285–295 mOsm/kg.
2. Sodium dominates the calculated estimate.
3. ADH and thirst are the principal osmotic regulators.

QUESTION 12
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Normal plasma colloid osmotic (oncotic) pressure is approximately:

Correct answer: ~25 mm Hg, generated mainly by albumin; opposes hydrostatic pressure across capillaries in the Starling equation; reduction (as in hypoalbuminaemia from nephrotic syndrome, cirrhosis, or malnutrition) tilts net flux toward filtration → oedema and ascites.

Distractors:

- "5 mm Hg." — Reveals misconception: student grossly underestimates. Correction: oncotic pressure is ~25 mm Hg; 5 would be insufficient to oppose capillary hydrostatic pressure.
- "100 mm Hg." — Reveals misconception: student picks an arterial pressure value. Correction: 100 mm Hg is roughly mean arterial pressure; oncotic pressure is much lower at ~25 mm Hg.
- "200 mm Hg." — Reveals misconception: student picks an unphysiological value. Correction: 200 mm Hg has no normal physiological correlate; oncotic pressure is ~25 mm Hg.

Explanation: Plasma colloid osmotic (oncotic) pressure ~25 mm Hg is generated mainly by albumin [Boron & Boulpaep ch.20]. Starling equation: net filtration = K_f × [(P_c − P_i) − σ(π_c − π_i)], where P_c is capillary hydrostatic, P_i is interstitial hydrostatic, π_c is plasma oncotic, π_i is interstitial oncotic, and σ is the reflection coefficient. Normal capillary hydrostatic pressure is ~32 mm Hg at the arteriolar end and ~15 at the venular end, with oncotic pressure ~25 throughout — net filtration at the arteriolar end and reabsorption at the venular end, with lymphatics returning remaining filtered fluid. Hypoalbuminaemia → reduced π_c → net filtration favoured → oedema: generalised oedema (anasarca) in severe nephrotic syndrome (urinary albumin loss), cirrhosis, and malnutrition; ascites in cirrhosis (compounded by portal hypertension); pulmonary oedema risk if cardiac compromise.

Hints:

1. The pressure is generated by plasma proteins, mostly albumin.
2. The opposing force is hydrostatic.
3. Loss of oncotic pressure produces oedema.

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Acute phase reactants — proteins increased during inflammation — include:

Correct answer: C-reactive protein (CRP), fibrinogen, haptoglobin, α1-antitrypsin, ceruloplasmin, ferritin, and serum amyloid A (all positive); albumin, transferrin, and transthyretin (prealbumin) are _negative_ acute phase reactants and decrease.

Distractors:

- "Albumin." — Reveals misconception: student picks a negative acute phase reactant. Correction: albumin is a _negative_ acute-phase reactant — it falls during inflammation; the positive reactants are CRP, fibrinogen, haptoglobin, α1-antitrypsin, ceruloplasmin, and ferritin.
- "Glucose." — Reveals misconception: student picks a metabolic substrate. Correction: glucose is not a plasma protein and is not classified as an acute phase reactant; the term refers to hepatic protein synthesis changes during inflammation.
- "Lipids." — Reveals misconception: student picks lipid molecules. Correction: lipids are not acute phase reactants; the term refers to specific hepatic plasma proteins.

Explanation: The acute phase response is the hepatic protein synthesis pattern change during inflammation, driven by IL-6, IL-1, and TNF-α [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Positive acute phase reactants include CRP (most sensitive; rises within hours; cardiovascular risk marker), fibrinogen (increases ESR; thrombosis risk factor), haptoglobin (binds free Hb; anti-haemolytic), α1-antitrypsin (anti-protease), ceruloplasmin (copper transport; antioxidant), ferritin (iron storage; raised in inflammation can confound iron-deficiency assessment), and serum amyloid A (chronic inflammation, amyloidosis). Negative acute phase reactants (decrease) include albumin, transferrin, and transthyretin/prealbumin — reflecting hepatic redirection of synthesis priorities. Clinical use: ESR (reflects fibrinogen and immunoglobulin-driven RBC stacking; non-specific), CRP (more sensitive and faster), procalcitonin (bacterial-specific; helpful for distinguishing bacterial infection from inflammation).

Hints:

1. The list of "positives" rises during inflammation.
2. Albumin and transferrin go in the _opposite_ direction.
3. CRP and ESR are the most commonly used clinical markers.

QUESTION 14
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The site of haematopoiesis (blood cell production) in adults is:

Correct answer: Bone marrow — mainly the axial skeleton (sternum, ribs, vertebrae, pelvis) and proximal long bones — derived from haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) that produce common myeloid (CMP) and common lymphoid (CLP) progenitors under cytokine control (EPO, TPO, G-CSF, M-CSF, GM-CSF, IL-3, IL-7).

Correct answer: 7.35–7.45, tightly regulated by buffer systems (bicarbonate the principal extracellular buffer, plus phosphate, protein/haemoglobin), respiratory CO₂ excretion (rapid), and renal HCO₃⁻ regeneration and H⁺ excretion (slow); deviations are described by Henderson–Hasselbalch and classified as respiratory or metabolic acidosis/alkalosis.

Distractors:

- "7.0." — Reveals misconception: student picks an acidotic value as normal. Correction: pH 7.0 is severe acidosis; normal arterial pH is 7.35–7.45.
- "6.8." — Reveals misconception: student picks a value incompatible with life. Correction: pH 6.8 is the lower extreme of survivable acidosis; normal is 7.35–7.45.
- "7.6." — Reveals misconception: student picks an alkalotic value as normal. Correction: pH 7.6 is severe alkalosis; normal is 7.35–7.45.

Explanation: Acid–base buffers: bicarbonate is the most important (HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ → H₂CO₃ → CO₂ + H₂O; lungs control CO₂; kidneys control HCO₃⁻); phosphate (H₂PO₄⁻ ↔ HPO₄²⁻; intracellular and urinary buffer); protein (mostly albumin and haemoglobin) [Costanzo ch.7]. Hints:

1. Normal arterial pH falls in a narrow range around 7.4.
2. Lungs handle CO₂; kidneys handle HCO₃⁻.
3. Henderson–Hasselbalch links pH, HCO₃⁻, and PaCO₂.

Stem: Acute haemolytic transfusion reaction from ABO incompatibility presents with:

Correct answer: Fever, chills, back/loin pain, haemoglobinuria, hypotension, and IV-site burning during transfusion — caused by recipient antibodies (anti-A and/or anti-B) attacking transfused RBCs → intravascular haemolysis, AKI, and DIC; management is to stop the transfusion immediately, supportive care (IV fluids, vasopressors), and treat DIC; almost always due to clerical error.

Distractors:

- "Mild urticaria with no other symptoms." — Reveals misconception: student picks an allergic reaction. Correction: mild urticaria suggests an _allergic_ transfusion reaction; ABO-incompatible haemolytic reactions are systemic and dangerous.
- "Delayed jaundice 2 weeks later." — Reveals misconception: student picks a delayed haemolytic reaction. Correction: delayed haemolytic reactions (e.g., Kidd antibodies) present 1–2 weeks later with mild jaundice and falling Hb; ABO-mismatch is _acute_ and severe.
- "Pulmonary oedema with hypertension." — Reveals misconception: student picks TACO. Correction: transfusion-associated circulatory overload presents with pulmonary oedema and hypertension; ABO-incompatible haemolytic reactions present with fever, back pain, haemoglobinuria, and hypotension.

Explanation: Transfusion reactions span a spectrum [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18]. Acute haemolytic (ABO-incompatibility) — severe IV-line burn, fever, chills, back/loin pain, haemoglobinuria, hypotension, and DIC; due to recipient anti-A and/or anti-B reacting with donor RBCs → complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis, AKI, and shock; almost always a clerical error (wrong patient, wrong unit); stop transfusion immediately, supportive care, treat DIC. Febrile non-haemolytic — most common; cytokine-mediated; pre-medicate with paracetamol; usually mild. Allergic — urticaria → severe anaphylaxis (especially in IgA-deficient patient with anti-IgA); manage with antihistamines for mild and adrenaline for anaphylaxis. TRALI (transfusion-related acute lung injury) — donor antibodies → pulmonary oedema within 6 hours; major cause of transfusion-related mortality; supportive care. TACO (transfusion-associated circulatory overload) — volume overload, especially in elderly or cardiac compromise; manage with diuretics, oxygen, and slowing future transfusions. Bacterial contamination — especially platelets (room temperature storage); fever, sepsis. Delayed haemolytic — anamnestic response to non-ABO antigens (Kidd, Duffy, Kell) 1–2 weeks later; mild jaundice and falling Hb. Iron overload (chronic transfusion). TA-GVHD (rare but fatal — irradiate blood for at-risk patients).

Hints:

1. The mechanism is recipient antibodies reacting with transfused RBC antigens.
2. Symptoms appear acutely during transfusion.
3. The cause is almost always a clerical error.

Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Anaemia of chronic disease (ACD) differs from iron deficiency anaemia in that it shows:

Correct answer: Normocytic (sometimes mildly microcytic) anaemia with raised hepcidin (driven by inflammation) blocking iron release from macrophages and reducing gut absorption — producing low transferrin saturation but _high or normal_ ferritin (in contrast to IDA's _low_ ferritin); treatment is to address the underlying inflammation; iron supplementation alone is usually ineffective.

Distractors:

- "Identical lab findings to iron deficiency." — Reveals misconception: student denies the distinguishing biomarkers. Correction: ferritin separates ACD (high/normal — acute-phase reactant) from IDA (low); the rest of the iron studies overlap.
- "Macrocytic megaloblastic anaemia." — Reveals misconception: student picks the B12/folate picture. Correction: ACD is normocytic (sometimes microcytic), not macrocytic megaloblastic.
- "Reversed by oral iron alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks the IDA-style treatment. Correction: hepcidin-mediated trapping of iron in stores prevents oral iron from entering the marrow effectively; addressing the underlying inflammation is the key intervention.

Explanation: Anaemia of chronic disease (ACD, also "anaemia of inflammation") is the second most common anaemia after IDA [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Pathophysiology: chronic inflammation → IL-6 → liver synthesis of hepcidin → degradation of ferroportin on enterocytes and macrophages → reduced iron absorption from gut and reduced release from macrophages → functional iron deficiency despite adequate stores; chronic inflammation also blunts EPO response and shortens RBC survival. Causes: chronic infection (TB, HIV, osteomyelitis, endocarditis), autoimmune disease (rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, IBD), malignancy, and chronic kidney disease (also has reduced EPO production). Lab findings: mild-to-moderate anaemia, normocytic (occasionally microcytic), low or normal MCV, low transferrin saturation (similar to IDA), but _high or normal_ ferritin (acute-phase reactant — distinguishes from IDA's low ferritin), low TIBC (vs high in IDA), high hepcidin. Treatment: address the underlying inflammation; iron supplementation alone is usually ineffective due to hepcidin block; in CKD, ESAs (epoetin, darbepoetin) ± IV iron; treating the underlying disease often resolves the anaemia.

Hints:

1. The driver is hepcidin from chronic inflammation.
2. Ferritin (an acute-phase reactant) is high or normal in ACD but low in IDA.
3. Treatment focuses on the underlying inflammatory disease.

Stem: Pregnancy haematology is best characterised by:

Correct answer: Plasma volume expansion ~50% with RBC mass expansion ~25% → relative haemodilution (Hb falls modestly to a lower limit ~110 g/L); markedly increased iron requirement (mother + fetus + placenta) — supplementation often needed; physiological hypercoagulable state — increased VTE risk requiring prophylactic LMWH in selected patients.

Distractors:

- "Plasma volume contracts and Hb rises." — Reveals misconception: student inverts pregnancy fluid balance. Correction: pregnancy _expands_ plasma volume substantially; Hb falls slightly from haemodilution.
- "Iron requirements fall during pregnancy." — Reveals misconception: student inverts iron demand. Correction: iron demand _rises_ dramatically — supplementation is often needed.
- "Hypocoagulable state with bleeding tendency." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the coagulation balance. Correction: pregnancy is a _hypercoagulable_ state with raised VTE risk; prophylactic LMWH is given in selected patients.

Explanation: Pregnancy haematology features a planned remodelling of blood volume and composition [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Plasma volume expands ~50% (~1500 mL increase by term) with RBC mass expanding ~25%, producing relative haemodilution and a modest fall in Hb to a lower limit of ~110 g/L (WHO-defined anaemia in pregnancy). Iron requirements rise dramatically (~1000 mg total: ~500 for maternal RBC mass, ~300 for fetus and placenta, ~200 for maternal blood loss at delivery); supplementation is often needed, with oral iron (or IV iron in intolerant patients). Folate requirements rise; supplementation (400 µg/day pre-conception and during the first trimester) reduces neural tube defect risk. The pregnant state is hypercoagulable: increased fibrinogen and factors VII, VIII, IX, X; reduced protein S; increased PAI-1 and PAI-2; reduced fibrinolysis; venous stasis from gravid uterus compression — together producing a 4–5-fold increase in VTE risk during pregnancy and ~20-fold in the puerperium. Prophylactic LMWH is given in selected patients with risk factors (previous VTE, thrombophilia, prolonged immobilisation, post-Caesarean). Postpartum haemorrhage and pre-eclampsia/HELLP syndrome remain major causes of maternal morbidity and mortality.

Hints:

1. Plasma expands more than RBC mass — so Hb falls relatively.
2. Iron and folate requirements rise; supplementation is routine.
3. Pregnancy is hypercoagulable — VTE risk is increased.

Stem: Sickle cell disease is best characterised by:

Correct answer: An autosomal recessive haemoglobinopathy with HbS (β-globin Glu→Val at position 6) that polymerises on deoxygenation → sickled RBCs; clinically vaso-occlusive crises (severe pain, acute chest syndrome, stroke risk in children), chronic haemolytic anaemia, splenic sequestration in young → autosplenectomy from infarcts → infection risk (encapsulated organisms — pneumococcus); treated with hydroxyurea (raises HbF), L-glutamine, voxelotor, crizanlizumab, vaccination, prophylactic penicillin in children, transfusion, and curative HSCT or gene therapy.

Distractors:

- "Iron deficiency anaemia." — Reveals misconception: student picks a nutritional cause. Correction: sickle cell disease is a haemoglobinopathy, not iron deficiency; iron is usually normal or elevated (from frequent transfusions).
- "Acquired in adulthood from infection." — Reveals misconception: student picks an acquired aetiology. Correction: sickle cell disease is autosomal recessive; symptoms typically begin at 6 months as HbF declines.
- "Reversible with vitamin supplementation." — Reveals misconception: student picks a nutritional therapy. Correction: sickle cell disease is genetic; treatments include hydroxyurea, novel disease-modifying agents, transfusion, transplant, and gene therapy.

Explanation: Sickle cell disease is an autosomal recessive haemoglobinopathy caused by a single point mutation in β-globin (Glu→Val at position 6) producing HbS [Berne & Levy — Blood; Ganong]. Pathophysiology: HbS polymerises on deoxygenation → sickled RBCs that obstruct microvasculature (vaso-occlusion) and have shortened lifespan (~10–20 days, chronic haemolytic anaemia). Symptoms emerge after 6 months of age as HbF declines. Clinical: vaso-occlusive crises (severe bone/abdominal pain, acute chest syndrome — fever and respiratory distress; stroke risk in children — primary prevention with TCD screening and chronic transfusion), chronic haemolytic anaemia, splenic sequestration in young children → autosplenectomy from repeated infarcts → infection risk (encapsulated organisms — pneumococcus, Haemophilus, meningococcus, Salmonella osteomyelitis), priapism, leg ulcers, dactylitis, cholelithiasis (pigment stones from haemolysis), avascular necrosis (femoral head), retinopathy, and renal medullary disease. Treatment: hydroxyurea (raises HbF, reduces crises), L-glutamine, voxelotor (HbS polymerisation inhibitor), crizanlizumab (anti-P-selectin antibody), vaccination (pneumococcal, meningococcal, Haemophilus), prophylactic penicillin in young children, hydration and analgesia for crises, exchange transfusion for severe events, allogeneic HSCT (curative, in young patients with matched donors), and gene therapy (newly approved CRISPR and lentiviral). Sickle cell trait (heterozygote) confers malaria resistance — explaining the high allele frequency in malaria-endemic regions.

Hints:

1. The disease is a single point mutation in β-globin.
2. Sickling occurs on deoxygenation and produces vaso-occlusive crises plus haemolysis.
3. Hydroxyurea raises HbF and reduces crises.

Stem: Aplastic anaemia is best characterised by:

Correct answer: Bone marrow failure with pancytopenia — anaemia, leukopenia (especially neutropenia), and thrombocytopenia — with hypocellular marrow on biopsy; causes include idiopathic, drug-induced (chloramphenicol, sulfa, NSAIDs, anticonvulsants), radiation, viral (parvovirus B19 — pure red cell aplasia; hepatitis), and Fanconi anaemia (genetic); treated with allogeneic HSCT (curative in young patients), immunosuppression (ATG + cyclosporine), and supportive care (transfusions, antimicrobials).

Distractors:

- "Hyperplastic marrow with leukocytosis." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the marrow picture. Correction: aplastic anaemia features _hypocellular_ marrow with pancytopenia; hyperplastic marrow with leukocytosis suggests leukaemia or reactive states.
- "Isolated thrombocytosis." — Reveals misconception: student picks a single-lineage abnormality. Correction: aplastic anaemia is _pancytopenia_ — all three lineages reduced; isolated thrombocytosis suggests essential thrombocythaemia or reactive states.
- "Reversible with iron and B12 supplementation." — Reveals misconception: student picks nutritional therapy. Correction: aplastic anaemia is marrow failure; treatment is HSCT or immunosuppression, not iron/B12 supplementation.

Explanation: Aplastic anaemia is bone marrow failure producing pancytopenia (anaemia, leukopenia/neutropenia, thrombocytopenia) with hypocellular marrow on biopsy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Ganong]. Causes: idiopathic (most common — autoimmune destruction of HSCs), drug-induced (chloramphenicol — historical; sulfonamides; NSAIDs; anticonvulsants; chemotherapy), radiation, viral (parvovirus B19 causes pure red cell aplasia, especially in chronic haemolytic anaemia like sickle cell; hepatitis viruses; HIV; EBV), and inherited bone marrow failure syndromes (Fanconi anaemia — autosomal recessive, short stature, skeletal anomalies, predisposition to AML and solid tumours; dyskeratosis congenita, Diamond–Blackfan, Shwachman–Diamond). Diagnosis: bone marrow biopsy (hypocellular, <25% of normal cellularity), exclusion of myelodysplastic syndromes and other causes of pancytopenia. Treatment: allogeneic HSCT (curative in young patients <40 with matched sibling donor — first-line); immunosuppression (anti-thymocyte globulin + cyclosporine ± eltrombopag — TPO mimetic — first-line if no transplant donor or >40 years); supportive care (RBC and platelet transfusions, antimicrobial prophylaxis, growth factors). Untreated severe aplastic anaemia has high mortality from infection and bleeding. Acquired pure red cell aplasia is treated similarly to aplastic anaemia.

Hints:

1. Pancytopenia with hypocellular marrow is the hallmark.
2. Drugs, viruses, and idiopathic causes are common.
3. HSCT is curative; immunosuppression is the alternative.

Pass 1 (Q1–10) develops the basics: total blood volume, the major plasma protein albumin, haematocrit, the three cellular components, plasma versus serum, globulins and fibrinogen, plasma viscosity, transferrin and iron transport, the haemoglobin tetramer, and the dominant extracellular ions.
