## Pass 1 — Comprehension and reasoning

QUESTION 1
Type: comprehension
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The cell membrane is a _selectively permeable barrier_. Explain how a single lipid bilayer with embedded proteins can simultaneously serve as both a barrier (excluding most molecules) and a regulated transport interface (permitting some molecules in and others out).

Model answer: The combination of barrier and regulated transport is achieved by separating the two functions architecturally — the _bilayer_ provides the barrier; the _embedded proteins_ provide the transport pathways [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. The phospholipid bilayer has a hydrophobic interior several nanometres thick. Hydrophilic and charged solutes — ions, sugars, amino acids, water-soluble drugs — cannot cross the bilayer significantly because they would have to shed their hydration shells, an enormously unfavourable energetic step. The bilayer therefore _passively excludes_ essentially every substance the cell needs to regulate. At the same time, hydrophobic substances (gases such as O₂ and CO₂, steroid hormones, lipid-soluble drugs) dissolve in the bilayer's hydrophobic core and cross freely by simple diffusion. The bilayer, by itself, is therefore a barrier to most physiologically interesting substances. Embedded _integral membrane proteins_ provide specific, regulated pathways for the excluded hydrophilic substances. These include channels (open or gated pores: aquaporins, ion channels), carriers (uniporters, symporters, antiporters), and pumps (ATP-driven against gradient: Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, Ca²⁺-ATPase, ABC transporters). Each protein selects for specific substrates and operates under its own regulation — gating by voltage, ligand binding, mechanical stretch, or post-translational modification. The cell adjusts permeability moment-to-moment by changing the number of transporters at the membrane (insertion/retrieval) and by gating those already there. The dual architecture — passive bilayer barrier + actively regulated protein pathways — lets the membrane be both impermeable to most substances _and_ selectively permeable to those the cell needs.

Elaborative explanation: This dual architecture allows steady, steep ionic gradients (low intracellular Na⁺, high intracellular K⁺, low intracellular Ca²⁺) to coexist with the rapid, controlled changes that underlie excitability, secretion, and contraction. The same architectural principle operates at every membrane in the cell — including organelle membranes — though the protein toolkit varies.

Hints:

1. Two structures sit in the membrane: the lipids and the proteins. They do different jobs.
2. What property of the bilayer makes it impermeable to most substances?
3. The proteins create exceptions — specific, regulated pathways for substances the bilayer would otherwise exclude.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the lipid bilayer as the _default barrier_, due to its hydrophobic interior.
- States which substances pass freely through the bilayer (small uncharged hydrophobic molecules — gases, steroids) and which do not (ions, sugars, amino acids).
- Identifies integral membrane proteins as the regulated transport pathways.
- Names at least two protein classes (channels, carriers, pumps).
- States that regulation occurs by changing transporter abundance, activity, or gating.

A Yellow answer captures the barrier-vs-protein division but omits which substances actually cross the bilayer freely, or names only one protein class.

A Red answer treats the membrane as uniformly permeable or impermeable, or claims the lipid bilayer itself does the selective transport, or omits the role of proteins.

Common misconceptions:

- "The bilayer is a sieve that lets small things through and excludes large things." Correction: size is not the principal selector — _charge_ and _polarity_ are. A small ion like Na⁺ is excluded; a much larger steroid hormone passes freely.

QUESTION 2
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Compare _simple diffusion_ and _facilitated diffusion_ across cell membranes. Both are passive (downhill) processes — what features distinguish them, and how would you experimentally distinguish one from the other?

Model answer: Simple diffusion and facilitated diffusion are both passive — neither uses metabolic energy and both move solute down its electrochemical gradient — but they differ in two structural features and three kinetic consequences [Costanzo ch.1; Berne ch.1].

Structural differences: _simple diffusion_ occurs directly through the lipid bilayer; the solute dissolves in the hydrophobic interior and crosses by random thermal motion. No protein is involved. _Facilitated diffusion_ uses an integral membrane protein — a channel (e.g., aquaporins for water, ion channels for ions) or a carrier (e.g., GLUT family for glucose). The solute does not dissolve in the bilayer; it interacts with the protein's binding site or pore.

Kinetic consequences (the experimental tells):

1. _Saturation_. Simple diffusion shows linear flux: doubling the concentration gradient doubles the flux without limit. Facilitated diffusion shows saturation: at high substrate concentrations, all binding sites are occupied and the rate plateaus at Tm. Plotting flux versus concentration for the two mechanisms produces a straight line for simple diffusion and a hyperbolic curve for facilitated diffusion.
2. _Stereospecificity_. Simple diffusion does not distinguish between stereoisomers (D-glucose and L-glucose cross at the same rate). Facilitated diffusion does — GLUT carriers transport D-glucose but not L-glucose. The D/L-glucose comparison is a classical experimental discriminator.
3. _Competitive inhibition_. Simple diffusion shows no competition between related solutes. Facilitated diffusion does — adding D-galactose inhibits D-glucose transport because the two compete for the same carrier binding site.

Other practical discriminators: facilitated diffusion can be inhibited by specific drugs (e.g., phloretin inhibits GLUT) and is temperature-sensitive in a different way from simple diffusion (carriers denature at high temperatures). Simple diffusion typically applies to lipid-soluble solutes (gases, steroids) and water (in part); facilitated diffusion applies to polar solutes the bilayer cannot handle (ions, sugars, amino acids, water through aquaporins).

Elaborative explanation: This distinction is foundational because most physiologically important solutes use facilitated diffusion (or active transport), and many drugs target the carriers and channels involved. Recognising that flux is _not_ simply linear in gradient — that there are saturable steps in the system — is the conceptual basis for understanding renal Tm-limited reabsorption, the dose-response of carrier-mediated drug transport, and pharmacokinetics generally.

Hints:

1. Both processes are passive — that's the same. What differs?
2. One process uses a protein; the other does not. What experimental tests reveal protein involvement?
3. The kinetics of binding to a protein give three discriminators: saturation, stereospecificity, competition.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States that both are passive (downhill, no ATP).
- Identifies the structural difference: simple diffusion through bilayer alone; facilitated diffusion uses protein channel or carrier.
- Names at least two of the three carrier-mediated kinetic features (saturation/Tm, stereospecificity, competition) as experimental discriminators.
- Explains the contrast between linear flux (simple) and saturating flux (facilitated) at high concentrations.
- Optional: contrasts the typical substrates of each (lipid-soluble vs polar).

A Yellow answer correctly identifies the structural difference but does not articulate the kinetic discriminators, or names only one of the three carrier features.

A Red answer treats facilitated diffusion as active (uses ATP), or claims simple diffusion shows saturation, or conflates the two mechanisms entirely.

QUESTION 3
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase moves 3 Na⁺ ions out of the cell and 2 K⁺ ions into the cell per ATP hydrolysed. This 3:2 stoichiometry makes the pump _electrogenic_. Explain what "electrogenic" means in this context and what physiological consequences follow from it.

Model answer: An electrogenic transporter moves a _net charge_ across the membrane each cycle, contributing directly to the membrane potential rather than transferring charge in a balanced way [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase moves 3 positive charges out (3 Na⁺) and 2 positive charges in (2 K⁺) — net 1 positive charge out of the cell per cycle. Over many cycles, the pump therefore contributes a small but persistent outward (hyperpolarising) current.

Physiological consequences:

1. _Direct contribution to resting membrane potential._ Most of the resting potential comes from the K⁺ equilibrium potential (K⁺ is the dominant resting permeability), but the electrogenic Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase contributes a few millivolts of additional negativity. In tissues where pump activity is high (e.g., active neurons), the pump's contribution can be measurable; in cells where the pump is inhibited (e.g., by ouabain), the membrane depolarises slightly even before ionic gradients have changed.

2. _Maintenance of the gradients themselves._ The ionic gradients (low intracellular Na⁺, high intracellular K⁺) created and maintained by this pump are the substrate for the resting potential, the action potential, and most secondary active transport. Without continuous pump activity, the gradients dissipate over minutes to hours.

3. _Energetic cost._ Because the pump moves a net charge against the electrochemical gradient, its energy cost is higher than a hypothetical electroneutral 3:3 pump would be. The cell pays this cost — Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase consumes 25–40% of resting ATP in many tissues, and substantially more in metabolically active tissues like brain and kidney — because the electrical contribution is functionally valuable.

4. _Cell volume regulation._ The pump's net export of solute (one charge means one solute particle leaves) helps maintain cell volume against the inward osmotic pressure that would otherwise be created by intracellular impermeant macromolecules (proteins, organic phosphates). Inhibiting the pump causes cells to swell — a feature of cellular oedema in ischaemia.

5. _Pharmacological exploitation._ Cardiac glycosides (ouabain, digoxin) inhibit the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. Pump inhibition raises intracellular Na⁺, reducing the driving force for the Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger, raising intracellular Ca²⁺, and increasing cardiac contractility. Recognising the chain — pump → Na⁺ gradient → secondary active transport → contractility — is essential to understanding cardiac glycoside therapy.

Elaborative explanation: The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase is therefore far more than a simple ion pump — it is the central energy converter that links cellular metabolism (ATP) to the gradients on which excitability, transport, and cell volume all depend. Its electrogenic stoichiometry is a small but functionally important detail.

Hints:

1. "Electrogenic" means the pump moves a net charge, not balanced charge.
2. The 3:2 stoichiometry produces what net charge per cycle?
3. List the physiological consequences of a small persistent outward current — direct membrane potential effect, gradient maintenance, volume regulation, drug exploitation.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines "electrogenic" correctly: pump moves net charge across the membrane per cycle.
- States the net charge per cycle (1 positive charge out per ATP).
- Identifies at least three physiological consequences (e.g., direct contribution to resting potential, gradient maintenance, cell volume regulation, energetic cost, pharmacological exploitation).
- Mentions the energetic cost (25–40% of resting ATP in many tissues).
- Optional: connects the pump's electrogenicity to cardiac glycoside pharmacology.

A Yellow answer defines electrogenic correctly but identifies only one or two consequences, or omits the energetic-cost / clinical-pharmacology angle.

A Red answer treats the pump as electroneutral, or claims it does not contribute to the membrane potential, or attributes the entire resting potential to the pump (rather than primarily to K⁺ permeability).

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Active transport moves substrates against their electrochemical gradients and is divided into _primary_ and _secondary_ active transport. Explain the architectural distinction between these two modes, give one specific example of each, and explain how secondary active transport is ultimately powered by ATP despite not hydrolysing ATP directly.

Model answer: Both forms of active transport move substrate uphill (against its electrochemical gradient), but they differ in the _immediate_ energy source [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1].

_Primary active transport_ uses ATP _directly_ — the transporter is itself an ATPase that hydrolyses ATP during the transport cycle. The energy released drives the conformational change that moves substrate against its gradient. Examples:

- Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase (P-type): 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per ATP, found in all cells.
- Ca²⁺-ATPase (P-type): SERCA (sarco-/endoplasmic reticulum Ca²⁺-ATPase) and PMCA (plasma-membrane Ca²⁺-ATPase) extrude Ca²⁺ against its gradient.
- H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (P-type): in gastric parietal cells, secretes H⁺ into the gastric lumen to acidify it.
- V-type H⁺-ATPases: in lysosomes and endosomes, maintain acidic intraluminal pH.
- ABC transporters (e.g., CFTR, P-glycoprotein): transport diverse substrates using ATP binding and hydrolysis.

_Secondary active transport_ does not hydrolyse ATP directly. Instead, it couples the uphill movement of one substrate to the downhill movement of another (usually Na⁺) — the favourable downhill movement provides the energy for the unfavourable uphill movement. Examples:

- SGLT1 (Na⁺-glucose symporter): glucose moves into the enterocyte against its concentration gradient by being co-transported with Na⁺ moving down its gradient.
- Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger (NHE-1): H⁺ moves out of the cell against its gradient by being exchanged for Na⁺ moving in.
- Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX): Ca²⁺ moves out of the cardiac myocyte against its gradient by exchange with 3 Na⁺ moving in.

Connection to ATP: secondary active transport's "energy source" is the ion gradient (usually Na⁺ from outside the cell). That gradient is itself maintained by primary active transport — specifically, the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, which uses ATP to extrude Na⁺ continuously. So secondary active transport is _indirectly_ powered by ATP — through the pump that maintains the Na⁺ gradient. If the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase fails (e.g., severe ATP depletion in ischaemia), the Na⁺ gradient collapses over minutes to hours, and _all_ secondary active transport in the cell fails along with it.

Elaborative explanation: This architectural distinction is essential for understanding why intracellular ATP supports so many transport processes that don't seem directly ATP-dependent. The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase is the central energy converter — every Na⁺-coupled secondary active transporter feeds off the gradient it maintains. This is also why many transport-dependent pathologies (renal tubular dysfunction, hypoxic neuronal injury) trace ultimately to ATP failure even when the transporters that fail are not themselves ATPases.

Hints:

1. Both forms move substrate uphill — what differs is the _immediate_ coupling.
2. The primary form hydrolyses ATP at the transporter itself. The secondary form uses something else as immediate energy.
3. Ultimately, both forms depend on ATP — but for different reasons.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines primary active transport: directly hydrolyses ATP at the transporter.
- Defines secondary active transport: uses an ion gradient (usually Na⁺) as immediate energy source.
- Names at least one specific example of each (e.g., Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase for primary; SGLT1 or NCX for secondary).
- Explains that secondary active transport is _indirectly_ powered by ATP via the pump that maintains the Na⁺ gradient.
- Optional: notes the consequence — secondary active transport collapses with ATP failure even though it doesn't directly use ATP.

A Yellow answer correctly distinguishes the two but does not articulate the indirect ATP dependence of secondary active transport, or gives only one valid example.

A Red answer claims secondary active transport is passive, or that it directly uses ATP, or conflates the two categories.

QUESTION 5
Type: comprehension
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Carrier-mediated transport (whether facilitated diffusion, primary active, or secondary active) shares three universal features that simple diffusion lacks: _saturation_, _stereospecificity_, and _competition_. Explain each of the three features, give a specific physiological example for each, and explain why these features all derive from a single underlying property of carrier proteins.

Model answer: All three features derive from the same underlying property: carrier proteins have a _finite number of binding sites_ with specific molecular geometry [Costanzo ch.1].

_Saturation._ The carrier has a limited number of binding sites per protein, and the membrane has a limited number of carrier proteins per unit area. At low substrate concentrations, most binding sites are unoccupied and transport rate increases linearly with substrate. As concentration rises, binding sites fill; at very high concentrations, every site is occupied. Beyond this point, additional substrate cannot find a binding site and transport rate plateaus at the _transport maximum_ (Tm). The kinetics resemble Michaelis-Menten enzyme kinetics, with Tm analogous to Vmax. _Physiological example_: glucose reabsorption in the proximal tubule. At normal plasma glucose, the filtered load is well below Tm and reabsorption is complete. Above the renal threshold (~180–200 mg/dL plasma glucose), the filtered load exceeds Tm and the excess spills into the urine — the classical glycosuria of uncontrolled diabetes.

_Stereospecificity._ The binding site has a specific three-dimensional geometry that recognises a particular molecular shape — including the chirality (handedness) of the substrate. The binding site that fits D-glucose does not fit its mirror image L-glucose, even though the two are chemically identical. _Physiological example_: SGLT carriers in the renal proximal tubule and intestinal brush border transport D-glucose efficiently but do not transport L-glucose. Pharmacologists exploit this — drugs that need to be selectively absorbed or excluded can sometimes be designed as the appropriate stereoisomer.

_Competition._ If two chemically related solutes both fit the carrier's binding site, they compete for the same finite number of sites. The presence of one inhibits the transport of the other. _Physiological example_: D-galactose competes with D-glucose for SGLT — adding galactose to a solution reduces the rate of glucose absorption because galactose occupies some of the binding sites. Another example: aminoaciduria from competition among related amino acids for shared carrier systems in the proximal tubule.

The unifying logic: all three features reflect the same architectural fact — _carriers have specific binding sites in finite numbers_. Saturation is what happens when sites are filled. Stereospecificity is what happens because the sites have geometric preferences. Competition is what happens when multiple solutes vie for the same sites. Simple diffusion has none of these features because there is no protein with binding sites — solutes cross by random thermal motion through the bilayer, with no specificity, no saturation, and no competition.

Elaborative explanation: These three features are the experimental fingerprint of carrier-mediated transport — distinguishing it from simple diffusion. They also generate clinical consequences: Tm-limited reabsorption produces threshold phenomena (glycosuria, phosphaturia); stereospecificity makes drug design predictable; competition explains aminoacidurias and certain drug interactions. The conceptual lesson — that all three features arise from a single underlying property — is more useful than memorising them as a list.

Hints:

1. Three features, one underlying cause.
2. The cause has to do with carrier proteins having a _limited_ number of _specific_ binding sites.
3. Saturation reflects the _limited_ part. Stereospecificity reflects the _specific_ part. What does competition reflect?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines saturation correctly (rate plateaus at Tm) and gives a physiological example (renal glucose reabsorption / glycosuria threshold).
- Defines stereospecificity correctly (binding site discriminates between stereoisomers) and gives an example (D-glucose vs L-glucose for SGLT).
- Defines competition correctly (related solutes vie for the same sites) and gives an example (D-galactose competing with D-glucose, or aminoaciduria).
- Articulates the unifying property: all three features arise from a finite number of specific binding sites.
- States that simple diffusion lacks all three features because no protein is involved.

A Yellow answer defines all three features but does not articulate the unifying property, or omits an example for one feature.

A Red answer treats one or more of the features as a separate property of unrelated origin, or attributes any of the three to simple diffusion as well.

QUESTION 6
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Digoxin is used in heart failure to increase cardiac contractility (positive inotropy). Decompose the molecular mechanism — from drug binding to enhanced contractile force — and explain why the therapeutic margin is narrow.

Model answer: Digoxin's positive inotropic effect operates through a four-step chain that links pump inhibition to enhanced contractility [Berne ch.1; Rapid Review ch.1].

_Step 1 — Drug binding._ Digoxin (and other cardiac glycosides such as ouabain) binds to the α subunit of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase on the cardiac myocyte plasma membrane. The binding is reversible and inhibits pump activity in a concentration-dependent way — partial inhibition is the therapeutic effect; complete inhibition is toxic.

_Step 2 — Rise in intracellular Na⁺._ With the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase partially inhibited, less Na⁺ is extruded per unit time. Intracellular Na⁺ rises modestly (typically from ~10 mmol/L toward ~15 mmol/L).

_Step 3 — Reduced Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger driving force._ The Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX) normally extrudes Ca²⁺ from the cardiac myocyte by exchanging 1 Ca²⁺ out for 3 Na⁺ in, using the inward Na⁺ gradient as its energy source. With intracellular Na⁺ elevated, the inward Na⁺ gradient is smaller, the driving force for NCX-mediated Ca²⁺ extrusion is reduced, and less Ca²⁺ is pumped out per beat. Some authors describe NCX in this state as approaching reversal (Na⁺ out, Ca²⁺ in) — though under normal therapeutic conditions it usually stays in forward mode, just less effectively.

_Step 4 — Higher intracellular Ca²⁺ → enhanced contractility._ Cytosolic Ca²⁺ is removed during diastole partly by NCX and partly by SERCA (which loads it into the sarcoplasmic reticulum). With NCX less effective, more Ca²⁺ remains cytosolic at the end of diastole; more Ca²⁺ is loaded into the SR (because SERCA continues to pump); and the next beat's Ca²⁺-induced calcium release is larger. Each beat produces a stronger contraction. The clinical observation — "digitalised" hearts contract more forcefully — is the integrated outcome.

_Why the therapeutic margin is narrow._ The same chain is dangerous beyond a certain point. Excessive pump inhibition produces excessive Na⁺ accumulation, which can drive NCX into reversal and load Ca²⁺ into the cell rapidly. Cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ overload triggers spontaneous Ca²⁺ release from the SR (delayed afterdepolarisations), causing arrhythmias. Pump inhibition in renal tubules also reduces K⁺ reabsorption (and ultimately K⁺ secretion), and hypokalaemia from concurrent diuretic use further sensitises the cardiac myocyte to digitalis toxicity (because K⁺ competes with digoxin for the α-subunit binding site — low K⁺ allows more digoxin binding). The therapeutic window between positive inotropy and lethal arrhythmia is therefore narrow, and serum digoxin levels and serum potassium are routinely monitored.

Elaborative explanation: Digoxin is the classical illustration of the chain Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → Na⁺ gradient → secondary active transport → cellular function. Recognising that pump inhibition produces effects far beyond the pump itself — through the entire downstream gradient-dependent system — is the conceptual key to understanding cardiac glycoside therapy and toxicity.

Hints:

1. Digoxin has a single direct molecular target. What does it inhibit?
2. Trace the chain: pump → ion gradient → secondary active transporter → cellular calcium → contractility.
3. The narrow margin reflects the same chain producing opposite effects depending on the degree of inhibition.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies digoxin's direct target: the α subunit of Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, which it inhibits.
- Traces the chain: pump inhibition → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → ↓ NCX driving force → ↑ cytosolic / SR Ca²⁺ → enhanced contractility.
- Articulates that the inotropic effect is _indirect_ — Ca²⁺ is the proximate cause of contractility, but Na⁺ is what changes first.
- Explains why the therapeutic margin is narrow (excessive pump inhibition → arrhythmias from Ca²⁺ overload).
- Optional: mentions hypokalaemia as a sensitiser to digoxin toxicity.

A Yellow answer correctly traces the chain but does not articulate why the margin is narrow, or omits the Ca²⁺ overload mechanism for arrhythmias.

A Red answer claims digoxin acts directly on Ca²⁺ channels or contractile proteins, or omits the Na⁺ gradient entirely from the mechanism.

QUESTION 7
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A child with cystic fibrosis has the F508del mutation in CFTR. Decompose the molecular cascade — from the gene mutation to multi-organ disease — and explain why drugs that "rescue" F508del-CFTR (e.g., lumacaftor/ivacaftor) work.

Model answer: F508del-CFTR produces multi-organ disease through a chain that begins at the level of protein folding and propagates outward [Berne ch.1; Rapid Review ch.1].

_Step 1 — Protein folding failure._ F508del is a deletion of the codon for phenylalanine at position 508 in the CFTR protein. The deleted residue lies in a region critical for proper folding of the first nucleotide-binding domain. The mutant protein synthesised on rough ER ribosomes cannot complete its folding correctly. ER quality-control machinery (chaperones, the proteasome) recognises the misfolded protein, retains it in the ER, and degrades it. Very little F508del-CFTR ever reaches the plasma membrane.

_Step 2 — Loss of CFTR function at epithelial apical membranes._ CFTR is normally an ATP-gated chloride channel on the apical membrane of epithelial cells in airways, sweat glands, pancreatic ducts, intestinal mucosa, and reproductive tract. With CFTR missing from the apical membrane, chloride secretion fails. CFTR also normally regulates other transporters (notably the epithelial Na⁺ channel ENaC, which it inhibits); without CFTR, ENaC is over-active, increasing Na⁺ reabsorption.

_Step 3 — Altered fluid composition at epithelial surfaces._ The combined loss of Cl⁻ secretion and gain of Na⁺ reabsorption depletes water from epithelial surfaces (water follows electrolytes osmotically). The airway surface liquid becomes thick and viscous; pancreatic and intestinal secretions become concentrated and obstructive; sweat ducts cannot reabsorb chloride from the primary sweat (producing the classical sweat-test finding of elevated sweat chloride).

_Step 4 — Multi-organ disease._ Airway: thick mucus traps inhaled bacteria, mucociliary clearance fails, recurrent infections develop, and inflammatory damage produces bronchiectasis and progressive respiratory failure (the predominant cause of death). Pancreas: ducts obstruct, exocrine secretions cannot reach the duodenum, pancreatic insufficiency develops with malabsorption and steatorrhoea. Intestine: meconium ileus in neonates from thick intestinal contents. Reproductive tract: bilateral absence of the vas deferens in males, causing infertility. Sweat glands: elevated sweat chloride as a diagnostic marker.

_Step 5 — Therapeutic rescue._ Two classes of CFTR modulator drugs target different steps in the chain. _Correctors_ (e.g., lumacaftor) help F508del-CFTR fold correctly enough to escape ER quality control, allowing some functional protein to reach the plasma membrane. _Potentiators_ (e.g., ivacaftor) increase the gating activity of CFTR that is already at the membrane, increasing the chloride conductance per channel. The combination (lumacaftor + ivacaftor, marketed as Orkambi) addresses both the trafficking defect and the gating deficiency. Newer triple combinations (elexacaftor + tezacaftor + ivacaftor) have produced even more substantial clinical benefit. These drugs have transformed cystic fibrosis from a disease of childhood mortality to one of substantially improved adult survival.

Elaborative explanation: Cystic fibrosis is the paradigmatic example of how a single missense or single-residue deletion in one protein can produce a disease that touches every secretory epithelium. It also illustrates the architectural lesson that _protein folding_ is one of the most fragile points in cellular function — many genetic diseases (alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, Marfan syndrome, some haemoglobinopathies) trace ultimately to misfolded proteins.

Hints:

1. The mutation deletes a single amino acid. What goes wrong with the protein next?
2. Trace the chain: misfolded protein → ER retention/degradation → empty apical membrane → loss of Cl⁻ secretion → thick secretions → multi-organ disease.
3. Modulator drugs target two of the steps in the chain — which two?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies F508del as a single-residue deletion in CFTR.
- Explains the folding failure and ER retention/degradation that follow.
- Connects loss of CFTR at the apical membrane to failed Cl⁻ secretion (and altered ENaC regulation).
- Connects altered electrolyte transport to thickened secretions.
- Names at least two organ systems affected and the clinical consequence in each (airway: bronchiectasis; pancreas: insufficiency; reproductive: infertility; sweat: elevated chloride; intestine: meconium ileus).
- Explains how modulator drugs work (correctors help folding; potentiators increase gating).

A Yellow answer traces the chain through to clinical features but does not address how modulator drugs rescue function, or names only one affected organ.

A Red answer treats CFTR as a sodium channel rather than a chloride channel, or attributes the disease to a different transporter class, or claims the protein never reaches the ER.

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A child with cholera presents with profuse watery diarrhoea, dehydration, and impending hypovolaemic shock. Oral rehydration solution (ORS) — containing glucose, sodium, and other electrolytes — can rescue this child even though the intestinal epithelium is actively secreting fluid into the lumen. Explain the underlying transport pathology of cholera and the mechanism by which ORS bypasses it.

Model answer: Cholera and the ORS rescue both operate through transport mechanisms in the intestinal epithelium [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1].

_The pathology of cholera._ Cholera toxin (produced by _Vibrio cholerae_ in the intestinal lumen) enters intestinal epithelial cells and ADP-ribosylates the Gαs subunit of a G-protein, locking adenylyl cyclase on. Intracellular cAMP rises chronically. The high cAMP activates the apical CFTR chloride channel persistently; chloride pours out of the cell into the intestinal lumen. Sodium follows passively (paracellular path) to maintain electroneutrality, and water follows osmotically. The result is massive secretion of fluid into the intestinal lumen — watery diarrhoea that can reach 1 L per hour and rapidly dehydrate the patient. Crucially, the _secretory_ pathology activates one set of transporters; the _absorptive_ pathway is not directly damaged.

_Why ordinary IV salt solutions are difficult._ IV fluids work but require sterile equipment, trained staff, and cold-chain — often unavailable where cholera outbreaks occur. Oral rehydration is the practical answer, but pure water or plain salt water is poorly absorbed in the face of active secretion: ions diffuse into the lumen as fast as they enter the cell, and net absorption is minimal.

_The ORS solution: SGLT1._ The intestinal apical membrane expresses _SGLT1_, a Na⁺-glucose symporter (secondary active transport). SGLT1 couples one molecule of glucose to one Na⁺ ion — both move from lumen into cell together. The Na⁺ gradient driving this is maintained by the basolateral Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. SGLT1 is _not damaged by cholera toxin_; it remains fully functional even when the cell is hyper-secreting chloride.

_How ORS rescues._ ORS contains both sodium and glucose at carefully balanced concentrations (typically 75 mmol/L Na⁺ and 75 mmol/L glucose in WHO standard ORS, with potassium and citrate). Glucose drives Na⁺ uptake via SGLT1; once Na⁺ enters the cell it can be pumped out across the basolateral membrane by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, raising blood Na⁺ slightly; the small interstitial-to-luminal osmotic gradient that develops pulls water through tight junctions and across the cell into the bloodstream. Net absorption now exceeds net secretion, and the patient rehydrates from oral fluid alone — even with the secretory pathology unchanged. The clinical pearl: ORS works because it exploits an _intact_ transport pathway (SGLT1) to overcome a _broken_ secretory state (toxin-activated CFTR).

Elaborative explanation: Oral rehydration therapy is one of the most important medical advances of the 20th century, credited with saving millions of lives — particularly children — from diarrhoeal mortality worldwide. Recognising that a single intact transporter (SGLT1) can rescue a patient with massive fluid loss is one of the most elegant examples of how cellular transport physiology translates directly to clinical practice. The same SGLT1-driven pathway underlies why glucose is added to sports drinks (modest enhancement of fluid absorption during exercise).

Hints:

1. Cholera toxin activates one transporter persistently. Which one?
2. The secretory pathology damages the _secretory_ side of the epithelium. Is the absorptive side also damaged?
3. ORS contains glucose. Why does adding glucose to a salt solution improve fluid absorption so dramatically?

- Notes that the secretory pathway is activated, not the absorptive pathway broken.

Stem: A patient receives 1 litre of each of three IV fluids on different days: (a) 5% dextrose in water, (b) 0.9% (isotonic) saline, and (c) 3% (hypertonic) saline. For each fluid, predict where the volume distributes between the ICF, interstitial fluid, and plasma compartments — and explain _why_ in terms of tonicity.

Model answer: The compartmental distribution of each IV fluid follows directly from its tonicity relative to plasma [Costanzo ch.1].

_(a) 5% dextrose in water._ The infused solution is iso-osmotic with plasma (5% dextrose = 278 mOsm/L), but the dextrose is rapidly metabolised once inside the body, leaving essentially free water. Free water distributes throughout total body water in proportion to existing volumes. With total body water roughly 60% of body weight, ICF is two-thirds of TBW (40% of body weight) and ECF is one-third of TBW (20% of body weight); within ECF, plasma is about 25% and interstitial fluid is about 75%. So 1 L of effective free water distributes approximately: ~667 mL into the ICF, ~250 mL into interstitial fluid, ~83 mL into plasma. This is therefore a _poor_ choice for resuscitating a hypovolaemic patient — most of the volume goes into cells, not the vascular compartment.

_(b) 0.9% (isotonic) saline._ Iso-osmotic and isotonic with plasma. There is no driving force for water to move between ECF and ICF, so the entire infused volume stays in the ECF. Within the ECF, plasma is about 25% and interstitial fluid is about 75% of the volume. So 1 L distributes: ~250 mL into plasma, ~750 mL into interstitial fluid, ~0 mL into ICF. This is the standard fluid for ECF volume replacement (e.g., haemorrhage, dehydration without electrolyte derangement) — but note that only about a quarter of the infused volume actually expands plasma volume.

_(c) 3% (hypertonic) saline._ Hypertonic with plasma — adds Na⁺ and Cl⁻ to the ECF without proportional water. The added solute raises ECF osmolality, creating an osmotic driving force for water to move _out of cells_ into the ECF. The ECF therefore expands by _more_ than the infused volume — initially the 1 L of infused fluid plus additional water drawn from the ICF. The ICF _shrinks_ during the process. Hypertonic saline is used in specific clinical scenarios — symptomatic hyponatraemia, refractory cerebral oedema (the high-osmolarity fluid pulls water from the brain across the intact blood-brain barrier) — but with care because rapid sodium correction can cause central pontine myelinolysis.

_The unifying principle._ Tonicity of the infused fluid relative to plasma determines water movement between ECF and ICF. Iso-osmotic fluids without metabolisable solutes stay in the ECF. Iso-osmotic fluids with rapidly metabolisable solutes (like dextrose) become free water and distribute throughout TBW. Hypertonic fluids draw water out of cells into the ECF.

_Clinical implications._ Choice of IV fluid depends on which compartment the clinician wants to expand. To expand plasma volume (e.g., haemorrhagic shock), isotonic crystalloid is the workhorse — but only ~25% reaches plasma, so blood products or colloids may be needed for severe hypovolaemia. To replace pure water deficit (e.g., diabetes insipidus with hypernatraemia), 5% dextrose or hypotonic saline is appropriate because the deficit is in total body water. To raise serum sodium quickly (e.g., severe symptomatic hyponatraemia), hypertonic saline is used but with rate limits to prevent demyelinating injury.

Elaborative explanation: This logic — tonicity → osmotic gradient → water movement → compartment redistribution — is one of the most useful clinical applications of basic transport physiology. It also explains why pure water is never given IV (it would be massively hypotonic and would lyse red cells in the line) and why IV solutions are always either isotonic (saline, Ringer's) or carry a metabolisable solute (5% dextrose) so they don't acutely lyse cells.

Hints:

1. The key concept is _tonicity_ — effective osmotic activity, considering only solutes that don't cross the cell membrane.
2. 5% dextrose has osmotically active dextrose initially, but the dextrose is rapidly metabolised. What does that leave?
3. Compartment volumes: ICF is 2/3 TBW, ECF 1/3 TBW; within ECF, plasma is 1/4 and interstitial fluid is 3/4.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- For 5% dextrose: identifies it as effectively free water, distributing throughout TBW (about two-thirds into ICF).
- For 0.9% saline: identifies it as iso-osmotic and isotonic, staying entirely in the ECF (about one-quarter in plasma, three-quarters in interstitial).
- For 3% saline: identifies it as hypertonic, drawing water _out_ of cells into the ECF.
- Articulates the unifying principle: tonicity of infused fluid relative to plasma determines redistribution.
- Connects to clinical scenarios where each fluid is appropriate (or inappropriate).

A Yellow answer correctly handles two of the three fluids but is unclear about one (commonly conflating 5% dextrose's behaviour with 0.9% saline's, or missing that hypertonic fluid pulls water out of cells).

A Red answer treats all three fluids identically, or claims that 5% dextrose distributes only to ECF, or that 0.9% saline distributes equally into ICF and ECF.

Stem: Glucose absorption in the small intestine is a textbook example of how transport mechanisms can be combined to move a substrate from a low-concentration compartment (the lumen, after a glucose meal has been digested) to a high-concentration compartment (the bloodstream) across an epithelial cell. Decompose the architecture — apical step, intracellular step, basolateral step — and explain why the SGLT1-GLUT2 combination is necessary rather than redundant.

Model answer: Intestinal glucose absorption uses a precisely staged combination of _secondary active transport_ on the apical side and _facilitated diffusion_ on the basolateral side, with the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase as the energy supply [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1].

_Apical step (lumen → enterocyte): SGLT1._ SGLT1 is a Na⁺-glucose symporter that couples movement of one glucose molecule into the cell with movement of one Na⁺ ion (the actual stoichiometry is 2 Na⁺ : 1 glucose for SGLT1, providing extra driving force, but the principle is the same with one). Both move from lumen into enterocyte together. Glucose is moving into a compartment where its concentration may already be higher than in the lumen (especially as digestion of luminal carbohydrate proceeds and the cell continues to absorb), so it is moving _uphill_ against its gradient. Na⁺ is moving down its gradient (intracellular Na⁺ is kept low by the basolateral Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase). The favourable Na⁺ gradient provides the driving force for uphill glucose movement. SGLT1 is therefore the active step — but it does not hydrolyse ATP directly.

_Intracellular step._ Glucose accumulates inside the enterocyte. There is now a higher concentration in the cell than in the bloodstream on the basolateral side.

_Basolateral step (enterocyte → bloodstream): GLUT2._ GLUT2 is a uniporter (passive facilitated diffusion). It allows glucose to leave the cell down its concentration gradient — from the high intracellular concentration into the lower bloodstream concentration. No energy is required for this step; it is the favourable downhill leg.

_Maintenance of the Na⁺ gradient: Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase on the basolateral membrane._ The basolateral membrane also carries Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, which continuously pumps Na⁺ out of the cell (against its gradient, using ATP). This maintains low intracellular Na⁺ and therefore preserves the inward Na⁺ gradient that powers SGLT1 on the apical side. ATP from cellular metabolism is the ultimate energy source for the entire absorptive process.

_Why the combination is necessary, not redundant._ SGLT1 alone could not perform absorption — once glucose entered the cell, it would have no easy exit on the basolateral side, and intracellular glucose would rise to inhibitory levels. GLUT2 alone could not perform absorption either — it is a passive uniporter and would simply equilibrate glucose between cell and lumen, never producing net absorption against a gradient. The two transporters are _staged_ so that the active step happens at the apical entry (where glucose moves uphill into the cell) and the passive step happens at the basolateral exit (where glucose moves downhill into the bloodstream). This combination — secondary active transport on one side, facilitated diffusion on the other — is the standard architecture for vectorial transport across epithelia, and it is repeated for amino acids, calcium, phosphate, and other absorbed substances in the intestine and the renal proximal tubule.

Elaborative explanation: This staged architecture is one of the most elegant features of epithelial physiology. It also explains why intestinal glucose absorption can continue against the prevailing blood-glucose level — SGLT1 doesn't care about blood glucose, only about luminal Na⁺ and glucose. The renal proximal tubule uses an analogous architecture (SGLT2 apically, GLUT2 basolaterally) for filtered glucose reabsorption. SGLT2 inhibitors (the gliflozins — empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) are a major class of antidiabetic drugs that block this reabsorption, intentionally promoting glycosuria as a glucose-lowering mechanism.

Hints:

1. Three transporters in series across the cell — apical, intracellular, basolateral. What does each do?
2. The apical step is _active_ — uphill against glucose's gradient. The basolateral step is _passive_ — downhill.
3. What fails if you have only one of the two transporters?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies SGLT1 as the apical Na⁺-glucose symporter (secondary active transport).
- Identifies GLUT2 as the basolateral glucose uniporter (passive facilitated diffusion).
- Identifies the basolateral Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase as the ultimate source of energy (maintains Na⁺ gradient).
- Explains the staged architecture: active uphill apical step, passive downhill basolateral step.
- Articulates why the combination is necessary (active alone or passive alone would not achieve net absorption against a gradient).

A Yellow answer correctly names the three transporters but does not articulate why the combination is necessary, or omits the role of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase.

A Red answer treats glucose absorption as simple diffusion, or names a single transporter as sufficient, or treats the basolateral GLUT2 step as also active.

QUESTION 11

Stem: A first-year medical student says: "All forms of active transport directly use ATP — that's what 'active' means." Identify the misconception, explain the correction, and use specific examples to show how ATP can power transport directly _or_ indirectly.

Model answer: The misconception is that "active" implies _direct_ ATP hydrolysis at the transporter [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. The correction: "active" means the transport moves substrate _against its electrochemical gradient_ — but the energy can come from ATP either _directly_ (primary active transport) or _indirectly_ through an ion gradient that is itself maintained by ATP (secondary active transport). Both forms are active; only one form involves ATPase activity at the transporter.

_Primary active transport._ The transporter is itself an ATPase — it hydrolyses ATP during the transport cycle, using the released energy to drive substrate against its gradient. Examples: Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase (P-type), Ca²⁺-ATPase (SERCA, PMCA — both P-type), H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (gastric parietal cell P-type), V-type H⁺-ATPase (lysosomal acidification), and ABC transporters (CFTR, P-glycoprotein, bile salt export pump). In each case, ATP hydrolysis at the transporter is part of the cycle.

_Secondary active transport._ The transporter does not hydrolyse ATP. Instead, it couples the uphill movement of one substrate to the downhill movement of another (usually Na⁺), using the favourable downhill gradient as its immediate energy source. Examples:

- SGLT1 (intestinal Na⁺-glucose symporter): glucose enters the cell against its gradient by being co-transported with Na⁺ moving down its gradient.
- Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger (NHE-1): H⁺ leaves the cell against its gradient in exchange for Na⁺ entering.
- Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX): Ca²⁺ leaves the cardiac myocyte against its gradient in exchange for 3 Na⁺ entering.

The Na⁺ gradient that powers all of these secondary active transporters is itself maintained by the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase — a primary active transporter that continuously hydrolyses ATP to extrude Na⁺. So secondary active transport is _indirectly_ powered by ATP, through the gradient that the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase maintains.

_Why this distinction matters._ If ATP synthesis fails (e.g., severe ischaemia, mitochondrial poisoning), all primary active transport stops within minutes — but so does secondary active transport, because the Na⁺ gradient collapses without continuous Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activity. Cellular Na⁺ rises, K⁺ falls, Ca²⁺ enters via NCX reversal, the cell depolarises, and downstream functions (excitability, secretion, contraction) all fail. The clinical picture of cellular ischaemia traces directly to this dependency of all transport on ATP.

The student's mental model can be repaired by recognising two layers: the _immediate_ energy source (ATP for primary active transport; ion gradient for secondary active transport) and the _ultimate_ energy source (always ATP, because the ion gradients are themselves maintained by ATP-driven pumps). "Active" refers to the uphill direction of transport, not to the specific mechanism of ATP coupling.

Elaborative explanation: The two-layer model is what allows physiologists to talk about secondary active transport as both "active" and "non-ATP-dependent" without contradiction. The two terms describe different aspects: directionality versus immediate coupling. Recognising this distinction is also clinically useful — it explains why ATP failure produces such a broad collapse of cellular function, even in transporters that don't appear to hydrolyse ATP themselves.

Hints:

1. "Active" describes a property of transport. Which property — direction, speed, or energy source?
2. Two ways ATP can power transport: directly at the transporter, or indirectly through an ion gradient.
3. Name a specific transporter that is "active" but does not hydrolyse ATP.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the misconception: that "active" implies direct ATP hydrolysis.
- Corrects: "active" means uphill movement; energy can be direct (primary) or indirect (secondary).
- Names at least one example of primary active transport and explains its direct ATP use.
- Names at least one example of secondary active transport and explains its indirect ATP dependence.
- Articulates the role of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase in maintaining the gradient that powers most secondary active transport.

A Yellow answer corrects the misconception but does not give specific examples of both primary and secondary active transport, or does not articulate the indirect ATP dependence.

A Red answer agrees with the student, or treats secondary active transport as passive, or claims all transporters either use ATP directly or are passive.

Stem: The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase is sometimes called the "master pump" of cellular physiology. Explain why this label is appropriate by tracing how its function supports excitability, secondary active transport, and cell volume regulation.

Model answer: The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase is the master pump because it sets up the gradients on which most of cellular physiology depends [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. Three functional consequences flow from its continuous activity.

_1. The resting membrane potential and excitability._ The pump maintains low intracellular Na⁺ (~14 mmol/L) and high intracellular K⁺ (~120 mmol/L). The K⁺ gradient is what drives the resting membrane potential: because the cell membrane at rest is dominantly permeable to K⁺ (through K⁺ leak channels), and because intracellular K⁺ is much higher than extracellular K⁺, K⁺ leaks outward, leaving the cell interior negative (resting potential ~−70 mV in many cell types). The Na⁺ gradient is what drives the action potential: when Na⁺ channels open during depolarisation, Na⁺ rushes inward (down its steep gradient), depolarising the cell to the Na⁺ equilibrium potential. Without the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase to maintain these gradients, the resting potential would slowly dissipate (over minutes to hours) and excitability would fail. The pump's electrogenic component (3 Na⁺ out for 2 K⁺ in) also contributes a few millivolts of additional negativity to the resting potential.

_2. Secondary active transport._ Most secondary active transporters in the body use the Na⁺ gradient (inward) as their energy source. SGLT carriers couple Na⁺ entry to glucose uptake; Na⁺-amino acid symporters carry essential amino acids; Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchangers extrude Ca²⁺ from the cardiac myocyte; Na⁺/H⁺ exchangers regulate cytosolic pH; Na⁺-bile-acid cotransporters reabsorb bile acids in the ileum. All of these transporters depend on the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase to maintain low intracellular Na⁺. If the pump fails, the Na⁺ gradient collapses, and _every Na⁺-coupled secondary active transport in the cell fails along with it_. The downstream functions — glucose absorption, amino acid uptake, calcium extrusion, pH regulation — all fail simultaneously.

_3. Cell volume regulation._ Cells contain large concentrations of impermeant macromolecules (proteins, organic phosphates) that exert osmotic pressure tending to draw water in. Without an opposing solute extrusion mechanism, cells would swell and lyse. The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase prevents this by extruding Na⁺ continuously — Cl⁻ and water follow osmotically, balancing the osmotic pressure of the impermeant macromolecules. When the pump fails (e.g., in severe ischaemia), Na⁺ accumulates intracellularly, water follows, and cells swell — _cellular oedema_ is one of the early consequences of ATP depletion.

_The "master pump" label._ The pump's centrality is captured by the fact that it consumes 25–40% of resting ATP in many tissues — and substantially more in metabolically active tissues like brain (where most of the basal metabolic rate goes to maintaining ionic gradients) and kidney (where the pump powers tubular reabsorption). When ATP fails, the pump fails, and _every_ downstream system that depends on the gradients it maintains begins to fail in parallel. Excitability fails (action potentials cease); secondary active transport fails (absorption stops); volume regulation fails (cells swell); intracellular Ca²⁺ rises (NCX runs in reverse); proteases and phospholipases activate; cell death follows. The cascade explains why even short ATP-deprivation produces broad cellular dysfunction.

Elaborative explanation: The "master pump" label captures both the centrality and the vulnerability of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. Recognising that pump failure produces systemic cellular collapse — not just one specific failure — is essential for understanding the pathophysiology of ischaemia, hypoxia, and many forms of cellular injury.

Hints:

1. Three downstream functions all depend on the gradients the pump maintains.
2. What ion gradient does the pump generate? What does that gradient power downstream?
3. What happens when the pump fails — to excitability, to absorption, to cell volume?

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Connects the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase to the resting membrane potential and excitability via the K⁺ and Na⁺ gradients.
- Connects the pump to secondary active transport (the Na⁺ gradient powers SGLT, NCX, NHE, etc.).
- Connects the pump to cell volume regulation (continuous Na⁺ extrusion balances impermeant intracellular macromolecules).
- States the energetic cost (25–40% of resting ATP).
- Articulates the consequence of failure: simultaneous breakdown of excitability, absorption, and volume regulation.

A Yellow answer covers two of the three functional consequences but omits one, or does not state the cost or failure consequence.

A Red answer treats the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase as just one of many pumps without acknowledging its centrality, or attributes the resting potential primarily to the pump's electrogenicity rather than to K⁺ permeability.

QUESTION 13
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Compare _ion channels_ (e.g., voltage-gated K⁺ channels), _carriers_ (e.g., GLUT1 uniporter), and _pumps_ (e.g., Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) in terms of their selectivity, gating, energy use, and transport rates. What architectural differences explain why these three classes have such different rate hierarchies (channels > carriers > pumps)?

Model answer: The three classes occupy distinct architectural niches with strikingly different functional properties [Berne ch.1].

_Channels._ Channels are pores through the membrane that conduct ions or small molecules when open. _Selectivity_: channels are highly selective for specific ions (a K⁺ channel conducts K⁺ ~1000× more than Na⁺); some channels are non-selective for cations or anions. _Gating_: most channels are gated — they open or close in response to a specific signal (voltage, ligand, mechanical stretch, second messenger). When open, they conduct rapidly; when closed, they conduct nothing. _Energy_: channels conduct passively (downhill) — no ATP. _Rate_: 10⁶–10⁸ molecules/second when open (roughly equivalent to filling a bucket from a fully open hose). Open pores like aquaporins, which conduct continuously, are even faster (~10⁹/s). The architectural reason for the high rate: ions diffuse through the open pore at near-aqueous diffusion rates; the channel does no chemistry, just allows passage.

_Carriers._ Carriers bind their substrate, undergo a conformational change, and release the substrate on the other side. _Selectivity_: high — the binding site has specific stereochemistry. _Gating_: most carriers are not "gated" in the channel sense, but they cycle: open to one side, bind, conformational change, open to the other side, release, reset. _Energy_: depends on the type — uniporters and symporters (when running with the gradient) are passive; symporters and antiporters running against gradient are secondary active transporters that use an ion gradient. _Rate_: 10²–10⁴ molecules/second per carrier — about a thousand times slower than channels. Architectural reason: each cycle requires multiple conformational changes, each of which has a finite time.

_Pumps._ Pumps are carriers that hydrolyse ATP during the transport cycle. _Selectivity_: very high — substrate binding sites are tightly designed. _Gating_: pumps don't "gate" in the channel sense; they cycle through ATP-bound, phosphorylated, and dephosphorylated states. _Energy_: ATP-dependent (primary active). _Rate_: 10²–10⁴ molecules/second — similar to non-pump carriers. Architectural reason: the transport cycle requires ATP binding, hydrolysis, conformational change, ion translocation, and resetting. ATP hydrolysis itself takes time.

_Why the rate hierarchy is what it is._ Channels are fast because they are _passive_ and _open_ — molecules just diffuse through. Carriers are slower because they require _conformational change_ per cycle. Pumps are slow because they require _both conformational change and ATP hydrolysis_ per cycle. The hierarchy maps onto increasing mechanistic complexity. The fact that channels can sustain high physiological flux (e.g., during the action potential, where Na⁺ enters at ~10⁷/s through each channel during the upstroke) while pumps work much more slowly is exactly what allows the cell to use channels for _fast_ signalling events and pumps for _slow_ maintenance of gradients between events.

_Functional consequences._ Pumps establish gradients slowly but persistently — they are the maintenance crew. Channels exploit those gradients in fast, regulated bursts — they are the action team. Carriers occupy a middle ground — selective passage of organic substrates without needing to be ultra-fast. The matching of mechanism to physiological function is the architectural elegance.

Elaborative explanation: This comparison clarifies why the cell uses different transporter types for different jobs. Action potentials (millisecond timescale) use channels, not pumps — pumps would be far too slow. Glucose absorption (over minutes after a meal) uses carriers — fast enough for the timescale, with the selectivity carriers offer. Establishing ionic gradients (continuously, against gradient) uses pumps — slow but inexorable. Recognising the architectural matching of speed to function is one of the most useful generalisations in transport physiology.

Hints:

1. Three transporter classes; three different architectures; three different rate ranges.
2. Channels: passive and open — fastest. Carriers: conformational change required — slower. Pumps: conformational change _and_ ATP hydrolysis — slowest.
3. The rate hierarchy maps onto the complexity of the transport mechanism.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- For channels: high selectivity, gating (voltage/ligand/mechanical), passive, fast (10⁶–10⁸/s).
- For carriers: high selectivity (stereospecificity), conformational change per cycle, may be passive or secondary active, intermediate rate (10²–10⁴/s).
- For pumps: high selectivity, ATP hydrolysis per cycle, primary active, slow rate (10²–10⁴/s).
- Articulates the mechanistic explanation for the rate hierarchy: channels are open conduits; carriers cycle; pumps cycle and hydrolyse ATP.
- Connects the rate matching to physiological function (channels for fast signalling, pumps for slow gradient maintenance).

A Yellow answer correctly distinguishes the three classes and names the rates but does not articulate the architectural reason for the hierarchy, or does not connect speed to physiological function.

A Red answer claims all three classes have similar rates, or that pumps are faster than channels, or that channels use ATP.

QUESTION 14
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A patient on long-term loop diuretic therapy (e.g., furosemide) develops hypokalaemia. Explain how loop diuretics — which act primarily on a transporter in the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle — produce hypokalaemia, decomposing the chain from drug action to plasma K⁺ change.

Model answer: Loop diuretics produce hypokalaemia through a chain that begins at NKCC2 in the thick ascending limb and propagates downstream to the distal nephron [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1].

_Step 1 — Drug action: NKCC2 inhibition._ Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) inhibit the Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ symporter (NKCC2; SLC12A1) on the apical membrane of thick ascending limb cells. NKCC2 normally reabsorbs Na⁺, K⁺, and 2 Cl⁻ from the tubular lumen — using the inward Na⁺ gradient (maintained by basolateral Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) as its energy source. Inhibition prevents this reabsorption.

_Step 2 — Increased Na⁺ delivery to distal nephron._ With NKCC2 inhibited, Na⁺ that would have been reabsorbed in the thick ascending limb passes onward to the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct.

_Step 3 — Increased Na⁺ reabsorption in the collecting duct via ENaC._ In the cortical collecting duct, principal cells reabsorb Na⁺ through the apical epithelial Na⁺ channel (ENaC). The rate of Na⁺ reabsorption here is sensitive to luminal Na⁺ delivery — more Na⁺ in the lumen, more Na⁺ reabsorbed via ENaC. Loop diuretics thus indirectly _increase_ Na⁺ reabsorption in the collecting duct.

_Step 4 — Increased K⁺ secretion in the collecting duct._ Na⁺ reabsorption through ENaC creates a lumen-negative electrical gradient (Na⁺ is moving from lumen into cell, taking positive charge with it). This negative gradient drives K⁺ _out_ of the cell into the lumen through apical K⁺ channels (ROMK). The result: increased K⁺ secretion into the urine. Each Na⁺ reabsorbed via ENaC favours one K⁺ secreted via ROMK — the same mechanism by which aldosterone increases K⁺ secretion (aldosterone increases ENaC activity).

_Step 5 — Aldosterone amplification._ Loop diuretics also reduce ECF volume, which activates RAAS. Aldosterone rises, further increasing ENaC activity in the collecting duct, further increasing Na⁺ reabsorption, further increasing K⁺ secretion. The volume contraction and the direct delivery effect are synergistic.

_Step 6 — Net K⁺ loss._ Total K⁺ excreted in the urine rises substantially. Over days to weeks of continuous loop diuretic therapy, total body K⁺ falls, and plasma K⁺ falls into the hypokalaemic range (<3.5 mmol/L). The patient may develop weakness, cramps, and ECG changes (T-wave flattening, U waves, arrhythmias).

_Clinical management._ Three approaches: (1) co-administer a K⁺-sparing diuretic that blocks ENaC (amiloride, triamterene) or aldosterone (spironolactone, eplerenone); (2) supplement K⁺ orally; (3) ensure adequate dietary K⁺. The first approach is the most physiologically targeted because it interrupts the same mechanism that produces the hypokalaemia.

Elaborative explanation: This question illustrates how a drug acting on a single transporter produces effects far beyond the immediate target — through downstream consequences propagating along the nephron. The same logic applies to thiazide diuretics (which inhibit the Na⁺-Cl⁻ cotransporter NCC in the distal convoluted tubule and produce hypokalaemia by similar mechanisms) and to many other drugs whose direct target is upstream of the eventual clinical effect. Recognising the chain — drug target → tubular delivery → distal nephron handling → systemic consequence — is one of the most useful conceptual moves in renal pharmacology.

QUESTION 15

Difficulty (F / I / A): a

Stem: Cells maintain steep concentration differences for Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, and H⁺ between the intracellular and extracellular compartments. Identify the principal transporter responsible for each gradient, and explain what happens to each gradient — and to the cell — when ATP fails (e.g., severe ischaemia or hypoxia).

Model answer: Each major ionic gradient is maintained by a specific transporter, and all of them depend ultimately on ATP [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1].

_Na⁺ and K⁺._ The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase (P-type, primary active) maintains low intracellular Na⁺ (~14 mmol/L vs ECF ~140 mmol/L) and high intracellular K⁺ (~120 mmol/L vs ECF ~4 mmol/L) by exporting 3 Na⁺ for 2 K⁺ per ATP. The pump is the dominant maintenance for both gradients.

_Ca²⁺._ Three transporters maintain low intracellular Ca²⁺ (~10⁻⁷ mol/L vs ECF ~10⁻³ mol/L — a four-orders-of-magnitude gradient). (i) The plasma-membrane Ca²⁺-ATPase (PMCA) extrudes Ca²⁺ from the cytoplasm using ATP directly (primary active). (ii) The sarco-/endoplasmic reticulum Ca²⁺-ATPase (SERCA) pumps Ca²⁺ from cytoplasm into the ER/SR lumen, also using ATP directly. (iii) The Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX) extrudes Ca²⁺ across the plasma membrane in exchange for 3 Na⁺ entering — secondary active transport, dependent on the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase to maintain the inward Na⁺ gradient.

_H⁺._ The Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger (NHE-1) extrudes H⁺ from the cytoplasm in exchange for Na⁺ entering — secondary active transport. Specialised cells use additional H⁺ pumps: gastric parietal cells use H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (primary active) to secrete H⁺ into the stomach lumen; lysosomes and renal intercalated cells use V-type H⁺-ATPases. All of these depend on ATP, either directly or via the Na⁺ gradient.

_What happens when ATP fails._ The cascade is rapid and broad.

(1) _Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase ceases._ Within minutes of ATP depletion, intracellular Na⁺ rises and K⁺ falls. The K⁺ gradient collapses; the resting membrane potential becomes less negative, then is lost. Excitability fails.

(2) _Na⁺ gradient collapses, taking down secondary active transport._ The inward Na⁺ gradient that powers SGLT, NHE, NCX, and Na⁺-amino acid symporters disappears. Secondary active transport stops. Glucose uptake fails. pH regulation via NHE-1 fails.

(3) _Ca²⁺ overload._ Without a Na⁺ gradient, NCX cannot extrude Ca²⁺. Without ATP, PMCA and SERCA cannot pump Ca²⁺ either. Worse, NCX can run in _reverse_ (Na⁺ out, Ca²⁺ in) under conditions of high intracellular Na⁺ and depolarised membrane potential. Cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ rises explosively — orders of magnitude.

(4) _Ca²⁺-activated damage._ High cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ activates phospholipases (which damage membranes), proteases (which damage cytoskeleton and other proteins), and endonucleases (which fragment DNA). Mitochondrial Ca²⁺ overload triggers mitochondrial permeability transition, releasing cytochrome c and committing the cell to apoptosis or necrosis.

(5) _Cell volume failure._ Without Na⁺ extrusion, intracellular Na⁺ rises and water follows osmotically — cells swell. Extreme swelling produces membrane disruption (necrotic cell death).

(6) _pH falls._ Anaerobic metabolism increases lactate production; without functional H⁺ extrusion (NHE-1), cytoplasmic pH falls (intracellular acidosis), further impairing enzyme function and metabolic recovery.

_The unifying lesson._ All major ionic gradients in the cell depend on ATP — either directly (primary active pumps) or indirectly (secondary active transporters that use the Na⁺ gradient). When ATP fails, _every_ gradient fails, and the cell follows a stereotyped cascade toward injury or death. This is the molecular basis of ischaemic cellular damage and the common pathway by which oxygen deprivation kills cells in stroke, myocardial infarction, and many other clinical settings.

Elaborative explanation: The architecture explains why even brief ischaemia produces broad cellular dysfunction: the entire transport-dependent system collapses in parallel, not one gradient at a time. It also explains why therapeutic interventions in ischaemia (early reperfusion, oxygen restoration, cooling to slow metabolism) work by restoring or sparing ATP, which then restores the pumps, which then re-establishes all the downstream gradients. There is no separate intervention for each transporter — restoring ATP restores the whole system.

Hints:

1. Four ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, H⁺) — each maintained by specific transporters. Match each.
2. When ATP fails, primary pumps stop _first_ — within minutes. Secondary active transporters fail next, because the Na⁺ gradient collapses.
3. What happens to intracellular Ca²⁺ when both PMCA, SERCA, and NCX all fail simultaneously? What does that Ca²⁺ activate?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase as the maintainer of Na⁺ and K⁺ gradients.
- Names at least two of the three Ca²⁺ pumps/exchangers (PMCA, SERCA, NCX).
- Names the Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger (NHE-1) as the main maintainer of cytosolic pH.
- Traces the failure cascade: pump failure → gradient collapse → secondary active transport failure → Ca²⁺ overload → enzyme activation and cellular damage.
- States the unifying lesson: all gradients ultimately depend on ATP, so ATP failure produces parallel collapse.

A Yellow answer covers two of the four gradients well but is incomplete on one or two, or does not articulate the failure cascade in mechanistic detail.

A Red answer treats the gradients as independent rather than ATP-dependent, or claims that secondary active transport continues without ATP, or omits the role of Ca²⁺ overload in cellular injury.
