## Pass 1 — Cortex, hemispheric specialisation, language, memory, reward

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The cerebral cortex is organised into approximately 52 cytoarchitectural regions (Brodmann areas, 1909), with broad functional divisions:

Correct answer: Frontal (executive function, motor control, personality), parietal (somatosensory, spatial awareness), temporal (auditory, memory, language comprehension), occipital (visual processing), plus limbic and insular cortex; the original Brodmann scheme remains widely used despite refinement by modern functional imaging.

Distractors:

- "Random arrangement." — Reveals misconception: student denies systematic cortical organisation. Correction: cortex is organised both cytoarchitecturally (Brodmann areas) and functionally (sensory, motor, association areas), not randomly.
- "Single uniform structure." — Reveals misconception: student treats cortex as homogeneous. Correction: cortex shows clear regional specialisation with distinct cytoarchitecture, connectivity, and function.
- "Duplicate of subcortical structures." — Reveals misconception: student denies cortical specificity. Correction: cortex provides higher-order functions (language, executive, perception) not duplicated by subcortical structures.

Explanation: Brodmann's 1909 cytoarchitectural map (52 areas) remains widely used [Berne & Levy ch.17; Boron & Boulpaep ch.16]. Frontal lobe contains M1 (area 4), premotor and SMA (area 6), frontal eye field (area 8), DLPFC (areas 9, 10, 46 — executive, working memory), orbitofrontal (areas 11, 47 — decision-making, social behaviour), anterior cingulate (areas 24, 32 — emotional regulation, attention), and Broca's area (areas 44, 45 — speech production, dominant). Parietal lobe contains S1 (areas 1, 2, 3), somatosensory association (area 5), parietal association (area 7 — spatial awareness), angular gyrus (area 39 — reading, writing), and supramarginal gyrus (area 40 — language). Temporal lobe contains A1 (areas 41, 42 — Heschl's gyrus), Wernicke's area (area 22 — language comprehension, dominant), visual association cortex (areas 20, 21 — face/object recognition), and entorhinal cortex (areas 28, 35 — memory). Occipital lobe contains V1 (area 17) and visual association V2/V3/V4 (areas 18, 19).

Hints:

1. The classical mapping was published in 1909.
2. There are roughly 52 regions defined by cytoarchitecture.
3. Each lobe has a primary sensory or motor area plus association cortex.

Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F

Stem: In right-handed individuals, the LEFT (dominant) hemisphere is typically responsible for:

Correct answer: Language (in ~95% of right-handers and ~70% of left-handers, supported by Broca's, Wernicke's, arcuate fasciculus, and angular gyrus); analytical, sequential, and logical processing; mathematical processing; and motor control of the right hand and somatosensation from the right hemibody.

Distractors:

- "Spatial cognition only." — Reveals misconception: student picks a non-dominant function. Correction: spatial cognition, mental rotation, and visuospatial attention are typically _non-dominant_ (right) hemisphere functions; language and analytical processing are dominant.
- "Spatial relationships only." — Reveals misconception: student picks the same non-dominant function. Correction: spatial relationships are primarily right-hemisphere; left dominance is for language and sequential analysis.
- "Music perception only." — Reveals misconception: student picks a right-hemisphere function. Correction: music processing (melody, prosody) is more right-hemispheric; language is left-dominant.

Explanation: The dominant (typically left) hemisphere specialises in language (Broca's area, Wernicke's area, arcuate fasciculus, angular gyrus), analytical/sequential cognition, mathematical processing, detailed pattern recognition, and reading and writing [Berne & Levy ch.17; Ganong]. The non-dominant (typically right) hemisphere specialises in spatial relationships, mental rotation, navigation, face recognition (FFA — often more right-dominant), music processing (melody, prosody), emotional valence detection in speech, visuospatial attention (lesions cause hemispatial neglect — typically left-sided neglect from right parietal damage), and holistic pattern recognition. ~95% of right-handers and ~50–70% of left-handers are left-dominant for language. The Wada test (sodium amytal injection into one carotid) is a classical pre-surgical method for testing language laterality. Split-brain studies (Sperry, Gazzaniga — Nobel 1981) in patients with corpus callosum sectioning for refractory epilepsy reveal each hemisphere's separate awareness, with the right hemisphere unable to verbally report what it perceives.

Hints:

1. Language is left-dominant in the great majority of right-handers.
2. Analytical and sequential processing also tend to be left-hemispheric.
3. The right hemisphere handles spatial cognition and prosody.

Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: A patient with a stroke in the dominant posterior superior temporal gyrus (Wernicke's area) shows:

Correct answer: Fluent but meaningless speech ("word salad"), paraphasic errors (substituted words), neologisms, impaired comprehension, and impaired repetition; patients are often _unaware_ of their deficit (anosognosia) and may appear paranoid or agitated, in contrast to Broca's aphasia where patients are aware and frustrated.

Distractors:

- "Non-fluent telegraphic speech with intact comprehension." — Reveals misconception: student gives the _Broca's_ aphasia pattern. Correction: non-fluent telegraphic speech with intact comprehension is Broca's aphasia; Wernicke's is fluent but meaningless with impaired comprehension.
- "Pure word deafness only." — Reveals misconception: student picks a narrow disconnection syndrome. Correction: pure word deafness reflects auditory cortex isolation from Wernicke's; Wernicke's aphasia is broader, affecting comprehension across modalities.
- "Agraphia only." — Reveals misconception: student picks an isolated writing deficit. Correction: agraphia can occur in isolation but Wernicke's aphasia involves _fluent meaningless speech, impaired comprehension, and impaired repetition_ — not just writing.

Explanation: Wernicke's aphasia (receptive aphasia) presents with fluent normal-rate normally-articulated but content-empty speech (paraphasic errors, neologisms, "word salad"), markedly impaired comprehension of spoken and written language, impaired repetition, and impaired reading and writing [Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Ganong]. Patients are often anosognostic — unaware of their deficit, speaking rapidly with conviction; if surrounded by people they cannot understand and who cannot understand them, they may become paranoid. The cause is a lesion in the dominant (usually left) posterior superior temporal gyrus (Brodmann 22) and adjacent inferior parietal lobe — commonly an MCA inferior division stroke. The aphasia spectrum: Broca's (non-fluent, telegraphic, intact comprehension, impaired repetition, often with right hemiparesis); conduction (fluent, intact comprehension, dramatically impaired repetition, paraphasic errors — arcuate fasciculus lesion); global (large MCA stroke affecting both Broca's and Wernicke's); transcortical motor (Broca-like with preserved repetition); transcortical sensory (Wernicke-like with preserved repetition); and anomic (the most common after recovery — naming difficulties).

Hints:

1. Speech is fluent but devoid of meaningful content.
2. Comprehension and repetition are both impaired.
3. Patients are typically _unaware_ of the deficit.

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Memory is broadly classified into:

Correct answer: Declarative (explicit) — episodic (events) and semantic (facts), dependent on hippocampus and medial temporal lobe — and non-declarative (implicit) — procedural (skills, basal ganglia and cerebellum), priming (cortex), conditioning (amygdala for fear; cerebellum for motor), and habituation/sensitisation; working memory is a separate brief-active-storage system in DLPFC.

Distractors:

- "Short-term and long-term only." — Reveals misconception: student uses a temporal-only classification. Correction: while short/long-term is one axis, the principled classification uses declarative vs non-declarative based on systems involved (hippocampus vs basal ganglia/cerebellum/cortex).
- "Single type only." — Reveals misconception: student denies memory subtypes. Correction: memory is multiple distinct systems with separable neural substrates, demonstrated by selective lesions (e.g., HM had spared procedural learning despite anterograde amnesia).
- "Visual and verbal only." — Reveals misconception: student picks a modality-based classification. Correction: visual/verbal distinguishes content; the principal classification distinguishes declarative from non-declarative based on conscious accessibility and neural substrate.

Explanation: Declarative memory is consciously recalled and includes episodic (personal experiences with time and place) and semantic (general facts and knowledge); both depend on hippocampus and medial temporal lobe for encoding, with later distributed cortical storage [Berne & Levy ch.17; Boron & Boulpaep ch.16]. Non-declarative memory is often unconscious and includes procedural (skills, habits — basal ganglia and cerebellum), priming (cortex), conditioning (Pavlovian — amygdala for fear, cerebellum for motor), and habituation/sensitisation (widespread). Working memory (DLPFC) is a separate brief active-storage system. Stages: encoding, consolidation (hippocampus to cortex; sleep-dependent), retrieval, and reconsolidation (memory becomes labile when retrieved). Hippocampus is critical for encoding and contains place cells (O'Keefe and Mosers, Nobel 2014). Memory disorders include Alzheimer's (hippocampal/entorhinal degeneration), Korsakoff syndrome (thiamine deficiency damages mammillary bodies and thalamus → severe anterograde amnesia plus confabulation), and transient global amnesia (brief severe amnesia of unclear aetiology).

Hints:

1. The principled split is declarative versus non-declarative.
2. Declarative depends on hippocampus; procedural depends on basal ganglia and cerebellum.
3. Working memory is separate, in DLPFC.

Stem: The hippocampus and medial temporal lobe are critical for encoding new [blank] memories:

Correct answer: Declarative (episodic and semantic) — bilateral hippocampal damage produces dramatic anterograde amnesia (inability to form new declarative memories) with relative preservation of procedural learning, demonstrated most famously in patient HM (Henry Molaison) after bilateral medial temporal lobectomy for epilepsy.

Distractors:

- "Procedural." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong system. Correction: procedural memory depends on basal ganglia and cerebellum, not hippocampus; HM could learn new motor skills despite hippocampal loss.
- "Working." — Reveals misconception: student picks a different memory system. Correction: working memory depends on DLPFC; the hippocampus is for new declarative memory encoding.
- "Implicit." — Reveals misconception: student picks the umbrella term for non-declarative. Correction: implicit (non-declarative) memory does not require hippocampus; declarative memory does.

Explanation: The hippocampus is essential for encoding new declarative memories [Boron & Boulpaep ch.16]. Bilateral damage produces dramatic anterograde amnesia, demonstrated by patient HM (Henry Molaison, 1953–2008, the most studied patient in cognitive neuroscience), who had bilateral medial temporal lobectomy for refractory epilepsy. HM lost the ability to form new declarative memories but could learn new motor skills (procedural memory dependent on basal ganglia and cerebellum, both spared). This dissociation established memory as a multi-system construct rather than a single function. Retrograde amnesia for recent events but preservation of older memories is consistent with a consolidation gradient (older memories transferred to cortex over time, no longer requiring hippocampus for retrieval). Hippocampal place cells (O'Keefe and Mosers, Nobel 2014) and the cognitive map provide a neuroanatomical substrate for spatial memory and navigation.

Hints:

1. The system is declarative, not procedural.
2. Bilateral lesions produce anterograde amnesia.
3. Patient HM is the iconic case demonstrating this.

Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Long-term potentiation (LTP) — the leading cellular model of learning — involves:

Correct answer: Activity-dependent strengthening of synapses through NMDA receptor activation (relieved Mg²⁺ block when postsynaptic membrane is depolarised), Ca²⁺ entry, CaMKII activation, AMPA receptor phosphorylation and insertion, and structural spine changes; first described in hippocampus (Bliss and Lomo 1973), with cooperativity, associativity, specificity, and long duration as defining properties.

Distractors:

- "Mass cell death." — Reveals misconception: student picks neurodegeneration. Correction: LTP is _strengthening_ of surviving synapses, not loss of cells; cell death weakens networks rather than potentiating them.
- "Random firing patterns." — Reveals misconception: student denies activity-dependence. Correction: LTP is highly specific to active synapses (Hebb's rule "cells that fire together wire together"); random firing does not produce LTP.
- "Pure inhibition." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong direction. Correction: LTP is potentiation (excitatory strengthening); long-term depression (LTD) is the opposite phenomenon.

Explanation: LTP is a persistent (hours to days) increase in synaptic strength after high-frequency stimulation, first described in hippocampus by Bliss and Lomo (1973) [Berne & Levy ch.17; Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Ganong]. Mechanism: strong stimulation → glutamate release → AMPA activation → depolarisation → relieves Mg²⁺ block of NMDA → Ca²⁺ entry → CaMKII activation → AMPA receptor phosphorylation and insertion → structural spine changes → late-phase LTP requires protein synthesis (CREB → gene transcription → growth factors). Properties (Hebbian): cooperativity (multiple fibres together produce LTP more reliably), associativity (weak input pairs with strong → both potentiated), specificity (only the active synapse), and long duration. LTD is the opposite — low-frequency stimulation produces persistent weakening via phosphatase activation (PP1, calcineurin) and AMPA receptor removal. Disease relevance: Alzheimer's (β-amyloid disrupts LTP), schizophrenia (NMDA hypofunction), depression (reduced hippocampal LTP, restored by antidepressants), and addiction (LTP-like changes in reward circuits).

Hints:

1. The mechanism requires both presynaptic glutamate release and postsynaptic depolarisation.
2. The receptor that opens calcium entry is NMDA.
3. CaMKII phosphorylation and AMPA insertion are downstream key steps.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Working memory — the brief active storage during ongoing cognition — depends most prominently on:

Correct answer: The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC, Brodmann areas 9 and 46) — single-unit recordings show sustained neuronal activity during the delay period of working memory tasks; dopamine D1 receptors are critical with an "inverted U" relationship (too little or too much D1 stimulation impairs function); impaired in ADHD, schizophrenia, frontal lobe lesions, and Alzheimer's.

Distractors:

- "Hippocampus." — Reveals misconception: student picks the declarative memory hub. Correction: hippocampus encodes new declarative memories; working memory is held actively in DLPFC.
- "Cerebellum." — Reveals misconception: student picks a coordination structure. Correction: while the cerebellum has some cognitive roles, the primary working memory area is DLPFC.
- "Brainstem." — Reveals misconception: student picks an arousal structure. Correction: the brainstem houses the reticular activating system maintaining wakefulness, but the active maintenance of information in mind is a DLPFC function.

Explanation: Working memory is the "mental workspace" used to hold information for current cognition (e.g., a phone number while dialling, mental arithmetic, multi-step instructions) [Berne & Levy ch.17; Ganong]. DLPFC neurons (Brodmann 9, 46) show persistent activity during the delay between stimulus and response in working memory tasks, reflecting active maintenance. Dopamine D1 receptors are critical, with an inverted U dose–response — both too little and too much D1 stimulation impair function. Baddeley's model: phonological loop (auditory-verbal), visuospatial sketchpad (visual-spatial), episodic buffer (multimodal integration), and central executive (attention allocation, supervision). Disorders include ADHD (DLPFC + dopamine dysfunction), schizophrenia (DLPFC hypofunction; cognitive symptoms), frontal lobe lesions (cognitive sequencing difficulty), and Alzheimer's. Tests include digit span (forward/backward) and n-back tasks. Pharmacological enhancement: stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamines — DA/NE in DLPFC; ADHD), caffeine (modest, via adenosine antagonism), and modafinil (wakefulness promoter).

Hints:

1. The relevant cortex sits in the prefrontal area.
2. Sustained neural firing during the delay period is the cellular signature.
3. Dopamine D1 modulation underpins the inverted-U relationship.

Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: The fusiform face area (FFA) — specialised for face recognition — is located in:

Correct answer: The ventral temporal lobe (fusiform gyrus); often more right-dominant; lesions cause prosopagnosia (inability to recognise faces despite intact vision and language); congenital/developmental forms affect ~2% of the population, and Capgras syndrome (familiar faces seen as impostors) reflects FFA-limbic disconnection.

Distractors:

- "Frontal lobe." — Reveals misconception: student misplaces FFA. Correction: FFA sits in the _ventral temporal_ lobe, not frontal; frontal lobe handles executive function and motor planning.
- "Parietal lobe." — Reveals misconception: student picks a sensory association cortex. Correction: parietal cortex handles spatial attention and integration; face recognition lives in the fusiform gyrus of ventral temporal cortex.
- "Cerebellum." — Reveals misconception: student picks a motor coordination structure. Correction: face recognition is a high-order visual function in cortex, not a cerebellar function.

Explanation: The FFA sits in the fusiform gyrus on ventral temporal cortex and is highly responsive to faces; adjacent areas specialise for objects, scenes, and body parts, together forming the ventral visual stream ("what" pathway) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Ganong]. Prosopagnosia (face blindness) can be acquired (right or bilateral ventral temporal damage from stroke, encephalitis, trauma) or developmental/congenital (without identifiable lesion, ~2% of population). Patients can describe faces in detail but cannot identify them as known individuals — even close family or themselves in a mirror — and often compensate with non-face cues (voice, hair, clothing, gait). Capgras syndrome (familiar people seen as impostors) reflects FFA-limbic disconnection — recognition is preserved but the emotional response to recognition is missing. Fregoli syndrome (strangers seen as familiar disguised people) is the converse. Other specialised neural areas include the parahippocampal place area (PPA — scene recognition), the extrastriate body area (EBA), and the visual word form area (VWFA — reading).

Hints:

1. The cortex is on the ventral surface of the temporal lobe.
2. Lesions cause inability to recognise faces (prosopagnosia).
3. Capgras syndrome is a recognition–emotion disconnection variant.

QUESTION 9
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The limbic system structure most critical for fear conditioning is:

Correct answer: The amygdala — almond-shaped structure in the anterior temporal lobe that links sensory input to emotional valence; lesions impair fear learning and emotional memory; bilateral amygdala damage (Klüver–Bucy syndrome with adjacent temporal lobe involvement) produces hyperorality, hypersexuality, and placidity; targeted pharmacologically by anxiolytics (benzodiazepines, SSRIs, β-blockers).

Distractors:

- "Hippocampus." — Reveals misconception: student picks a memory structure. Correction: hippocampus is critical for declarative memory encoding; fear conditioning is principally amygdala.
- "Hypothalamus alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks the autonomic effector. Correction: hypothalamus mediates downstream autonomic responses to fear, but the _learning_ of fear associations is amygdala.
- "Cerebellum." — Reveals misconception: student picks a coordination structure. Correction: the cerebellum participates in some forms of motor conditioning (eye-blink) but is not the primary fear conditioning structure; that is amygdala.

Explanation: The amygdala is critical for fear conditioning (Pavlovian association of a neutral stimulus with an aversive outcome), emotional memory tagging, threat detection (rapid pre-conscious), and social-emotional processing (recognising fear/anger in faces) [Berne & Levy ch.17; Boron & Boulpaep ch.16]. Pathway: sensory thalamus → amygdala (fast "low road" bypassing cortex for rapid response); sensory cortex → amygdala (slower "high road" providing context); amygdala → hypothalamus (autonomic), brainstem (motor freezing), prefrontal cortex (conscious awareness). Lesion studies: bilateral amygdala damage produces loss of fear response and difficulty recognising fear in others; Klüver–Bucy syndrome (with temporal lobe involvement) adds hyperorality, hypersexuality, and placidity; Urbach–Wiethe disease (bilateral amygdala calcification) produces individuals who don't experience fear in dangerous situations. Disorders with amygdala dysfunction: PTSD (hyperactivity, impaired fear extinction — trauma-focused CBT, EMDR, prazosin for nightmares, SSRIs); anxiety disorders (hyperactivity — SSRIs, SNRIs, benzodiazepines acutely, CBT); phobias (specific learned fears — exposure therapy works by extinction).

Hints:

1. The structure is in the anterior temporal lobe.
2. Its principal role is associating stimuli with emotional valence.
3. Klüver–Bucy syndrome includes its bilateral destruction.

QUESTION 10
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The brain's reward system is anchored by ascending dopaminergic projections from:

Correct answer: The ventral tegmental area (VTA, midbrain) to the nucleus accumbens (ventral striatum) and prefrontal cortex — the mesolimbic and mesocortical pathways; dopamine release encodes reward prediction error (Schultz model); activated by drugs of abuse (cocaine, amphetamines, opioids, nicotine, alcohol, cannabis), food, sex, and social interactions; dysregulated in addiction.

Distractors:

- "Substantia nigra to motor cortex." — Reveals misconception: student picks the nigrostriatal motor pathway. Correction: SNc → dorsal striatum is the _motor_ dopaminergic pathway lost in Parkinson's; reward is mesolimbic VTA → nucleus accumbens.
- "Locus coeruleus." — Reveals misconception: student picks a noradrenergic nucleus. Correction: LC is the major noradrenergic nucleus (arousal, attention); reward dopamine arises from VTA.
- "Raphe nuclei." — Reveals misconception: student picks the serotonergic source. Correction: raphe nuclei provide serotonergic projections (mood, sleep); reward dopamine arises from VTA.

Explanation: The reward system has four major dopaminergic pathways [Berne & Levy ch.17; Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Ganong]. Mesolimbic: VTA → nucleus accumbens (motivation, reward, addiction). Mesocortical: VTA → prefrontal cortex (cognitive control, executive function). Nigrostriatal: SNc → dorsal striatum (motor function, lost in Parkinson's). Tuberoinfundibular: hypothalamus → median eminence (inhibits prolactin). Reward prediction error (Schultz): dopamine neurons fire to _unexpected_ reward (positive), reduce firing when expected reward fails (negative), and fire to cues that predict reward after learning — a computational signal driving association learning. All major addictive drugs increase nucleus accumbens dopamine: cocaine blocks DAT, amphetamines reverse DAT, opioids disinhibit VTA, nicotine activates nicotinic receptors on DA neurons, alcohol increases VTA firing, cannabis CB1 disinhibits VTA. Repeated exposure produces plasticity → addiction. Disorders: substance use disorder, depression (anhedonia), Parkinson's (motor + behavioural), schizophrenia (mesolimbic hyperactivity → positive symptoms; mesocortical hypofunction → negative/cognitive symptoms). Treatments exploiting reward biology: naltrexone (μ antagonist, alcohol/opioids), acamprosate (alcohol), varenicline (smoking — partial nicotinic agonist), methadone/buprenorphine (opioid replacement), aripiprazole (D2 partial agonist).

Hints:

1. The midbrain nucleus that drives reward is the VTA.
2. Its target in the ventral striatum is the nucleus accumbens.
3. All major addictive drugs converge on this pathway.

QUESTION 11
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Normal adult sleep cycles through stages of:

Correct answer: NREM (3 stages, formerly 4) and REM, alternating in ~90-minute cycles, with 4–6 cycles in a 7–9 hour sleep; NREM dominates the first half of the night (especially deep N3) and REM dominates the second half; total sleep is ~75% NREM and ~25% REM in adults.

Distractors:

- "Single uniform stage." — Reveals misconception: student denies sleep architecture. Correction: sleep has multiple stages with distinct EEG, autonomic, and physiological features cycling throughout the night.
- "Continuous REM only." — Reveals misconception: student narrows sleep to REM. Correction: REM is only ~25% of total sleep; NREM dominates, especially early in the night.
- "Continuous wakefulness." — Reveals misconception: student denies sleep entirely. Correction: sleep is a regulated state with characteristic EEG and physiological signatures.

Explanation: NREM stages: N1 (theta waves, ~5%; light sleep), N2 (sleep spindles and K-complexes, ~50%; most of total sleep), and N3 (delta waves >25% of epoch, ~15–20%; deep slow-wave sleep with high arousal threshold, growth hormone release, memory consolidation, and glymphatic clearance) [Guyton & Hall ch.61; Berne & Levy ch.17]. REM (~20–25%) shows EEG resembling wakefulness, rapid eye movements, atonia of skeletal muscle (except diaphragm and eye muscles), vivid dreams, increased autonomic variability, penile/clitoral tumescence, and cerebral metabolism similar to wakefulness. Cycles are ~90 minutes; first cycles have short REM and long N3, later cycles longer REM and less N3. Two-process regulation: process S (homeostatic — adenosine accumulation; caffeine antagonises A1/A2A) and process C (circadian — SCN-driven, melatonin from pineal in darkness). Neural circuits: wakefulness (RAS, locus coeruleus NA, tuberomammillary histamine, basal forebrain ACh); NREM (VLPO GABA inhibits arousal nuclei); REM (pontine reticular formation cholinergic with aminergic suppression). Functions: memory consolidation, glymphatic clearance, synaptic homeostasis, immune function, hormonal regulation. Sleep disorders: insomnia, OSA, narcolepsy, REM sleep behaviour disorder, restless legs syndrome.

Hints:

1. Sleep is divided into NREM and REM stages.
2. The cycle period is ~90 minutes.
3. Deep slow-wave sleep is concentrated in the first half of the night.

QUESTION 12
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: EEG patterns during waking and sleep are characterised by:

Correct answer: Awake/alert — low-voltage fast (mostly beta 13–30 Hz, gamma 30+ Hz; alpha 8–13 Hz over occipital with eyes closed); drowsy — alpha; N1 — theta 4–7 Hz; N2 — theta plus sleep spindles and K-complexes; N3 — high-amplitude slow delta <4 Hz; REM — low-voltage fast (paradoxically resembling waking despite atonia).

Distractors:

- "Identical waveforms at all times." — Reveals misconception: student denies state-dependent EEG. Correction: EEG shows characteristic frequency content for each state, used to stage sleep and detect pathology.
- "Pure delta always." — Reveals misconception: student narrows EEG to one band. Correction: delta dominates only N3; awake EEG has beta/alpha/gamma; REM resembles awake.
- "Pure beta always." — Reveals misconception: student narrows EEG to one band in the wrong direction. Correction: beta dominates active concentration and anxiety; sleep stages have other frequencies.

Explanation: EEG frequency bands and clinical correlates [Guyton & Hall ch.61; Berne & Levy ch.17]: gamma (30+ Hz — higher cognitive functions, attention), beta (13–30 Hz — active concentration, problem-solving, anxiety), alpha (8–13 Hz — relaxed wakefulness with eyes closed, prominent over occipital, blocked by eye opening), theta (4–7 Hz — drowsy, light sleep), delta (0.5–4 Hz — deep sleep N3; pathological in awake adults — head injury, encephalopathy). Special events: sleep spindles (12–14 Hz bursts in N2 — thalamocortical oscillations; memory consolidation), K-complexes (sharp negative wave + slow positive wave; N2; triggered by sensory stimuli), sawtooth waves (REM). Pathological patterns: epileptic discharges (spikes, sharp waves, generalised spike-wave 3 Hz in absence epilepsy), burst suppression (severe encephalopathy, anaesthesia, hypothermia), triphasic waves (hepatic encephalopathy, uraemia), PLEDs (focal lesion — herpes encephalitis, stroke), FIRDA (diffuse encephalopathy), electrocerebral silence (brain death, with confirmatory criteria). Standard 10-20 system uses 19–21 electrodes. Used for epilepsy, encephalopathy, sleep studies (with EOG/EMG = polysomnography), and brain death confirmation.

Hints:

1. Different states of consciousness produce different dominant frequencies.
2. Slow waves (delta) dominate the deepest sleep; fast waves dominate alert wakefulness.
3. REM EEG paradoxically resembles waking EEG.

Stem: Narcolepsy is best characterised by:

Symptom clusters: intrusion (involuntary memories, flashbacks, nightmares, intense distress at reminders, physiological reactivity), avoidance (of trauma-related thoughts, feelings, situations), negative alterations in cognition/mood (inability to recall key features, persistent negative beliefs, distorted blame, persistent negative emotional state, anhedonia, detachment, inability to experience positive emotions), and arousal/reactivity (irritability, reckless behaviour, hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, concentration problems, sleep disturbance). Pathophysiology: amygdala hyperactivity, prefrontal hypofunction, hippocampal volume reduction (predisposing or consequent), impaired fear extinction, and HPA axis dysregulation. Treatment: trauma-focused CBT (cognitive processing therapy, prolonged exposure) and EMDR are gold-standard psychotherapies; SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine — FDA approved) and venlafaxine for symptoms; prazosin (α1 antagonist) for nightmares (though recent trials mixed); avoid benzodiazepines (worsen extinction). Group therapy and peer support also help. Comorbid depression, substance use, and chronic pain are common.

Hints:

1. Symptoms cluster as intrusion + avoidance + hyperarousal + negative cognitions/mood.
2. Trauma-focused CBT and EMDR are first-line psychotherapies.
3. Prazosin is used for nightmares.

Stem: Eating disorders (anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge-eating disorder) share which features?

- "Pure metabolic disorders requiring only nutritional therapy." — Reveals misconception: student dismisses the psychological and behavioural components. Correction: eating disorders are biopsychosocial; nutrition alone is insufficient — psychological therapy is essential.

2. Anorexia has low weight; bulimia/BED may have normal or high weight.

Stem: Substance use disorder reflects:

Correct answer: A chronic, relapsing dysregulation of the mesolimbic reward circuit (VTA → nucleus accumbens) with tolerance, withdrawal, craving, and loss of behavioural control over substance use; treatment depends on the drug — opioids (methadone, buprenorphine, naltrexone), alcohol (naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram), nicotine (varenicline, NRT, bupropion), plus behavioural therapy (motivational interviewing, CBT, contingency management) and peer support (AA, NA).

Distractors:

- "Pure character weakness without biological basis." — Reveals misconception: student dismisses neurobiology. Correction: addiction has well-defined neurobiology in the reward circuit; treatment combines biological and behavioural approaches.
- "Acute self-limiting condition." — Reveals misconception: student picks an acute model. Correction: addiction is chronic and relapsing, with persistent vulnerability long after acute withdrawal; treatment is ongoing.
- "Only treatable with abstinence; no medication has a role." — Reveals misconception: student dismisses pharmacotherapy. Correction: medication-assisted treatment (methadone/buprenorphine for opioids, naltrexone/acamprosate for alcohol, varenicline/NRT/bupropion for nicotine) substantially improves outcomes.

Explanation: Substance use disorder is a chronic relapsing condition characterised by tolerance, withdrawal, craving, and loss of control over substance use, with continued use despite harm [Berne & Levy ch.17; Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Ganong]. DSM-5 uses 11 criteria, with severity (mild 2–3, moderate 4–5, severe 6+). Behavioural therapy: motivational interviewing (engagement), CBT, contingency management, 12-step facilitation; peer support (AA, NA, SMART Recovery). Harm reduction (needle exchange, supervised consumption, take-home naloxone) reduces deaths and disease transmission. Withdrawal management is substance-specific and may be life-threatening (alcohol — delirium tremens; benzodiazepines — seizures).

Hints:

1. The disease is chronic and relapsing, with neurobiology in the reward circuit.
2. Medication-assisted treatment is highly effective for opioids, alcohol, and nicotine.
3. Behavioural therapy and peer support complement pharmacotherapy.

Stem: Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is best characterised by:

Correct answer: Persistent deficits in social communication and interaction (impaired social-emotional reciprocity, deficits in non-verbal communication, difficulty developing/maintaining relationships) plus restricted, repetitive behaviours (stereotyped movements, insistence on sameness, restricted interests, sensory abnormalities) — onset in early developmental period; pathophysiology involves altered brain connectivity with strong genetic component; multidisciplinary support emphasises early behavioural intervention.

Pharmacotherapy is symptomatic — risperidone or aripiprazole for irritability; SSRIs for anxiety/depression; stimulants for ADHD; melatonin for sleep. Adults with ASD benefit from vocational support and accommodations.

Hints:

1. Two core symptom domains: social communication deficits and restricted/repetitive behaviours.
2. Onset is in early childhood.
3. Vaccines do not cause autism.

Stem: Circadian rhythm disorders include:

Correct answer: Delayed sleep phase disorder (sleep onset and wake later than desired — common in adolescents), advanced sleep phase disorder (sleep onset and wake earlier — common in elderly), shift work sleep disorder, jet lag, and non-24-hour sleep–wake disorder (more common in totally blind individuals); managed with timed light exposure, melatonin, and behavioural strategies plus, where appropriate, scheduled sleep adjustment.

Distractors:

- "Always require pharmacological treatment." — Reveals misconception: student over-medicalises. Correction: many circadian disorders respond to behavioural and light interventions; pharmacology (melatonin, ramelteon) is adjunctive.
- "Indistinguishable from primary insomnia." — Reveals misconception: student conflates with insomnia. Correction: circadian disorders are timing problems (the patient sleeps well but at the wrong time); insomnia is difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep at the desired time.
- "Pure paediatric problem." — Reveals misconception: student narrows to children. Correction: circadian disorders affect all ages; shift work sleep disorder and jet lag are common in adults.

Explanation: Circadian rhythm sleep–wake disorders reflect misalignment between the endogenous circadian clock (suprachiasmatic nucleus) and the desired or required sleep–wake schedule [Guyton & Hall ch.61; Ganong]. Delayed sleep phase disorder: characteristic late sleep onset (after midnight) and late wake; common in adolescents (developmental delay of circadian phase); managed with morning bright light, evening melatonin (low dose 0.3–0.5 mg, several hours before desired sleep onset), and gradual phase advance. Advanced sleep phase disorder: early sleep onset and very early waking; more common in elderly; managed with evening bright light. Shift work sleep disorder: misalignment from rotating or night shifts; managed with strategic light, scheduled napping, careful caffeine timing, and modafinil/armodafinil where indicated for excessive sleepiness. Jet lag: travel across time zones (eastward worse than westward); managed with light exposure on arrival, melatonin, and gradual schedule adjustment. Non-24-hour sleep–wake disorder: free-running circadian rhythm slightly longer than 24 hours, more common in totally blind people lacking light entrainment; treated with tasimelteon (MT1/MT2 melatonin receptor agonist). Daily light exposure, especially morning light, is a powerful zeitgeber. Caffeine and screens (blue light) before sleep delay phase.

Hints:

1. The patient sleeps well but at the wrong time of day.
2. Light is the dominant zeitgeber.
3. Melatonin and timed light exposure are the cornerstones of therapy.

Stem: Brain death is defined as:

Correct answer: Total irreversible cessation of cortical and brainstem function — assessed by strict bedside criteria (deep coma with known cause, no confounding factors, absent brainstem reflexes — pupil, corneal, oculovestibular, gag, cough, no motor response to noxious stimuli — and a positive apnoea test); confirmatory tests (EEG, cerebral blood flow studies, evoked potentials) used in some jurisdictions; legally equivalent to death and a prerequisite for deceased organ donation.

Distractors:

- "Persistent vegetative state with eye opening." — Reveals misconception: student picks PVS. Correction: PVS retains brainstem function (sleep–wake cycles, eye opening); brain death has _total_ loss of brainstem function and is irreversible.
- "Coma with normal brainstem reflexes." — Reveals misconception: student denies the brainstem criterion. Correction: brain death requires _absent_ brainstem reflexes; coma with intact reflexes is not brain death.
- "Severe dementia." — Reveals misconception: student conflates cognitive decline with brain death. Correction: dementia is gradual loss of cognitive function with intact brainstem; brain death is sudden total loss of all brain function.

Explanation: Brain death is the legal and clinical equivalent of death in most jurisdictions, defined as irreversible cessation of all functions of the entire brain, including the brainstem [Berne & Levy ch.17; Ganong]. Determination requires (1) coma with known irreversible cause (e.g., severe traumatic brain injury, large intracranial haemorrhage, prolonged anoxia); (2) exclusion of confounders (drug intoxication, hypothermia <36 °C, severe metabolic/electrolyte disturbance, neuromuscular blockade); (3) bedside cranial nerve testing showing absent brainstem reflexes — pupillary light reflex (CN II/III), corneal reflex (CN V/VII), oculocephalic ("doll's eye") and oculovestibular (cold caloric — CN III/VI/VIII), gag and cough (CN IX/X), facial movement to noxious stimuli (CN V/VII), no motor response other than spinal reflexes; (4) apnoea test — patient pre-oxygenated, ventilator disconnected, PaCO₂ allowed to rise to >60 mm Hg or 20 mm Hg above baseline; absence of respiratory effort confirms apnoea. Confirmatory tests (EEG showing electrocerebral silence, cerebral blood flow studies, evoked potentials) are required in some jurisdictions and useful when bedside testing is not possible (e.g., severe facial trauma). The clinical examination is typically repeated after a defined interval. Brain death determination has medico-legal implications including organ donation, withdrawal of mechanical ventilation, and time-of-death certification.

Hints:

1. The diagnosis requires absence of all brainstem reflexes plus apnoea.
2. Confounders (drugs, hypothermia, severe metabolic) must be excluded.
3. Brain death is legally equivalent to death.

QUESTION 32
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: Disorders of consciousness include coma, persistent vegetative state, minimally conscious state, and locked-in syndrome. They differ in that:

Correct answer: Coma is unarousable unresponsiveness with eyes closed and no sleep–wake cycles; persistent vegetative state has preserved sleep–wake cycles and eye opening but no awareness; minimally conscious state has fluctuating but reproducible signs of awareness; locked-in syndrome has full awareness with quadriplegia from a ventral pontine lesion (sparing the reticular activating system and vertical eye movements/blinking).

Distractors:

- "All four are clinically indistinguishable." — Reveals misconception: student denies meaningful distinctions. Correction: the four states are clinically distinct, with very different prognoses and management implications; misdiagnosis is common but consequential.
- "Locked-in syndrome means total brain failure." — Reveals misconception: student conflates locked-in with brain death. Correction: locked-in syndrome features intact cortical function; the patient is fully aware but unable to move except for vertical eye movements and blinking.
- "Persistent vegetative state has preserved awareness." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong state. Correction: PVS lacks awareness despite preserved arousal (sleep–wake cycles, eye opening); awareness is the feature distinguishing minimally conscious state from PVS.

Explanation: Disorders of consciousness reflect the dissociation between _arousal_ (brainstem reticular activating system) and _awareness_ (cortical and thalamocortical networks) [Berne & Levy ch.17; Ganong]. Coma: unarousable unresponsiveness with eyes closed and no sleep–wake cycles, typically from severe brainstem lesions or large bilateral cortical damage; usually evolves within 2–4 weeks (recovery, vegetative state, or death). Persistent vegetative state (now "unresponsive wakefulness syndrome"): preserved arousal (sleep–wake cycles, eye opening, brainstem reflexes) without awareness or purposeful interaction; cortical function lost but brainstem preserved; persistent if >1 month. Minimally conscious state: fluctuating but reproducible signs of awareness (visual tracking, simple command-following, intelligible verbalisation, purposeful movement) — distinguishing from PVS is clinically important but difficult; emergence is signalled by reliable communication or functional object use. Locked-in syndrome: full awareness with quadriplegia and bulbar paralysis from a ventral pontine lesion (typically basilar artery occlusion) sparing the reticular activating system and vertical eye movements/blinking — patients can communicate via eye movements (Jean-Dominique Bauby's "The Diving Bell and the Butterfly"). Brain death is total loss of cortical and brainstem function. Distinguishing these states matters for prognosis, communication, and treatment decisions; functional MRI and EEG paradigms can sometimes detect awareness in apparently unresponsive patients ("covert consciousness").

Hints:

1. The key distinction is between arousal (brainstem) and awareness (cortex).
2. Locked-in syndrome retains awareness despite paralysis.
3. Brain death is total loss of both arousal and awareness.
