QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F

Stem: Describe the cellular and protein components of blood and their major functions.

Model answer: Blood is a complex tissue with multiple cellular and protein components serving diverse roles.

Cellular components (~45% of blood volume):

- Erythrocytes (RBCs) ~5 × 10¹²/L — anucleate biconcave discs containing haemoglobin; carry O₂ and CO₂; lifespan ~120 days.
- Leukocytes (WBCs) ~4–10 × 10⁹/L — neutrophils (50–70%; bacterial defence), lymphocytes (20–40%; adaptive immunity), monocytes (2–10%; → tissue macrophages), eosinophils (1–4%; parasites/allergy), basophils (<1%; allergy/mast cell).
- Platelets ~150–400 × 10⁹/L — anucleate fragments from megakaryocytes; primary haemostasis; lifespan ~7–10 days.

All derive from haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) in adult bone marrow.

Plasma (~55% of blood volume):

Proteins (~7 g/dL total):

- Albumin (~3.5–5.0 g/dL) — synthesised by liver; major contributor to oncotic pressure; carrier protein for drugs, bilirubin, hormones.
- Globulins:
  - α-globulins (α1-antitrypsin, transcortin, α-fetoprotein, haptoglobin, ceruloplasmin, α2-macroglobulin) — varied transport, anti-protease, copper transport, RBC haemolysis-binding.
  - β-globulins (transferrin, complement, β-microglobulin) — iron transport, immune defence.
  - γ-globulins (immunoglobulins) — antibodies; from plasma cells.
- Fibrinogen (1.5–4 g/L) — clotting protein.
- Other clotting factors (prothrombin, factors V, VII, IX, X, XI, XII, XIII).
- Complement components (C3, C4, etc.) — innate immunity.
- Lipoproteins — lipid transport.
- Hormones, enzymes, growth factors.

Electrolytes:

- Cations: Na⁺ ~140, K⁺ ~4, Ca²⁺ ~2.4, Mg²⁺ ~0.8 (mEq/L or mmol/L).
- Anions: Cl⁻ ~100, HCO₃⁻ ~24, PO₄³⁻ ~1, SO₄²⁻ small.

Other: glucose, lipids, urea, creatinine, bilirubin, gases.

Functions:

1. Transport — O₂, CO₂, nutrients, hormones, drugs, metabolic waste.
2. Homeostasis — temperature regulation, pH buffering, fluid balance.
3. Defence — innate (complement, neutrophils, monocytes) + adaptive (lymphocytes, antibodies) immunity.
4. Haemostasis — Platelets + clotting factors.
5. Communication — Hormones, cytokines.

(Berne & Levy, 7e — Blood; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.18.)

Stem: Explain the regulation of erythropoiesis. How does the body adjust RBC production to oxygen demand?

Model answer: Erythropoiesis is exquisitely tuned to oxygen demand through a feedback loop centred on EPO (erythropoietin) released from the kidney.

Steps:

1. Hypoxia sensing — Renal peritubular interstitial fibroblasts (~5% of cortical cells) sense tissue O₂ tension.
2. HIF stabilisation — At normal O₂, HIF-α (hypoxia-inducible factor) is hydroxylated by prolyl hydroxylases (PHD), recognised by VHL E3 ligase, ubiquitinated, and degraded. With hypoxia, HIF-α stabilises and dimerises with HIF-β.
3. Gene transcription — HIF-1α/2α activates EPO gene transcription. Also activates VEGF (angiogenesis), GLUT1 (glucose uptake), glycolytic enzymes (anaerobic adaptation).
4. EPO release — Plasma EPO rises, half-life ~5 hours.
5. Marrow action — EPO binds EPO receptor on erythroid progenitors (CFU-E, BFU-E) → JAK2/STAT5 signalling → proliferation + survival + differentiation → reticulocyte → mature RBC.
6. Time course — Reticulocytosis within ~3–4 days; mature RBC contribution ~7–14 days.

Other regulatory factors:

- Iron — Essential for haem synthesis. Deficiency limits effective erythropoiesis.
- Vitamin B12 / folate — DNA synthesis. Deficiency → megaloblastic anaemia.
- Other haematopoietic factors — IL-3, GM-CSF, SCF (stem cell factor).
- Androgens — Mild stimulation of erythropoiesis (explaining higher male Hb).
- Thyroid hormones — Permissive role.
- Inflammation — Reduces erythropoiesis (anaemia of chronic disease — hepcidin-mediated iron sequestration; cytokine inhibition of progenitors).

Pathological states:

Reduced EPO production:

- CKD — Loss of renal mass; treated with recombinant EPO.
- Anaemia of chronic disease — Cytokine suppression.

Excessive EPO:

- Chronic hypoxia — High altitude, COPD, cyanotic heart disease, sleep apnoea — physiological response.
- EPO-secreting tumours — Renal cell carcinoma, hepatocellular carcinoma, hemangioblastoma — pathological.
- Doping — Recombinant EPO illegally for athletic performance.

Polycythaemia vera — JAK2 mutation gives EPO-independent proliferation (low EPO).

Modern HIF-stabilisers (roxadustat, daprodustat, vadadustat) — Inhibit prolyl hydroxylases → stabilise HIF → endogenous EPO production. Newer oral alternatives to recombinant EPO for CKD anaemia.

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.34; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.33.)

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe plasma proteins. What are the major proteins, their functions, and the consequences of their deficiencies?

Model answer: Plasma proteins (~ 60-80 g/L total). (1) _Albumin_ - 35-50 g/L; produced in liver; multiple functions: oncotic pressure (~ 75% of plasma oncotic), transport of fatty acids, bilirubin, calcium, drugs (warfarin, phenytoin), hormones; pH buffering. Deficiency: liver failure, nephrotic syndrome (urinary loss > 3.5 g/day), malnutrition, sepsis -> oedema, ascites, drug pharmacokinetic changes. (2) _Globulins_ - α1 (α1-antitrypsin - protease inhibitor; deficiency causes lung emphysema, liver disease), α2 (haptoglobin - binds free Hb; ceruloplasmin - copper transport, ferroxidase activity; α2-macroglobulin); β (transferrin - iron transport; complement C3, C4); γ (immunoglobulins - IgG, IgA, IgM, IgD, IgE - humoral immunity). (3) _Fibrinogen_ - 2-4 g/L; clotting factor I; cleaved by thrombin to fibrin. Acute-phase reactant rises with inflammation. (4) _Coagulation factors_ - liver-produced (most); vitamin K-dependent (II, VII, IX, X, protein C, S - require carboxylation by VKORC1; warfarin inhibits). (5) _Acute-phase reactants_ - rise with inflammation: CRP, fibrinogen, ferritin, haptoglobin, ceruloplasmin, complement, α1-antitrypsin, SAA. (6) _Other_ - lipoproteins (HDL, LDL, VLDL, chylomicrons), hormones (ACTH-binding, thyroxine-binding, sex hormone-binding, vitamin D-binding globulins), enzymes (AST, ALT, amylase, lipase, alkaline phosphatase, troponin), apolipoproteins, antibodies. Clinical: serum protein electrophoresis (SPEP) separates albumin and globulin bands; abnormal patterns - monoclonal spike (multiple myeloma, MGUS), polyclonal hypergammaglobulinaemia (autoimmune, infection, chronic liver disease), hypoalbuminaemia (nephrotic - low albumin, high α2; cirrhosis - low albumin, polyclonal γ; protein-losing enteropathy), low immunoglobulins (CVID, multiple myeloma with paraprotein replacing). Pathological: (1) _Multiple myeloma_ - monoclonal Ig (IgG > IgA > light chain only); CRAB criteria (Ca, renal, anaemia, bone); SPEP/IFE, free light chains, marrow biopsy; treatment with proteasome inhibitors (bortezomib), immunomodulators (lenalidomide), monoclonal Ab (daratumumab), stem cell transplant. (2) _Waldenström macroglobulinaemia_ - IgM monoclonal gammopathy; hyperviscosity. (3) _MGUS_ - monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance; small spike, no end-organ damage; surveillance. (4) _AL amyloidosis_ - light chain misfolding -> amyloid deposits; treatment of underlying clonal plasma cell disease. (5) _Macroglobulinaemia_. (6) _Hypogammaglobulinaemia_ - infection susceptibility; IVIG replacement [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: Plasma proteins are diverse functional molecules - albumin for oncotic and transport, globulins for immunity and transport, fibrinogen for clotting. Deficiencies and dysproteinaemias produce wide-ranging clinical pictures - albumin deficiency causes oedema, immunoglobulin abnormalities cause infection susceptibility or organ damage from monoclonal protein.

Hints:

1. Albumin: oncotic + transport.
2. Globulins: α (transport, antiproteases), β (transport, complement), γ (immunoglobulins).
3. Fibrinogen: clotting; acute-phase reactant.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names ≥ 4 plasma protein categories with primary functions.
- Identifies α1-antitrypsin deficiency and its consequences.
- Names ≥ 2 vitamin K-dependent factors.
- Distinguishes monoclonal from polyclonal gammopathies.
- Mentions multiple myeloma and its CRAB criteria.

A Yellow answer covers some proteins but misses gammopathies or specific deficiencies.

A Red answer treats plasma proteins as a single category.

Common misconceptions:

- "All globulins are immunoglobulins.": Correction: γ-globulins are immunoglobulins; α and β globulins include transport proteins, protease inhibitors, complement, and others.
- "Albumin deficiency only causes oedema.": Correction: Also causes drug pharmacokinetic changes (free fraction of highly bound drugs rises) and altered transport of fatty acids, bilirubin, hormones.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the ABO and Rh blood group systems. How do they affect transfusion and pregnancy?

Model answer: Blood groups based on antigens on RBC surface; > 30 systems but ABO and Rh are most clinically important. ABO: encoded on chromosome 9; A and B alleles encode glycosyltransferases that add specific sugars to H antigen; O is null (no transferase). Phenotypes: A (genotype AA or AO), B (BB or BO), AB (AB - co-dominant), O (OO). Pre-formed natural antibodies: type A has anti-B; type B has anti-A; type O has anti-A and anti-B; type AB has neither. Clinical: incompatible ABO transfusion -> rapid intravascular haemolysis (acute haemolytic transfusion reaction); fever, chills, back pain, haemoglobinuria, DIC, AKI; potentially fatal. Type O is universal donor (RBCs); type AB is universal recipient. ABO compatibility table essential. Rh system: ~ 50 antigens; D is most clinically important (immunogenic). RhD positive (DD or Dd) ~ 85% of Caucasians, ~ 95-99% of African and Asian populations; RhD negative (dd) ~ 15% Caucasians. No pre-formed anti-D; antibodies form only after RhD+ blood exposure (transfusion or pregnancy). Haemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn (HDFN): RhD-negative mother becomes sensitised by RhD-positive fetus during pregnancy/delivery; subsequent RhD-positive pregnancy -> maternal anti-D IgG crosses placenta -> attacks fetal RBC -> fetal anaemia, hydrops fetalis, kernicterus. Prevention: anti-D immunoglobulin (Rhogam) given at 28 weeks to RhD-negative mothers, and within 72h of any sensitising event (delivery if RhD+ baby, miscarriage, abortion, antenatal bleeding, amniocentesis, abdominal trauma) - binds fetal RBCs preventing maternal sensitisation. Other Rh antigens: C, c, E, e - can also cause HDFN. Other clinically relevant systems: (1) Kell (K, k - K is highly immunogenic; HDFN); (2) Duffy (Fy^a, Fy^b - related to malaria resistance); (3) Lewis (Le^a, Le^b); (4) MNS, P, I, Lutheran; (5) ABO subgroups (A1, A2, etc). Crossmatch: pretransfusion test - patient serum + donor RBCs to detect any unexpected antibody. Transfusion: (1) Whole blood/RBCs - ABO and RhD compatible; (2) FFP - ABO compatible (AB FFP universal); (3) Platelets - ABO and RhD compatible preferred; (4) Cryoprecipitate; (5) Specialty - leucoreduced (reduces febrile reactions, CMV, HLA alloimmunisation), irradiated (prevent transfusion-associated GVHD in immunocompromised), washed (allergic reactions), CMV-negative (CMV-naive immunocompromised, neonates, pregnant women). Adverse reactions: (1) Acute haemolytic - ABO mismatch (rare with crossmatching - typically clerical error); (2) Febrile non-haemolytic - cytokines from leucocytes; reduced by leucoreduction; (3) Allergic - IgA in IgA-deficient recipient; pretreatment with antihistamines; (4) Anaphylactic - severe; treat with adrenaline; (5) TRALI (Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury) - non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema; donor antibodies against recipient leucocytes; reduced by male-only plasma; (6) TACO (Transfusion-Associated Circulatory Overload) - elderly, cardiac patients; cautious volume; (7) Transfusion-associated GVHD - rare; lethal; prevent by irradiation in at-risk; (8) Bacterial contamination - especially platelets stored at room temp; (9) Viral - vCJD, HIV, HBV, HCV (risk now extremely low with screening); (10) Iron overload - chronic transfusion (thalassaemia, sickle cell, MDS); chelation [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: ABO and Rh systems illustrate how cell-surface antigens dictate transfusion safety and obstetric care. The pre-formed anti-A/anti-B antibodies make ABO compatibility critical. Anti-D prophylaxis has dramatically reduced HDFN. Modern transfusion medicine optimises safety through crossmatching, leucoreduction, irradiation, and component-specific use.

Hints:

1. A, B, AB, O - co-dominant alleles + pre-formed antibodies.
2. Rh D - immunogenic, no pre-formed anti-D; sensitisation through pregnancy/transfusion.
3. Anti-D immunoglobulin prevents HDFN.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names ABO genetics and pre-formed antibodies.
- Identifies acute haemolytic reaction from ABO mismatch.
- Explains HDFN mechanism and anti-D prophylaxis.
- Names ≥ 3 transfusion adverse reactions.
- Mentions universal donor (O) and universal recipient (AB) for RBCs.

A Yellow answer covers ABO/Rh but misses HDFN or transfusion reactions.

A Red answer treats blood groups as administrative categories.

Common misconceptions:

- "Type O is universal donor for everything.": Correction: O _RBCs_ are universal donor; for plasma, AB is universal donor (no anti-A or anti-B in AB plasma).
- "Anti-D is needed for all RhD-negative pregnancies.": Correction: Only at 28 weeks routinely + after sensitising events; if cord blood after delivery is RhD-positive, give anti-D postpartum.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe haemostasis. How are primary, secondary, and fibrinolytic mechanisms coordinated?

Model answer: _Haemostasis_ - balance between procoagulant and anticoagulant forces; complex sequential cascade. (1) _Vasoconstriction_ - immediate; reduces blood loss and slows flow at site of injury. (2) _Primary haemostasis_ (platelets - seconds-minutes): vascular injury -> exposed subendothelial collagen and tissue factor; vWF (released by endothelial Weibel-Palade bodies and platelets) binds GPIb-IX-V on platelets -> initial platelet adhesion. ADP, TXA2, thrombin activate platelets -> shape change, granule release (ADP, TXA2, serotonin, fibrinogen, vWF) -> recruitment of more platelets; GPIIb/IIIa receptor activation -> binds fibrinogen -> platelet-platelet aggregation; _platelet plug_ forms. Drugs targeting primary haemostasis: aspirin (irreversible COX-1 -> reduced TXA2 from platelets - effect lasts platelet lifetime ~ 7-10 days); clopidogrel/prasugrel/ticagrelor (P2Y12 ADP receptor antagonists); abciximab/eptifibatide/tirofiban (GPIIb/IIIa antagonists); dipyridamole. (3) _Secondary haemostasis_ (coagulation cascade - minutes): tissue factor (exposed subendothelium) + factor VIIa -> activates IX and X; intrinsic pathway amplifies (XII, XI, IX, VIII); common pathway: Xa + Va + Ca²⁺ + phospholipid (prothrombinase complex) -> converts prothrombin (II) to thrombin (IIa); thrombin cleaves fibrinogen (I) to fibrin -> stabilised by factor XIII (transglutaminase) crosslinking. Modern view: tissue-factor pathway initiates; thrombin generation amplifies through intrinsic factors; final clot product - fibrin-platelet meshwork. Vitamin K-dependent factors: II, VII, IX, X, protein C, S - γ-carboxylation by VKORC1 (warfarin inhibits). Tests: PT/INR (extrinsic + common; warfarin), aPTT (intrinsic + common; heparin, factor deficiency, lupus anticoagulant), thrombin time (fibrinogen, thrombin issue), fibrinogen level, factor assays, mixing studies, viscoelastic (TEG/ROTEM). (4) _Fibrinolysis_ (clot dissolution - hours-days): plasminogen converted to plasmin by tissue plasminogen activator (tPA, from endothelium) and urokinase; plasmin cleaves fibrin -> D-dimers and FDPs. Antifibrinolytic regulation: PAI-1 (inhibits tPA), α2-antiplasmin (inhibits plasmin), TAFI (thrombin-activatable fibrinolysis inhibitor). (5) _Anticoagulant systems_: (a) Antithrombin III (binds and inhibits IIa, IXa, Xa, XIa, XIIa; potentiated 1000-fold by heparin); (b) Protein C/S - thrombin + thrombomodulin -> activated protein C (APC), with cofactor S, inactivates Va and VIIIa; deficiencies cause thrombophilia; APC resistance (factor V Leiden) common; (c) Tissue factor pathway inhibitor (TFPI). Dynamic balance: localised clot formation at injury, contained by anticoagulant systems; systemic balance maintained. Disorders: (1) _Bleeding_ - platelet (mucocutaneous bleeding, petechiae) vs coagulation (joint, deep tissue, delayed); haemophilia A (factor VIII), B (IX) - X-linked; vWD (most common inherited bleeding disorder; impairs both adhesion and factor VIII delivery); thrombocytopenia; thrombocytopathy (uremia, drugs); liver disease (synthetic failure); vitamin K deficiency; DIC. (2) _Thrombosis_ - venous (DVT, PE - mostly Virchow triad: stasis, hypercoagulability, endothelial injury) vs arterial (atherothrombotic - typically endothelial injury + platelet activation + flow turbulence); inherited thrombophilias (factor V Leiden ~ 5% Caucasians, prothrombin gene mutation, antithrombin/protein C/S deficiencies, antiphospholipid syndrome); acquired (cancer, OCP, pregnancy, immobilisation, surgery, COVID-19). Anticoagulants: heparin (UFH - antithrombin enhancer; APTT-monitored); LMWH (enoxaparin - anti-Xa; weight-based); fondaparinux (anti-Xa); warfarin (vitamin K antagonist - INR; many drug-food interactions); DOACs (factor Xa inhibitors - rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban; thrombin inhibitor - dabigatran; minimal monitoring; specific reversal - andexanet alfa for Xa, idarucizumab for dabigatran); thrombolytics (alteplase, tenecteplase, streptokinase). The haemostatic system is one of the most exquisitely balanced in physiology - tipped one way produces bleeding, the other thrombosis. Modern anticoagulation has revolutionised cardiovascular medicine (AF stroke prevention, VTE) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: Haemostasis is a paradigmatic example of dynamic balance in physiology. Three sequential phases (primary, secondary, fibrinolysis) coordinated by feedback loops, with anticoagulant systems preventing runaway clot. Bleeding and thrombosis are opposite failures of the same machine; pharmacology targets specific steps (aspirin, P2Y12, GPIIb/IIIa, heparin, warfarin, DOACs, thrombolytics).

Hints:

1. Primary (platelets) -> Secondary (coagulation cascade) -> Fibrinolysis.
2. Vitamin K-dependent factors: II, VII, IX, X, protein C, S.
3. Anticoagulants: heparin, warfarin, DOACs; antiplatelets: aspirin, P2Y12.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the three phases of haemostasis.
- Identifies vWF and platelet adhesion in primary haemostasis.
- Names the coagulation cascade with key factors.
- Distinguishes platelet vs coagulation bleeding patterns.
- Names ≥ 4 anticoagulants/antiplatelets with mechanisms.

A Yellow answer covers cascade but misses fibrinolysis or specific drugs.

A Red answer treats clotting as a single process.

Common misconceptions:

- "Bleeding and thrombosis are unrelated.": Correction: Both reflect imbalance of the same machine; antibodies (eg, antiphospholipid) can produce both.
- "All anticoagulation is the same.": Correction: Mechanisms differ: heparin enhances antithrombin; warfarin inhibits vitamin K cycle; DOACs directly inhibit Xa or thrombin; choice based on indication, monitoring, reversibility.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe leucocyte classification and the basic immune cell roles.

Model answer: Leucocytes (white blood cells) - normal count 4-11 x 10^9/L. Five major types: (1) _Neutrophils_ (~ 60-70% of total) - polymorphonuclear (lobed nuclei); first responders to bacterial infection; phagocytosis; release reactive oxygen species, lysozyme, myeloperoxidase, defensins, neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs); life span ~ 6-10 hours in circulation; total transit short. Increased: bacterial infection, inflammation, tissue necrosis, stress, glucocorticoids (demargination), G-CSF therapy, some leukaemias. Decreased (neutropenia): chemotherapy, infections (severe), B12/folate deficiency, hypersplenism, autoimmune (Felty), cyclic. Severe (< 0.5 x 10^9/L) high infection risk. (2) _Lymphocytes_ (~ 20-40%) - mononuclear cells; smaller than neutrophils. Subtypes: (a) T cells (~ 70% of lymphocytes; CD3+; CD4 helper, CD8 cytotoxic, regulatory); cellular immunity, viral defence, intracellular pathogens; (b) B cells (~ 20%; CD19+, CD20+); humoral immunity, antibody production; (c) NK cells (~ 10%; CD16+, CD56+); innate immunity against viruses and tumours. Increased: viral infection, lymphocytic leukaemias, lymphoma. Decreased: HIV, immunosuppression, congenital immunodeficiencies, chemotherapy. (3) _Monocytes_ (~ 2-8%) - largest leucocyte; tissue precursors (transform to macrophages, dendritic cells in tissue); long-lived; antigen presentation, phagocytosis, cytokine production; tissue macrophages (Kupffer cells liver, microglia brain, alveolar macrophages lung, osteoclasts bone, splenic, etc); dendritic cells - professional APCs initiating adaptive immunity. (4) _Eosinophils_ (~ 1-4%) - parasite defence, allergic reactions, asthma; granules contain MBP, ECP, EDN, EPX; release leukotrienes, IL-5; can cause tissue damage in chronic conditions (eosinophilic oesophagitis, eosinophilic gastroenteritis, hypereosinophilic syndrome, eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis - EGPA/Churg-Strauss). (5) _Basophils_ (~ 0-1%) - similar to mast cells; histamine, heparin, IL-4; allergic reactions; chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML) classically associated with basophilia. Mast cells - tissue-resident; allergic reactions, parasitic; degranulate via FcεRI cross-linking. Bone marrow stem cells: HSC -> myeloid (granulocytes, monocytes, megakaryocytes -> platelets; erythrocytes) and lymphoid (T, B, NK) lineages. Pluripotent stem cell self-renewal essential. Cytokines drive specific lineage differentiation: G-CSF (neutrophils), GM-CSF, M-CSF (monocytes), IL-3, EPO (erythroid), TPO (megakaryocytes). Innate vs adaptive immunity: innate (neutrophils, monocytes/macrophages, NK, complement, lysozyme, defensins, TLRs) - rapid, non-specific; adaptive (T and B cells) - slower, antigen-specific, memory. Coordination: APCs phagocytose, process, and present antigen on MHC I (intracellular pathogens to CD8 T) or MHC II (extracellular to CD4 T); CD4 T cells help B cells (humoral) or activate macrophages (cell-mediated); CD8 T cells kill infected cells. Memory cells provide durable protection - basis of vaccination. Inflammation: vascular (vasodilation, increased permeability) + cellular (neutrophil margination, transmigration, chemotaxis) + plasma protein involvement (complement, coagulation, kinin); pattern of leucocytosis depends on cause - bacterial (neutrophilia), viral (lymphocytosis or atypical), parasitic (eosinophilia), allergic (eosinophilia, basophilia). Modern immunology has produced biological agents targeting specific pathways (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-23, JAK, BTK, etc) for autoimmune disease, transplantation, and cancer (immune checkpoint inhibitors - anti-CTLA-4, anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18-19; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: Leucocytes are a heterogeneous family with overlapping but distinct functions in innate and adaptive immunity. Differential white cell count interpretation is a fundamental clinical skill - neutrophilia for bacterial; lymphocytosis for viral; eosinophilia for parasites/allergy; basophilia rare but suggestive of CML. Modern immunology has translated this knowledge into precision biologics for autoimmune disease and cancer.

Hints:

1. 5 leucocyte types: neutrophil, lymphocyte, monocyte, eosinophil, basophil.
2. Innate (rapid, non-specific) vs adaptive (slow, specific, memory).
3. Differential pattern indicates likely cause.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the five major leucocyte types with proportions and primary roles.
- Distinguishes T, B, NK lymphocyte subtypes.
- Identifies innate vs adaptive immunity components.
- Maps differential pattern to likely cause (eg, neutrophilia bacterial, lymphocytosis viral).
- Mentions cytokines driving lineage differentiation (G-CSF, EPO, TPO).

A Yellow answer covers cells but misses functional roles or differential interpretation.

A Red answer treats all leucocytes as a single group.

Common misconceptions:

- "All white cells fight infection.": Correction: Neutrophils fight bacterial infections; lymphocytes handle viruses and intracellular pathogens; eosinophils handle parasites/allergies - each has specialised role.
- "Lymphocytosis is always reactive.": Correction: Reactive (viral, post-vaccination) is common; clonal lymphocytosis (CLL, lymphoma) needs investigation if persistent or with abnormal cells.

Stem: Describe haematopoiesis. How does the bone marrow maintain steady-state blood cell production?

Model answer: Haematopoiesis - production of all blood cells from haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) in bone marrow. HSC properties: (1) Self-renewal (asymmetric division produces another HSC plus differentiated progeny); (2) Pluripotency (can give rise to all blood cell lineages); (3) Quiescence (most HSCs are dormant, dividing rarely; protects from damage and exhaustion). Sites of haematopoiesis through life: (a) Yolk sac (early embryonic); (b) Liver and spleen (mid-fetal; resumed in adulthood as extramedullary haematopoiesis when needed); (c) Bone marrow (from late fetal onwards; in adult: axial skeleton - vertebrae, sternum, ribs, pelvis, proximal long bones); (d) Thymus (T cell maturation). Differentiation cascade: HSC -> multipotent progenitor (MPP) -> common myeloid progenitor (CMP, erythroid/megakaryocyte/granulocyte/monocyte lineages) and common lymphoid progenitor (CLP, T/B/NK lineages). Lineage-specific cytokines: (1) Erythropoietin (EPO) - kidney peritubular fibroblasts; rises with hypoxia (HIF-2α stable in low O2 -> transcribes EPO); drives erythropoiesis. Recombinant EPO (epoetin) for renal anaemia, chemo-induced anaemia. (2) Thrombopoietin (TPO) - liver (mainly); regulates platelets; recombinant agonists (romiplostim, eltrombopag). (3) G-CSF (filgrastim, pegfilgrastim) - granulocyte production; used after chemo to reduce febrile neutropenia. (4) GM-CSF (sargramostim) - granulocytes and monocytes. (5) M-CSF, IL-3, IL-5, IL-7, SCF, FLT3L. Stromal niche: HSC microenvironment in marrow - osteoblasts, mesenchymal stromal cells, endothelial cells, sympathetic nerves, macrophages; Notch, Wnt, CXCL12 (SDF-1), SCF signalling regulate HSC maintenance and homing. Regulation: feedback from circulating cell counts; sensors detect demand. Examples: hypoxia -> EPO; thrombocytopenia -> TPO; bacterial infection -> emergency granulopoiesis (G-CSF, GM-CSF, IL-1, IL-6, TNF). Bone marrow assessment: aspirate (cytology, flow cytometry, cytogenetics, molecular) + trephine biopsy (architecture, cellularity, fibrosis, infiltration). Cellularity: 100% in newborns; ~ 50% in middle age; lower in elderly; "100 - age = % cellularity" rule of thumb. Disorders of haematopoiesis: (1) _Aplastic anaemia_ - HSC failure; pancytopenia + hypocellular marrow; immune-mediated (idiopathic, viral, drugs); treat with immunosuppression (ATG + cyclosporine), HSC transplant; (2) _Myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS)_ - clonal HSC disease with ineffective haematopoiesis (cytopenias) + dysplasia + risk of AML; lower-risk - supportive (transfusion, EPO, lenalidomide for del(5q)), higher-risk - hypomethylating agents (azacitidine, decitabine), HSC transplant; (3) _Myeloproliferative neoplasms_ - clonal HSC overproduction: PV (JAK2 V617F, EPO-independent erythrocytosis), ET (essential thrombocythemia, JAK2/CALR/MPL), PMF (primary myelofibrosis); risk of thrombosis, AML transformation; treatment includes hydroxyurea, JAK inhibitors (ruxolitinib), aspirin, phlebotomy; (4) _Acute leukaemias_ (AML, ALL) - clonal proliferation of immature blasts; treatment varies; CAR-T cell therapy revolutionising B-ALL, lymphomas; (5) _Chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML)_ - BCR-ABL fusion (Philadelphia chromosome); treated with TKIs (imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib, ponatinib, asciminib) - revolutionised outcomes; (6) _Bone marrow failure syndromes_ - inherited (Fanconi anaemia, dyskeratosis congenita, Diamond-Blackfan, Shwachman-Diamond) - all have defined molecular bases; (7) _Megaloblastic anaemia_ (B12, folate deficiency) - impaired DNA synthesis; (8) _Marrow infiltration_ - leukaemia, lymphoma, metastasis -> displaces normal haematopoiesis; (9) _Marrow fibrosis_ - PMF, post-radiation. Stem cell transplantation: allogeneic (sibling-matched, unrelated, haploidentical, cord blood) - cures many haematological malignancies and inherited disorders; conditioning (chemo +/- TBI) eradicates host marrow + suppresses graft rejection; risks GVHD, infection. Autologous - patient's own HSCs; used in lymphoma, multiple myeloma after high-dose chemo. Future: gene therapy for inherited disorders (sickle cell with CRISPR; β-thalassemia; SCID-X1) - landmark approvals 2023-2024 [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Berne & Levy ch.4].

Elaborative explanation: Haematopoiesis is one of the most regulated and continuously active production systems in the body - billions of cells made daily. Stem cells and lineage-specific cytokines orchestrate it. Disease (failure, dysplasia, neoplasia) reveals the mechanisms; therapy (cytokines, transplantation, gene therapy) exploits them.

Hints:

1. HSCs in bone marrow - self-renew + pluripotent + quiescent.
2. Lineage-specific cytokines: EPO, TPO, G-CSF, GM-CSF, others.
3. Disorders: aplastic, MDS, MPN, leukaemia.

- Identifies sites of haematopoiesis through development.
- Names ≥ 3 lineage-specific cytokines with their target lineages.
- Names ≥ 3 haematological disorders with mechanism.
- Mentions stem cell transplantation principles.

A Yellow answer covers stem cells but misses cytokine specificity or disease.

- "EPO is universal blood cell stimulator.": Correction: EPO is _erythroid-lineage-specific_; TPO for platelets; G-CSF for neutrophils; cytokines are highly lineage-specific.

Stem: Describe the metabolic and nutritional regulation of erythropoiesis. How do EPO, iron, B12, and folate interact?

Model answer: Erythropoiesis requires adequate substrates and signals. (1) _Erythropoietin (EPO)_ - 165-aa glycoprotein produced by peritubular fibroblasts of renal cortex (~ 90%) and liver (~ 10%). Regulation: hypoxia (low O2 delivery to kidney) stabilises HIF-2α (which is normally degraded by prolyl hydroxylase using O2 as substrate) -> HIF-2α drives EPO transcription. Once secreted, EPO acts on EPO receptors on erythroid progenitors (CFU-E, proerythroblasts) -> JAK2/STAT5 signalling -> survival, proliferation, differentiation. Time to effect: reticulocytosis in 5-7 days; new RBC pool in 4-6 weeks. Failure of EPO production - chronic kidney disease - normocytic normochromic anaemia treated with recombinant EPO (epoetin alfa, darbepoetin). (2) _Iron (Fe)_ - essential for haem synthesis (4 atoms per Hb molecule). Total body iron ~ 4 g (~ 70% in Hb, 25% in storage as ferritin/hemosiderin in liver, spleen, marrow; 5% in myoglobin and enzymes). Daily turnover ~ 25 mg (mostly recycling from senescent RBCs by macrophages); intake ~ 1-2 mg/day to replace losses (sweat, skin, GI shedding, menstrual). _Iron handling_: dietary haem and non-haem iron absorbed by enterocytes (DMT1 for non-haem; HCP1 for haem); stored as ferritin or exported via ferroportin to plasma; loaded onto transferrin (binds 2 Fe³⁺ atoms; saturation normally ~ 30%); delivered to cells via transferrin receptor (TFR1) -> endosomal release -> incorporated into haem in mitochondria. _Hepcidin_ (liver) - master regulator; binds ferroportin and triggers internalisation/degradation -> reduces iron absorption and macrophage release; hepcidin upregulated by iron sufficiency, BMP6/SMAD signalling, inflammation (IL-6 - explains anaemia of inflammation/chronic disease) - useful in iron overload but problematic in inflammation; downregulated by hypoxia, increased erythropoietic demand (erythroferrone from erythroblasts), iron deficiency. _Iron deficiency_ - microcytic hypochromic anaemia; commonest anaemia globally; causes - blood loss (GI, menstrual, surgical), inadequate intake (vegetarian/vegan, infants, pregnancy), malabsorption (coeliac, post-gastrectomy, H. pylori); diagnosis - low ferritin (most specific; but acute-phase reactant - can be falsely normal in inflammation), low TSAT, raised TIBC, low MCV, low Hb; treat - oral iron (ferrous sulphate; alternate-day dosing improves absorption per BTS guidance), IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose) when oral failure or rapid replenishment needed; treat underlying cause. _Iron overload_ - haemochromatosis (HFE C282Y/H63D - hepcidin deficiency); secondary - chronic transfusion (thalassaemia, MDS); causes liver, cardiac, pancreatic damage; treat phlebotomy or chelation (deferasirox, deferoxamine, deferiprone). (3) _Vitamin B12 (cobalamin)_ - cofactor for methionine synthase (homocysteine -> methionine; required for DNA synthesis methylation) and methylmalonyl-CoA mutase (propionate metabolism). Source: animal products (meat, fish, eggs, dairy); not in plants - vegan deficiency. Absorption requires intrinsic factor (IF, gastric parietal cells) -> binds B12 -> absorbed in terminal ileum via cubilin/amnionless. Stores ~ 3-4 years (very efficient enterohepatic circulation). Deficiency: pernicious anaemia (autoimmune anti-IF, anti-parietal), atrophic gastritis (elderly, PPI), gastric surgery, terminal ileal disease (Crohn, resection, diverticulosis, fish tapeworm), vegan, drugs (metformin, PPI, H2 blockers). Features - macrocytic megaloblastic anaemia (MCV > 100, hypersegmented neutrophils, oval macrocytes); subacute combined degeneration of cord (dorsal columns + corticospinal); peripheral neuropathy; cognitive impairment; glossitis; diarrhoea. Diagnosis - low B12; raised methylmalonic acid + homocysteine. Treat - parenteral hydroxocobalamin (PA, malabsorption) or oral high-dose. (4) _Folate (folic acid, vitamin B9)_ - cofactor for thymidylate synthase, methionine synthase, purine synthesis. Source: leafy greens, legumes, fortified flour. Absorption proximal jejunum. Stores ~ 3-4 months (less than B12). Deficiency: dietary (alcoholism, elderly), increased demand (pregnancy - neural tube defect risk; haemolysis), malabsorption (coeliac), drugs (methotrexate - folate antagonist; phenytoin; trimethoprim), chronic disease. Features - macrocytic megaloblastic anaemia (similar to B12 but no neurological features). Diagnosis - low serum folate; raised homocysteine; normal MMA (helps distinguish from B12). Treat - oral folic acid 5 mg daily; in pregnancy - 400 mcg from preconception to prevent NTDs (5 mg if high risk). Critical: when both B12 and folate are deficient, treat B12 first - giving folate alone in B12 deficiency can correct anaemia but allow neurological deterioration to progress. (5) _Other haematinics_ - copper (caeruloplasmin loads iron onto transferrin), B6 (haem synthesis), riboflavin, vitamin E. Anaemia investigation algorithm by MCV: microcytic (Fe deficiency, thalassaemia, anaemia of chronic disease, sideroblastic, lead), normocytic (acute blood loss, haemolysis, renal failure, MM, marrow failure, mixed deficiency), macrocytic (B12, folate, alcohol, hypothyroid, liver, MDS, drugs - methotrexate, hydroxyurea) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: Erythropoiesis depends on EPO signal + iron + B12 + folate; deficiency in any produces anaemia. The hepcidin-ferroportin axis is the master iron regulator; understanding it explains anaemia of chronic disease and the rationale for newer hepcidin-targeting therapies. B12/folate distinction matters because giving folate alone in B12 deficiency masks anaemia but lets neuropathy progress.

Hints:

1. EPO drives erythropoiesis; iron, B12, folate are essential substrates.
2. Hepcidin/ferroportin = iron master regulator.
3. Anaemia by MCV: micro, normo, macrocytic - different causes.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names EPO and its hypoxia-responsive regulation.
- Identifies the hepcidin-ferroportin axis.
- Distinguishes B12 from folate deficiency on neurological features and tests.
- Lists ≥ 3 causes of microcytic and ≥ 3 macrocytic anaemia.
- Mentions the rule to treat B12 before folate.

A Yellow answer covers haematinics but misses regulation or differential.

A Red answer treats anaemia as a single entity.

Common misconceptions:

- "Iron deficiency = give iron.": Correction: Always investigate the cause (especially GI bleeding in older men or postmenopausal women); iron deficiency without obvious cause warrants endoscopy.
- "All macrocytic anaemia is B12/folate.": Correction: Also alcohol, hypothyroidism, MDS, drugs (methotrexate, hydroxyurea), liver disease.

Stem: Describe blood cell turnover and senescence. How are aged erythrocytes removed, and what is the rate of red cell production needed to maintain steady state?

Model answer: Blood cell turnover is one of the most rapid in the body. _RBC_ lifespan ~ 120 days; daily turnover ~ 1% of total mass = ~ 200 billion RBCs/day produced and removed. RBC ageing: as RBC ages, energy supplies (ATP) deplete (RBC has no nucleus, no mitochondria, depends on glycolysis); membrane stability decreases; surface markers change. Senescent RBCs lose deformability and CD47 ("don't eat me"), expose phosphatidylserine ("eat me" signal) - removed primarily by _splenic macrophages_ (red pulp) - extravascular haemolysis. Liver Kupffer cells also clear damaged RBCs. Iron is recycled (~ 25 mg/day - efficient); haem broken down: haem oxygenase produces biliverdin + Fe + CO; biliverdin reductase -> bilirubin -> conjugated in liver -> excreted in bile. Splenectomy increases RBC longevity slightly but loses efficient removal of damaged or parasitised RBCs (sickle, malaria, hereditary spherocytosis). _Reticulocyte count_ - reflects bone marrow output; normal 0.5-1.5% of RBCs (~ 50-150 x 10^9/L); rises with increased erythropoiesis (haemolysis, blood loss, after iron/B12/folate replacement, EPO therapy); falls with marrow failure or substrate deficiency. _Reticulocyte production index_ (RPI) corrects for haematocrit and maturation - useful for assessing whether anaemia has appropriate marrow response (RPI > 2-3 = appropriate; < 2 = inadequate marrow). _Haemolysis_ - shortened RBC lifespan; can be intravascular (within blood vessels - PNH, mechanical, severe transfusion reaction, severe G6PD crisis) or extravascular (in spleen/liver - hereditary spherocytosis, AIHA, sickle cell, thalassaemia). Markers: raised reticulocytes, raised LDH, raised unconjugated bilirubin, low haptoglobin (binds free Hb in plasma); intravascular adds free haemoglobinuria, schistocytes, methaemalbuminaemia, hemosiderinuria. _WBC_ turnover - much faster than RBC. Neutrophils survive only 6-10 hours in circulation; tissue half-life similar; total daily production ~ 100 billion. Lymphocytes - some recirculate for years (memory cells); naive cells short-lived. Eosinophils ~ 8-12 days. Monocytes ~ 1-3 days in blood, then transform to macrophages (months in tissue). _Platelet_ lifespan ~ 7-10 days; 1/3 sequestered in spleen; daily production ~ 100 billion from bone marrow megakaryocytes. Splenectomy raises platelet count (loss of pooling). _Megakaryocyte_ - large polyploid cell; produces platelets by fragmentation of cytoplasm. _Bone marrow output_ in steady state: erythrocytes ~ 200 billion/day; neutrophils ~ 100 billion/day; platelets ~ 100 billion/day; total ~ 4 x 10^11 cells/day. Bone marrow has substantial reserve - can increase RBC production 6-8 fold in haemolysis or hypoxia (with adequate substrates). Disorders: (1) Aplastic anaemia - global failure -> pancytopenia + low reticulocytes; (2) Megaloblastic anaemia - ineffective erythropoiesis; intramedullary haemolysis; raised LDH, mildly raised bilirubin; low retic; (3) Splenectomy effects - target cells, Howell-Jolly bodies, Pappenheimer bodies, Heinz bodies; thrombocytosis; lymphocytosis; risk of overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (encapsulated organisms - pneumococcus, Haemophilus, meningococcus); vaccination + lifelong antibiotic prophylaxis; (4) Hypersplenism - splenomegaly (any cause - portal hypertension, lymphoma, infection, storage disease, infiltration) -> increased pooling and destruction -> cytopenias. Therapeutic implications: marrow recovery after chemo - typically takes 2-3 weeks; G-CSF accelerates neutrophil recovery; transfusions cover platelets/RBCs in interim; opportunistic infections during nadir [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Blood cell turnover illustrates the marrow's enormous productive capacity (4 x 10^11 cells/day) and the careful regulation of cell senescence. Reticulocyte count is a clinically essential index of marrow response. Haemolysis vs marrow failure are distinguished by retic count + biochemistry. Splenectomy has lasting consequences for cell handling and infection risk.

Hints:

1. RBC lifespan 120d, daily turnover 1%; ~ 200 billion/day.
2. Splenic macrophages remove senescent/damaged RBCs.
3. Reticulocyte count = marrow output index.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names RBC lifespan and daily turnover.
- Identifies splenic extravascular and intravascular haemolysis.
- Names markers of haemolysis (LDH, bilirubin, retic, haptoglobin).
- Distinguishes appropriate vs inappropriate marrow response by retic count.
- Mentions splenectomy consequences (cell appearance, infection risk).

A Yellow answer covers turnover but misses retic interpretation or haemolysis markers.

A Red answer treats senescence as passive.

Common misconceptions:

- "RBCs die randomly.": Correction: RBCs lose CD47 and expose PS as they age; macrophages preferentially remove the senescent ones. Damaged RBCs (sickle, parasitised) cleared faster.
- "Reticulocyte count alone diagnoses anaemia cause.": Correction: Combined with MCV, ferritin, B12/folate, blood film; the _pattern_ of all together points to mechanism.

Stem: Describe iron deficiency anaemia. What is the diagnostic approach and management?

Model answer: _Iron deficiency anaemia (IDA)_ - the most common anaemia globally; affects ~ 25% of world population. Pathophysiology: total body iron stores depleted -> reduced haem synthesis -> microcytic hypochromic RBCs -> reduced oxygen-carrying capacity. Causes (always identify the underlying cause): (1) _Blood loss_ - most common cause in adults: menstrual (heavy menstrual bleeding), GI (peptic ulcer, gastritis, oesophagitis, hookworm/parasites, colorectal cancer, IBD, angiodysplasia, NSAIDs, aspirin, varices), urinary (rare), epistaxis, surgery, trauma; (2) _Inadequate intake_ - vegetarian/vegan, infants > 6 months without iron-fortified food, elderly, poverty; (3) _Malabsorption_ - coeliac disease, post-gastrectomy, H. pylori (chronic gastritis, achlorhydria), inflammatory bowel disease, bariatric surgery; (4) _Increased demand_ - pregnancy (~ 1000 mg additional needed; severe anaemia risks include preterm delivery, low birth weight), lactation, growth in children. Clinical features: pallor (conjunctival, palms, mucous membranes), fatigue, dyspnoea on exertion, palpitations, dizziness, headaches, restless legs syndrome, pica (craving for ice/clay/dirt), angular stomatitis, glossitis, koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails), Plummer-Vinson syndrome (oesophageal webs, dysphagia, IDA, post-cricoid web - rare). Severe - heart failure. Investigation: (1) _FBC and film_ - microcytic hypochromic anaemia (MCV < 80, MCH < 27); pencil cells, target cells; later thrombocytosis (reactive); (2) _Ferritin_ - most specific for iron stores; low (< 15-20 ng/mL diagnostic; > 100 essentially excludes iron deficiency); but acute-phase reactant - can be falsely raised in inflammation/liver disease/malignancy/CKD - need TSAT in those contexts; (3) _TSAT (transferrin saturation)_ - serum iron/TIBC; low (< 20%) in IDA; (4) _TIBC_ - raised in IDA (compensation); (5) _Soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR)_ - raised in true IDA but not in anaemia of chronic disease - useful when ferritin uncertain; (6) Reticulocytes - low or normal (substrate-depleted marrow). Distinguish from: (a) _Anaemia of chronic disease (AOCD)_ - normal/raised ferritin, low TSAT, low TIBC; mechanism via hepcidin upregulation by IL-6; treat underlying disease; iron does not help (unless deficient as well); (b) _Thalassaemia trait_ - microcytic with normal/raised ferritin; family history; ethnicity (Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, South Asian); Hb electrophoresis confirms; iron supplementation not indicated; (c) _Sideroblastic anaemia_ - rare; normal/raised iron; abnormal mitochondrial haem synthesis; ringed sideroblasts on bone marrow (Prussian blue stain); inherited or acquired. Management: (1) _Identify and treat underlying cause_ - critical; iron alone treats anaemia but not the disease (eg, iron deficiency in postmenopausal woman or older man warrants OGD/colonoscopy to exclude GI cancer); (2) _Replenish iron stores_: (a) _Oral iron_ - first-line; ferrous sulphate (60-65 mg elemental iron per tab); ferrous fumarate; ferrous gluconate; alternate-day dosing (every 2 days; recent evidence suggests _better absorption_ due to lower hepcidin elevation - newer recommendation); take on empty stomach (food, especially calcium, tea, coffee reduce absorption ~ 50%); vitamin C enhances; side effects (constipation, GI upset, dark stools - common; ~ 20% intolerance); duration - until Hb normalises + 3-6 months to replenish stores. (b) _IV iron_ - for oral intolerance, malabsorption, ongoing losses faster than oral can replace, severe deficiency, CKD, pregnancy; new formulations (ferric carboxymaltose, iron isomaltoside, ferumoxytol, iron sucrose) - low rates of severe reactions; useful in IBD, severe IDA. Older formulations (iron dextran) had higher anaphylaxis risk - largely replaced. (c) _RBC transfusion_ - reserved for severe anaemia with cardiovascular compromise or inability to tolerate iron treatment delay; (3) Monitor response - reticulocytosis in 5-7 days; Hb rise ~ 1-2 g/dL per 2-3 weeks; aim for normal Hb + repleted ferritin (often 100+); (4) Lifestyle - dietary advice (red meat, leafy greens, beans, fortified cereals; vitamin C); (5) Treatment failure - reconsider diagnosis (occult bleeding? malabsorption? wrong diagnosis?), check adherence, consider IV iron. Iron replacement in pregnancy - common; oral first-line, IV if intolerant or severe deficiency. Iron is critical for fetal development, maternal Hb, postpartum recovery [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: IDA is enormously common but the cause matters at least as much as the iron replacement. In adults without obvious source (especially older men, postmenopausal women), GI investigation is mandatory. Modern IV iron formulations are safer and faster than older ones; alternate-day oral dosing improves absorption by reducing hepcidin response.

Hints:

1. Microcytic hypochromic; ferritin low (most specific).
2. Always identify the _cause_ (GI bleed common in older adults).
3. Treat with oral iron alternate-day or IV iron.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names IDA with the microcytic hypochromic profile.
- Lists ≥ 3 categories of cause (blood loss, intake, malabsorption, demand).
- Identifies ferritin as primary diagnostic marker (with caveats in inflammation).
- Distinguishes from anaemia of chronic disease and thalassaemia.
- Mentions GI investigation in adults without obvious source.

A Yellow answer covers IDA but misses cause investigation.

A Red answer treats IDA without identifying cause.

- "IDA is just nutritional.": Correction: In adults, blood loss (especially GI) is the most common cause; nutritional causes more common in pregnancy and children; missed colorectal cancer is a serious consequence of treating IDA without investigating.

Stem: Describe the megaloblastic anaemias. How do B12 and folate deficiency differ in causes, presentation, and management?

Model answer: _Megaloblastic anaemia_ - macrocytic anaemia (MCV > 100) due to impaired DNA synthesis, producing 'megaloblastic' erythroid precursors in marrow with nuclear-cytoplasmic asynchrony (large cells with delayed nuclear maturation). Most common causes: B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, drugs (methotrexate, hydroxyurea, phenytoin, trimethoprim). Pathophysiology: B12 and folate are cofactors in nucleotide synthesis (thymidylate synthase requires methylenetetrahydrofolate); deficiency -> impaired DNA synthesis -> ineffective erythropoiesis (intramedullary haemolysis), pancytopenia in severe cases, hypersegmented neutrophils, oval macrocytes, dyserythropoiesis with raised LDH and unconjugated bilirubin. _B12 (cobalamin) deficiency_: Absorption: dietary B12 + intrinsic factor (parietal cell) -> binds in stomach (with R protein) -> trypsin in duodenum frees B12 -> binds IF -> absorbed in _terminal ileum_ via cubilin/amnionless. Stores ~ 3-4 years (very efficient enterohepatic circulation). Causes: (1) _Pernicious anaemia_ (most common - autoimmune; anti-IF antibodies and/or anti-parietal cell antibodies; associated with other autoimmune disorders - thyroid, vitiligo, Addison; T1DM); (2) _Malabsorption_ - atrophic gastritis (elderly, H. pylori, post-gastrectomy), terminal ileal disease (Crohn, ileal resection), diverticulosis (bacterial overgrowth), tropical sprue, fish tapeworm (Diphyllobothrium latum); (3) _Drugs_ - PPI, H2 blockers (reduce parietal acid secretion needed for B12 release from food), metformin (mechanism unclear - inhibits B12 absorption), nitrous oxide (oxidises cobalt; recreational nitrous oxide can cause severe deficiency - increasing concern); (4) _Vegan diet_ - rare cause but possible; (5) _Inherited_ - cobalamin transport defects (Imerslund-Gräsbeck). Clinical features: anaemia (pallor, fatigue, dyspnoea), glossitis (smooth red painful tongue - Hunter glossitis), GI symptoms (anorexia, weight loss); _neurological_ - peripheral neuropathy (paraesthesiae, symmetric, distal - commonly described as 'walking on cotton'), subacute combined degeneration of cord (dorsal columns + corticospinal -> sensory ataxia, Romberg-positive, hyperreflexia, Babinski; severe cases - paraplegia), cognitive impairment (mood, memory, dementia in advanced), optic atrophy. Neurological features can occur _without_ anaemia (especially in folate-supplemented populations); a subset of patients have neuropsychiatric features as the main presentation. Investigation: low serum B12 (< 200 pg/mL); raised methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine - both elevated (more sensitive than B12 itself in early/mild deficiency); blood film and MCV (macrocytic, hypersegmented neutrophils, oval macrocytes); look for evidence of pernicious anaemia (anti-IF antibodies - specific but only ~ 50% sensitive; anti-parietal cell antibodies - sensitive but not specific). Treatment: parenteral hydroxocobalamin (typically IM 1 mg three times weekly for 2 weeks, then every 2-3 months for life in PA). Oral high-dose (1-2 mg daily) is effective in many causes including PA (1-2% absorbed by passive diffusion). Neurological recovery may be slow and incomplete in advanced cases. _Folate deficiency_: Source - leafy greens, legumes, fortified flour. Absorption: proximal jejunum. Stores ~ 3-4 months (much shorter than B12). Causes: (1) _Inadequate intake_ - alcoholism (most common in developed world; multiple mechanisms - intake, malabsorption, increased turnover, hepatic), elderly, poor diet; (2) _Malabsorption_ - coeliac, tropical sprue, Crohn; (3) _Increased demand_ - pregnancy (essential for neural tube development; ~ 600 mcg/day; supplement 400 mcg/day from preconception, 5 mg if high risk), lactation, haemolysis (chronic), exfoliative skin diseases, malignancy; (4) _Drugs_ - methotrexate (DHFR inhibitor - antifolate), trimethoprim (also antifolate), phenytoin, sulfasalazine. Clinical features: similar haematological to B12 (megaloblastic anaemia, macrocytosis, hypersegmented neutrophils); glossitis; _NO_ neurological features (key distinction from B12). Investigation: low serum folate (best taken before any fortified food or supplement); low RBC folate (more reliable for chronic stores); raised homocysteine; _normal_ MMA (key difference from B12 deficiency); blood film. Treatment: oral folic acid 5 mg daily for 4 months (until full repletion); pregnancy - 400 mcg from preconception (5 mg if high risk - prior NTD baby, antiepileptic drugs, BMI > 30, T1DM, sickle cell, thalassaemia). Critical caveat: when both B12 and folate are deficient, _treat B12 first_. Giving folate alone in B12 deficiency can correct anaemia but allow neurological deterioration to progress. Differentials for megaloblastic without B12/folate deficiency: (1) Drugs (methotrexate, hydroxyurea, antiretrovirals, phenytoin, mycophenolate); (2) MDS; (3) Inherited disorders (Imerslund-Gräsbeck, congenital folate malabsorption). Differentials for macrocytic anaemia in general: alcohol, hypothyroidism, liver disease, MDS, drugs. Functional B12 deficiency: nitrous oxide (recreational use - increasing concern; oxidises cobalt, inactivates B12) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: Megaloblastic anaemia teaches the importance of nucleotide synthesis cofactors. B12 and folate appear similar haematologically but B12 has neurological features that make distinction critical. The MMA-homocysteine pattern (both raised in B12; only homocysteine in folate) is a useful biochemical discriminator.

Hints:

1. Megaloblastic = macrocytic + hypersegmented neutrophils + dyserythropoiesis.
2. B12: PA, malabsorption, vegan, drugs - includes neurological features.
3. Folate: alcohol, intake, pregnancy, drugs - no neurology.
4. Treat B12 first if both deficient.

Stem: Describe haemolytic anaemia. What are the major causes, diagnostic markers, and management approaches?

Model answer: _Haemolytic anaemia_ - shortened RBC lifespan with increased destruction; can overcome marrow capacity to compensate. Classification: (1) _Inherited_ - haemoglobinopathies (sickle cell, thalassaemia), enzyme deficiencies (G6PD, pyruvate kinase), membrane defects (hereditary spherocytosis, elliptocytosis, ovalocytosis). (2) _Acquired_ - immune (autoimmune - warm vs cold; alloimmune - transfusion reactions, HDFN), microangiopathic (TTP, HUS, DIC, HELLP, malignant hypertension, mechanical heart valves), drugs, infections (malaria, babesiosis, Clostridium), PNH, hypersplenism. Sites: (a) _Intravascular_ - within blood vessels (PNH, ABO mismatch, severe G6PD crisis, mechanical, severe AIHA); (b) _Extravascular_ - in spleen/liver macrophages (hereditary spherocytosis, AIHA, sickle cell, thalassaemia). Diagnostic markers - common to all haemolysis: (1) _Anaemia_ - normocytic typically (microcytic in thalassaemia; macrocytic with high reticulocytosis - reticulocytes are larger); (2) _Reticulocytosis_ - marrow response (RPI > 2-3); (3) _Raised LDH_ - released from lysed RBCs; (4) _Raised unconjugated bilirubin_ - increased haem breakdown; (5) _Reduced haptoglobin_ - binds free Hb; (6) Blood film abnormalities (specific to cause). Markers specific for _intravascular_: free haemoglobinaemia, haemoglobinuria (red urine, dipstick positive for blood without RBCs), hemosiderinuria (later), schistocytes (mechanical), methaemalbumin. Specific causes: (1) _Hereditary spherocytosis_ (HS) - autosomal dominant (~ 75%) defect in RBC cytoskeleton (ankyrin, spectrin, band 3, protein 4.2); RBCs lose membrane in spleen, become spherocytes (smaller, denser, less deformable); jaundice, splenomegaly, anaemia; pigment gallstones; aplastic crisis with parvovirus B19 (transient red cell aplasia). Diagnosis: family history, blood film (spherocytes), eosin-5-maleimide (EMA) flow cytometry (replaces older osmotic fragility test). Treatment: folic acid; splenectomy in moderate-severe (rarely complete cure but reduces haemolysis; consider in childhood for severe; prophylactic vaccination + lifelong antibiotic). (2) _G6PD deficiency_ - X-linked; most common enzyme deficiency in humans (~ 400 million worldwide); G6PD generates NADPH from G6P -> protects against oxidative stress (regenerates GSH from GSSG). Without it, oxidative stress -> denatured Hb (Heinz bodies), bite cells (splenic removal); episodic intravascular haemolysis triggered by oxidants - drugs (sulfonamides, dapsone, primaquine, nitrofurantoin, methylene blue, rasburicase), infections, fava beans, mothballs. African and Mediterranean variants common (the latter more severe). Diagnosis: G6PD activity (false-normal during acute episode due to retics; repeat at 6 weeks). Treatment: avoid triggers; supportive care during crisis; rarely transfusion. (3) _Pyruvate kinase deficiency_ - autosomal recessive; chronic non-spherocytic haemolysis; raised 2,3-BPG (R-shifted O2 curve - tolerated lower Hb); treat splenectomy, supportive. (4) _Sickle cell anaemia_ - homozygous HbSS; valine for glutamate at β6; HbS polymerises in deoxy state -> sickling. See dedicated discussion. (5) _Thalassaemia_ - quantitative Hb defect; ineffective erythropoiesis + extravascular haemolysis. (6) _Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia (AIHA)_: (a) _Warm AIHA_ - IgG against RBC; binds at 37°C; extravascular splenic haemolysis; spherocytes on film; positive direct antiglobulin (Coombs) test (DAT) - IgG +/- C3; causes: idiopathic, lymphoproliferative (CLL, lymphoma), autoimmune (SLE), drugs (methyldopa - rare now). Treatment: corticosteroids first-line (1 mg/kg/day), rituximab, splenectomy (in refractory), other immunosuppressants; folic acid; transfusion challenging (find least incompatible). (b) _Cold AIHA_ - IgM at low temperatures; intravascular haemolysis; cold agglutinins on film; Coombs C3+; causes: idiopathic, Mycoplasma, EBV, lymphoma. Treatment: keep warm, rituximab, sutimlimab (anti-C1s, FDA-approved 2022). (c) Drug-induced haemolysis - several mechanisms (immune complex - eg, ceftriaxone; drug-adsorption - eg, penicillin; autoimmune - eg, methyldopa). (7) _Microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (MAHA)_ - mechanical RBC fragmentation as they pass through abnormal microvasculature; schistocytes on film: (a) TTP - ADAMTS13 deficiency (congenital or acquired anti-ADAMTS13 IgG; pentad: MAHA + thrombocytopenia + AKI + neurological + fever; emergency - plasma exchange); (b) HUS - typical (E. coli O157 Shiga toxin) or atypical (complement dysregulation - eculizumab); (c) DIC - many causes; (d) HELLP syndrome (pregnancy: haemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, low platelets); (e) Malignant hypertension; (f) Mechanical heart valves. (8) _Paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH)_ - acquired clonal HSC mutation in PIGA -> defective GPI anchor -> deficient CD55, CD59 on RBCs -> uncontrolled complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis. Triad: haemolysis + thrombosis (especially hepatic, splanchnic) + bone marrow failure. Diagnosis: flow cytometry (CD55, CD59 deficiency on RBCs and granulocytes; FLAER for GPI-AP deficiency). Treatment: eculizumab (anti-C5) and ravulizumab - blocks terminal complement; transformative; thrombosis prophylaxis; HSCT for severe cases. (9) _Malaria_ - intracellular parasitic infection of RBC; haemolysis + cytokine storm + sequestration. Common diagnostic challenges: distinguishing thalassaemia trait from IDA in microcytic anaemia (Hb electrophoresis); spherocytes on AIHA vs HS (DAT distinguishes); MAHA with thrombocytopenia and AKI requires urgent TTP/HUS workup [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: Haemolytic anaemia is a vast category united by accelerated RBC destruction. The diagnostic algorithm starts with confirming haemolysis (retic, LDH, bilirubin, haptoglobin) then identifying the cause (film, DAT, electrophoresis, enzyme assay, flow cytometry). Treatment varies hugely - from steroids (warm AIHA) to plasma exchange (TTP) to eculizumab (PNH, atypical HUS, cold AIHA).

Hints:

1. Haemolysis = retic up + LDH up + bilirubin up + haptoglobin down.
2. Inherited: HS, G6PD, sickle, thalassaemia.
3. Acquired: AIHA (warm/cold), MAHA (TTP, HUS, DIC), PNH, infection, mechanical.

Stem: Describe sickle cell disease. What are the major complications, and how is each managed?

Model answer: _Sickle cell disease (SCD)_ - autosomal recessive haemoglobinopathy; mutation in HBB gene (Glu6Val on β chain) -> HbS; homozygous (HbSS) is sickle cell anaemia; heterozygous (HbAS) is sickle trait (largely asymptomatic; mild renal concentrating defect, splenic infarction at altitude). Compound heterozygotes - HbSC (sickle cell + HbC; milder, more frequent retinopathy), HbS-β-thalassaemia (depends on β-thalassaemia type). Pathophysiology: HbS polymerises in _deoxygenated_ state -> RBCs sickle (irreversible after multiple cycles); sickled RBCs adhere to endothelium, occlude microvasculature, are removed by spleen (extravascular haemolysis); chronic haemolysis + vaso-occlusion + endothelial dysfunction + inflammation. Vaso-occlusion underlies most acute complications. Epidemiology: ~ 1/350 African Americans homozygous; protective effect of HbAS against falciparum malaria (heterozygote advantage in endemic regions); high prevalence sub-Saharan Africa, Mediterranean, Middle East, South Asia. Acute complications: (1) _Vaso-occlusive crisis (VOC)_ - severe pain in bones, limbs, back, chest, abdomen; common precipitants (infection, dehydration, cold, hypoxia, stress, altitude). Management - hydration (avoid overload), opioid analgesia (often need PCA; do NOT undertreat - addiction risk vastly overstated; opioid stigma a barrier to care), warmth, oxygen if hypoxic, oral or IV fluids; rule out infection. (2) _Acute chest syndrome (ACS)_ - pulmonary infarct/infection; new infiltrate + fever/respiratory symptoms; leading cause of mortality in SCD; multifactorial - infection (chlamydia, mycoplasma, viral), fat embolism (from bone marrow infarct), pulmonary microvascular sickling, hypoventilation. Management - antibiotics empirical (covering atypicals), oxygen, analgesia, _exchange transfusion_ (rapid removal of sickle Hb; especially severe cases or rising Hb above 90), bronchodilators, incentive spirometry. (3) _Stroke_ - 11% of children with HbSS by age 20; ischaemic in children, haemorrhagic in adults; transcranial Doppler (TCD) screening for high velocity (> 200 cm/s) in children identifies high risk; chronic transfusion programme reduces stroke risk. New treatments may change paradigm. (4) _Splenic sequestration_ - blood pooled in spleen; rapid drop in Hb, hypovolaemia; emergency transfusion; risk of subsequent functional asplenia. (5) _Aplastic crisis_ - parvovirus B19 infection -> transient red cell aplasia; rapid drop in Hb without corresponding rise in retics; supportive transfusion until recovery. (6) _Priapism_ - prolonged painful erection from sickling in cavernous tissue; ischaemic priapism is medical emergency - aspiration, intracavernosal phenylephrine, exchange transfusion. (7) _Acute multiorgan failure_. Chronic complications: (1) _Functional asplenia_ - by ~ 5 years from autoinfarction; _risk of overwhelming sepsis_ with encapsulated organisms (pneumococcus, Haemophilus, meningococcus, Salmonella - especially osteomyelitis). Vaccinations + lifelong penicillin V (children) or amoxicillin prophylaxis. (2) _Avascular necrosis_ (AVN) of femoral and humeral heads - bone infarction; hip replacement often needed. (3) _Retinopathy_ - especially HbSC; proliferative; laser treatment. (4) _Renal_ - hyposthenuria, papillary necrosis, FSGS, CKD, RCC (medullary) risk. (5) _Pulmonary hypertension_ - chronic hypoxia, intravascular haemolysis (free Hb scavenges NO), chronic emboli; rising mortality risk. (6) _Cholelithiasis_ - pigment stones from chronic haemolysis. (7) _Leg ulcers_ - chronic, painful. (8) _Bone changes_ - dactylitis ("hand-foot syndrome" - first presentation often), osteomyelitis (Salmonella, S. aureus), AVN, osteopenia. (9) _Pregnancy complications_ - higher maternal/fetal mortality; increased crises; transfusion may be needed. (10) Iron overload from chronic transfusion. Management: (1) _Disease-modifying therapy_: (a) _Hydroxyurea_ - increases HbF (which doesn't sickle); reduces VOC, ACS, mortality; mainstay since 1995; (b) _L-glutamine_ (Endari) - reduces oxidative damage; modest VOC reduction; (c) _Crizanlizumab_ (anti-P-selectin) - reduces VOC; (d) _Voxelotor_ - HbS oxygenation modifier; reduces haemolysis but evidence on clinical events evolving; (e) _Disease-modifying transfusion_ - exchange transfusion for stroke prevention, ACS, severe disease; (f) _Curative therapies_: HSC transplant (sibling-matched) - >85% cure for selected; _gene therapy_ (FDA 2023 - exa-cel CRISPR-based and lovo-cel gene addition for severe SCD) - landmark advances; (g) Splenectomy in selected (sequestration). (2) _Prevention_: vaccinations (pneumococcal, Hib, meningococcal, influenza, COVID, hepatitis), penicillin prophylaxis until ~ 5 years (children), folic acid, hydration, avoidance of triggers (cold, dehydration, altitude, hypoxia). (3) _Multidisciplinary care_ - haematologist, primary care, pain specialist, psychologist (chronic disease burden), dental (infection foci), social work; transition from paediatric to adult care challenging. (4) _Newborn screening_ - heel-prick blood sample identifies SCD in many countries; allows early intervention. (5) _Genetic counselling_ - autosomal recessive inheritance; carrier screening for at-risk populations; pre-conception/prenatal counselling. Outcomes: median life expectancy historically ~ 40 years, improving substantially with hydroxyurea and modern care; gene therapy approvals (2023) may transform prognosis for those with access [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: SCD is a multi-system disease producing acute crises and chronic organ damage. Hydroxyurea has been the mainstay since 1995, shown to reduce mortality. Recent gene therapy approvals are landmark advances. The opioid stigma in SCD is a notable failing of medicine - patients in pain crises are often undertreated due to misplaced concerns about addiction.

Hints:

1. HbS polymerises in deoxy state - RBCs sickle - vaso-occlusion + haemolysis.
2. Major acute: VOC, ACS, stroke, sequestration, aplastic crisis.
3. Chronic: AVN, retinopathy, renal, pulmonary HTN, leg ulcers.
4. Hydroxyurea + transfusion + gene therapy (2023).

Stem: Describe the thalassaemias. How do α and β thalassaemia differ in genetics, pathophysiology, and clinical presentation?

Model answer: _Thalassaemias_ - inherited disorders of haemoglobin synthesis (quantitative defect; reduced production of α or β chain). Result: imbalance between α and β chains -> ineffective erythropoiesis (intramedullary haemolysis) + extravascular haemolysis -> microcytic hypochromic anaemia + iron overload (transfusion + increased gut absorption) + bone marrow expansion. Common in Mediterranean, Middle East, South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia (heterozygote advantage against malaria in some populations). _α-Thalassaemia_: 4 α genes total (2 on each chromosome 16); deletions or mutations of 1, 2, 3, or 4 genes. (1) _Silent carrier_ (1 gene affected) - asymptomatic, normal indices; (2) _α-thalassaemia trait_ (2 genes - cis or trans) - mild microcytic anaemia, normal Hb; differential with IDA (normal/raised ferritin in trait); (3) _HbH disease_ (3 genes affected) - excess β chains form HbH (β4 tetramers); chronic haemolytic anaemia, splenomegaly, sometimes transfusion dependent, jaundice, gallstones, leg ulcers; iron loading; (4) _Hb Bart hydrops fetalis_ (4 genes - α^0 homozygous) - excess γ chains form Hb Bart (γ4); incompatible with extrauterine life - fetal demise or severe hydrops in late pregnancy; intrauterine transfusion or postnatal HSCT possible. _β-Thalassaemia_: 2 β genes total; mutations include β^+ (reduced synthesis), β^0 (no synthesis); diverse mutations. (1) _β-thalassaemia minor (trait)_ - heterozygous; asymptomatic microcytic anaemia (often mistaken for IDA - distinguish by Hb electrophoresis showing raised HbA2 > 3.5%, sometimes raised HbF); MCV very low for the degree of anaemia; ferritin normal/high; family history; (2) _β-thalassaemia intermedia_ - varied genotypes; symptomatic but not transfusion-dependent typically; less severe than major; (3) _β-thalassaemia major (Cooley anaemia)_ - homozygous or compound heterozygous; severe transfusion-dependent anaemia from infancy (4-6 months when γ to β switch should complete); failure to thrive, frontal bossing, maxillary hypertrophy ("chipmunk facies"), hepatosplenomegaly (extramedullary haematopoiesis), pathological fractures, pallor; iron overload (organ damage). Untreated - early death. Management: (1) _Regular transfusion_ (every 3-4 weeks) to maintain Hb > 90-100, suppressing endogenous erythropoiesis and growth; (2) _Iron chelation_ (deferoxamine SC infusion - older; deferasirox oral; deferiprone oral) - reduces transfusional iron overload; assess iron with ferritin and MRI T2* (cardiac and hepatic); (3) *Splenectomy* in selected (transfusion requirement rising significantly); risk of overwhelming sepsis - vaccinations, lifelong antibiotic; (4) *Hydroxyurea* - increases HbF; modest benefit in transfusion-dependent; (5) *Luspatercept* - newer agent (TGF-β ligand trap); reduces transfusion requirement. (6) *Curative*: HSC transplant (sibling-matched best; haploidentical and unrelated also possible; conditioning regimens; risk of GVHD); gene therapy (zynteglo - lovotibeglogene autotemcel - β-globin gene addition; FDA 2022; expensive but potentially curative for transfusion-dependent β-thalassaemia); CRISPR-based gene editing (BCL11A targeting to reactivate HbF) - exa-cel / Casgevy approved 2023 for transfusion-dependent β-thalassaemia and SCD. Diagnosis: family history, ethnicity, blood film (microcytosis, hypochromia, target cells, basophilic stippling), Hb electrophoresis (raised HbA2 in β-trait; HbH/Bart in α; HbF). Genetic counselling and prenatal screening common in at-risk populations. Differential: IDA (microcytic hypochromic); thalassaemia and IDA can co-exist - check ferritin in microcytic anaemia. The Mentzer index (MCV/RBC count) > 13 suggests IDA, < 13 suggests thalassaemia (helpful but not definitive). Iron supplementation in thalassaemia trait is *unhelpful\* and may cause iron overload; do not give iron without proven deficiency [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Thalassaemias illustrate how quantitative gene defects (vs qualitative in sickle) produce profound anaemia. The clinical spectrum from silent carrier to hydrops fetalis or Cooley anaemia depends on number of affected genes. Modern gene therapy approvals are transforming prognosis for transfusion-dependent disease.

Stem: Describe disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC). How does it cause both bleeding and thrombosis, and how is it managed?

Model answer: _Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)_ - acquired syndrome of widespread activation of coagulation -> intravascular fibrin formation + microvascular thrombosis + consumption of platelets/clotting factors -> bleeding. Always _secondary_ to an underlying cause; treat both. Causes: (1) _Sepsis_ - especially gram-negative; release of LPS, cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6), tissue factor expression by monocytes/endothelium; most common cause; (2) _Trauma_ - massive tissue injury releases tissue factor; haemorrhagic shock; head injury (brain rich in TF); (3) _Malignancy_ - especially adenocarcinomas (mucin-secreting - pancreatic, gastric, colorectal, ovarian, lung, prostate); promyelocytic leukaemia (APL - profound DIC; emergency); chronic compensated DIC; (4) _Obstetric_ - placental abruption (most common - massive TF release), amniotic fluid embolism (severe); HELLP, severe pre-eclampsia, retained dead fetus, septic abortion; (5) _Severe burns, frostbite_; (6) _Severe transfusion reaction_; (7) _Snake envenomation_; (8) _Pancreatitis_ (severe); (9) _Liver failure_ (mimics DIC; difficult differential); (10) _Aortic aneurysm_ (chronic, low-grade). Pathophysiology: (1) Initiation - tissue factor exposure + endothelial damage; (2) Amplification - thrombin generation; (3) Microvascular thrombosis - fibrin in capillaries; multiorgan dysfunction (kidney, liver, lung - ARDS, brain); (4) Consumption - platelets, clotting factors (fibrinogen, V, VIII), antithrombin, protein C; (5) Compensatory fibrinolysis - plasmin activation -> further depletion; raised D-dimers, FDPs; can dominate ("hyperfibrinolytic") -> bleeding predominant. Net result: simultaneous bleeding (consumption) and thrombosis (microvascular fibrin). Clinical features: (1) _Bleeding_ - oozing from venepunctures, IV sites; ecchymoses, petechiae; mucosal bleeding (oral, GI, GU); haematuria; surgical wound; ICH; (2) _Thrombotic_ - microvascular dysfunction across organs - AKI, acute liver failure, ARDS, encephalopathy, ischaemic limb (purpura fulminans - severe), digital gangrene; (3) Underlying cause features (sepsis, abruption, etc); (4) Subclinical DIC may be detected only by labs (early). Diagnosis: (1) Underlying condition consistent with DIC; (2) ISTH score or laboratory pattern: low platelets (declining), prolonged PT and aPTT, low fibrinogen (mostly < 1 g/L though can be relatively normal in early/sepsis), raised D-dimer (cardinal feature - reflects fibrinolysis of cross-linked fibrin); blood film - schistocytes (microangiopathic). Differential: TTP/HUS (more thrombocytopenia + AKI + neurological + fever; ADAMTS13 helps), HELLP (pregnancy + liver dysfunction), hepatic failure (similar coagulopathy but no microvascular thrombosis; raised LFTs), severe vitamin K deficiency, hypothermia coagulopathy. Management: (1) _Treat the underlying cause_ - antibiotics for sepsis (often broad-spectrum + source control), delivery for placental abruption, ATRA + chemotherapy for APL (induces DIC remission), surgery for trauma, etc; (2) _Supportive_: (a) Platelets - if thrombocytopenia + bleeding (target > 50, > 100 if CNS/eye); (b) FFP - replace clotting factors; (c) Cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate for fibrinogen < 1.5 g/L; (d) PCC if life-threatening bleeding from coagulopathy; (e) RBC transfusion as needed; (3) _Anticoagulation_ - controversial; LMWH may help in chronic compensated DIC (Trousseau syndrome with malignancy) and selected cases; _not_ standard in acute DIC because of bleeding risk; (4) _Antifibrinolytics_ (tranexamic acid) - in selected cases; controversial in DIC because they may worsen microvascular thrombosis; useful in _hyperfibrinolytic_ DIC (eg, APL) where bleeding predominant; (5) _Specific agents_: recombinant thrombomodulin (used in Japan, Asia; mixed evidence); activated protein C (drotrecogin - withdrawn; PROWESS-SHOCK negative); antithrombin concentrate; (6) Minimise iatrogenic bleeding (avoid unnecessary punctures, lines, IM injections). Prognosis depends on underlying cause; mortality high in sepsis-induced DIC. Watch for tissue ischaemia (purpura fulminans), thromboembolic events, multi-organ failure. Recovery requires resolution of underlying cause - then coagulation factors return over hours-days [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: DIC illustrates how unchecked activation of coagulation produces both thrombosis and bleeding. The dual pathology requires management of both - treating bleeding with replacement, addressing thrombosis through cause control. Modern understanding distinguishes acute decompensated from chronic compensated DIC and identifies hyperfibrinolytic vs procoagulant phenotypes.

Hints:

1. DIC = secondary; bleeding + thrombosis; consumptive coagulopathy.
2. Sepsis, trauma, malignancy, obstetric are major causes.
3. Treat underlying + replacement (platelets, FFP, cryo) + cautious anticoagulation.

- Lists ≥ 3 underlying causes.

- Notes that anticoagulation is controversial.

No splenomegaly typically (vs MDS, leukaemia). Diagnosis: (1) FBC - pancytopenia of varying severity; (2) Reticulocytes - very low; (3) Blood film - no abnormal cells (vs leukaemia/MDS); (4) Bone marrow aspirate + trephine - hypocellular without dysplasia or infiltration; (5) Cytogenetics, flow cytometry, viral testing, autoimmune/PNH workup; (6) Inherited workup - chromosomal breakage testing (Fanconi - mitomycin C or DEB stress test), telomere length (DC), genetic panel; (7) HLA typing for transplant planning. Management: (1) _Supportive_ - PRBC + platelet transfusions (irradiated, leukoreduced, CMV-screened), antibiotics for infection (broad-spectrum at first sign of fever in severe AA), antifungals as needed, antivirals; G-CSF can be used short-term but does not reverse AA. (2) _Definitive_: (a) _Allogeneic HSCT from HLA-matched sibling donor_ - best outcome for younger patients (< 50 years; some centres extend); first-line in severe AA; conditioning (Cy + ATG); ~ 80-85% survival in young adults with sibling donor. (d) Refractory/relapsed AA: matched unrelated donor (MUD) HSCT, haploidentical HSCT, repeated IST. (e) Inherited bone marrow failure syndromes - HSCT often curative (with reduced-intensity conditioning for Fanconi due to chromosomal sensitivity to alkylators); androgens (oxymetholone, danazol) may help DC; gene therapy on horizon. (3) Long-term: monitor for clonal evolution (MDS, AML, PNH) - common in AA; second cancers in inherited BMF; iron overload from transfusions. (2) _Pure red cell aplasia (PRCA)_ - selective erythroid failure; reticulocytes very low; near-normal WBC and platelets. Causes: parvovirus B19 (in immunocompromised - chronic; typical patients - transient), thymoma, autoimmune (associated with autoimmune disease, drugs - phenytoin, isoniazid, EPO antibodies), inherited (Diamond-Blackfan), large granular lymphocyte leukaemia. Treatment - underlying (IVIG for parvovirus, thymectomy for thymoma, immunosuppression for autoimmune). (3) _Myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS)_ - clonal HSC disease with ineffective haematopoiesis -> cytopenia + dysplasia; risk of progression to AML. WHO/IPSS-R stratification. Treatment: lower-risk - supportive, EPO, lenalidomide for del(5q); higher-risk - hypomethylating agents (azacitidine, decitabine), HSCT [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Aplastic anaemia is a striking syndrome - empty marrow with cytopenias. Distinction from MDS, leukaemia, and inherited BMF is essential because management differs. Allogeneic HSCT cures young patients with matched donors; immunosuppression with ATG + ciclosporin + eltrombopag is the alternative. The clonal evolution risk (PNH, MDS, AML) requires lifelong monitoring.

Hints:

1. AA = pancytopenia + hypocellular marrow without infiltration.
2. Causes: idiopathic (immune), drugs, viral, inherited.
3. Treatment: HSCT (sibling-matched, young) or IST (ATG + CsA + eltrombopag).

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names AA and the diagnostic criteria.
- Lists ≥ 3 causes (idiopathic, drugs, viral, inherited).
- Distinguishes from MDS and leukaemia.
- Names HSCT vs IST as definitive treatments.
- Mentions clonal evolution risk.

A Yellow answer covers AA but misses inherited BMF or treatment options.

A Red answer treats AA as one of many anaemias.

Common misconceptions:

- "Inherited bone marrow failure is rare and irrelevant.": Correction: Important to identify - changes management (chromosomal fragility in Fanconi affects conditioning regimen), surveillance (cancer risk), and family counselling.
- "HSCT is too risky for AA.": Correction: In young patients with matched sibling donor, HSCT is the best treatment with > 80% survival; consider early.

QUESTION 17
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the design principles of haematopoiesis. How does the body produce 4 x 10^11 cells per day reliably?

Model answer: _Haematopoiesis_ produces ~ 4 x 10^11 blood cells/day - one of the largest production systems in the body. Design principles: (1) _Hierarchical organisation_ - rare HSCs (~ 1 in 10,000-100,000 marrow cells) -> abundant differentiated cells via amplifying intermediate progenitors. Each HSC division asymmetric: one daughter retains stemness, one differentiates - preserves the stem pool while producing committed progeny. (2) _Lineage-specific signals_ - cytokines (EPO, TPO, G-CSF, GM-CSF) drive proliferation/differentiation of committed progenitors. Each lineage has dedicated regulatory loops (eg, EPO regulated by hypoxia; TPO inversely with platelet mass). (3) _Niche regulation_ - HSCs depend on specific bone marrow microenvironment (endothelial cells, mesenchymal stromal cells, osteoblasts, sympathetic nerves) that maintains quiescence and self-renewal; CXCL12/CXCR4, SCF/Kit, Notch, Wnt signalling. Disruption of niche -> mobilisation (used clinically for HSC harvesting with G-CSF or plerixafor). (4) _Quiescence + reserves_ - most HSCs are dormant; protects from damage and exhaustion; emergency activation when needed. Reserves can support increased demand 6-8 fold without exhaustion (e.g., chronic haemolysis, hypoxia). (5) _Age-related decline_ - HSC self-renewal capacity diminishes with age; clonal haematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) - clonal expansion of mutant HSCs in older adults; predisposes to MDS/AML and cardiovascular disease ("clonal haematopoiesis as cardiovascular risk factor"). (6) _Plasticity_ - lineage switching can occur in disease (e.g., MDS), tissue distribution of haematopoiesis changes with development (yolk sac -> liver -> bone marrow). (7) _Quality control_ - apoptosis of damaged cells; ineffective erythropoiesis in megaloblastic anaemia, MDS shows when this becomes excessive. (8) _Feedback loops_ - cytokine production responsive to circulating cell counts (EPO-erythropoiesis, TPO-platelet); negative feedback maintains homeostasis. (9) _Distinct sites for specific functions_ - thymus for T cell maturation; bone marrow for B cell development; lymph nodes/spleen for activation. Allows specialised microenvironments for each step. (10) _Mobilisation and homing_ - HSCs can leave marrow and recirculate; CXCR4/CXCL12 axis governs homing; clinically exploited (apheresis for HSC transplant; plerixafor mobilisation). Why so productive: (1) Rapid turnover of effector cells (neutrophils 6-10 hours; RBCs 120 days; platelets 7-10 days); (2) Need for surge capacity (infection, blood loss, hypoxia); (3) Replacement of senescent/damaged cells (RBC ~ 1% daily turnover); (4) Lymphoid clonal expansion during immune response. Why so reliable: (1) Stem cell pool quiescence and self-renewal protection; (2) Multiple lineage cytokines provide redundancy and specificity; (3) Niche signals coordinate; (4) Apoptosis removes damaged cells; (5) Mature cells have defined lifespans, preventing accumulation. Disorders reveal design failures: (1) HSC failure -> aplastic anaemia; (2) Lineage cytokine deficiency -> isolated cytopenias (renal anaemia from EPO loss; pure red cell aplasia from anti-EPO antibodies; ITP from anti-platelet antibodies in some cases); (3) Niche disruption -> myelofibrosis (MPN with marrow fibrosis displacing normal haematopoiesis); (4) Clonal disorders -> MDS, MPN, leukaemia; (5) Maturation arrest -> congenital BMF, leukaemia. Therapeutic advances: (1) Recombinant cytokines - EPO, G-CSF, TPO agonists; (2) HSCT (allogeneic, autologous) - cures haematological malignancies and inherited disorders; (3) Gene therapy - lentiviral (β-thalassaemia, SCD with lovo-cel), CRISPR (exa-cel/Casgevy for SCD and β-thal); (4) CAR-T cell therapy - re-engineering T cells to target malignancies; (5) Targeted therapies - JAK inhibitors (PMF, PV), BTK inhibitors (CLL, MCL), BCR-ABL TKIs (CML), FLT3 inhibitors (AML), proteasome inhibitors and immunomodulators (multiple myeloma); (6) Cellular therapies - mesenchymal stromal cells, regulatory T cells (in trials for GVHD, autoimmune); (7) Bispecific antibodies (BiTEs - blinatumomab for ALL); (8) Immune checkpoint inhibitors (cHL, some lymphomas). Future: stem cell expansion in vitro (UM171, others) - allows cord blood transplant; pluripotent-stem-cell-derived blood cells; in vivo gene editing. Clinical principle: respect the haematopoietic system - it is one of the most regulated in the body, and disorders span solely structural (aplastic) to clonal/neoplastic (MDS, leukaemia) to functional (cytokine, factor deficiencies). Modern haematology integrates molecular/genomic, cellular, and clinical perspectives [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Haematopoiesis is one of the most rapidly producing and regulated systems in the body. Hierarchical organisation, niche signals, and lineage-specific cytokines together produce ~ 4 x 10^11 cells daily reliably. Modern haematology builds on this framework with HSCT, gene therapy, and targeted molecular therapies that translate physiology into cure for many previously fatal diseases.

Hints:

1. Hierarchical (HSC -> differentiated) + niche + lineage cytokines.
2. Quiescence + reserves + feedback loops.
3. Disease reveals design failures.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Articulates ≥ 5 design principles.
- Identifies HSCs as rare, self-renewing, multipotent.
- Names ≥ 3 lineage-specific cytokines.
- Mentions clonal haematopoiesis (CHIP) and its implications.
- Discusses therapeutic advances (HSCT, gene therapy, CAR-T).

A Yellow answer covers basics but misses the design framework.

A Red answer treats haematopoiesis as a passive process.

Common misconceptions:

- "Stem cells are abundant.": Correction: True HSCs are rare (~ 1/10,000-100,000 marrow cells); rarity protects them from exhaustion.
- "Disorders of haematopoiesis are rare.": Correction: Cytopenias and clonal haematopoiesis are common in older adults; the implications for cardiovascular and cancer risk are emerging frontiers.

QUESTION 18
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Synthesise blood physiology. How do plasma, RBCs, leucocytes, and platelets together support oxygenation, immunity, and haemostasis?

Model answer: Blood physiology integrates four functional axes - transport, immunity, haemostasis, communication - implemented by plasma + cells. (1) _Plasma_ - aqueous matrix; transports gases (small fraction; most O2 in RBCs), nutrients (glucose, amino acids, lipids), wastes (urea, creatinine), hormones, drugs; carries proteins (albumin for oncotic pressure and transport; globulins for immunity and transport; clotting factors); maintains pH (bicarbonate buffer + plasma proteins); maintains volume (Starling forces). (2) _Erythrocytes_ - O2 and CO2 transport; CO2 carried as bicarbonate (~ 70%; chloride shift in tissues), bound to Hb (~ 23%; carbamino), dissolved (~ 7%); also acid-base buffering via Hb; production regulated by EPO; lifespan ~ 120 days; daily turnover ~ 1%. Disorders - anaemia (impaired O2 delivery), polycythaemia (raised viscosity, thrombosis), haemoglobinopathies. (3) _Leucocytes_ - immunity: innate (neutrophils, monocytes/macrophages, NK, dendritic) - rapid, broad; adaptive (T and B lymphocytes) - slow, specific, memory; coordinated through cytokines, chemokines, antigen presentation. Disorders - immunodeficiency (infection susceptibility), autoimmunity (self-attack), leukaemias/lymphomas, allergic. (4) _Platelets_ - haemostasis: primary plug formation; vWF + GPIb adhesion; ADP/TXA2 activation; GPIIb/IIIa aggregation; granule release. Disorders - thrombocytopenia (bleeding), thrombocytosis (thrombosis), thrombocytopathy (drugs - aspirin, P2Y12 antagonists). (5) _Coagulation system_ - secondary haemostasis; cascade leading to fibrin clot; balanced by anticoagulant systems (antithrombin, protein C/S) and fibrinolysis (plasmin); maintains haemostatic equilibrium. (6) _Communication_ - blood as conduit: hormones (endocrine), cytokines (immune), metabolic substrates, drugs, blood-borne signals to circumventricular organs (thirst, pH, osmolarity sensing). Integration examples: (a) _Acute blood loss_ - immediate vasoconstriction (autonomic), platelet activation (primary haemostasis), coagulation activation (secondary), erythropoietin release (chronic), bone marrow expansion of erythropoiesis, aldosterone/ADH for volume retention; (b) _Sepsis_ - widespread cytokine release, activation of coagulation (DIC risk), activation of complement, neutrophil mobilisation, fever, vascular permeability changes; (c) _Pregnancy_ - plasma volume expansion, RBC mass rise less proportionally (dilutional anaemia), hypercoagulable state, immunological tolerance of fetus; (d) _Exercise_ - increased cardiac output, RBC oxygen delivery to muscles, glycogen mobilisation, platelets activated at high intensity; (e) _Altitude_ - hypoxia stimulates EPO -> rise in RBC mass; pulmonary vasoconstriction; right ventricular hypertrophy. Design principles: (1) _Compartmentalisation_ - plasma and cellular elements separable but interactive; (2) _Specialisation_ - each cell type with defined function; (3) _Continuous renewal_ - rapid turnover; HSCs continuously replenish; (4) _Regulation_ - cytokines, hormones, and feedback loops maintain homeostasis; (5) _Multifunction_ - each component contributes to multiple physiological functions; (6) _Robustness_ - reserves and redundancy support physiological challenges. Clinical synthesis: (1) Blood is the most accessible body system; (2) Comprehensive blood evaluation (FBC, smear, biochemistry, coagulation, special tests) provides extensive diagnostic information; (3) Understanding blood physiology underpins large fraction of internal medicine - haematology, oncology, immunology, transfusion medicine, ICU care, anaesthesia, paediatrics; (4) Blood is the substrate for many therapies (transfusion, plasma exchange, leukapheresis, HSCT, blood products, gene therapy); (5) Many acute medical emergencies present through blood (sepsis, DIC, haemorrhage, severe anaemia, leukaemia, immune dysfunction). Disease patterns reveal physiology: anaemia from many causes, immunodeficiency from many causes, bleeding/thrombosis from many causes - each illustrates how the integrated system can fail. Modern haematology is built on this integrated understanding plus molecular detail, allowing precise diagnosis and increasingly precise therapy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Blood is one of the most accessible and informative body systems. Its components together support oxygenation, immunity, haemostasis, and intercellular communication. Each cell type has specialised function but disorders illustrate the integration - sepsis affects all systems; aplastic anaemia leaves blood elements deficient; pregnancy reshapes all axes. Modern haematology builds on this framework with molecular precision.

Hints:

1. Plasma + RBCs + WBCs + platelets cooperate.
2. Transport + immunity + haemostasis + communication.
3. Disease reveals integrated function.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the four major axes of blood function.
- Describes ≥ 3 specific cell-functional roles.
- Discusses ≥ 2 integration examples (acute blood loss, sepsis, pregnancy).
- Articulates design principles (compartmentalisation, specialisation, regulation).
- Synthesises rather than listing components.

A Yellow answer covers components but misses integration.

A Red answer treats blood as a passive fluid.

Common misconceptions:

- "Blood is just liquid for transport.": Correction: Blood is a complex tissue integrating four major axes; comprehensive evaluation reveals multiple body systems.
- "Each blood cell has one function.": Correction: RBCs not only carry oxygen but also CO2 and acid-base buffering; platelets contribute to inflammation and thrombosis; lymphocytes contribute to memory and tumour surveillance.
