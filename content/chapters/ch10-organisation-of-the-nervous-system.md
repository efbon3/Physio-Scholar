## Pass 1 — Divisions of the nervous system, cellular components, glial functions

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The principal anatomical and functional divisions of the nervous system are best classified as which of the following?

Correct answer: A central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) plus a peripheral nervous system (cranial and spinal nerves with their ganglia), with further functional subdivision into a somatic (voluntary) and autonomic (involuntary) limb, and an afferent (sensory) and efferent (motor) limb.

Distractors:

- "Cortical versus subcortical only." — Reveals misconception: student conflates an intracranial anatomical layering with the global division of the nervous system. Correction: cortical/subcortical describes regions within the brain itself; the principal global division is CNS versus PNS, not cortical versus subcortical.
- "Sympathetic versus parasympathetic only." — Reveals misconception: student names a subdivision of the autonomic limb as if it were the principal division. Correction: sympathetic and parasympathetic are subdivisions of the autonomic nervous system, which is itself one functional limb of the broader CNS+PNS scheme.
- "Sensory versus motor only." — Reveals misconception: student names the afferent/efferent functional axis as the only division. Correction: afferent and efferent describe the direction of information flow; they are one of several axes of subdivision and are not the principal anatomical division.

Explanation: The CNS comprises the brain and spinal cord; the PNS comprises the 12 pairs of cranial nerves, 31 pairs of spinal nerves, and their associated ganglia [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Guyton & Hall ch.46]. Functional subdivisions overlay this anatomical scheme: the somatic nervous system mediates voluntary control of skeletal muscle and conscious sensation; the autonomic nervous system controls smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands subconsciously, with sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric subdivisions [Costanzo ch.3]. The afferent (sensory) limb carries information toward the CNS; the efferent (motor) limb carries it away. These axes are not mutually exclusive — a single peripheral nerve fascicle commonly carries somatic motor, somatic sensory, and autonomic fibres together.

Hints:

1. There are two principal anatomical compartments, distinguished by whether the tissue lies inside the bony skull and vertebral canal or outside them.
2. Each compartment is then subdivided functionally — voluntary versus involuntary, and direction of information flow.
3. Sympathetic versus parasympathetic is a sub-subdivision of the autonomic limb, not the headline division of the whole nervous system.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Within the central nervous system, the most numerous cells are:

Correct answer: Glial cells (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, and ependymal cells), which outnumber neurons in most CNS regions; the long-quoted 10:1 ratio is a regional approximation but glia consistently exceed neurons in the cerebral cortex and white matter.

Distractors:

- "Neurons." — Reveals misconception: student assumes the named "thinking" cell of the CNS must also be the most numerous. Correction: neurons are the signalling cells but they are outnumbered by glia in most regions; modern stereology refines the ratio away from a strict 10:1 but does not reverse it.
- "Endothelial cells." — Reveals misconception: student picks vascular cells that line capillaries throughout the CNS. Correction: endothelial cells are abundant within the cerebral microvasculature but are far less numerous than neurons or glia in the parenchyma as a whole.
- "Macrophages." — Reveals misconception: student conflates resident microglia with infiltrating bone-marrow-derived macrophages. Correction: outside infection or injury, the CNS contains very few infiltrating macrophages; resident immune surveillance is performed by microglia, which are themselves a glial subtype.

Explanation: Glia outnumber neurons substantially in most CNS regions [Berne & Levy ch.4; Boron & Boulpaep ch.10]. The four classical CNS glial types — astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, ependymal cells — together perform structural support, myelination, immune surveillance, K⁺ and transmitter buffering, and CSF lining roles. The ratio is part of why grey matter is mostly cell bodies + synapses + glia, while white matter is myelinated axon tracts dominated by oligodendrocytes. Disorders of glia (multiple sclerosis, gliomas, Alexander disease) are at least as clinically important as disorders of neurons.

Hints:

1. The cells that do _not_ generate action potentials still outnumber the ones that do.
2. Four classical types: astrocyte, oligodendrocyte, microglia, ependymal.
3. The classical ratio quoted in textbooks is roughly 10:1 in favour of these support cells.

QUESTION 3
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Myelination of axons in the central nervous system is performed by:

Correct answer: Oligodendrocytes — a single oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple internodes on several axons (up to ~50 internodes), in contrast to PNS Schwann cells, where one Schwann cell wraps a single internode of one axon.

Distractors:

- "Schwann cells." — Reveals misconception: student applies the PNS pattern to the CNS. Correction: Schwann cells myelinate peripheral axons; CNS myelination is the job of oligodendrocytes and disorders of the two segregate clinically (multiple sclerosis attacks CNS oligodendrocyte myelin; Guillain-Barré and Charcot-Marie-Tooth involve PNS Schwann cell myelin).
- "Astrocytes." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a structural CNS support role to myelination. Correction: astrocytes do not myelinate; their roles are K⁺ buffering, glutamate uptake, BBB end-feet, and metabolic support. CNS myelin comes only from oligodendrocytes.
- "Microglia." — Reveals misconception: student conflates immune-surveillance cells with myelinating cells. Correction: microglia are CNS innate immune cells; they prune synapses and phagocytose debris, but they do not produce myelin.

Explanation: Oligodendrocytes ensheathe CNS axons; one cell can wrap segments of many axons [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Berne & Levy ch.4]. Schwann cells myelinate PNS axons one internode at a time. The biological difference matters clinically: multiple sclerosis is an autoimmune attack on CNS oligodendrocyte myelin; Guillain-Barré and Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease involve PNS Schwann cell myelin. CNS regeneration after injury is poor partly because oligodendrocyte-derived myelin contains inhibitory molecules (Nogo, MAG, OMgp); PNS regeneration is more successful in part because Schwann cells provide a permissive environment via the Bands of Büngner.

Hints:

1. The CNS and PNS use different cells for myelination.
2. The CNS cell wraps many axons; the PNS cell wraps one.
3. Multiple sclerosis is the disease that targets the CNS form.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: All of the following are recognised functions of astrocytes EXCEPT:

Correct answer: Direct generation of myelin sheaths around CNS axons — this is the role of oligodendrocytes; astrocytes do not myelinate.

Distractors:

- "K⁺ buffering through Kir4.1 channels." — Reveals misconception: student wrongly treats spatial K⁺ buffering as a non-astrocytic function. Correction: astrocytic Kir4.1 channels and gap-junctional coupling between astrocytes mediate spatial K⁺ buffering, redistributing extracellular K⁺ released by active neurons.
- "Glutamate uptake and conversion to glutamine." — Reveals misconception: student forgets that synaptic glutamate is cleared by astrocytes, not just by diffusion. Correction: astrocytes express EAAT1/GLAST and EAAT2/GLT-1 to take up cleft glutamate, convert it to glutamine via glutamine synthetase, and recycle it back to neurons (the glutamate–glutamine shuttle).
- "Contribution to the blood-brain barrier via end-feet on capillaries." — Reveals misconception: student ascribes BBB integrity to endothelium alone. Correction: while tight junctions between endothelial cells are the structural seal, astrocytic end-feet cover ~99% of the abluminal capillary surface and provide signalling that induces and maintains BBB-typical properties.

Explanation: Astrocytes have many roles but myelination is not among them [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Berne & Levy ch.4]. They provide structural scaffolding, buffer extracellular K⁺ (Kir4.1 + gap-junctional spatial buffering), take up cleft glutamate (EAAT1, EAAT2) and recycle it as glutamine, support neuronal metabolism via the lactate shuttle, contribute to the BBB through perivascular end-feet, release gliotransmitters (D-serine, ATP, glutamate), regulate cerebral blood flow through neurovascular coupling, and undergo reactive gliosis after injury (forming the glial scar). CNS myelin is produced exclusively by oligodendrocytes.

Hints:

1. Three of the four options are real astrocyte functions; one is the job of a different glial cell.
2. The cell that wraps CNS axons in myelin is not an astrocyte.
3. The astrocyte's relationship with the capillary is signalling, not myelination.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The resident innate immune cells of the CNS — derived from yolk-sac primitive macrophages and capable of antigen presentation, phagocytosis, and cytokine release — are:

Correct answer: Microglia, which colonise the CNS during embryogenesis from yolk-sac primitive macrophages, surveil the parenchyma in a ramified resting state, and become amoeboid and phagocytic on activation.

Distractors:

- "Astrocytes." — Reveals misconception: student conflates reactive gliosis with primary innate immunity. Correction: astrocytes participate in CNS injury responses but they are not the primary phagocytes or antigen-presenting cells; that role belongs to microglia.
- "Oligodendrocytes." — Reveals misconception: student forgets that oligodendrocytes are dedicated to myelination, not immunity. Correction: oligodendrocytes are themselves a target of autoimmunity in MS; they do not function as immune cells.
- "Ependymal cells." — Reveals misconception: student treats CSF-lining cells as immune sentinels. Correction: ependymal cells line the ventricles and central canal and contribute to CSF movement via cilia; they are not innate immune cells.

Explanation: Microglia are the brain's resident innate immune cells [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong]. In healthy brain they have ramified processes and dynamically surveil the parenchyma; with injury, infection, or neurodegeneration they retract processes, become amoeboid, phagocytose debris, present antigen, and release cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6). Crucially, they are _not_ derived from bone-marrow monocytes but from yolk-sac primitive macrophages that colonise the CNS during embryogenesis and self-renew locally. Activated microglia have been implicated in Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, making them a target of intense translational interest.

Hints:

1. The innate immune cells of the CNS are themselves a kind of glia.
2. Their developmental origin is unusual — yolk sac, not bone marrow.
3. They are the parenchymal counterparts of tissue macrophages elsewhere.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The cells that line the cerebral ventricles and the central canal of the spinal cord, contributing to CSF movement through ciliary beating, are:

Correct answer: Ependymal cells, a single-layered ciliated epithelium derived from neuroepithelium; the choroid plexus epithelium is a specialised modified ependyma that produces CSF.

Distractors:

- "Astrocytes." — Reveals misconception: student treats astrocytes as a generic CNS lining. Correction: astrocytes occupy the parenchyma; the ventricular surfaces are lined by ependymal cells, not astrocytes.
- "Microglia." — Reveals misconception: student picks the immune cell as a structural lining. Correction: microglia are scattered through the parenchyma and are not arranged as an epithelium lining the ventricles.
- "Oligodendrocytes." — Reveals misconception: student names the myelinating cell as a luminal epithelium. Correction: oligodendrocytes wrap white-matter axons in myelin; they do not line the ventricular system.

Explanation: Ependymal cells form the single-layer ciliated epithelium lining the ventricular system and central canal [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Berne & Levy ch.4]. Their cilia beat to assist CSF circulation; they participate in the brain–CSF interface, and specialised tanycytes in the third ventricle have transport and sensing functions linking CSF to the hypothalamus. The choroid plexus epithelium — a heavily modified, secretory ependyma — produces CSF by active Na⁺ secretion (apical Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) with osmotic water following.

Hints:

1. The lining of a fluid-filled cavity is usually an epithelium.
2. CSF circulation is helped along by beating cilia at the cavity surface.
3. The same lineage forms the choroid plexus that secretes the fluid.

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The total volume of cerebrospinal fluid in an average adult, distributed across the ventricles, cisterns, and subarachnoid space, is approximately:

Correct answer: ~150 mL — about 25 mL within the ventricles and ~125 mL in the cisterns and subarachnoid space; CSF is produced at ~500 mL/day, so the entire pool is replaced three to four times each day.

Distractors:

- "30–50 mL." — Reveals misconception: student underestimates the CSF compartment, perhaps confusing it with ventricular volume alone. Correction: ventricular CSF is only ~25 mL, but the subarachnoid space holds another ~125 mL; total CSF volume is ~150 mL.
- "300–500 mL." — Reveals misconception: student conflates daily _production_ with _total volume_. Correction: ~500 mL/day is the production rate; the total pool is ~150 mL, recycled multiple times daily.
- "1000 mL." — Reveals misconception: student assumes the cranial CSF compartment is much larger than the brain. Correction: total CSF volume is far smaller than the brain; ~150 mL is the consensus figure.

Explanation: Total CSF volume is ~150 mL (range ~100–160 mL), with production of ~0.3–0.4 mL/min (~500 mL/day) [Guyton & Hall ch.62; Berne & Levy ch.4]. Imbalance between production and absorption produces hydrocephalus — communicating if absorption fails at the arachnoid villi (post-meningitic, post-haemorrhagic, normal-pressure hydrocephalus) or non-communicating if there is obstruction along the ventricular pathway (aqueductal stenosis is the classic congenital cause). The high turnover means that drugs given intrathecally have a relatively short residence time in the CSF compartment.

Hints:

1. The total volume is much smaller than blood volume.
2. About one-sixth of the CSF lives inside the ventricular system; the rest is in subarachnoid spaces.
3. Daily _production_ is roughly three to four times _total volume_.

QUESTION 8
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Cerebrospinal fluid is produced primarily by:

Correct answer: The choroid plexuses of the lateral, third, and fourth ventricles, where modified ependymal epithelium actively secretes Na⁺ across the apical (CSF-facing) membrane via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, with water following osmotically; production is largely independent of intracranial pressure within the physiological range.

Distractors:

- "The arachnoid villi." — Reveals misconception: student reverses the direction of CSF traffic at the arachnoid villi. Correction: arachnoid villi _absorb_ CSF into the dural venous sinuses; they do not produce it.
- "The pia mater." — Reveals misconception: student treats the innermost meningeal layer as secretory. Correction: the pia is a thin connective-tissue investment of the brain surface; it is not secretory.
- "The ependymal cells of the central canal." — Reveals misconception: student assumes the spinal central canal is a major CSF source. Correction: ordinary ependymal cells contribute only a small amount of CSF; the bulk (~70%) comes from the choroid plexuses, which are specialised secretory ependyma in the ventricles.

Explanation: Choroid plexuses produce ~70% of CSF; the remainder comes from the ependymal lining of ventricles, periarteriolar perivascular spaces, and brain extracellular fluid [Guyton & Hall ch.62; Boron & Boulpaep ch.10]. Choroid plexus epithelium is polarised: the apical (CSF-facing) membrane carries Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, which actively pumps Na⁺ into the CSF, with HCO₃⁻ and Cl⁻ following and water moving osmotically through aquaporin-1. Carbonic anhydrase activity supplies HCO₃⁻, which is why carbonic-anhydrase inhibitors (acetazolamide) reduce CSF production — a mechanism exploited in idiopathic intracranial hypertension.

Hints:

1. CSF production is a _secretion_ requiring active Na⁺ transport, not a passive ultrafiltrate.
2. The secretory tissue lives inside the ventricles and is a modified epithelium.
3. Acetazolamide reduces this process by inhibiting carbonic anhydrase.

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The standard pathway of CSF flow from production to absorption is:

Correct answer: Lateral ventricles → foramen of Monro → third ventricle → aqueduct of Sylvius → fourth ventricle → foramina of Luschka and Magendie → subarachnoid space → arachnoid villi → dural venous sinuses.

Distractors:

- "Lateral ventricles → directly to the subarachnoid space." — Reveals misconception: student bypasses the third and fourth ventricles. Correction: CSF must pass sequentially through the third and fourth ventricles before exiting via Luschka and Magendie; there is no direct route from lateral ventricles to subarachnoid space.
- "Subarachnoid space → ventricles → spinal cord." — Reveals misconception: student reverses the direction of net CSF flow. Correction: net flow is _outward_ from the choroid plexus secretion site to the arachnoid villi; CSF does not normally flow from subarachnoid space back into the ventricles.
- "Choroid plexus → arachnoid villi directly." — Reveals misconception: student collapses the entire ventricular pathway into a one-step shortcut. Correction: the choroid plexus releases CSF into the ventricular system, which must traverse the foramen of Monro, aqueduct of Sylvius, and the fourth-ventricle outflow foramina before reaching the arachnoid villi.

Explanation: This is the canonical CSF circulation pathway [Guyton & Hall ch.62; Berne & Levy ch.4]. Obstruction at any step produces hydrocephalus — _non-communicating_ (obstructive) if the block is between ventricles and subarachnoid space (e.g., aqueductal stenosis, fourth-ventricle tumour) and _communicating_ if absorption fails at the arachnoid villi (e.g., post-haemorrhagic, post-meningitic). Movement of CSF along this pathway depends on choroid-plexus secretory pressure, ciliary beating, cardiac pulsations of the choroid arteries, and respiratory pressure cycles transmitted through the vertebral venous plexus.

Hints:

1. Production starts in the lateral ventricles; absorption ends at the dural venous sinuses.
2. There are three named "narrow" passages: foramen of Monro, aqueduct of Sylvius, and the foramina of Luschka and Magendie.
3. Each narrow passage is a potential site of obstructive hydrocephalus.

QUESTION 10
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The three meningeal layers, named from the outermost (closest to skull) to the innermost (closest to brain), are:

Correct answer: Dura mater (outer, tough, double-layered with venous sinuses between its layers), arachnoid mater (middle, with the CSF-filled subarachnoid space below it), and pia mater (innermost, intimately apposed to the brain surface and following gyri and sulci).

Distractors:

- "Pia mater → arachnoid mater → dura mater." — Reveals misconception: student reverses the order. Correction: the pia is innermost, against the brain; the dura is outermost, against the skull.
- "Arachnoid → dura → pia." — Reveals misconception: student misplaces the dura between arachnoid and pia. Correction: the arachnoid lies _between_ dura and pia; there is no meningeal arrangement that puts dura inside arachnoid.
- "Pia → dura → arachnoid." — Reveals misconception: student scrambles the order entirely. Correction: the unique anatomical fact is that the arachnoid lies between dura and pia, never outside the dura or between dura and skull.

Explanation: From skull inward: dura → arachnoid → pia [Berne & Levy ch.4; Boron & Boulpaep ch.10]. The dura is tough and double-layered, with venous sinuses running between its periosteal and meningeal layers. The arachnoid mater is a thin avascular membrane with the CSF-filled subarachnoid space below it, traversed by the cerebral arteries and cranial nerves. The pia mater is intimately attached to the brain surface and follows every gyrus and sulcus. The clinically critical haemorrhage syndromes follow these layers: epidural haematoma (between skull and dura, typically arterial — middle meningeal artery, lucid interval after temporal trauma); subdural haematoma (between dura and arachnoid, typically venous — torn bridging veins, slow accumulation in the elderly with brain atrophy); subarachnoid haemorrhage (within the subarachnoid space, typically arterial — ruptured berry aneurysm, "thunderclap" headache).

Hints:

1. The outer layer is the toughest and the inner layer is the most delicate.
2. The CSF-filled subarachnoid space sits between the middle and inner layers.
3. Each of the three potential or real spaces around these layers can host a clinically distinct haemorrhage.

QUESTION 11
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Compared with plasma, normal cerebrospinal fluid contains:

Correct answer: Lower glucose (~60–70% of plasma), much lower protein (<0.45 g/L lumbar), similar Na⁺, lower K⁺ and Ca²⁺, higher Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻, with pH ~7.33 and very few cells (<5 lymphocytes/mm³).

Distractors:

- "Higher protein concentration." — Reveals misconception: student treats CSF as a passive plasma ultrafiltrate. Correction: normal CSF protein is much _lower_ than plasma (<0.45 g/L) because the BBB excludes plasma proteins; raised CSF protein is a sensitive marker of barrier disruption or intrathecal synthesis.
- "Equal protein and glucose." — Reveals misconception: student equates CSF directly with plasma. Correction: normal CSF differs systematically — protein is much lower and glucose is ~60–70% of plasma; equality is not the normal state.
- "Higher glucose." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the glucose ratio. Correction: CSF glucose is _lower_ than plasma; markedly low CSF glucose is a feature of bacterial meningitis (organisms consuming it) and TB or fungal meningitis.

Explanation: Normal CSF: glucose ~2.5–4.4 mmol/L (i.e. ~60–70% of plasma); protein <0.45 g/L (lumbar) — much lower in cisternal samples; Na⁺ ~145 mmol/L (similar to plasma); K⁺ ~3 mmol/L (lower); Ca²⁺ ~1.2 mmol/L (lower); Mg²⁺ ~1.2 mmol/L (higher); Cl⁻ ~125 mmol/L (higher); pH ~7.33; <5 lymphocytes/mm³ [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong]. This composition reflects active secretion, not passive filtration. Pathologic patterns: bacterial meningitis lowers CSF glucose markedly, raises protein, and produces neutrophilic pleocytosis; viral meningitis preserves glucose, modestly raises protein, and produces a lymphocytic pleocytosis; TB / fungal meningitis lowers glucose, raises protein, and produces a lymphocytic pleocytosis.

Hints:

1. The choroid plexus actively secretes CSF, so its composition is _not_ identical to plasma.
2. Glucose and protein are the two most clinically useful "low-versus-plasma" markers.
3. Bacterial meningitis exaggerates the glucose difference further.

## Pass 2 — Coverings, CSF dynamics, ICP, and the blood-brain barrier

QUESTION 12
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The blood-brain barrier (BBB) is formed structurally and functionally by:

Correct answer: Tight junctions between cerebral capillary endothelial cells (with no fenestrations and few pinocytotic vesicles), supported by pericytes, astrocyte end-feet, and basement membrane — together constituting the neurovascular unit.

Distractors:

- "Astrocyte foot processes alone." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the seal to astrocytes rather than to endothelial tight junctions. Correction: astrocytic end-feet contribute to BBB induction and maintenance, but the structural seal is endothelial tight junctions.
- "Microglia." — Reveals misconception: student picks an immune cell as a barrier. Correction: microglia are immune sentinels in the parenchyma; they are not part of the capillary wall and do not form a barrier.
- "The pia mater." — Reveals misconception: student equates a meningeal layer with the BBB. Correction: the pia separates parenchyma from the subarachnoid space but is not the BBB; the BBB sits at the cerebral capillary endothelium.

Explanation: Cerebral capillary endothelium is unusual: tight junctions (claudin-5, occludin, ZO-1) seal the paracellular route, fenestrations are absent, and pinocytosis is minimal; transcellular transport is highly selective [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Berne & Levy ch.4]. Pericytes embrace the capillary; astrocyte end-feet cover ~99% of the abluminal surface and provide signalling that maintains BBB integrity. Together they form the **neurovascular unit**. Lipid-soluble molecules (O₂, CO₂, ethanol, anaesthetics, heroin) cross freely; water-soluble molecules require specific transporters (GLUT1 for glucose, MCT1 for monocarboxylates, large neutral amino acid transporter for amino acids, P-glycoprotein efflux for xenobiotics). Disruption of the BBB occurs in stroke, trauma, infection, and tumours and is exploited radiologically (contrast enhancement marks the leaky capillary).

Hints:

1. The "seal" is at the endothelial cell, not at the surrounding glia.
2. The named molecular components of the seal are tight-junction proteins.
3. Astrocytes induce and maintain barrier properties but do not themselves form the seal.

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: The "circumventricular organs" (CVOs) lack a normal blood-brain barrier so that the brain can sample the systemic circulation directly. They include:

Correct answer: Area postrema, organum vasculosum of the lamina terminalis (OVLT), subfornical organ, posterior pituitary, median eminence, and pineal gland — collectively the sites where the brain "tastes" blood for emetic toxins, osmolality, hormones, and circadian cues.

Distractors:

- "Hippocampus, amygdala, and cerebellum." — Reveals misconception: student names large grey-matter structures as if they were leaky to plasma. Correction: hippocampus, amygdala, and cerebellum sit behind a normal BBB and do not directly sample blood.
- "Frontal cortex and occipital cortex." — Reveals misconception: student picks neocortical regions. Correction: cortical regions have a normal BBB; CVOs are small, midline structures clustered around the third and fourth ventricles.
- "Caudate and putamen." — Reveals misconception: student names basal-ganglia structures as CVOs. Correction: the basal ganglia have a normal BBB; striatal capillaries are not fenestrated.

Explanation: CVOs have fenestrated capillaries and an incomplete BBB, allowing the brain to sense systemic signals [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong]. The area postrema senses emetic toxins (hence the chemoreceptor trigger zone for vomiting), the OVLT and subfornical organ sense osmolality and angiotensin II (driving thirst and ADH release), the posterior pituitary releases ADH and oxytocin into the systemic circulation, the median eminence is where hypothalamic releasing hormones reach portal vessels supplying the anterior pituitary, and the pineal gland secretes melatonin. Drugs that target CVOs include dopamine antagonists for chemotherapy-induced nausea (acting on D₂ receptors in the area postrema).

Hints:

1. Most CVOs are clustered along the midline near the third or fourth ventricle.
2. Each CVO has a sampling or secretory role linking blood and brain.
3. Vomiting and thirst are two prototypical CVO-mediated functions.

QUESTION 14
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: According to the Monro–Kellie doctrine, within the rigid adult skull the sum of brain, blood, and CSF volumes is constant; the clinical implication is that:

Correct answer: Compensation initially keeps ICP near normal as CSF is displaced into the spinal subarachnoid space and venous blood is reduced; once compensation is exhausted, small additional volume increases produce large ICP rises along a steep pressure–volume (compliance) curve.

Distractors:

- "Intracranial pressure rises immediately with any small volume change." — Reveals misconception: student ignores the compensatory phase. Correction: small early volume increases are buffered by CSF and venous-blood displacement; ICP rises sharply only after compensation is exhausted, producing the characteristic "knee" of the pressure–volume curve.
- "The skull expands to accommodate volume changes." — Reveals misconception: student treats the adult skull as deformable. Correction: the adult skull is rigid; expansion occurs only in infants whose fontanelles are still open, which is why infantile hydrocephalus presents with macrocephaly.
- "The brain shrinks to accommodate any volume." — Reveals misconception: student attributes infinite brain compliance. Correction: brain tissue is largely incompressible on a short time-scale; compensation is via CSF and venous blood, not parenchymal shrinkage.

Explanation: The fixed cranial volume must equal brain + blood + CSF [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Guyton & Hall ch.62]. Initial volume increases (e.g., from a slowly growing tumour or chronic subdural collection) are buffered by displacement of CSF into the spinal canal and reduction of intracranial venous blood; ICP stays near normal during this phase. When this compensation is exhausted, the pressure–volume curve becomes steep, and small additional volumes produce large pressure rises — explaining why a tumour can be silent for months and then decompensate suddenly. Acute insults (haemorrhage, severe oedema) outpace the slow CSF–blood compensation and cause rapid ICP rise from the outset.

Hints:

1. The skull is rigid in the adult, so total intracranial volume cannot change.
2. Brain tissue is essentially incompressible; the buffers are CSF and venous blood.
3. The pressure–volume curve has a flat compensated phase followed by a steep "knee".

CBF thresholds: at ~50 mL/100 g/min CBF is normal; below ~20 mL/100 g/min electrical activity ceases (oligaemia); below ~10 mL/100 g/min membrane failure and infarction occur within minutes. The penumbra — region of CBF between these thresholds — is electrically silent but still viable, and reperfusion within the therapeutic window can salvage it. Intravenous tPA within 4.5 hours of symptom onset for selected patients; mechanical thrombectomy for large-vessel occlusions up to 24 hours in some cases (with imaging-confirmed penumbra). Permissive blood-pressure management is the rule — aggressive lowering in the first 24 hours can worsen perfusion in territory with impaired autoregulation.

Hints:

1. The deficit is sudden, focal, and arterial-territory in pattern.
2. There is a salvageable rim of brain around an irretrievably damaged core.
3. Reperfusion has a strict time window measured in hours.

QUESTION 29
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The "Cushing reflex" — bradycardia, hypertension, and irregular respiration — in a patient with raised intracranial pressure represents:

Correct answer: A sympathetically driven systemic hypertension to maintain CPP in the face of rising ICP, with reflex bradycardia from baroreceptor activation and irregular (Cheyne–Stokes, then ataxic, then apnoeic) respiration from brain-stem compromise; it is an ominous late sign of impending brain-stem herniation.

Distractors:

- "A primary cardiovascular event." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the triad to cardiac pathology. Correction: the triad is a _neurological_ response to raised ICP, not a primary cardiac arrhythmia or hypertensive emergency.
- "Normal physiological response that resolves spontaneously." — Reveals misconception: student treats the triad as benign. Correction: the Cushing reflex is a pre-terminal sign demanding urgent intervention to lower ICP.
- "A diagnostic indicator of cardiac arrhythmia." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cardiac arrhythmia as the underlying problem. Correction: the bradycardia is reflexive (baroreceptor-mediated); the underlying problem is intracranial.

Explanation: Originally described by Harvey Cushing [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong]. Mechanism: rising ICP → falling CPP → brain-stem ischaemia → activation of central sympathetic nuclei → systemic hypertension to restore CPP → baroreceptor activation → reflex bradycardia. Irregular respiration (Cheyne–Stokes, then ataxic, then apnoea) reflects brain-stem compromise. The triad is a pre-terminal sign — interventions are needed urgently: head elevation, hyperventilation, mannitol or hypertonic saline, surgical decompression. The Cushing reflex teaches that the body will sacrifice systemic homeostasis to protect cerebral perfusion — an evolutionary priority.

Hints:

1. The triad is bradycardia, hypertension, and abnormal breathing.
2. The systemic hypertension is the body's attempt to keep CPP up.
3. The bradycardia is a reflex response to that hypertension.

QUESTION 30
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The form of cerebral oedema seen acutely in ischaemic stroke — where neurons and glia take up water as ATP-dependent ion pumps fail — is:

Correct answer: Cytotoxic oedema — cellular swelling from failure of Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, with an intact BBB and intracellular water accumulation; preferentially affects grey matter and is poorly responsive to corticosteroids.

Distractors:

- "Vasogenic oedema." — Reveals misconception: student picks a BBB-disruption mechanism for an acute ischaemic event. Correction: vasogenic oedema involves leak of plasma protein and water across a disrupted BBB into the interstitium (around tumours, abscesses, contusions); it preferentially affects white matter and _is_ responsive to corticosteroids.
- "Interstitial oedema." — Reveals misconception: student picks the CSF-transudation mechanism of obstructive hydrocephalus. Correction: interstitial oedema results from CSF moving into periventricular white matter when ventricular pressure is high; it is a feature of hydrocephalus, not stroke.
- "Osmotic oedema." — Reveals misconception: student picks the plasma-hypotonicity mechanism (SIADH, dialysis disequilibrium). Correction: osmotic oedema follows acute drops in plasma osmolality; it is not the dominant mechanism in early ischaemic stroke.

Explanation: **Cytotoxic** — acute stroke, anoxic injury, hypoglycaemia: cells swell with intact BBB; treated by reperfusion if possible, with mannitol or hypertonic saline as temporising palliation only [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong]. **Vasogenic** — brain tumours, abscesses, contusions, severe hypertension (PRES): responsive to corticosteroids (dexamethasone) for tumour-related oedema. **Interstitial** — obstructive hydrocephalus: relieved by CSF diversion. **Osmotic** — acute hyponatraemia, dialysis. All four types raise ICP if extensive, but each requires a different management approach: dexamethasone is helpful for vasogenic peri-tumoural oedema but ineffective and potentially harmful in cytotoxic stroke oedema.

Hints:

1. Acute ischaemia first damages ATP-dependent ion homeostasis.
2. The BBB is initially still intact in this form of oedema.
3. Steroids do _not_ help this form; they help the form caused by BBB leak.

QUESTION 31
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: Among brain-herniation syndromes complicating raised intracranial pressure, the one most often associated with ipsilateral pupillary dilation due to compression of the third cranial nerve is:

Correct answer: Uncal (transtentorial) herniation — the medial temporal lobe (uncus) herniates over the tentorium, compressing CN III (causing ipsilateral pupil dilation) and the ipsilateral cerebral peduncle, often producing contralateral hemiparesis.

Distractors:

- "Subfalcine (cingulate) herniation." — Reveals misconception: student picks a midline-shift syndrome. Correction: subfalcine herniation pushes the cingulate gyrus under the falx and risks anterior cerebral artery compromise; it does _not_ directly compress the third nerve.
- "Tonsillar herniation." — Reveals misconception: student picks the most lethal herniation rather than the one with the third-nerve pattern. Correction: tonsillar herniation drives the cerebellar tonsils through the foramen magnum, compressing the brain stem with apnoea and death — but the signature sign is brain-stem failure, not unilateral pupillary dilation.
- "Upward (transtentorial) herniation." — Reveals misconception: student picks the rarer posterior-fossa pattern. Correction: upward herniation occurs when a posterior-fossa mass pushes the brain stem upward against the tentorium; it is rare and not the classical "blown pupil" syndrome.

Explanation: Uncal herniation is the classic "blown pupil" syndrome [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong]. Early, the parasympathetic fibres on the surface of CN III are compressed → ipsilateral fixed dilated pupil. Later, motor fibres are involved → ophthalmoplegia and oculomotor palsy ("down and out" eye position with ptosis). The contralateral hemiparesis arises from compression of the ipsilateral cerebral peduncle, which contains descending corticospinal fibres that have not yet decussated. Sometimes the _contralateral_ peduncle is pushed against the tentorial edge ("Kernohan's notch phenomenon") — producing falsely localising _ipsilateral_ hemiparesis, an important pitfall. Tonsillar herniation is the deadliest — sudden brain-stem compromise → respiratory arrest. All herniations are neurosurgical emergencies.

Hints:

1. The "blown pupil" sign points to compression of one specific cranial nerve.
2. That cranial nerve runs adjacent to the medial temporal lobe over the tentorial edge.
3. The medial temporal lobe structure that herniates is named the uncus.

QUESTION 32
Type: application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: Therapeutic hypothermia (target core temperature 32–36 °C) after cardiac arrest is thought to reduce neurological injury principally by:

Correct answer: Reducing CMRO₂ by ~6–8% per °C of cooling, attenuating excitotoxic glutamate release, free-radical generation, and apoptotic signalling — together protecting the ischaemic brain in the period of greatest secondary injury.

Distractors:

- "Improving CBF directly." — Reveals misconception: student attributes neuroprotection to a flow effect. Correction: hypothermia tends to lower CBF in proportion to the lowered metabolic demand; the protective effect is from reduced demand and modulation of injury cascades, not from a flow increase.
- "Increasing intracranial pressure." — Reveals misconception: student picks a deleterious effect as the protective mechanism. Correction: raising ICP would worsen outcomes; the goal of hypothermia is to _lower_ the cerebral metabolic rate and indirectly help control oedema.
- "Stimulating neurogenesis." — Reveals misconception: student picks a long-term regenerative explanation for an acute neuroprotective intervention. Correction: hypothermia's benefit is delivered in the first 24–48 hours after arrest by limiting secondary injury, not by triggering neurogenesis.

Explanation: Mild therapeutic hypothermia after out-of-hospital cardiac arrest with return of spontaneous circulation has been associated with improved neurological outcomes in randomised trials [Boron & Boulpaep ch.10; Ganong]. Cooling reduces cerebral metabolic demand (CMRO₂) by ~6–8% per °C, attenuates the cascade of secondary injury (excitotoxic glutamate release, Ca²⁺ overload, free radicals, apoptosis, inflammation), and helps control cerebral oedema. The protocol is typically maintained for 24 hours, then slow rewarming. Risks include shivering, infection, arrhythmia, and electrolyte disturbance. The principle generalises: any intervention that lowers cerebral O₂ demand or raises tolerance to ischaemia (e.g., barbiturate coma in some refractory ICP cases) can buy time for reperfusion or oedema resolution.

Hints:

1. The cerebral metabolic rate falls steeply with temperature.
2. The benefit is most visible in the first 24–48 hours after the ischaemic insult.
3. Several cell-injury cascades (excitotoxicity, free radicals, apoptosis) are slowed at lower temperature.
