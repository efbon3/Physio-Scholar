## Pass 1 — ANS organisation: divisions, anatomy, transmitters, receptors

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The autonomic nervous system has three major divisions:

Correct answer: Sympathetic ("fight or flight" — thoracolumbar T1–L2 outflow), parasympathetic ("rest and digest" — craniosacral CN III/VII/IX/X plus S2–S4), and enteric (intrinsic to the gut wall, ~100 million neurons in myenteric and submucosal plexuses); most organs receive dual innervation with opposing effects, with some synergistic exceptions (e.g., salivary glands).

Distractors:

- "Cortical and subcortical." — Reveals misconception: student picks an anatomical layering. Correction: cortical/subcortical describes brain regions, not the autonomic divisions; the ANS divides into sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric.
- "Voluntary and involuntary." — Reveals misconception: student picks the somatic vs visceral divide. Correction: the ANS is _all_ involuntary; the principled internal subdivisions are sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric.
- "Brainstem and spinal cord." — Reveals misconception: student picks anatomical sources rather than functional divisions. Correction: while preganglionic neurons originate in brainstem and cord, the divisions of the ANS are sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric.

Explanation: The ANS comprises sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric divisions [Berne & Levy ch.11]. Sympathetic mobilises the body for stress (T1–L2 thoracolumbar outflow); parasympathetic restores (CN III, VII, IX, X cranial outflow plus S2–S4 sacral); enteric is intrinsic to the gut wall, with ~100 million neurons in the myenteric (Auerbach's) and submucosal (Meissner's) plexuses, capable of driving peristalsis even after extrinsic denervation. Most organs receive dual sympathetic + parasympathetic innervation with opposing effects, but a few are synergistic (salivary glands receive both for distinct types of secretion). The enteric system is sometimes called the "second brain" given its size and operational autonomy.

Hints:

1. The classical two divisions plus a third one inside the gut wall.
2. Sympathetic prepares for stress; parasympathetic restores.
3. The third division can drive its own peristalsis without extrinsic input.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Sympathetic preganglionic neurons originate from:

Correct answer: The intermediolateral cell column of T1–L2 spinal cord (thoracolumbar outflow); short preganglionic axons exit through the ventral root and white ramus to synapse in paravertebral chain ganglia, prevertebral ganglia (coeliac, superior mesenteric, inferior mesenteric), or directly on adrenal medullary chromaffin cells.

Distractors:

- "Cranial nerves only." — Reveals misconception: student picks parasympathetic origins. Correction: cranial outflow is parasympathetic (CN III/VII/IX/X); sympathetic outflow is thoracolumbar (T1–L2).
- "Cerebellum." — Reveals misconception: student picks a coordination structure. Correction: the cerebellum has no preganglionic autonomic neurons; sympathetic preganglionic cell bodies sit in the spinal cord lateral horn at T1–L2.
- "S2–S4 spinal cord." — Reveals misconception: student picks sacral parasympathetic origin. Correction: S2–S4 is sacral parasympathetic outflow; sympathetic outflow is from T1–L2.

Explanation: Sympathetic outflow originates in the lateral horn (intermediolateral cell column) at T1–L2 [Berne & Levy ch.11; Costanzo ch.2]. Preganglionic axons exit the ventral root and enter the sympathetic chain via the white ramus, where they may synapse in the paravertebral chain ganglion at the same level, travel up or down the chain to a different level, pass through to a prevertebral ganglion (coeliac, superior mesenteric, inferior mesenteric), or synapse directly on adrenal medullary chromaffin cells (the "modified ganglion" that releases adrenaline and noradrenaline directly into the bloodstream). Postganglionic axons rejoin spinal nerves via the grey ramus and supply effectors. Sympathetic preganglionic axons are short; postganglionic axons are long.

Hints:

1. The sympathetic outflow is thoracolumbar.
2. Preganglionic cell bodies sit in the lateral horn at T1–L2.
3. Some preganglionic axons go directly to the adrenal medulla.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Parasympathetic preganglionic neurons originate from:

Correct answer: Cranial nuclei (CN III Edinger–Westphal, CN VII superior salivatory, CN IX inferior salivatory, CN X dorsal motor nucleus and nucleus ambiguus) and sacral cord (S2–S4) — the craniosacral outflow; preganglionic axons are long, synapsing in or near the target organ, with short postganglionic axons to the effector.

Distractors:

- "T1–L2." — Reveals misconception: student picks sympathetic outflow. Correction: T1–L2 is the thoracolumbar sympathetic outflow; parasympathetic outflow is craniosacral.
- "Cerebellum." — Reveals misconception: student picks a coordination structure. Correction: the cerebellum has no preganglionic autonomic neurons; parasympathetic preganglionic neurons sit in cranial brainstem nuclei and S2–S4 cord.
- "Brainstem reticular formation." — Reveals misconception: student picks an arousal/modulation structure. Correction: the reticular formation modulates many functions but is not the source of parasympathetic preganglionic axons; those originate in specific cranial nuclei and S2–S4.

Explanation: Cranial parasympathetic outflow includes CN III (Edinger–Westphal — pupillary constriction and accommodation), CN VII (superior salivatory — submandibular, sublingual, and lacrimal glands), CN IX (inferior salivatory — parotid gland), and CN X (vagus — heart, lungs, liver, GI tract to splenic flexure, and pancreas) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14]. Sacral parasympathetic (S2–S4) supplies the distal colon, bladder, and reproductive organs. Pre/postganglionic relationship in parasympathetic is opposite to sympathetic: long preganglionic axons synapse in or near the target organ, with short postganglionic axons to the effector. The vagus is by far the largest parasympathetic nerve, innervating most thoracic and abdominal viscera.

Hints:

1. The outflow is craniosacral, not thoracolumbar.
2. Four cranial nerves carry parasympathetic preganglionic fibres.
3. The vagus is the largest parasympathetic nerve.

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: In the sympathetic limb, preganglionic neurotransmission uses [blank]; postganglionic neurotransmission uses [blank]:

Correct answer: ACh on nicotinic (Nn) receptors at the ganglion; noradrenaline on α and β adrenergic receptors at the effector — with notable exceptions (sweat glands and some skeletal-muscle vasodilator fibres are sympathetic _cholinergic_, releasing ACh on muscarinic receptors).

Distractors:

- "Noradrenaline; ACh." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the order. Correction: preganglionic transmission in _both_ sympathetic and parasympathetic uses ACh on nicotinic receptors; sympathetic *post*ganglionic uses noradrenaline.
- "Glutamate; GABA." — Reveals misconception: student picks CNS-style fast transmitters. Correction: the autonomic ganglion uses ACh/nicotinic; postganglionic sympathetic uses noradrenaline; glutamate and GABA are CNS, not peripheral autonomic.
- "Dopamine; ACh." — Reveals misconception: student picks dopamine as a sympathetic preganglionic transmitter. Correction: dopamine has limited autonomic roles; the canonical sequence is ACh (preganglionic, nicotinic) → noradrenaline (postganglionic).

Explanation: Sympathetic chain transmission: preganglionic neurons release ACh onto nicotinic (Nn type) receptors on postganglionic cell bodies; postganglionic neurons release noradrenaline onto α1, α2, β1, β2, and β3 adrenergic receptors at the effector [Berne & Levy ch.11; Costanzo ch.2]. Exceptions include sweat glands (sympathetic cholinergic muscarinic), some skeletal-muscle vasodilator fibres (sympathetic cholinergic), and the adrenal medulla (preganglionic ACh on chromaffin cells, which release ~80% adrenaline and ~20% noradrenaline directly into the bloodstream as a "modified postganglionic neuron"). Parasympathetic transmission uses ACh on nicotinic at the ganglion and ACh on muscarinic at the effector. The nicotinic-then-muscarinic ACh-only pattern in parasympathetic and the nicotinic-then-NE pattern in sympathetic underpin most autonomic pharmacology.

Hints:

1. The ganglion is always cholinergic-nicotinic.
2. Sympathetic post-ganglionic is usually noradrenergic — but with notable exceptions.
3. Sweat glands break the rule by being sympathetic cholinergic.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The five subtypes of muscarinic receptors are distributed differently:

Correct answer: M1 (CNS, gastric parietal cells), M2 (heart — slows rate via Gαi reducing cAMP and Gβγ opening GIRK; presynaptic autoreceptors), M3 (smooth muscle, glands — most parasympathetic effects), M4 (CNS), M5 (CNS) — all GPCRs, with M1/M3/M5 coupling to Gq (PLC → IP₃ + DAG → Ca²⁺) and M2/M4 coupling to Gi.

Distractors:

- "All identical." — Reveals misconception: student denies subtype heterogeneity. Correction: the five muscarinic subtypes have distinct G-protein coupling and tissue distribution that drive selective drug effects.
- "M1 only on heart." — Reveals misconception: student misplaces M1 and ignores cardiac M2. Correction: M2 is the cardiac muscarinic receptor (slows rate); M1 is principally CNS and gastric parietal cells.
- "All on skeletal muscle." — Reveals misconception: student conflates muscarinic with skeletal NMJ. Correction: skeletal muscle uses _nicotinic_ receptors at the NMJ; muscarinic receptors are on smooth muscle, glands, heart, and CNS.

Explanation: All muscarinic receptors are GPCRs but with different G-protein coupling [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Berne & Levy ch.11]. M1, M3, M5 couple to Gq (PLC → IP₃ + DAG → Ca²⁺ rise), producing smooth-muscle contraction, gland secretion, and neuronal effects. M2, M4 couple to Gi (reduced cAMP and ion-channel modulation, e.g., GIRK opening in cardiac muscle). Tissue distribution: M1 (CNS for cognition and memory; gastric parietal cells for acid secretion), M2 (heart SA and AV nodes — bradycardia and slowed conduction; presynaptic autoreceptors), M3 (smooth muscle — bronchoconstriction, GI motility, urinary detrusor contraction, pupil constriction; glands — saliva and sweat), M4 and M5 (CNS). Selective agonists/antagonists are clinically important: pirenzepine (M1, gastric ulcer — historical), ipratropium and tiotropium (M3 antagonists, bronchodilators), oxybutynin and solifenacin (M3, overactive bladder), atropine (non-selective antagonist).

Hints:

1. Five subtypes distribute to different tissues.
2. M2 lives in the heart and slows it.
3. M3 is the canonical "parasympathetic effector" receptor in smooth muscle and glands.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The five major adrenergic receptor types are:

Correct answer: α1 (Gq → PLC → IP₃ + DAG → vasoconstriction, pupil dilation, sphincter contraction), α2 (Gi → reduced cAMP; presynaptic autoreceptors and central sympatholysis), β1 (Gs → cAMP → cardiac stimulation, renin release), β2 (Gs → cAMP → smooth muscle relaxation in bronchi, uterus, vasculature), and β3 (Gs → cAMP → adipose lipolysis and bladder detrusor relaxation).

Distractors:

- "M1, M2, M3, M4, M5." — Reveals misconception: student lists muscarinic subtypes as adrenergic. Correction: M1–M5 are _muscarinic_ (cholinergic) receptors; the adrenergic types are α1, α2, β1, β2, β3.
- "Just α and β." — Reveals misconception: student under-counts. Correction: each of α and β has subtypes (α1/α2; β1/β2/β3) with distinct distributions and coupling.
- "D1, D2, D3, D4, D5." — Reveals misconception: student lists dopaminergic receptors. Correction: D1–D5 are _dopaminergic_ receptors; adrenergic receptors are α and β with their subtypes.

Explanation: All adrenergic receptors are GPCRs [Berne & Levy ch.11; Costanzo ch.2]. α1 (Gq) drives vasoconstriction (most arteries), pupil dilation (radial muscle of iris), prostate smooth muscle, urinary sphincter, and piloerection — antagonists prazosin/doxazosin (BPH and HTN), tamsulosin (BPH-selective). α2 (Gi) sits presynaptically on noradrenergic terminals (autoreceptor reducing NE release), on platelets (aggregation), on pancreatic β cells (inhibits insulin), and centrally — agonists clonidine and dexmedetomidine (sedation, antihypertensive — central α2 reduces sympathetic outflow). β1 (Gs) drives cardiac stimulation (SA node — increased rate; AV node — increased conduction; ventricular muscle — increased contractility) and renin release from juxtaglomerular cells — antagonists metoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol (cardioselective β-blockers). β2 (Gs) relaxes bronchial smooth muscle, uterine smooth muscle, skeletal-muscle vasculature, with metabolic effects (gluconeogenesis, insulin release) — agonists salbutamol, salmeterol (bronchodilators). β3 (Gs) drives adipose lipolysis/thermogenesis and bladder detrusor relaxation — agonist mirabegron (overactive bladder). Adrenaline activates all classes; noradrenaline α1/α2/β1 > β2; phenylephrine α1; isoproterenol β1/β2.

Hints:

1. Two main families: α and β.
2. Each family has subtypes with distinct distributions.
3. β1 is the cardiac driver; β2 is the bronchial relaxant.

QUESTION 7
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The adrenal medulla is best described as:

Correct answer: A "modified sympathetic ganglion" — preganglionic sympathetic axons (T5–T11, greater splanchnic) synapse directly on chromaffin cells (neural-crest derived, like postganglionic neurons), which release ~80% adrenaline and ~20% noradrenaline (plus a small amount of dopamine) directly into the bloodstream, exerting hormonal effects on distant target organs.

Distractors:

- "Has its own nervous tissue." — Reveals misconception: student treats the medulla as autonomous. Correction: chromaffin cells are not "their own nervous tissue" but modified postganglionic sympathetic neurons innervated by preganglionic axons.
- "Has both sympathetic and parasympathetic innervation." — Reveals misconception: student denies the sympathetic specificity. Correction: the medulla is innervated only by preganglionic _sympathetic_ fibres; there is no parasympathetic component.
- "Is purely endocrine." — Reveals misconception: student denies the neural component. Correction: chromaffin cells are derived from neural crest like sympathetic postganglionic neurons; they are best understood as _neuro-endocrine_, not purely endocrine.

Explanation: The adrenal medulla is a modified sympathetic ganglion: embryologically derived from neural crest (same as postganglionic sympathetic neurons), innervated by preganglionic sympathetic fibres from T5–T11 via the greater splanchnic nerve (bypassing the chain), with chromaffin cells equivalent to postganglionic neurons but lacking axons — they release catecholamines into adrenal venous blood instead of synaptic clefts [Berne & Levy ch.11; Costanzo ch.2]. Output is ~80% adrenaline plus ~20% noradrenaline plus a small amount of dopamine in humans (compared with postganglionic sympathetic terminals which release predominantly noradrenaline). Function is hormonal — circulatory mobilisation in stress. Phaeochromocytoma is the adrenal medullary catecholamine-secreting tumour with paroxysmal hypertension, headache, palpitations, and sweating; the "rule of 10s" (10% bilateral, extra-adrenal, malignant, genetic, paediatric) is a classical (now refined) mnemonic, and treatment requires α-blockade _before_ β-blockade to avoid unopposed α-stimulation hypertensive crisis.

Hints:

1. Embryologically the medulla is neural crest, like postganglionic sympathetic neurons.
2. It is innervated by sympathetic preganglionic axons.
3. Its output is mostly adrenaline, released into the bloodstream.

QUESTION 8
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Acetylcholine in the synaptic cleft is rapidly hydrolysed by:

Correct answer: Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) — to acetate and choline; choline is recovered by the Na⁺-coupled high-affinity choline transporter (CHT1) and recycled to ACh by choline acetyltransferase; cleft ACh is hydrolysed within ~1 ms, preventing desensitisation and allowing rapid signal transmission.

Distractors:

- "Monoamine oxidase." — Reveals misconception: student picks the catecholamine-degrading enzyme. Correction: MAO degrades catecholamines (NE, dopamine, 5-HT); ACh is degraded by AChE.
- "COMT." — Reveals misconception: student picks another catecholamine-degrading enzyme. Correction: catechol-O-methyltransferase methylates catecholamines extracellularly; it does not act on ACh.
- "Diffusion alone." — Reveals misconception: student denies enzymatic clearance. Correction: ACh is rapidly hydrolysed enzymatically; passive diffusion alone would be far too slow for signal-by-signal transmission.

Explanation: AChE in the synaptic cleft hydrolyses ACh within ~1 ms; cleft choline is recovered by CHT1 and ACh is resynthesised by ChAT [Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Berne & Levy ch.11]. The brevity of cleft ACh prevents desensitisation and allows rapid signal transmission. AChE inhibitors prolong ACh action — therapeutic in myasthenia gravis (pyridostigmine, neostigmine), Alzheimer's disease (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine — CNS-penetrant), and reversal of NMJ blockade (neostigmine). Toxic AChE inhibitors include organophosphates (parathion, malathion, sarin) — irreversible after "ageing"; treatment is atropine plus pralidoxime (oxime reactivator before ageing). ACh's dual role at nicotinic (NMJ, autonomic ganglia, adrenal medulla) and muscarinic (parasympathetic effectors) receptors means AChE inhibition affects all cholinergic transmission.

Hints:

1. The enzyme is in the synaptic cleft and acts within milliseconds.
2. Choline is recovered and recycled.
3. Inhibitors of this enzyme are used in myasthenia and Alzheimer's.

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Noradrenaline released from sympathetic terminals is removed primarily by:

Correct answer: Reuptake into the presynaptic terminal via the noradrenaline transporter (NET, SLC6A2; ~80% of clearance), with secondary metabolism by intracellular monoamine oxidase (MAO) and extracellular catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT); final urinary metabolites include vanillylmandelic acid and 3-methoxy-4-hydroxyphenylglycol.

Distractors:

- "AChE." — Reveals misconception: student picks the cholinergic-degrading enzyme. Correction: AChE degrades ACh; noradrenaline is removed by reuptake (NET), MAO, and COMT.
- "Diffusion alone." — Reveals misconception: student denies active clearance. Correction: passive diffusion alone is too slow for signal-by-signal sympathetic transmission; reuptake is the dominant mechanism.
- "Active export from the body." — Reveals misconception: student suggests active _export_ as a clearance mechanism. Correction: noradrenaline is taken back up _into_ the presynaptic terminal — recovered, not exported.

Explanation: Noradrenaline disposal: NET on the presynaptic terminal recovers ~80%, and the recovered NE is reused or metabolised by MAO inside the neuron [Berne & Levy ch.11; Costanzo ch.2]. COMT methylates catecholamines extracellularly. Final metabolites include VMA (vanillylmandelic acid), HVA (homovanillic acid for dopamine), and MHPG. NET inhibitors raise synaptic NE — TCAs (amitriptyline) block NET and SERT, SNRIs (duloxetine, venlafaxine) block both NET and SERT, cocaine blocks NET/SERT/DAT, and amphetamines reverse NET to release NE. MAO inhibitors (phenelzine, tranylcypromine) raise brain NE/5-HT/dopamine and treat depression but cause the "tyramine reaction" — dietary tyramine in fermented foods is normally degraded by MAO; with MAOI it accumulates and displaces NE from vesicles → hypertensive crisis ("cheese reaction"). COMT inhibitors (entacapone) extend levodopa effect in Parkinson's.

Hints:

1. The dominant clearance is reuptake into the presynaptic terminal.
2. Inside the neuron, MAO degrades NE.
3. Outside, COMT methylates catecholamines.

Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The enteric nervous system has approximately how many neurons?

Correct answer: ~100 million — comparable to the spinal cord — distributed across two plexuses (myenteric/Auerbach for motility, submucosal/Meissner for secretion and local blood flow); functionally autonomous (peristalsis and secretion continue even after extrinsic denervation); modulated by sympathetic (inhibitory) and parasympathetic (excitatory) input, with interstitial cells of Cajal acting as pacemakers.

Distractors:

- "100,000." — Reveals misconception: student grossly underestimates. Correction: the ENS has ~100 million neurons — orders of magnitude larger than 100,000.
- "1 million." — Reveals misconception: student still underestimates by ~100×. Correction: the ENS has ~100 million neurons, comparable in number to the entire spinal cord.
- "10 billion." — Reveals misconception: student over-counts. Correction: the entire human brain has ~86 billion neurons; the ENS has ~100 million — large for a peripheral system but not at brain scale.

Explanation: The enteric nervous system, the "second brain", contains ~100 million neurons in two plexuses [Berne & Levy ch.11; Boron & Boulpaep ch.14; Guyton & Hall ch.62]. The myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus between longitudinal and circular muscle layers controls motility; the submucosal (Meissner's) plexus in the submucosa controls secretion and local blood flow. The ENS operates autonomously — transplanted gut continues to function — though it is modulated by sympathetic (inhibitory) and parasympathetic (excitatory) extrinsic input. Neurotransmitters include ACh, NO, VIP, substance P, NPY, 5-HT (most of the body's 5-HT is in gut), and ATP. Interstitial cells of Cajal generate slow waves that act as pacemakers. ENS diseases: Hirschsprung (congenital absence of distal colonic ganglia), Chagas (T. cruzi destruction of myenteric neurons → megacolon, megaoesophagus), achalasia (loss of inhibitory ENS at LOS), diabetic gastroparesis (vagal + ENS dysfunction), and IBS (gut-brain axis disorder).

Hints:

1. The ENS is large enough to be called the "second brain".
2. It has two plexuses with distinct roles.
3. It can drive peristalsis without extrinsic input.

## Pass 2 — ANS pharmacology and end-organ effects

QUESTION 11
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Atropine — a non-selective muscarinic antagonist — produces:

Correct answer: Decreased GI motility and secretions, mydriasis with cycloplegia, tachycardia, urinary retention, dry mouth, dry skin (anti-secretory at sweat glands), hyperthermia (loss of evaporative cooling), and CNS effects (excitation, hallucinations at high doses) — the classical "hot as a hare, dry as a bone, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter, red as a beet" picture.

Distractors:

- "Increased GI motility, miosis, sweating." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the muscarinic antagonist effects. Correction: atropine _decreases_ GI motility and sweating and produces _mydriasis_; the listed effects are those of a muscarinic _agonist_.
- "No effects." — Reveals misconception: student denies pharmacological action. Correction: atropine has prominent multi-system effects from blocking parasympathetic muscarinic receptors throughout the body.
- "Bradycardia and miosis." — Reveals misconception: student picks parasympathomimetic effects. Correction: bradycardia and miosis are _muscarinic agonist_ effects; atropine, a muscarinic antagonist, produces tachycardia and mydriasis.

Explanation: Atropine blocks muscarinic receptors throughout the body, mirroring "anti-parasympathetic" effects [Costanzo ch.2; Berne & Levy ch.11]. Heart (M2): tachycardia. Eye (M3 sphincter pupillae and ciliary muscle): mydriasis and cycloplegia → blurred near vision. Smooth muscle: bronchodilation, reduced GI motility, reduced bladder tone (urinary retention). Glands: dry mouth, dry skin (anti-secretory), reduced sweating → hyperthermia (especially dangerous in children and the elderly). CNS (M1): high doses → confusion, hallucinations, agitation ("anticholinergic delirium"). The mnemonic "Mad as a hatter, dry as a bone, red as a beet, hot as a hare, blind as a bat" captures the toxidrome. Therapeutic uses: bradyarrhythmias, pre-anaesthetic secretion reduction, cycloplegic eye exam, organophosphate-poisoning antidote. Drugs with anticholinergic side effects (TCAs, first-gen antihistamines, antipsychotics, anti-Parkinson agents, OTC cold preparations) contribute to cumulative anticholinergic burden, which is associated with cognitive decline in the elderly.

Hints:

1. Atropine antagonises muscarinic receptors body-wide.
2. Effects mirror "anti-parasympathetic".
3. Hyperthermia and delirium are hallmarks of toxicity.

QUESTION 12
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: β-blockers (propranolol, metoprolol) are clinically used for:

Correct answer: Hypertension, angina, post-MI secondary prevention, heart failure (when stable, started low and titrated), atrial fibrillation rate control, thyrotoxicosis, migraine prophylaxis, essential tremor, and performance/social anxiety; non-selective β-blockers should be avoided in asthma, and β-blockade _alone_ is dangerous in pheochromocytoma (unopposed α stimulation).

Distractors:

- "Asthma exacerbation." — Reveals misconception: student picks an indication for which β-blockers are contraindicated. Correction: non-selective β-blockers can precipitate bronchoconstriction in asthma; they are not used to treat asthma exacerbations (β2 agonists are).
- "Pheochromocytoma alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks β-blocker monotherapy in pheochromocytoma. Correction: in pheochromocytoma, β-blockade _without_ prior α-blockade leads to unopposed α-stimulation and hypertensive crisis; α-blockade must come first.
- "No clinical use." — Reveals misconception: student denies utility. Correction: β-blockers are among the most widely used cardiovascular drugs, with multiple evidence-based indications.

Explanation: β-blockers reduce cardiac contractility, rate, conduction, and renin release, lowering blood pressure and providing antiarrhythmic and anti-anginal effects [Berne & Levy ch.11; Ganong]. Non-selective (propranolol) blocks β1 and β2 (avoid in asthma); cardioselective (metoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol) is mostly β1 (safer in mild airway disease); combined α1+β blockade (carvedilol, labetalol) provides vasodilation plus cardioprotection; nebivolol vasodilates via NO. Clinical uses include hypertension (especially with concurrent CAD or AF), heart failure (carvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate — proven mortality benefit; start low and titrate), post-MI (reduces reinfarction and sudden death), angina, AF rate control, arrhythmias, migraine prophylaxis (propranolol classic), essential tremor (propranolol), thyrotoxicosis (symptomatic; propranolol also inhibits T4→T3 conversion), and performance/social anxiety. Adverse effects include bradycardia, AV block, hypotension, bronchospasm, masking of hypoglycaemia symptoms in diabetics, fatigue, depression, sleep disturbance, sexual dysfunction, and cold extremities. Withdrawal causes rebound tachycardia — taper off.

Hints:

1. β-blockers have many evidence-based cardiovascular indications.
2. They are problematic in asthma and dangerous in unprepared pheochromocytoma.
3. Cardioselective agents are safer in mild airway disease.

QUESTION 13
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The autonomic effects on heart rate are:

Correct answer: Sympathetic (β1) increases rate (positive chronotropy via cAMP-mediated acceleration of If/HCN channels in the SA node), while parasympathetic (M2 via Gαi reducing cAMP and Gβγ opening GIRK channels) decreases rate; both operate simultaneously, with parasympathetic dominant at rest (vagal tone) and sympathetic dominant during stress.

1. The two limbs work simultaneously, in opposite directions.
2. Vagal tone dominates at rest; sympathetic dominates with stress.

Localising by order: first-order (hypothalamus → cervical cord) is central, often with neurological signs (lateral medullary infarct — Wallenberg syndrome); second-order (preganglionic, T1 → superior cervical ganglion via the chain) flags Pancoast tumour at the lung apex, cervical rib, or trauma; third-order (postganglionic, SCG → eye via carotid plexus) flags carotid dissection (urgent — risk of stroke; needs urgent vascular imaging), cluster headache, or cavernous sinus pathology. Pharmacological tests: cocaine 4–10% (blocks NET — normal eye dilates, Horner-affected eye does not because no NE is in the synapse); apraclonidine (α2 agonist; in chronic Horner, post-denervation supersensitivity reverses anisocoria — affected pupil dilates more than normal). Hydroxyamphetamine (releases stored NE) further distinguishes preganglionic (intact response) from postganglionic (no response — terminal NE stores depleted).

Hints:

1. The triad is miosis + ptosis + anhidrosis on one side.
2. The lesion is somewhere along the sympathetic pathway to the eye.
3. A postganglionic Horner with neck pain raises urgent suspicion of carotid dissection.

Stem: Multiple system atrophy (MSA) presents with:

Correct answer: A synucleinopathy combining prominent autonomic failure (severe orthostatic hypotension, urinary symptoms, erectile dysfunction) with cerebellar (MSA-C) or parkinsonian (MSA-P) features and REM sleep behaviour disorder; poor response to levodopa, progressive course over 5–10 years.

Distractors:

- "Pure cerebellar ataxia without autonomic features." — Reveals misconception: student strips out autonomic failure. Correction: MSA's defining feature is the _combination_ of autonomic failure with cerebellar or parkinsonian features.
- "Pure cognitive impairment without movement features." — Reveals misconception: student picks a dementia phenotype. Correction: MSA is primarily a movement disorder with autonomic failure; pure cognitive decline points to other diagnoses (Alzheimer's, FTD).
- "Reversible with levodopa." — Reveals misconception: student equates MSA-P with idiopathic Parkinson's. Correction: MSA-P responds _poorly_ to levodopa — a useful clinical clue distinguishing MSA from idiopathic PD.

Explanation: MSA is a synucleinopathy (α-synuclein in oligodendroglial cytoplasmic inclusions, distinguishing it from PD's neuronal Lewy bodies) [Berne & Levy ch.11; Ganong]. REM sleep behaviour disorder commonly precedes other features by years (as in PD and DLB). MRI may show "hot cross bun" sign in pons (MSA-C) or putaminal atrophy with hyperintense slit (MSA-P). Prognosis is progressive over 5–10 years with respiratory or aspiration complications terminally.

Hints:

1. Autonomic failure is prominent — distinguishing MSA from idiopathic Parkinson's.
2. Either parkinsonian (MSA-P) or cerebellar (MSA-C) features dominate.
3. Levodopa response is typically poor.

Stem: A 35-year-old woman presents with episodic hypertensive crises accompanied by headache, palpitations, and sweating. Diagnostic workup should begin with:

Correct answer: 24-hour urinary metanephrines (or plasma free metanephrines) — the most sensitive and specific biochemical test for pheochromocytoma; if positive, anatomical imaging with CT or MRI of adrenals and abdomen, then MIBG scan if needed; treatment requires α-blockade _first_ (phenoxybenzamine), then β-blockade, then surgical resection.

Distractors:

- "Brain MRI to rule out pituitary adenoma." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong endocrine workup. Correction: while pituitary disease can cause endocrine syndromes, paroxysmal hypertension with the HPD triad points to pheochromocytoma; biochemical workup with metanephrines comes first.
- "Empirical β-blocker without further workup." — Reveals misconception: student treats first without diagnosis. Correction: β-blockade _alone_ in undiagnosed pheochromocytoma is dangerous (unopposed α-stimulation hypertensive crisis); diagnosis must precede treatment, and α-blockade must precede β-blockade.
- "Reassurance and lifestyle advice." — Reveals misconception: student dismisses a serious finding. Correction: paroxysmal severe hypertension with the HPD triad is a red-flag presentation; reassurance without workup risks fatal hypertensive crisis.

Explanation: Pheochromocytoma diagnosis starts with biochemical testing — plasma free metanephrines or 24-hour urinary metanephrines and catecholamines [Berne & Levy ch.11; Ganong]. Metanephrines are the most sensitive and specific because they are continuously released by tumour cells, whereas catecholamines are released episodically. Positive biochemistry triggers anatomical imaging (CT or MRI of adrenals and abdomen for paragangliomas), then MIBG scan if anatomical imaging is non-localising or to assess for metastases. Genetic testing is now recommended given the high rate of hereditary syndromes (~30–40% with modern testing — MEN 2A/2B, von Hippel–Lindau, NF1, SDH mutations). Treatment is sequential: α-blockade first (phenoxybenzamine 10–20 mg twice daily, titrated up to control BP and produce orthostatic symptoms — the marker of adequate blockade), then β-blockade (after adequate α-blockade, often atenolol or metoprolol for tachycardia control), then surgical resection (typically laparoscopic adrenalectomy) with 1–2 weeks of pre-op preparation and careful intra-operative BP management. Post-op risk of hypotension is usually transient. Beware: never start β-blockade first — unopposed α-stimulation can produce severe hypertensive crisis.

Hints:

1. The most useful first test is biochemical — metanephrines.
2. Imaging follows positive biochemistry.
3. Treatment sequence is α-blockade, then β-blockade, then surgery.

Stem: Neurogenic shock differs from haemorrhagic shock in that:

Correct answer: Neurogenic shock (from acute spinal cord injury with loss of sympathetic outflow above T6) presents with hypotension _plus bradycardia_ (parasympathetic intact via vagus, sympathetic chronotropic drive lost) — whereas haemorrhagic shock presents with hypotension _plus tachycardia_ (compensatory sympathetic response intact). Treatment includes vasopressors (noradrenaline) plus atropine for severe bradycardia.

Distractors:

- "Neurogenic shock has tachycardia and haemorrhagic shock has bradycardia." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the heart-rate patterns. Correction: neurogenic shock has _bradycardia_; haemorrhagic shock has _tachycardia_.
- "Both produce identical haemodynamics." — Reveals misconception: student denies the diagnostic difference. Correction: the heart-rate response is a key distinguishing feature.
- "Neurogenic shock improves with fluid resuscitation alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks fluids as monotherapy. Correction: while fluids are part of treatment, neurogenic shock is principally a vasodilatory shock and usually requires vasopressors and atropine for severe bradycardia.

Explanation: Neurogenic shock arises from acute spinal cord injury above T6 with loss of descending sympathetic outflow, producing hypotension from vasodilation and bradycardia from unopposed parasympathetic (vagal) tone [Berne & Levy ch.11; Ganong]. The contrast with haemorrhagic shock (hypotension plus compensatory tachycardia from intact sympathetic response) is diagnostically critical. Distinct from spinal _shock_, which is the period of acute flaccid areflexia after cord transection (a motor/reflex phenomenon, not a haemodynamic one). Treatment of neurogenic shock: judicious fluid resuscitation (avoid fluid overload), vasopressors (noradrenaline first-line for vasoconstriction; phenylephrine alternative; some use dopamine), atropine for severe bradycardia, and careful management of cord injury and associated injuries. Always assess for associated haemorrhagic shock from concomitant trauma — both can coexist.

Hints:

1. Hypotension _with_ bradycardia points to neurogenic shock.
2. Hypotension _with_ tachycardia points to haemorrhagic shock.
3. Treatment includes vasopressors and atropine.

Stem: A patient experiences a brief loss of consciousness while standing in a hot crowded room, preceded by nausea, sweating, and pallor, with rapid spontaneous recovery on lying down. The most likely mechanism is:

Correct answer: Vasovagal (neurocardiogenic) syncope — excessive vagal activation triggered by emotional or orthostatic stimuli produces bradycardia and vasodilation → cerebral hypoperfusion → syncope; the most common cause of syncope; tilt-table testing can reproduce the response; treatment is trigger avoidance, hydration, salt, leg-crossing/muscle-tensing manoeuvres, with midodrine, β-blockers, fludrocortisone, or pacemaker in severe cases.

Distractors:

- "Acute ischaemic stroke." — Reveals misconception: student picks a focal vascular event. Correction: acute stroke usually produces persistent focal neurological deficit, not a brief loss of consciousness with rapid recovery; vasovagal syncope is far more common in this scenario.
- "Hypoglycaemic seizure." — Reveals misconception: student picks a metabolic/seizure cause. Correction: hypoglycaemia produces sweating and loss of consciousness but typically with prolonged confusion and a history of diabetes/insulin use; seizures usually have post-ictal confusion.
- "Cardiac arrhythmia (sustained VT)." — Reveals misconception: student picks a primary cardiac cause. Correction: while cardiac causes of syncope (arrhythmia, structural heart disease) are important to exclude, the classical vasovagal pattern with prodrome and rapid recovery on lying flat fits this scenario.

Explanation: Vasovagal syncope (neurocardiogenic syncope) is the most common cause of syncope [Berne & Levy ch.11; Ganong]. Triggers include hot crowded rooms, emotional stress, prolonged standing, the sight of blood, micturition, and intense pain. Mechanism: triggers activate central pathways producing paradoxical reflex bradycardia and vasodilation (the "Bezold–Jarisch reflex") → cerebral hypoperfusion → syncope. Prodrome: nausea, sweating, pallor, light-headedness, blurring of vision. Recovery on lying flat is typically rapid (seconds to a minute). Diagnosis: clinical history, supine and standing BP, ECG to exclude cardiac causes; head-up tilt-table testing can reproduce the response. Treatment: education, trigger avoidance, hydration and salt, physical counter-pressure manoeuvres (leg crossing, muscle tensing, squatting), and (for refractory cases) midodrine, fludrocortisone, β-blockers, and dual-chamber pacemaker for severe cardioinhibitory phenotype. Differential includes orthostatic hypotension, cardiac arrhythmia, structural heart disease, seizures, and metabolic causes — a thorough syncope workup is mandatory.

Hints:

1. Triggers are emotional or orthostatic.
2. Prodrome of nausea, sweating, and pallor precedes syncope.
3. Recovery on lying flat is rapid.

Stem: Diabetic autonomic neuropathy commonly manifests as:

Correct answer: A syndrome of postural hypotension, gastroparesis (early satiety, postprandial fullness, vomiting), erectile dysfunction, neurogenic bladder, gustatory sweating, and silent myocardial infarction (loss of cardiac afferents → no anginal pain) — markedly worsening prognosis; prevention requires tight glycaemic control plus comorbidity management.

Distractors:

- "Acute monoplegia." — Reveals misconception: student picks a stroke-like presentation. Correction: diabetic autonomic neuropathy is chronic and multi-system; acute monoplegia points to vascular events.
- "Pure cerebellar ataxia." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cerebellar syndrome. Correction: cerebellar ataxia is not a feature of diabetic autonomic neuropathy; the syndrome is autonomic dysfunction, not cerebellar.
- "Bilateral facial pain." — Reveals misconception: student picks a trigeminal pain syndrome. Correction: diabetic autonomic neuropathy is typically painless; trigeminal neuralgia and other facial pain syndromes are unrelated.

Explanation: Diabetic autonomic neuropathy is a common late complication of diabetes, particularly with long duration and poor control [Berne & Levy ch.11]. Cardiovascular: resting tachycardia, postural hypotension, silent MI (loss of cardiac afferent innervation), increased risk of sudden death. Gastrointestinal: gastroparesis (early satiety, postprandial fullness, vomiting, glycaemic instability), diarrhoea/constipation. Genitourinary: erectile dysfunction (neurogenic plus vascular), neurogenic bladder, retrograde ejaculation. Sudomotor: anhidrosis (lower limbs), hyperhidrosis (face, trunk), gustatory sweating. Pupillary: reduced reactivity. Diagnosis is largely clinical, supplemented by autonomic testing (heart rate variability, Valsalva ratio, head-up tilt) and gastric emptying studies. Treatment: glycaemic control to slow progression, plus organ-specific therapy — fludrocortisone/midodrine/droxitalpa for orthostatic hypotension, prokinetics (metoclopramide, domperidone, erythromycin) for gastroparesis, PDE5 inhibitors for erectile dysfunction, intermittent catheterisation for bladder, foot care to prevent ulcers. Diabetic autonomic neuropathy markedly worsens prognosis.

Hints:

1. The syndrome is multi-system and chronic.
2. Silent MI from loss of cardiac afferents is a major hazard.
3. Tight glycaemic control is the cornerstone of prevention.

Stem: A newborn presents with delayed passage of meconium, abdominal distension, and bilious vomiting. The most likely diagnosis is:

Correct answer: Hirschsprung disease — congenital absence of enteric ganglia (neural crest cell migration failure) in the distal colon, producing a tonically contracted, non-peristalsing aganglionic segment with proximal megacolon; rectal biopsy shows absence of ganglion cells and increased AChE staining; treatment is surgical resection of the aganglionic segment with pull-through anastomosis.

Distractors:

- "Necrotising enterocolitis." — Reveals misconception: student picks a different neonatal GI emergency. Correction: NEC presents with bloody stools, abdominal distension, and pneumatosis intestinalis on imaging in premature infants; Hirschsprung's hallmark is delayed meconium and distal aganglionic colon.
- "Pyloric stenosis." — Reveals misconception: student picks an upper GI obstruction. Correction: pyloric stenosis presents with non-bilious projectile vomiting at 3–6 weeks of age in formerly healthy infants; Hirschsprung's presents earlier with delayed meconium and bilious vomiting.
- "Meconium ileus from cystic fibrosis." — Reveals misconception: student picks another cause of delayed meconium. Correction: meconium ileus is a recognised differential and is associated with CF; Hirschsprung shows a transition zone on contrast enema and absence of ganglia on rectal biopsy.

Explanation: Hirschsprung disease results from failed migration of neural crest cells into the distal hindgut during embryogenesis, producing absent ganglia in the myenteric and submucosal plexuses of the rectosigmoid (most cases), descending colon (some), or longer segments [Berne & Levy ch.11; Boron & Boulpaep ch.14]. The aganglionic segment is tonically contracted (no inhibitory enteric input — no nitric oxide and VIP), proximal bowel becomes dilated (megacolon). Presentation: delayed passage of meconium beyond 48 hours, abdominal distension, bilious vomiting, failure to thrive; older children present with chronic constipation and growth failure. Diagnosis: contrast enema shows transition zone; anorectal manometry shows absent rectoanal inhibitory reflex; rectal biopsy is definitive (absent ganglia, increased AChE staining, hypertrophied nerve trunks). Associations: Down syndrome, RET mutations, MEN 2A. Treatment: surgical resection with pull-through anastomosis (Soave, Duhamel, or Swenson procedures). Complication: enterocolitis (Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis, HAEC) with sepsis is a feared complication requiring decompression and antibiotics.

Hints:

1. Failed neural crest migration produces an aganglionic distal segment.
2. The neonate has delayed meconium passage and bilious vomiting.
3. Rectal biopsy is diagnostic.

Stem: A child of Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry presents with absent fungiform papillae of the tongue, severe orthostatic hypotension, gastrointestinal dysmotility, decreased pain and temperature sensation, and emotional lability. The most likely diagnosis is:

Correct answer: Familial dysautonomia (Riley–Day syndrome / hereditary sensory and autonomic neuropathy type III, HSAN3) — autosomal recessive, ~99% in Ashkenazi Jews, caused by mutations in the IKBKAP/ELP1 gene; severe autonomic dysfunction (orthostasis, GI dysmotility, temperature instability, hyperhidrosis or anhidrosis), pain insensitivity, sensory deficits, and absence of fungiform papillae are hallmarks.

Distractors:

- "Diabetic autonomic neuropathy." — Reveals misconception: student picks an acquired adult autonomic neuropathy. Correction: familial dysautonomia presents in childhood with characteristic features (absent fungiform papillae, Ashkenazi Jewish heritage, IKBKAP mutation); diabetic neuropathy is acquired in long-standing diabetes.
- "Multiple system atrophy." — Reveals misconception: student picks an adult-onset synucleinopathy. Correction: MSA presents in mid-late adulthood with parkinsonian/cerebellar plus autonomic features; familial dysautonomia presents in childhood with the described phenotype.
- "Pure autonomic failure (PAF)." — Reveals misconception: student picks an idiopathic adult autonomic failure. Correction: PAF is an adult synucleinopathy with isolated autonomic failure; familial dysautonomia is a hereditary childhood-onset syndrome with sensory and autonomic features.

Explanation: Familial dysautonomia (Riley–Day syndrome, HSAN3) is an autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in IKBKAP (now ELP1), encoding a protein required for elongator complex function and tRNA modification, with selective developmental loss of small unmyelinated sensory and autonomic neurons [Berne & Levy ch.11; Ganong]. Almost exclusively in Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry (carrier frequency ~1/30). Clinical features: severe orthostatic hypotension (often without compensatory tachycardia), gastrointestinal dysmotility (gastroparesis, vomiting crises), temperature instability (hyperhidrosis or anhidrosis), pain insensitivity (lacking small unmyelinated fibres), absent fungiform papillae of the tongue (a diagnostic hallmark — produces decreased taste and absent reflex tearing), absent deep tendon reflexes, emotional lability and "dysautonomic crises", scoliosis, and cardiovascular instability. Diagnosis: genetic testing for IKBKAP mutation; pathognomonic absent fungiform papillae; histamine intradermal test (no flare). Management is symptomatic, multidisciplinary — fludrocortisone/midodrine for orthostatic hypotension, gastrostomy/fundoplication, careful anaesthesia (hypersensitive autonomic responses), and supportive care; gene-targeted therapies are in development.

Hints:

1. Childhood onset with severe autonomic and sensory features.
2. Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry is a major clue.
3. Absent fungiform papillae of the tongue is a diagnostic hallmark.

Pass 1 (Q1–10) develops ANS organisation: the sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric divisions; preganglionic and postganglionic anatomy; cholinergic and adrenergic neurotransmission; muscarinic and adrenergic receptor subtypes; the adrenal medulla as a modified sympathetic ganglion; AChE and noradrenaline disposal; and the enteric "second brain". Pass 2 (Q11–22) covers pharmacology and end-organ effects: atropine, β-blockers, autonomic effects on heart rate, eye, bronchi, GI motility, bladder, and sweat glands; pheochromocytoma; and the dose-dependent effects of adrenaline, noradrenaline, and dopamine. Pass 3 (Q23–32) covers clinical anchors: orthostatic hypotension, autonomic dysreflexia, Horner syndrome, multiple system atrophy, pheochromocytoma workup, neurogenic shock versus haemorrhagic shock, vasovagal syncope, diabetic autonomic neuropathy, Hirschsprung disease, and familial dysautonomia.
