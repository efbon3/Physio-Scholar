Stem: The two arms of the immune system are:

Correct answer: Innate (rapid, non-specific, no memory; physical/chemical barriers, complement, neutrophils, macrophages, NK cells, dendritic cells, toll-like receptors) and adaptive (slow, antigen-specific, develops memory; T cells, B cells, antibodies)

- "Sympathetic and parasympathetic" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "None" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Innate immunity** — Always ready, fast (minutes-hours), non-specific. Components:

- **Physical barriers** — Skin, mucous membranes, mucus, cilia.
- **Chemical barriers** — Stomach acid, antimicrobial peptides (defensins, cathelicidins), lysozyme.
- **Complement system** — Plasma proteins activated in cascade (classical, alternative, lectin pathways) → opsonisation, MAC, anaphylatoxins.
- **Phagocytes** — Neutrophils, monocytes/macrophages.
- **NK cells** — Kill virus-infected and tumour cells.
- **Dendritic cells** — Antigen presentation to T cells (link to adaptive).
- **Pattern recognition receptors** — TLRs, NLRs, RLRs sense PAMPs (pathogen-associated molecular patterns).

**Adaptive immunity** — Antigen-specific, slower (days), memory-forming. Components:

- **T cells** — CD4 helper, CD8 cytotoxic; thymus-developed.
- **B cells** — Antibody-secreting plasma cells; bone marrow developed.
- **Antibodies** — IgG, IgM, IgA, IgD, IgE.
- **Cytokines** — Coordinate response.

The two arms are integrated — innate triggers adaptive (via dendritic cells); adaptive amplifies innate (e.g., antibodies enhance phagocytosis) (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.18; Murphy 2017).

Stem: The most numerous leukocyte in adult peripheral blood is:

Correct answer: Neutrophil (50–70% of WBCs); first responder in bacterial infection; multilobed nucleus; ~10–14 day total lifespan with only 6–10 hours in circulation

Distractors:

- "Lymphocyte" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Monocyte" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Eosinophil" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Neutrophil** — Most abundant white cell. Key features:

- **Multilobed nucleus** ("polymorphonuclear").
- **Granules** — Primary (azurophilic; myeloperoxidase, lysozyme, defensins, elastase), secondary (specific; lactoferrin, collagenase), tertiary (gelatinase).
- **Function** — Phagocytosis of bacteria, fungi.
- **Mechanism** — Recognise opsonised pathogens (C3b, IgG); engulf into phagosome → fuse with lysosome → respiratory burst (NADPH oxidase generates ROS) + granule contents → kill organism.
- **NETs** (neutrophil extracellular traps) — DNA + histones + granules expelled to entrap pathogens.
- **Lifespan** — ~10 days total, but only 6–10 hours in circulation (then to tissues or apoptosis).
- **Production** — ~10¹¹/day under stress; G-CSF stimulates production.

**Neutrophilia** — Bacterial infection, inflammation, stress, corticosteroids (demargination + reduced extravasation), tissue necrosis. **Left shift** (immature band forms) — severe infection.

**Neutropenia** — Chemotherapy, viral infection (HIV), autoimmune, severe sepsis. Risk: infection (especially fungal, opportunistic). G-CSF (filgrastim) to support recovery.

**Chronic granulomatous disease** — NADPH oxidase defect → no respiratory burst → recurrent catalase-positive bacterial/fungal infections (Staph aureus, Aspergillus).

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.18; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.34.)

**Polarisation**:

- **M1** (classically activated) — Pro-inflammatory; bactericidal.
- **M2** (alternatively activated) — Anti-inflammatory; tissue repair.

Stem: The cells specialising in destroying virus-infected and tumour cells without prior sensitisation are:

Correct answer: NK (natural killer) cells — innate lymphocytes; recognise "missing self" (cells with absent or low MHC I — characteristic of viral infection or tumour); release perforin and granzymes; secrete IFN-γ

Distractors:

- "T cells" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Plasma cells" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Dendritic cells" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **NK cells** — Large granular lymphocytes. Innate immunity. ~5–10% of lymphocytes.

**Recognition** — "Missing self" hypothesis:

- Inhibitory receptors (KIRs) bind MHC I → inhibit killing of normal cells.
- Activating receptors recognise stress ligands (MIC-A, MIC-B, ULBPs) on infected/transformed cells.
- Loss of MHC I (common in viral infection or tumour) removes inhibition → NK kills.
- ADCC (antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity) — FcγRIII (CD16) binds antibody-coated targets.

**Mechanism**:

- Release perforin (forms membrane pores) + granzymes (serine proteases that activate caspases) → apoptosis.
- Secrete IFN-γ → activates macrophages, enhances adaptive immunity.

**Roles**:

- Anti-viral (especially herpes, EBV).
- Anti-tumour surveillance.
- Pregnancy (uterine NK cells regulate placentation).

**NK cell deficiency** → severe susceptibility to herpes virus infections.

(Murphy 2017; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e.)

Stem: The complement cascade has three activation pathways:

Correct answer: Classical (antigen-antibody complexes), alternative (spontaneous activation, microbial surfaces), lectin (mannose-binding lectin); all converge on C3 convertase → C3 cleavage → C3b (opsonin) and C3a (anaphylatoxin); terminal pathway forms membrane attack complex (C5b–9, MAC) for cell lysis

Distractors:

- "Single pathway" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Two pathways only" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "None" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Complement system** — Series of >30 plasma proteins. Three pathways:

**1. Classical pathway**:

- Trigger: Antigen-antibody complex (IgG, IgM).
- C1q binds antibody → C1r/C1s activation → cleaves C4 + C2 → C4b2a (C3 convertase).
- Important in adaptive immunity.

**2. Alternative pathway**:

- Trigger: Spontaneous low-level C3 hydrolysis ("tick-over") → C3b interacts with factor B + factor D → C3bBb (C3 convertase). Stabilised by properdin.
- Important in innate immunity (microbial surface lacks regulatory proteins).

**3. Lectin pathway**:

- Trigger: Mannose-binding lectin (MBL) binds mannose on microbial surface.
- MASP-2 cleaves C4 + C2 (similar to classical) → C3 convertase.

**All three converge** at **C3 cleavage**:

- **C3b** — Major opsonin (binds CR1, CR3 on phagocytes → enhanced phagocytosis).
- **C3a** — Anaphylatoxin (mast cell degranulation, vascular permeability).

**Terminal pathway** (C5–C9):

- C5 cleaved → C5a (anaphylatoxin, neutrophil chemotaxis) + C5b.
- C5b → C6, C7, C8, C9 → **MAC** (membrane attack complex) → pore in target cell membrane → osmotic lysis.

**Functions**:

- **Opsonisation** (C3b).
- **Lysis** (MAC) — Especially Neisseria.
- **Anaphylatoxins** (C3a, C5a) — Inflammation, chemotaxis.

**Regulation**:

- **CD55 (DAF)** — Decay accelerating factor; dissociates C3 convertase.
- **CD59** — Inhibits MAC.
- **C1 inhibitor** — Regulates classical pathway.

**Deficiencies**:

- **Early components** (C1q, C2, C4) — Lupus-like syndromes, recurrent pyogenic infections.
- **C3 deficiency** — Severe recurrent pyogenic infections.
- **Late components (C5–C9)** — Susceptibility to **Neisseria** (meningococcus, gonococcus).
- **C1 inhibitor deficiency** — Hereditary angioedema (recurrent angio-oedema). Treated with C1-INH replacement, kallikrein inhibitor (lanadelumab), bradykinin receptor antagonist (icatibant).
- **CD55/CD59 deficiency** — PNH (acquired); intravascular haemolysis. Treated with eculizumab (anti-C5).

(Murphy 2017; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e.)

Stem: The thymus is the primary lymphoid organ for:

Correct answer: T cell maturation — selection processes (positive selection: T cells with TCRs binding self-MHC are kept; negative selection: T cells with TCRs binding self-antigen + MHC too strongly are deleted, ensuring central tolerance); thymus is most active in childhood, involutes by adulthood

- "Plasma cells" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "NK cells" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **T cell development** in thymus:

1. **Bone marrow** — Precursor T cells migrate to thymus.
2. **Thymic cortex**:
   - **Positive selection** — Thymocytes with TCRs binding self-MHC moderately survive; others die by neglect. CD4+ vs CD8+ commitment determined.
3. **Thymic medulla**:
   - **Negative selection** — Thymocytes with TCRs binding self-antigen + self-MHC too strongly are deleted (ensures central tolerance).
   - **AIRE (autoimmune regulator)** transcription factor in medullary thymic epithelial cells — drives expression of "tissue-restricted antigens" (insulin, thyroglobulin, etc.) for negative selection. **AIRE mutations** → APECED syndrome (autoimmune polyendocrinopathy-candidiasis-ectodermal dystrophy).
4. **Survivors** (~3% of thymocytes) → enter periphery as naïve T cells.

**Adult thymic involution** — Begins at puberty; thymus shrinks, function declines. Most T cell production during childhood. Adult T cell pool maintained by peripheral expansion.

**Severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID)**:

- T cell + B cell deficiency.
- Causes: ADA, RAG, IL-2Rγ, others.
- Recurrent infections from infancy.
- Treatment: HSCT (curative if early), gene therapy, ADA enzyme replacement.

**DiGeorge syndrome** — 22q11.2 deletion. Thymic aplasia/hypoplasia → T cell deficiency. Hypocalcaemia (parathyroid aplasia), cardiac defects, characteristic facies.

(Murphy 2017; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e.)

- "None" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **CD4+ T helper cell subsets**:

**Th1**:

- Master TF: T-bet.
- Cytokines: IFN-γ, IL-2, TNF.
- Function: Activate macrophages (kill intracellular pathogens — TB, Listeria); drive cell-mediated immunity.
- Diseases: Th1-driven autoimmunity (RA, T1DM, MS, IBD, granulomatous disease).

**Th2**:

- Master TF: GATA3.
- Cytokines: IL-4, IL-5, IL-13.
- Function: B cell help (especially IgE class switching); eosinophil activation; helminth immunity.
- Diseases: Allergic asthma, atopic dermatitis, allergic rhinitis.

**Th17**:

- Master TF: RORγt.
- Cytokines: IL-17, IL-22.
- Function: Extracellular bacteria, fungi (especially mucocutaneous Candida); barrier maintenance.
- Diseases: Psoriasis, ankylosing spondylitis, IBD; targeted by anti-IL-17 (secukinumab) and anti-IL-23 (ustekinumab) biologics.

**Treg (regulatory T cells)**:

- Master TF: FOXP3.
- Cytokines: IL-10, TGF-β.
- Function: Suppress immune response, peripheral tolerance.
- Diseases: FOXP3 mutation → IPEX syndrome (severe early autoimmune disease).

**Tfh (T follicular helper)**:

- Master TF: Bcl-6.
- In germinal centres of lymph nodes.
- Help B cells undergo affinity maturation, class switching, plasma cell differentiation.

**Therapeutic targeting**:

- Anti-cytokine biologics (anti-IL-17, IL-23, IL-4Rα, IL-5) for autoimmune/allergic disease.
- JAK inhibitors block downstream signalling.
- IL-2-based therapies for cancer.
- Treg-based therapies in development for transplant, autoimmunity.

(Murphy 2017.)

Explanation: **CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs)**:

**Recognition**:

- TCR + CD8 binds antigen on **MHC class I** (presented on all nucleated cells, displaying intracellular antigens — viruses, tumour neoantigens).
- Activation requires costimulation (CD28-B7) and cytokines (IL-2 from activated CD4 Th cells).

- **CAR-T cell therapy** — Patient's CD8 T cells engineered with chimeric antigen receptor (e.g., anti-CD19 for B-cell lymphomas/leukaemias). Approved for relapsed/refractory ALL, lymphomas, multiple myeloma.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The five classes of immunoglobulins are:

Correct answer: IgM (first response, pentamer with J chain), IgG (most abundant in serum, four subclasses, crosses placenta), IgA (mucosal, dimer with secretory component, also in milk), IgE (mast cell binding, allergy/parasites), IgD (B cell receptor, function unclear)

Distractors:

- "IgA, IgD, IgE only" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Only IgM and IgG" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "IgX, IgY" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Immunoglobulin classes**:

**IgG** (75% of serum Ig):

- Four subclasses (IgG1, 2, 3, 4) with different functions.
- Half-life ~21 days (longest of any class — FcRn-mediated recycling).
- **Crosses placenta** → neonatal protection.
- Functions: opsonisation (FcγR), complement activation (classical), neutralisation, ADCC.

**IgM** (10%):

- Pentamer with J chain (or BCR monomer).
- First antibody in primary response.
- Strong complement activator.
- Stays intravascular (large size).
- Anti-A, anti-B antibodies in ABO system are IgM.

**IgA** (15%):

- Two forms: serum monomer + secretory dimer (with J chain + secretory component).
- Mucosal immunity (gut, respiratory, urogenital, salivary, tears, sweat).
- In breast milk (passive immunity to infant).
- Most abundant Ig produced (~5 g/day; mostly excreted in mucosa).
- IgA deficiency — most common humoral immunodeficiency; usually asymptomatic but increased infections, anaphylaxis to IgA-containing blood products.

**IgE** (<0.001%):

- Binds high-affinity FcεRI on mast cells/basophils → arms them.
- Allergen cross-linking → degranulation → allergy.
- Helminth defence (binds parasite, recruits eosinophils).
- Targeted by **omalizumab** (anti-IgE) for severe asthma, urticaria.

**IgD** (<1%):

- B cell receptor (along with IgM) on naïve B cells.
- Function in serum unclear.

**Class switching** — B cells can switch from IgM → IgG, IgA, or IgE depending on cytokine signals (IFN-γ → IgG; TGF-β → IgA; IL-4 → IgE). AID enzyme catalyses class switch recombination.

**Hyper-IgM syndrome** — CD40L deficiency on T cells → no class switching → only IgM produced (high) → recurrent infections.

(Murphy 2017; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e.)
