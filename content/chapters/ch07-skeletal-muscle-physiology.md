## Pass 1 — Architecture, sarcomere components, sliding-filament logic, cross-bridge cycle

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The functional contractile unit of skeletal muscle, demarcated by adjacent Z lines, is called the:

Correct answer: Sarcomere — bounded by two Z lines (~2 µm at rest), with a central A band (thick filaments) flanked by two I bands (thin filaments only); the H band is the central portion of the A band where only thick filaments lie, with the M line at its centre.

Distractors:

- "Myofibril." — Reveals misconception: student names the next-larger structural unit. Correction: a myofibril is a long bundle of repeating sarcomeres in series; the sarcomere is the _single_ contractile unit between adjacent Z lines.
- "T tubule." — Reveals misconception: student conflates a membrane invagination involved in excitation with the contractile unit. Correction: T tubules are invaginations of the sarcolemma that conduct depolarisation deep into the fibre; they are not contractile units.
- "Triad." — Reveals misconception: student conflates the structural arrangement that mediates excitation–contraction coupling with the contractile unit. Correction: the triad is one T tubule plus two flanking SR terminal cisternae at the A–I junction; it triggers Ca²⁺ release but is not itself the contractile unit.

Explanation: The sarcomere is the repeating contractile unit, ~2 µm in length at rest. It is bounded by two Z lines (Z discs), with a central A band (containing the thick filaments) flanked by two I bands (thin filaments only). The H band is the central portion of the A band where only thick filaments lie, with the M line at its centre [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Organisation of Skeletal Muscle; Costanzo ch.1 — Skeletal Muscle]. During contraction, the A band remains fixed in length; the H and I bands shorten as thin and thick filaments slide past one another (sliding filament theory).

Hints:

1. The unit is bounded by two Z lines.
2. Its rest length is ~2 µm.
3. It is the smallest unit that produces force on activation.

QUESTION 2
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: During contraction of skeletal muscle, which sarcomere band or zone changes most prominently in width?

Correct answer: The H band and I band shorten while the A band remains constant.

Distractors:

- "The A band shortens." — Reveals misconception: student assumes the thick filaments themselves shorten. Correction: the A band corresponds to the length of the thick filaments, which do not shorten — only the relative positions of thick and thin filaments change.
- "The thick filaments shorten." — Reveals misconception: student misunderstands the sliding-filament mechanism. Correction: thick and thin filaments retain their lengths; the sarcomere shortens because the filaments slide past one another.
- "The Z lines move apart." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the direction of Z-line movement. Correction: Z lines move _toward_ each other during contraction (the sarcomere shortens), not apart.

Explanation: The sliding filament theory holds that thick and thin filaments do not themselves shorten; rather, they slide past one another. Because the A band corresponds to the length of the thick filaments, it remains constant. The H band (zone with no thin–thick overlap) and I band (zone with no thick filaments) shorten as thin filaments slide inward [Costanzo ch.1; Guyton & Hall ch.6]. The Z lines move toward each other, and the sarcomere as a whole shortens.

Hints:

1. The thick filaments are responsible for the A band — does this band change?
2. Two zones shorten; one stays the same.
3. Z lines move toward each other.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The thin-filament protein whose Ca²⁺-binding subunit triggers the conformational shift in tropomyosin that uncovers myosin-binding sites on actin is:

Correct answer: Troponin C (the Ca²⁺-binding subunit of the troponin complex).

Distractors:

- "Troponin I." — Reveals misconception: student confuses the inhibitory subunit with the Ca²⁺-sensor subunit. Correction: TnI binds and inhibits actin in the resting state; Ca²⁺ binding to TnC pulls TnI off actin.
- "Troponin T." — Reveals misconception: student confuses the tropomyosin-binding subunit with the Ca²⁺-sensor subunit. Correction: TnT binds tropomyosin and anchors the troponin complex; it is not the Ca²⁺ sensor.
- "Tropomodulin." — Reveals misconception: student names a thin-filament capping protein. Correction: tropomodulin caps the pointed end of the thin filament; it does not bind Ca²⁺ in the activation cascade.

Explanation: The troponin complex has three subunits — Troponin T (binds tropomyosin), Troponin I (binds and inhibits actin), and Troponin C (binds Ca²⁺). Ca²⁺ binding to TnC pulls TnI off actin and shifts tropomyosin away from the myosin-binding sites, allowing cross-bridge formation [Berne & Levy ch.12; Costanzo ch.1]. Each tropomyosin dimer covers seven actin monomers (the structural basis of "co-operative" activation, since once a few cross-bridges form, tropomyosin moves further away and additional sites are exposed).

Hints:

1. The complex has three subunits — T, I, and C.
2. Look at the letter — the Ca²⁺-binding one shares its initial.
3. It is the upstream sensor; the others act downstream.

QUESTION 4
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Which of the following is the correct order of events in one cross-bridge cycle, starting from the rigor state?

Correct answer: ATP binds the rigor cross-bridge → cross-bridge detaches from actin → ATP is hydrolysed (ADP·Pi remains bound), re-cocking the head → cocked head re-binds actin → Pi release triggers the power stroke → ADP release returns the head to the rigor state.

Distractors:

- "ATP hydrolysis → power stroke → ATP binding → detachment." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the role of ATP in detachment. Correction: ATP binding (not hydrolysis) is what causes the head to detach from actin; hydrolysis re-cocks the head for the next stroke.
- "Power stroke → ATP binding → ATP hydrolysis → cross-bridge detachment." — Reveals misconception: student places the power stroke at the start without rigor-state context. Correction: from the rigor state, ATP binding causes detachment first; the power stroke occurs after the cocked head re-binds actin and releases Pi.
- "Ca²⁺ binding to myosin → ATP binding → power stroke." — Reveals misconception: student places the Ca²⁺ sensor on myosin. Correction: in skeletal muscle, Ca²⁺ binds Troponin C on the _thin_ filament, not myosin; the cross-bridge cycle is then permitted to proceed.

Explanation: From the rigor state (myosin tightly bound to actin, no nucleotide), ATP binds the myosin head and detaches it from actin; ATP is hydrolysed to ADP·Pi, which re-cocks the head; the cocked head with ADP·Pi binds a new actin site weakly; Pi release triggers the power stroke (~10 nm displacement of actin relative to myosin); ADP release leaves the head in the rigor configuration, ready for the next ATP [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Cross-Bridge Cycling; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Guyton & Hall ch.6]. Without ATP (post-mortem or severe anoxia), cross-bridges remain attached in the rigor state — the molecular basis of rigor mortis.

Hints:

1. ATP must bind first to release the cross-bridge from rigor.
2. Hydrolysis (not binding) re-cocks the head.
3. Pi release triggers the power stroke.

Bloom's level: remember

Stem: The proteins responsible for anchoring the thin filament to the Z line and determining its length are:

Correct answer: α-Actinin (cross-links actin filaments at the Z line) and nebulin (a "molecular ruler" running along the thin filament that helps define its length).

Distractors:

- "Titin alone." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a thick-filament-tethering function to thin-filament anchoring. Correction: titin runs from Z line to M line and tethers the _thick_ filament; the thin filament is anchored by α-actinin and length-defined by nebulin.
- "Tropomodulin alone." — Reveals misconception: student names the pointed-end capping protein and assumes it does the anchoring. Correction: tropomodulin caps the pointed end of the thin filament (away from Z line) but does not anchor it to the Z line; α-actinin does the anchoring.
- "Desmin." — Reveals misconception: student confuses an intermediate-filament cytoskeletal protein with thin-filament-Z-line anchoring. Correction: desmin aligns adjacent myofibrils at the Z line, but does not anchor the thin filament directly.

Explanation: α-Actinin cross-links actin filaments at the Z line, and nebulin runs along the thin filament from the Z line and helps define its length (a "length ruler"). Tropomodulin caps the pointed end of the thin filament. Titin (the largest known protein) tethers the thick filament to the Z line and provides passive elasticity. Desmin is a cytoskeletal intermediate filament that aligns adjacent myofibrils at the Z line [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Organisation; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. Mutations in nebulin underlie nemaline myopathy.

Hints:

1. Two proteins are involved — one anchors, the other defines length.
2. The anchoring protein cross-links actin filaments.
3. The length-determining protein has a name suggesting "thread"/"ruler".

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The very large protein that spans from the Z line to the M line, providing passive elastic recoil and centring the thick filament in the sarcomere, is:

Correct answer: Titin (also called connectin) — ~3,000–3,800 kDa, the largest known protein in humans.

Distractors:

- "Nebulin." — Reveals misconception: student confuses thin-filament length-ruler with thick-filament tether. Correction: nebulin runs along the thin filament; titin runs from Z line to M line tethering and centring the _thick_ filament.
- "Desmin." — Reveals misconception: student names an intermediate filament. Correction: desmin links adjacent myofibrils laterally at the Z line; it does not run within a single sarcomere from Z to M line.
- "Dystrophin." — Reveals misconception: student names a sub-sarcolemmal cytoskeletal protein. Correction: dystrophin is at the inside of the sarcolemma (linking F-actin to the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex), not within the sarcomere.

Explanation: Titin (~3,000–3,800 kDa) is the largest known protein and runs the half-sarcomere from Z line to M line. It is responsible for the passive tension of stretched muscle (its PEVK and Ig-domain regions act like molecular springs) and helps re-centre the thick filament during cycling [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. Mutations in titin underlie certain dilated cardiomyopathies and inherited muscular dystrophies. Titin also plays a signalling role through interactions with calpain protease and stretch-sensitive kinase domains.

Hints:

1. The protein spans the half-sarcomere.
2. It is the largest single polypeptide encoded by the human genome.
3. It accounts for most of the passive elasticity of stretched muscle.

Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Surrounding the sarcolemma is a sub-membrane cytoskeletal protein whose absence (X-linked recessive) causes Duchenne muscular dystrophy. This protein is:

Correct answer: Dystrophin — links sub-sarcolemmal F-actin to the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex (DGC) and thence to laminin in the basal lamina; loss → membrane fragility, repeated contraction-induced damage, and progressive muscle necrosis.

Distractors:

- "Spectrin." — Reveals misconception: student substitutes a related cytoskeletal protein from another tissue. Correction: spectrin is the major sub-membrane cytoskeleton of erythrocytes (and of neurons in axon initial segments), not the disease-relevant protein in DMD.
- "Vimentin." — Reveals misconception: student names an intermediate-filament protein. Correction: vimentin is an intermediate filament in mesenchymal cells; DMD reflects loss of dystrophin, a different cytoskeletal protein at the sarcolemma.
- "Caldesmon." — Reveals misconception: student names a smooth-muscle thin-filament regulator. Correction: caldesmon regulates smooth-muscle contraction; it has no role in DMD or in sarcolemmal stability.

Explanation: Dystrophin links the sub-sarcolemmal cytoskeleton (F-actin) to the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex (DGC; α/β-dystroglycan, sarcoglycans, syntrophins, dystrobrevin) in the membrane and thence to the basal lamina (laminin α2, dystroglycan). Loss of dystrophin (DMD gene, Xp21) leads to membrane fragility, repeated contraction-induced damage, calcium leak, mitochondrial dysfunction, fibrosis, fatty replacement, and progressive proximal weakness — Duchenne muscular dystrophy [Berne & Levy ch.12; Guyton & Hall ch.6]. Becker muscular dystrophy is caused by partial-loss-of-function mutations of the same gene (truncated but partly functional protein). Calf pseudohypertrophy and elevated serum creatine kinase are characteristic. Therapies: corticosteroids, exon-skipping antisense oligonucleotides (eteplirsen for exon 51 amenable mutations; golodirsen, viltolarsen for exon 53; casimersen for exon 45), and gene therapy approaches in development.

Hints:

1. The protein name shares the disorder's name.
2. The gene is on the X chromosome (Xp21).
3. The protein links membrane cytoskeleton to the basal lamina.

QUESTION 8
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The arrangement at the A–I junction of skeletal muscle in which a T tubule is flanked on both sides by terminal cisternae of the sarcoplasmic reticulum is called a:

Correct answer: Triad (one T tubule + two SR terminal cisternae).

Distractors:

- "Dyad." — Reveals misconception: student names the cardiac arrangement. Correction: dyads (one T tubule + one terminal cisterna) are the cardiac arrangement; skeletal muscle has triads.
- "Diaphragm." — Reveals misconception: student confuses a muscle with a sub-cellular structure. Correction: the diaphragm is the principal muscle of inspiration; "triad" is the term for the T-tubule/SR arrangement.
- "Z disc." — Reveals misconception: student conflates a sarcomere boundary structure with the EC-coupling structure. Correction: the Z disc is a sarcomere boundary; the triad is the T tubule + SR arrangement.

Explanation: In mammalian skeletal muscle, each T tubule is flanked by two SR terminal cisternae — a **triad** at the A–I junction (two triads per sarcomere). The triad allows DHPRs in the T-tubule membrane to mechanically couple to RyR1 channels in the SR, enabling rapid voltage-controlled Ca²⁺ release [Berne & Levy ch.12; Costanzo ch.1]. **Cardiac muscle** has **dyads** (one T tubule + one terminal cisterna), with T tubules located at the Z line rather than the A–I junction, and uses Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release rather than mechanical coupling.

Hints:

1. Count the structures involved — three (1 + 2).
2. The arrangement is at the A–I junction in skeletal muscle.
3. Cardiac muscle uses a different (smaller) arrangement.

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The thick-filament myosin protein consists of:

Correct answer: A hexamer with two heavy chains (~200 kDa each) plus four light chains (two essential and two regulatory; ~20 kDa each).

Distractors:

- "A monomer with one heavy chain." — Reveals misconception: student underestimates the multimeric structure. Correction: myosin II is a hexamer of two heavy chains plus four light chains; it is not a single-chain monomer.
- "A dimer of two identical light chains only." — Reveals misconception: student inverts heavy- and light-chain identity. Correction: the heavy chains form the long coiled-coil tail and the head; the light chains are smaller regulatory subunits associated with the head's neck.
- "A pentamer with five identical subunits." — Reveals misconception: student confuses myosin with a pentameric channel. Correction: myosin II is a hexamer with two distinct subunit types (heavy and light), not a pentamer of identical subunits.

Explanation: Myosin II is a hexamer: two heavy chains (~200 kDa each) plus four light chains (two essential, two regulatory; ~20 kDa each). The heavy chains form a long α-helical coiled-coil tail and a globular head at the N-terminus. The head (S1 fragment) contains the actin-binding site and the ATPase activity; the light chains associate with the neck region and modulate head function [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. Phosphorylation of the regulatory light chains modulates contractility, especially in _smooth_ muscle (where it is the principal switch — myosin light-chain kinase).

Hints:

1. Six subunits in total.
2. Two heavy and four light.
3. The heads project from the long tail.

QUESTION 10
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The actin-binding S1 head of myosin contains the catalytic site for:

Correct answer: An actin-activated Mg²⁺-ATPase — each cycle of ATP binding, hydrolysis, and product release is mechanically coupled to one power stroke.

Distractors:

- "Adenylyl cyclase." — Reveals misconception: student names an enzyme of GPCR signalling. Correction: adenylyl cyclase converts ATP to cAMP; it is unrelated to the myosin power-stroke cycle.
- "Phosphodiesterase." — Reveals misconception: student names an enzyme that degrades cyclic nucleotides. Correction: PDE breaks down cAMP/cGMP; it is not the catalytic activity of myosin.
- "Phospholipase C." — Reveals misconception: student names a phosphoinositide-cleaving enzyme. Correction: PLC cleaves PIP₂ to IP3 and DAG; it is unrelated to myosin's contractile cycle.

Explanation: The S1 fragment of myosin is an actin-activated Mg²⁺-ATPase. Each cycle of ATP binding, hydrolysis, and product release is mechanically coupled to one power stroke (ADP·Pi-bound head binds actin, Pi release triggers the stroke, ADP release returns the head to the rigor state, fresh ATP binding releases the head) [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. Myosin ATPase rate is the principal molecular determinant of contraction velocity — fast-twitch fibres express isoforms with high ATPase activity (MyHC-IIx and IIb), giving rapid Vmax; slow-twitch fibres express MyHC-I (low ATPase), with slower Vmax but greater economy of force.

Hints:

1. The catalytic activity acts on a nucleotide.
2. It is "actin-activated" (binding of actin accelerates the rate).
3. The product hydrolysis cycle drives the power stroke.

## Pass 2 — Excitation–contraction coupling, Ca²⁺ handling, twitch and tetanus, length-tension, force-velocity

QUESTION 11
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The signal that triggers Ca²⁺ release from the SR in skeletal-muscle excitation–contraction coupling is:

Correct answer: A conformational change in the dihydropyridine receptor (DHPR; an L-type Ca²⁺ channel acting as a voltage sensor) caused by T-tubule depolarisation, which mechanically opens the apposed RyR1 channel in the SR — Ca²⁺ entry through the DHPR is **not required**.

Distractors:

- "Ca²⁺ entering the cell through the DHPR." — Reveals misconception: student applies the cardiac CICR mechanism to skeletal muscle. Correction: in skeletal muscle, the DHPR acts purely as a voltage sensor and mechanically opens RyR1 — Ca²⁺ entry through the DHPR is not necessary; in cardiac muscle, Ca²⁺ entry through the DHPR is essential to trigger CICR.
- "cAMP-dependent phosphorylation of the RyR." — Reveals misconception: student introduces a regulatory mechanism that does not initiate skeletal ECC. Correction: PKA can phosphorylate RyR2 (cardiac) to modulate Ca²⁺ release, but is not the trigger in skeletal-muscle ECC.
- "IP₃ produced at the plasma membrane." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a metabotropic-receptor mechanism to skeletal-muscle ECC. Correction: IP₃-mediated Ca²⁺ release from the ER occurs in many cell types via IP3 receptors, but skeletal-muscle ECC is mechanically coupled DHPR–RyR1, not IP3-driven.

Explanation: Skeletal-muscle ECC is unique in that it does not require Ca²⁺ entry through the DHPR. The DHPR (an L-type Ca²⁺ channel, Cav1.1, in the T-tubule membrane) acts as a voltage sensor; on depolarisation it undergoes a conformational change and physically (mechanically) opens the apposed RyR1 in the SR membrane, releasing stored Ca²⁺ into the cytosol [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Excitation–Contraction Coupling; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9; Guyton & Hall ch.7]. This contrasts with cardiac muscle, where Ca²⁺ entry through the DHPR (Cav1.2) is required to trigger Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release through RyR2. In smooth muscle, IP₃-mediated SR Ca²⁺ release plus voltage-gated and store-operated Ca²⁺ entry contribute. Mutations in CACNA1S (DHPR α₁s) or RYR1 underlie hypokalaemic periodic paralysis and malignant hyperthermia respectively.

Hints:

1. The trigger involves a _mechanical_ coupling, not Ca²⁺ entry.
2. The DHPR acts as a sensor; the RyR1 acts as the release channel.
3. Cardiac muscle uses a related but different mechanism (CICR).

QUESTION 12
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Relaxation of skeletal muscle following a twitch occurs primarily because cytosolic Ca²⁺ is removed by:

Correct answer: Active reuptake into the SR by SERCA, which uses 1 ATP to transport 2 Ca²⁺ ions inward.

Distractors:

- "Ca²⁺ extrusion through the plasma-membrane Ca²⁺-ATPase (PMCA)." — Reveals misconception: student names a real pump but incorrect predominant mechanism. Correction: PMCA contributes a small fraction of Ca²⁺ removal in muscle; the dominant mechanism is SR reuptake by SERCA, which has both higher capacity and the role of refilling stores for the next twitch.
- "Diffusion alone." — Reveals misconception: student underestimates active transport. Correction: passive diffusion alone could not lower cytosolic Ca²⁺ rapidly enough to terminate a twitch; ATP-driven SERCA is required.
- "Binding to troponin T." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong troponin subunit and conflates binding with removal. Correction: Ca²⁺ binds Troponin C (the sensor); binding to TnC does not "remove" Ca²⁺ — it triggers contraction. Removal is via SERCA reuptake into the SR.

Explanation: SERCA (Sarco/Endoplasmic Reticulum Ca²⁺-ATPase) transports two Ca²⁺ ions into the SR lumen per ATP hydrolysed [Berne & Levy ch.12; Costanzo ch.1]. SERCA1 is the dominant isoform in fast-twitch skeletal muscle; SERCA2a (regulated by phospholamban — relieved on β-adrenergic phosphorylation) predominates in slow-twitch and cardiac muscle. Loss of SERCA1 function causes **Brody disease** — exercise-induced impaired relaxation. Cardiac glycosides (digoxin) act on a distinct system (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → ↓ Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchange → ↑ cytosolic Ca²⁺ in cardiomyocytes) — not directly on SERCA.

Hints:

1. The mechanism is active and ATP-dependent.
2. Ca²⁺ goes back into the SR, not out of the cell.
3. Two Ca²⁺ ions per ATP.

QUESTION 13

Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Inside the SR lumen, Ca²⁺ is stored bound to a low-affinity, high-capacity Ca²⁺-buffer protein called:

Correct answer: Calsequestrin (in the lumen of the terminal cisternae; tethered near the RyR by triadin and junctin).

Distractors:

- "Calmodulin." — Reveals misconception: student names a cytosolic Ca²⁺-binding protein with high affinity. Correction: calmodulin is a small cytosolic Ca²⁺ sensor that activates many enzymes (e.g., MLCK, CaMKII); it is _not_ the SR storage protein.
- "Parvalbumin." — Reveals misconception: student names another cytosolic Ca²⁺ buffer. Correction: parvalbumin is a high-affinity cytosolic Ca²⁺ buffer in fast-twitch fibres (helps speed relaxation by sequestering cytosolic Ca²⁺ until SERCA can act); it is not in the SR lumen.
- "Calbindin." — Reveals misconception: student names a cytosolic Ca²⁺ buffer in intestinal/renal/CNS epithelia. Correction: calbindin (and calretinin) are cytosolic — they buffer Ca²⁺ in epithelia and neurons but are not the SR-luminal storage protein.

Explanation: Calsequestrin sits in the lumen of the terminal cisternae and stores Ca²⁺ at high concentration (low affinity / high capacity, ~50 Ca²⁺ ions per protein) so that, when the RyR opens, a steep concentration gradient drives rapid Ca²⁺ release into the cytosol [Berne & Levy ch.12]. Triadin and junctin tether calsequestrin near the RyR1 in skeletal muscle (and RyR2 in cardiac muscle, where calsequestrin-2 is the isoform). Mutations in calsequestrin-2 underlie a form of catecholaminergic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (CPVT). Calmodulin and calbindin are cytosolic Ca²⁺-binding proteins; parvalbumin is a fast-cytosolic Ca²⁺ buffer in fast-twitch fibres.

Hints:

1. The protein is _inside_ the SR lumen (not cytosolic).
2. Low affinity but high capacity allows large stores at moderate free [Ca²⁺].
3. It is tethered near the release channel.

QUESTION 14
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A single brief stimulus produces a twitch contraction; rapid repeated stimulation results in fused tetanus. The principal mechanism behind tetanus is:

Correct answer: Repeated SR Ca²⁺ release before the previous Ca²⁺ has been fully sequestered, sustaining cytosolic [Ca²⁺] above the level needed for full troponin saturation and producing the maximal force the fibre can generate.

Distractors:

- "Action potential summation in the muscle fibre." — Reveals misconception: student attributes mechanical summation to AP summation. Correction: action potentials are followed by an absolute refractory period and do _not_ summate; what summates is the slower mechanical Ca²⁺-driven force generation.
- "Recruitment of additional motor units." — Reveals misconception: student conflates whole-muscle force grading with single-fibre tetanus. Correction: recruitment is one mechanism for whole-muscle force grading at the population level, but tetanus refers to summation of force within a single fibre/motor unit driven repeatedly.
- "A change in myosin ATPase rate." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a kinetic change to a sustained-Ca²⁺ phenomenon. Correction: myosin ATPase rate does not change moment to moment with stimulation frequency; tetanus is explained by sustained cytosolic Ca²⁺ keeping troponin C saturated.

Explanation: A single twitch reflects a brief Ca²⁺ transient that decays before peak force is reached. With repeated stimulation faster than the rate of Ca²⁺ reuptake, cytosolic [Ca²⁺] does not fall enough between stimuli — troponin C remains saturated, and force builds to the maximum (fused tetanus) [Berne & Levy ch.12; Costanzo ch.1]. Note that muscle action potentials themselves do not summate (they are followed by a refractory period); it is the slower mechanical / Ca²⁺ events that summate. Tetanic force is typically 3–5× single-twitch force in mammalian skeletal muscle. **Cardiac muscle cannot tetanise** — its long action potential (~200 ms with Ca²⁺-mediated plateau) gives a refractory period that outlasts the contractile event, protecting diastolic filling.

Hints:

1. The summing event is mechanical, not electrical.
2. SR Ca²⁺ does not have time to be fully sequestered.
3. Tetanic force is greater than single-twitch force.

QUESTION 15
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The skeletal-muscle length–tension relationship has its peak (maximum active force) at a sarcomere length of approximately:

Correct answer: 2.0–2.2 µm — the sarcomere length giving optimal overlap between thick and thin filaments and therefore the maximum number of cross-bridges that can form.

Distractors:

- "1.0 µm." — Reveals misconception: student picks a value below physiological optimum. Correction: at sarcomere lengths well below ~1.6 µm, thin filaments collide and the H zone closes against the Z line, sharply reducing force.
- "3.6 µm." — Reveals misconception: student picks a value above physiological optimum. Correction: at sarcomere lengths beyond ~3.6 µm there is essentially no thin–thick overlap, and active force falls to zero.
- "0.5 µm." — Reveals misconception: student picks an unphysiologically short value. Correction: real sarcomeres do not approach 0.5 µm; this is below the thick filament length itself.

Explanation: Maximum active force occurs at the sarcomere length that gives the optimal overlap between thick and thin filaments — about 2.0–2.2 µm in mammalian skeletal muscle [Berne & Levy ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. At shorter lengths thin filaments collide or overlap, and the H zone closes against the Z line, reducing force. At longer lengths overlap is reduced, and beyond ~3.6 µm there is no overlap and active force is zero. Passive (titin-based) elastic tension rises sharply only at lengths beyond Lo. The length–tension relationship is the molecular basis of the **Frank-Starling** law in cardiac muscle (Ch. 21).

Hints:

1. Optimal overlap is the determinant.
2. Below ~1.6 µm and above ~3.6 µm, active force is reduced.
3. The physiological resting length lies near 2.0–2.2 µm.

Stem: The skeletal-muscle force–velocity relationship shows that:

Correct answer: Velocity is highest when the load is zero (Vmax) and falls hyperbolically as load increases, reaching zero when load equals the maximum isometric tension that the muscle can produce.

Distractors:

- "Velocity is highest at maximum load." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the relationship. Correction: velocity is _highest_ at zero load and _zero_ at maximum isometric load; high load and high velocity cannot occur simultaneously.
- "Force and velocity are independent." — Reveals misconception: student assumes muscle output is independent of load. Correction: force and velocity are tightly inversely related; the relationship is a hallmark of the cross-bridge cycle kinetics.
- "Force rises with velocity." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the direction. Correction: at higher shortening velocities, fewer cross-bridges are attached at any instant, so force _falls_; the curve is monotonically decreasing.

Explanation: Maximum shortening velocity (Vmax) is observed at zero external load. Vmax is set principally by myosin ATPase activity (the cross-bridge cycling rate), which is why fast-twitch fibres expressing high-ATPase myosin isoforms (MyHC-IIx, IIb) have higher Vmax than slow-twitch fibres (MyHC-I). The product of force × velocity is power; power is maximum at intermediate loads (~30% of maximum isometric force) — a fact that informs ergonomic and athletic training prescriptions.

1. As load increases, velocity _decreases_.
2. Vmax occurs at zero load; F-max occurs at zero velocity.
3. The shape of the curve is hyperbolic.

Stem: The whole-muscle force generated by a brain-driven voluntary effort is graded by:

Correct answer: Recruitment of more motor units (in size-principle order: small/slow first, large/fast progressively) PLUS increasing the firing rate of recruited motor units (rate coding → unfused or fused tetanus).

Distractors:

- "Varying the amplitude of the muscle action potential." — Reveals misconception: student treats action potentials as graded. Correction: action potentials are all-or-none — they do not vary in amplitude. Force grading uses recruitment and rate coding, not AP amplitude.
- "Releasing different neurotransmitters at the NMJ." — Reveals misconception: student introduces neurotransmitter switching where none occurs. Correction: the NMJ uses ACh as the sole neurotransmitter; force grading occurs at the levels of motor-unit recruitment and rate coding, not transmitter identity.
- "Lengthening the action potential." — Reveals misconception: student treats AP duration as a force-grading variable. Correction: AP duration is essentially fixed at a few ms; force grading is via recruitment and rate coding, not AP duration.

Explanation: Force is graded chiefly by (i) **recruitment** — larger force requires recruiting more motor units, in order of increasing motor-unit size (Henneman's size principle: small slow-fatigue-resistant first, large fast last); and (ii) **rate coding** — increasing firing rate of recruited motor units, which produces unfused or fused tetanus and increased force per fibre [Berne & Levy ch.12; Guyton & Hall ch.6]. Action potential amplitude does not vary — APs are all-or-none. Different muscles use these two strategies to different degrees: fine-control muscles (e.g., extraocular, finger lumbricals) rely heavily on rate coding because they have small motor-unit pools with little recruitment range; large limb muscles use recruitment more extensively.

Hints:

1. Two complementary mechanisms grade force.
2. APs are all-or-none — amplitude is not a control variable.
3. The Henneman principle determines recruitment order.

Stem: Rigor mortis after death is best explained by:

Correct answer: Loss of ATP needed for cross-bridge detachment, while Ca²⁺ leaks out of the SR and binds Troponin C — the muscle locks in the actin-bound state.

Distractors:

- "Failure of Ca²⁺ release." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the Ca²⁺ direction. Correction: in death, Ca²⁺ leaks _out of_ the SR into the cytosol (because ATP-dependent SERCA fails), activating troponin and locking on cross-bridges; release is increased, not failed.
- "Excess intracellular Mg²⁺." — Reveals misconception: student attributes rigor to a Mg²⁺ change. Correction: Mg²⁺ has no specific role in rigor; the cause is ATP loss preventing detachment.
- "Persistent autonomic stimulation." — Reveals misconception: student attributes rigor to ongoing nerve activity in the dead. Correction: rigor occurs after death without nerve activity; it is a local biochemical phenomenon at the cross-bridges.

Explanation: Cross-bridge detachment requires ATP binding to the myosin head; without ATP, myosin remains bound to actin in the rigor state. Concurrently, ATP-dependent SERCA activity ceases and Ca²⁺ leaks from the SR into the cytosol, activating troponin C and locking cross-bridges in the actin-bound state [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Cross-Bridge Cycling; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. Rigor mortis classically appears within 2–6 hours of death (faster in warm conditions and in muscles that were active near the time of death), reaches maximum at ~12 hours, and resolves over 24–48 hours as autolytic protein degradation breaks cross-bridges. Forensic value: timing of rigor offers an estimate of post-mortem interval; warm environments and prior exertion accelerate onset. The same locked cross-bridge state in the cycle diagram is the reason curare-paralysed muscles (which do not depolarise but retain ATP) do not enter rigor.

Hints:

1. Cross-bridges cannot detach without ATP.
2. SR Ca²⁺ leaks into the cytosol post-mortem, activating contraction.
3. Rigor develops over hours and resolves with autolytic protein degradation.

Stem: Muscle fatigue during sustained intense exercise is best explained by:

Correct answer: A combination of phosphocreatine depletion, accumulation of inorganic phosphate (which inhibits cross-bridge cycling), depletion of glycogen, lactate / H⁺ accumulation, and reduced SR Ca²⁺ release; ATP itself is rarely depleted to levels that cause rigor.

Distractors:

- "Exhaustion of ATP — fibres going into rigor." — Reveals misconception: student equates fatigue with terminal ATP loss. Correction: regulatory mechanisms reduce force well before ATP depletion would cause rigor; physiological fatigue is reversible.
- "Loss of motor neurons." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a rapid reversible phenomenon to a neurodegenerative process. Correction: motor neuron loss (e.g., in motor neuron disease) is irreversible and chronic; exercise fatigue is acute and reversible after rest.
- "Fall in core body temperature." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the temperature direction. Correction: during exercise core temperature rises, not falls; the rise can contribute to fatigue at extremes (e.g., heat stroke).

Explanation: Muscle fibres do not, under physiological exercise, run out of ATP enough to enter rigor — a battery of regulatory signals reduces force well before that critical depletion. Fast glycolytic fibres are the most susceptible to rapid fatigue; slow oxidative fibres are most fatigue-resistant. Recovery after fatigue: PCr re-synthesis (~minutes via oxidative phosphorylation), Pi clearance, pH restoration, glycogen replenishment (hours).

Hints:

1. Multiple metabolic factors converge.
2. ATP itself is not depleted to rigor levels in physiological exercise.
3. CNS / central drive is also a contributor.
