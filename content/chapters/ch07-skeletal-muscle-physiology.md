# Chapter 7 — Skeletal Muscle Physiology — MCQs

---

QUESTION 1

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The functional contractile unit of skeletal muscle, demarcated by adjacent Z lines, is called the:

Correct answer: Sarcomere

Distractors:

- "Myofibril" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "T tubule" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Triad" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: The sarcomere is the repeating contractile unit, ~2 μm in length at rest. It is bounded by two Z lines (also called Z discs), with a central A band (containing thick filaments) flanked by two I bands (thin filaments only). The H band is the central portion of the A band where only thick filaments lie, with the M line at its centre (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Organisation of Skeletal Muscle; Costanzo, 7e — Skeletal Muscle).

QUESTION 2

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: During contraction, which sarcomere band/zone changes most prominently in width?

Correct answer: The H band and I band shorten while the A band remains constant

Distractors:

- "The A band shortens" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The thick filaments shorten" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The Z lines move apart" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: The sliding filament theory holds that thick and thin filaments do not themselves shorten; rather, they slide past one another. Because the A band corresponds to the length of the thick filaments, it remains constant. The H band (zone with no thin-thick overlap) and I band (zone with no thick filaments) shorten as thin filaments slide inward. The Z lines move toward each other (Costanzo, 7e — Skeletal Muscle; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.6).

QUESTION 3

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The thin filament protein whose Ca²⁺-binding subunit triggers the conformational shift in tropomyosin that uncovers myosin-binding sites on actin is:

Correct answer: Troponin C

Distractors:

- "Troponin I" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Troponin T" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Tropomodulin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: The troponin complex has three subunits — Troponin T binds tropomyosin, Troponin I binds and inhibits actin, Troponin C binds Ca²⁺. Ca²⁺ binding to TnC pulls TnI off actin and shifts tropomyosin away from the myosin-binding sites, allowing cross-bridge formation. Each tropomyosin dimer covers seven actin monomers (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Costanzo, 7e — Skeletal Muscle).

QUESTION 4

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Which of the following is the correct order of events in one cross-bridge cycle, starting from the rigor state?

Correct answer: ATP binding → cross-bridge detachment → ATP hydrolysis → cocked head re-binding → power stroke (Pi and ADP release)

Distractors:

- "ATP hydrolysis → power stroke → ATP binding → detachment" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Power stroke → ATP binding → ATP hydrolysis → cross-bridge detachment" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Ca²⁺ binding to myosin → ATP binding → power stroke" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: From the rigor state (myosin tightly bound to actin, no nucleotide), ATP binds the myosin head and detaches it; ATP is hydrolysed to ADP·Pi, which re-cocks the head; the cocked head with ADP·Pi binds a new actin site; Pi release triggers the power stroke (~10 nm displacement); ADP release returns the head to rigor. Without ATP (e.g., post-mortem or anoxia), the cycle cannot detach and rigor mortis ensues (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Cross-Bridge Cycling; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.6).

QUESTION 5

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The protein that anchors the thin filament to the Z line and helps determine its length is:

Correct answer: α-Actinin (anchors actin to Z line) and nebulin (length ruler along the thin filament)

Distractors:

- "Titin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Tropomodulin alone" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Desmin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: α-Actinin cross-links actin filaments at the Z line; nebulin runs along the thin filament from the Z line and helps define its length. Tropomodulin caps the pointed end. Titin (the largest known protein) tethers the thick filament to the Z line and provides passive elasticity. Desmin is a cytoskeletal intermediate filament that aligns adjacent myofibrils at the Z line (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Organization; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

QUESTION 6

Difficulty (F / I / A): i

Stem: The very large protein that spans from the Z line to the M line, providing passive elastic recoil and centring the thick filament in the sarcomere, is:

Correct answer: Titin (connectin)

Distractors:

- "Nebulin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Desmin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 7

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Surrounding the sarcolemma is a basement-membrane-associated cytoskeletal protein whose absence (X-linked recessive) causes Duchenne muscular dystrophy. This protein is:

Correct answer: Dystrophin

Distractors:

- "Spectrin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Vimentin" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Caldesmon" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Dystrophin links the sub-sarcolemmal cytoskeleton (F-actin) to the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex (DGC) in the membrane and thence to the basal lamina (laminin, dystroglycan). QUESTION 8

Difficulty (F / I / A): i

Stem: The arrangement at the A-I junction of skeletal muscle in which a T tubule is flanked on both sides by terminal cisternae of the sarcoplasmic reticulum is called a:

Correct answer: Triad

Distractors:

- "Dyad" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Diaphragm" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Z disc" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: In mammalian skeletal muscle, each T tubule is flanked by two SR terminal cisternae — a "triad." (Cardiac muscle has dyads, with one terminal cisterna per T tubule, and the T tubules in the heart are at the Z line rather than the A-I junction.) QUESTION 9

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The thick filament myosin protein consists of:

Correct answer: A hexamer with two heavy chains and four light chains (two essential and two regulatory)

Distractors:

- "A monomer with one heavy chain" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "A dimer of two identical light chains only" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "A pentamer with five identical subunits" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Myosin II is a hexamer: two heavy chains (~200 kDa each) plus four light chains (two essential, two regulatory; ~20 kDa each). The heavy chains form a long α-helical coiled-coil tail and a globular head at the N-terminus. The head (S1 fragment) contains the actin-binding site and the ATPase activity. Phosphorylation of the regulatory light chains modulates contractility, especially in smooth muscle (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

QUESTION 10

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The actin-binding S1 head of myosin contains the catalytic site for:

Correct answer: ATPase (specifically, an actin-activated Mg²⁺-ATPase)

Distractors:

- "Adenylyl cyclase" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Phosphodiesterase" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

QUESTION 11

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The signal that triggers Ca²⁺ release from the SR in skeletal-muscle excitation-contraction coupling is:

Correct answer: A conformational change in the DHPR (a voltage sensor) caused by T-tubule depolarisation, which mechanically opens the RyR1 channel

Distractors:

- "Ca²⁺ entering the cell through the DHPR" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "cAMP-dependent phosphorylation of the RyR" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "IP₃ produced at the plasma membrane" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Skeletal-muscle ECC is unique in that it does not require Ca²⁺ entry through the DHPR. The DHPR (an L-type Ca²⁺ channel in the T-tubule membrane) acts as a voltage sensor; on depolarisation it undergoes a conformational change and physically (mechanically) opens the apposed RyR1 in the SR membrane, releasing stored Ca²⁺ into the cytosol. This contrasts with cardiac muscle, where Ca²⁺ entry through the DHPR is required to trigger Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release through RyR2 (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Excitation-Contraction Coupling; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.7).

QUESTION 12

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Relaxation of skeletal muscle following a twitch occurs primarily because cytosolic Ca²⁺ is removed by:

Correct answer: Active reuptake into the SR by SERCA, which uses 1 ATP to transport 2 Ca²⁺ ions inward

Distractors:

- "Ca²⁺ extrusion through the plasma membrane Ca²⁺-ATPase (PMCA)" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Diffusion alone" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Binding to troponin T" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: SERCA (Sarco/Endoplasmic Reticulum Ca²⁺-ATPase) transports two Ca²⁺ ions into the SR lumen per ATP hydrolysed. SERCA1 is the dominant isoform in fast-twitch skeletal muscle; SERCA2a (regulated by phospholamban) predominates in slow-twitch and cardiac muscle. Loss of SERCA1 function causes Brody disease — exercise-induced impaired relaxation (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Costanzo, 7e — Skeletal Muscle).

Correct answer: Calsequestrin

QUESTION 14

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A single brief stimulus produces a twitch contraction; rapid repeated stimulation results in fused tetanus. The principal mechanism behind tetanus is:

Correct answer: Repeated Ca²⁺ release before the previous Ca²⁺ has been fully sequestered, sustaining cytosolic [Ca²⁺] above the level needed for full troponin saturation

Distractors:

- "Action potential summation in the muscle fibre" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Recruitment of additional motor units" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "A change in myosin ATPase rate" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: A single twitch reflects a brief Ca²⁺ transient that decays before peak force is reached. With repeated stimulation faster than the rate of Ca²⁺ reuptake, cytosolic [Ca²⁺] does not fall enough between stimuli — troponin C remains saturated, and force builds to the maximum (fused tetanus). Note that muscle action potentials themselves do not summate (they are followed by a refractory period); it is the slower mechanical/Ca²⁺ events that summate (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Costanzo, 7e).

QUESTION 15

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The skeletal-muscle length-tension relationship has its peak (maximum active force) at a sarcomere length of approximately:

Correct answer: 2.0–2.2 μm

Distractors:

- "1.0 μm" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "3.6 μm" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "0.5 μm" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Maximum active force occurs at the sarcomere length that gives the optimal overlap between thick and thin filaments — about 2.0–2.2 μm in mammalian skeletal muscle. At shorter lengths thin filaments collide or overlap, and the H zone closes against the Z line, reducing force. At longer lengths overlap is reduced, and beyond ~3.6 μm there is no overlap and active force is zero. Passive (titin-based) elastic tension rises sharply only at lengths beyond Lo (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

QUESTION 16

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The skeletal-muscle force-velocity relationship shows that:

Correct answer: Velocity is highest when the load is zero (Vmax) and falls as load increases, reaching zero when load equals maximum isometric tension

Distractors:

- "Velocity is highest at maximum load" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Force and velocity are independent" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Force rises with velocity" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Maximum shortening velocity (Vmax) is observed at zero external load. As load increases, velocity falls hyperbolically and reaches zero at the maximum isometric tension that the muscle can produce against an immovable load. Vmax is set principally by myosin ATPase activity (the cross-bridge cycling rate), which is why fast-twitch fibres expressing high-ATPase myosin isoforms have higher Vmax than slow-twitch fibres (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Costanzo, 7e).

QUESTION 17

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: An isometric contraction is one in which:

Correct answer: Muscle length stays constant; force changes

Distractors:

- "Force stays constant; muscle length changes" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Both length and force are held constant" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "ATP is not consumed" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Isometric: length is fixed, force is measured (e.g., pushing against an immovable wall). Isotonic: load (force) is held constant, length changes (e.g., lifting a constant weight). All real-life movements are mixtures (auxotonic). Both consume ATP — even isometric contractions, because cross-bridges must continually cycle to maintain force (Costanzo, 7e — Skeletal Muscle).

QUESTION 18

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Compared with cardiac muscle, the skeletal-muscle action potential is:

Correct answer: Much shorter (~2–5 ms), permitting rapid repetitive firing and tetanic summation

Distractors:

- "Much longer (~200 ms) and produces a long absolute refractory period that prevents tetanus" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Identical in time course" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Triggered by Ca²⁺ rather than Na⁺" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Skeletal-muscle action potentials last only a few milliseconds (Na⁺ in / K⁺ out, similar to nerve), with a brief absolute refractory period. Cardiac action potentials are ~200 ms long because of a sustained Ca²⁺-mediated plateau, with a long refractory period that _prevents_ tetanus and protects diastolic filling. The brevity of the skeletal-muscle action potential is what allows tetanic summation (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

QUESTION 19

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The whole-muscle force generated by a brain-driven voluntary effort is graded by:

Correct answer: Recruitment of more motor units (size-principle order: small/slow before large/fast) and increasing firing rate of recruited units

Distractors:

- "Varying the amplitude of the muscle action potential" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Releasing different neurotransmitters at the NMJ" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Lengthening the action potential" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Force is graded chiefly by (i) recruitment — larger force requires recruiting more motor units, in order of increasing motor-unit size (Henneman's size principle: small/slow-fatigue-resistant first, large/fast last); and (ii) rate coding — increasing firing rate of recruited motor units, which produces unfused or fused tetanus and increased force per fibre. Action potential amplitude does not vary — APs are all-or-none (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.6).

QUESTION 20

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The principal molecular determinant of whether a muscle fibre is "fast-twitch" or "slow-twitch" is its expression of:

Correct answer: A particular myosin heavy-chain (MHC) isoform — Type I (slow) vs Types IIa/IIx/IIb (fast)

Distractors:

- "A particular SERCA isoform" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "A particular troponin C isoform" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The dystrophin gene" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Type I fibres express slow myosin heavy chain (MyHC-I) with low ATPase rate — slow contraction, fatigue-resistant, oxidative metabolism, abundant mitochondria and myoglobin (red colour). QUESTION 21

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Henneman's "size principle" of motor-unit recruitment states that as voluntary force increases:

Correct answer: Small slow motor units (Type I) are recruited first; larger fast units (Type IIa, then Type IIx) are recruited progressively as more force is required

Distractors:

- "Large fast motor units are recruited first" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Motor units are recruited in random order" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "All motor units fire simultaneously at threshold" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Small α-motor neurons (innervating slow Type I fibres) have higher input resistance and lower threshold, so they reach firing threshold first. As effort increases, progressively larger and faster motor units are recruited. The advantage: the most fatigue-resistant fibres are recruited first for the most-used low-force tasks; the rapidly fatiguing high-force fibres are spared for occasional bursts (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

QUESTION 22

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The immediate (first few seconds) source of ATP regeneration in contracting skeletal muscle is:

Correct answer: The phosphocreatine (creatine phosphate) reservoir, via the creatine kinase reaction (PCr + ADP ↔ Cr + ATP)

Distractors:

- "Oxidative phosphorylation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Anaerobic glycolysis with lactate production" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "β-oxidation of fatty acids" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Resting muscle ATP is small (~5 mM) but is rapidly regenerated by phosphocreatine via creatine kinase. The PCr reservoir buffers ATP for the first ~5–10 seconds of intense contraction. Beyond this, anaerobic glycolysis dominates for tens of seconds (yielding lactate and 2 ATP per glucose). For sustained activity, oxidative phosphorylation of glucose, lactate, and fatty acids takes over (~37 ATP per glucose-1-phosphate, far higher per glucose). Some creatine kinase localises near the M line, suited for rapid resynthesis of ATP at the cross-bridge (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Energy Sources; Costanzo, 7e).

QUESTION 23

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Rigor mortis after death is best explained by:

Correct answer: Loss of ATP needed for cross-bridge detachment, while Ca²⁺ leaks out of the SR and binds troponin C — the muscle locks in the actin-bound state

Distractors:

- "Failure of Ca²⁺ release" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Excess intracellular Mg²⁺" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Persistent autonomic stimulation" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Cross-bridge detachment requires ATP binding to the myosin head; without ATP, myosin remains bound to actin in the rigor state. Concurrently, ATP-dependent SERCA activity ceases and Ca²⁺ leaks from the SR into the cytosol, activating troponin and locking cross-bridges on. Rigor is broken later by autolytic protein degradation. The same cross-bridge-locked state is exploited by the rigor configuration in the cross-bridge cycle diagram (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Cross-Bridge Cycling; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

QUESTION 24

Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Malignant hyperthermia, a rare anaesthesia-triggered hypermetabolic crisis, is caused most often by mutations in:

Correct answer: The RYR1 gene (skeletal-muscle ryanodine receptor), causing exaggerated Ca²⁺ release in response to volatile anaesthetics or succinylcholine

Distractors:

- "The DMD gene (dystrophin)" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The CACNA1S gene only" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "The myostatin gene" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Most malignant hyperthermia (MH) mutations are in RYR1 (a smaller proportion in CACNA1S, the DHPR α₁s subunit). Volatile anaesthetics (halothane, sevoflurane, isoflurane) and depolarising muscle relaxants (succinylcholine) trigger uncontrolled Ca²⁺ release from the SR, sustained cross-bridge cycling, runaway ATP hydrolysis, heat production, severe acidosis, hyperkalaemia, rhabdomyolysis, and hyperthermia. Treatment: discontinue the trigger, dantrolene (which blocks RyR1), aggressive cooling, and supportive care (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

QUESTION 25

Difficulty (F / I / A): i

Correct answer: A loss of the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex link to the basal lamina, resulting in sarcolemmal fragility and progressive muscle fibre necrosis

QUESTION 26

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Muscle fatigue during sustained intense exercise is best explained by:

Correct answer: A combination of phosphocreatine depletion, accumulation of inorganic phosphate (which inhibits cross-bridge cycling), depletion of glycogen, lactate/H⁺ accumulation, and reduced SR Ca²⁺ release; ATP itself is rarely depleted to levels that cause rigor

Distractors:

- "Exhaustion of ATP — fibres going into rigor" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Loss of motor neurons" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Fall in core body temperature" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Muscle fibres do not, under physiological exercise, run out of ATP enough to enter rigor — a battery of regulatory signals reduces force well before that critical depletion. Contributors include accumulation of Pi (which directly slows the power stroke), H⁺ (lowers pH, depresses myofibrillar Ca²⁺ sensitivity), depleted PCr and glycogen, and reduced SR Ca²⁺ release/sensitivity. Central fatigue (reduced descending drive) also contributes. Fast glycolytic fibres are the most susceptible to rapid fatigue (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Fatigue; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

QUESTION 27

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Muscle hypertrophy with resistance training is mediated principally by:

Correct answer: Increased synthesis of contractile proteins per fibre, via mechanotransduction-activated mTORC1/IGF-1/Akt signalling

Distractors:

- "Increased number of muscle fibres (hyperplasia)" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Stem: Denervation of a skeletal muscle (e.g., transection of its motor nerve) typically produces, over weeks:

Correct answer: Atrophy of muscle fibres, with extra-junctional spread of nicotinic ACh receptors and supersensitivity to ACh ("denervation supersensitivity")

Distractors:

- "No change in fibre composition" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Hypertrophy of fast-twitch fibres only" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.
- "Conversion of all fibres to Type I" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception worth naming.

Explanation: Without trophic input from the motor nerve, fibres atrophy and the previously NMJ-restricted nicotinic ACh receptors spread along the entire sarcolemma, increasing ACh sensitivity (denervation supersensitivity). The widespread receptors include the embryonic γ-subunit-containing isoform. Clinically this matters because succinylcholine can trigger massive K⁺ efflux from the receptor-rich sarcolemma in burn, denervation, or prolonged immobilisation patients, producing life-threatening hyperkalaemia (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9; Ganong, 26e).
