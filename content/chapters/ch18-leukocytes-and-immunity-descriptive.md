QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Compare innate and adaptive immunity. How are they linked, and how do they cooperate to defend against infection?

Model answer: _Innate_ — rapid (mins-hours), non-specific, no memory; physical barriers (skin, mucus, cilia), chemical (acid, lysozyme, defensins), complement, neutrophils, monocytes/macrophages, NK cells, dendritic cells (DCs), TLRs/PRRs sensing PAMPs/DAMPs. _Adaptive_ — slow (days), antigen-specific, memory-forming; CD4 T helper, CD8 cytotoxic, B cells/plasma cells/antibodies (IgG, IgM, IgA, IgD, IgE). _Linkage_ — DCs phagocytose antigen, present on MHC II to CD4 T cells in lymph nodes (immunological synapse); intracellular antigens on MHC I to CD8. CD4 T cells help B cells (humoral) and macrophages (cell-mediated). _Cooperation_ — antibodies opsonise for phagocytes; complement amplifies; cytokines (IL-2, IFN-γ, IL-4, IL-17, TNF-α) coordinate. Memory T and B cells provide durable protection — basis of vaccination [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

QUESTION 2

Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: Describe the four types of hypersensitivity reactions (Gell and Coombs) with clinical examples.

Model answer: _Type I — Immediate (IgE-mediated)_: allergen → IgE on mast cells/basophils → degranulation (histamine, tryptase, leukotrienes); minutes; hay fever, asthma, urticaria, food allergy, anaphylaxis. Treat: antihistamines, corticosteroids, adrenaline (anaphylaxis); allergen avoidance, immunotherapy. _Type II — Antibody-mediated cytotoxic_: IgG/IgM against cell surface antigens → complement, ADCC, opsonisation; AIHA, ITP, transfusion reactions, Goodpasture, MG, Graves, pemphigus, ANCA vasculitis. _Type III — Immune complex_: Ag-Ab complexes deposit in tissues → complement → neutrophil-mediated damage; SLE, post-streptococcal GN, serum sickness, hypersensitivity pneumonitis. _Type IV — Delayed (T-cell-mediated)_: 24-72h; CD4 Th1 + macrophages or CD8 cytotoxic; TB skin test (Mantoux), contact dermatitis, GVHD, transplant rejection, T1DM. Treatment varies by type; Type II/III often immunosuppression [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: Describe the complement system - pathways, regulation, and clinical disorders.

Model answer: _Complement_ — ~30 proteins; 3 activation pathways: (1) Classical (Ag-Ab complexes, C1q-C1r-C1s); (2) Lectin (MBL → mannose on microbes); (3) Alternative (spontaneous C3 hydrolysis, amplified on microbes). All converge on C3 convertase → C3a (anaphylatoxin) + C3b (opsonin). C5 convertase → C5a (anaphylatoxin/chemotactic) + C5b → MAC (C5b-9, lyses Gram-negative). Functions: opsonisation (C3b), inflammation (C3a, C5a), MAC lysis, immune complex clearance, B-cell activation. _Regulation_: factor H/I (degrade C3b), DAF (CD55) and CD59 prevent host damage; C1 inhibitor blocks initial activation. _Clinical_: deficiencies — C1 INH (HAE - angioedema, treat icatibant/C1INH); C5-C9 (Neisseria); CD55/CD59 (PNH); excess — autoimmune, atypical HUS (eculizumab), AMD, MG (eculizumab), NMOSD. Vaccinate Neisseria before complement inhibition therapy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: Describe T-cell development, selection, and subsets.

Model answer: T-cell precursors leave bone marrow → thymus. _Positive selection_ (cortex): TCR engages self-MHC at moderate affinity → survives; cells failing to engage MHC die by neglect. _Negative selection_ (medulla): TCR strongly recognises self-peptide-MHC → apoptosis (central tolerance); AIRE drives ectopic expression of tissue antigens (APECED if mutated). Survivors → CD4+ (MHC II) helper T cells or CD8+ (MHC I) cytotoxic T cells. _CD4 subsets_: Th1 (IFN-γ, intracellular pathogens, autoimmunity), Th2 (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, parasites, allergies), Th17 (IL-17, mucosal/fungal, autoimmunity), Treg (FoxP3+, IL-10, TGF-β, suppress autoimmunity), Tfh (B-cell help in germinal centres). _CD8_: cytotoxic via perforin/granzyme/Fas-FasL. Memory T cells: central memory (lymph nodes), effector memory (tissues), tissue-resident memory. Clinical: SCID, DiGeorge, IPEX (FoxP3 mutation), checkpoint inhibitors (anti-CTLA-4, anti-PD-1) — cancer therapy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe B-cell development, antibody class switching, and somatic hypermutation.

Model answer: B cells develop in bone marrow: pro-B → pre-B (μ chain, surrogate light chain) → immature B (IgM+) → mature B (IgM+IgD+). _VDJ recombination_ (RAG-1/2) generates diversity in heavy and light chain variable regions. _Negative selection_ removes self-reactive immature B cells (apoptosis or receptor editing). Mature B cells exit to periphery. On antigen encounter (with T-cell help in germinal centres): (1) _Somatic hypermutation_ (AID enzyme) refines affinity; (2) _Class-switch recombination_ (AID): IgM → IgG, IgA, or IgE depending on cytokine signals (IFN-γ → IgG2, IL-4 → IgE, TGF-β → IgA). Effector cells: plasma cells (high antibody output) and memory B cells. Antibody isotypes: IgG (4 subclasses; major serum, opsonisation, neutralisation, complement), IgM (pentamer, complement, first response), IgA (mucosal dimer, neutralisation), IgE (allergy/parasite), IgD. Disorders: CVID (low IgG/A/M), X-linked agammaglobulinaemia (BTK mutation - no B cells), hyper-IgM (CD40L mutation - no class switching) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe MHC structure and function. Why does MHC matter for organ transplantation?

Model answer: _Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC)_ — set of cell-surface glycoproteins presenting peptide antigens to T cells. _MHC class I_ (HLA-A, -B, -C in humans): expressed on all nucleated cells; presents endogenous peptides (8-9 aa) to CD8 T cells; viral and tumour surveillance. Loaded via TAP transporter, β2-microglobulin associates. _MHC class II_ (HLA-DR, -DP, -DQ): expressed on APCs (DCs, macrophages, B cells); presents exogenous peptides (12-25 aa) to CD4 T cells; bacterial defence and antibody help. Loaded via invariant chain in lysosome. _Polymorphism_ — most polymorphic loci in human genome; thousands of alleles per locus; ensures population-level coverage of pathogens. _HLA matching_ in transplantation: identical twins ideal; HLA-identical sibling next; matched unrelated donor (MUD); haploidentical with post-transplant cyclophosphamide. Mismatched HLA → recognition by recipient T cells → acute cellular rejection. Disease associations: HLA-B27 (ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis), HLA-DR3/DR4 (T1DM), HLA-B\*5701 (abacavir hypersensitivity - test before prescribing) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: Describe the structure and function of antibodies. How does each immunoglobulin isotype contribute differently?

Model answer: _Antibody structure_ — Y-shaped heterotetramer: 2 heavy + 2 light chains, disulfide-linked. Variable region (Fab) — antigen binding (hypervariable CDRs); Constant region (Fc) — effector functions. Hinge allows flexibility. Light chain: κ or λ (one type per cell - light-chain restriction). Functions: (1) Neutralisation (block toxin/virus binding); (2) Opsonisation (Fc receptors on phagocytes); (3) Complement activation (classical pathway via C1q-IgG/IgM); (4) ADCC (NK cells via FcγRIII); (5) Mast cell sensitisation (IgE via FcεRI); (6) Mucosal protection (IgA secretory dimer). _Isotypes_: IgM (pentamer, first response, very efficient complement, transient); IgG (4 subclasses; major serum, opsonisation, crosses placenta, long half-life ~21 days from FcRn recycling); IgA (mucosal dimer with secretory component, breast milk passive immunity); IgE (allergy and helminth defence; FcεRI on mast cells); IgD (BCR; little secreted). FcRn extends IgG and albumin half-life - exploited by efgartigimod (anti-FcRn for autoimmune disease) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: A 5-year-old has recurrent bacterial infections (sinopulmonary, skin) and low IgG. Decompose primary immunodeficiency.

Model answer: _Primary immunodeficiency disorders (PIDs)_ — > 450 known genetic immunodeficiencies. Categorise: (1) _Antibody deficiencies_ (most common) — XLA (BTK mutation, no B cells, no Ig); CVID (low IgG/A/M, normal B cell numbers); selective IgA deficiency (asymptomatic mostly; anaphylaxis to IgA-containing blood products). Treat: IVIG/SCIG. (2) _Combined T+B_ — SCID (X-linked common γ chain, ADA, others; lethal without treatment by year 1; TREC newborn screening; HSCT or gene therapy curative). (3) _Phagocyte defects_ — CGD (NADPH oxidase mutation; recurrent staph/aspergillus; bowel disease; treat IFN-γ, prophylactic antibiotics, HSCT); LAD (integrin defect; delayed cord separation; severe infections). (4) _Complement defects_ — terminal (Neisseria); C1 INH (HAE). (5) _Innate immune defects_ — TLR/MyD88 (pyogenic infections); IFN-γ pathway (mycobacterial); JAK-STAT pathway. Workup: FBC + film, immunoglobulins, lymphocyte subsets (flow cytometry), specific antibody responses (vaccines), complement, neutrophil oxidative burst (DHR), genetic panels. Management: anti-microbial prophylaxis, IVIG, vaccinations (avoid live), HSCT in selected, gene therapy emerging [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: A 25-year-old with rash, joint pains, and pancytopenia. ANA positive. Decompose SLE.

Model answer: _Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)_ — autoimmune multisystem disease; F:M 9:1; peak 15-44 years. Pathogenesis: loss of self-tolerance; defective apoptotic clearance → exposure to nuclear antigens → autoantibodies (anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, ANA, anti-Ro/La, anti-cardiolipin, lupus anticoagulant) → immune complex deposition → complement activation → tissue damage. Triggers: UV light, infections (EBV), drugs (hydralazine, procainamide - drug-induced lupus, often anti-histone+, milder, reversible), hormonal (oestrogen). _Clinical features_ (ACR/EULAR 2019 criteria): malar/discoid rash, photosensitivity, oral ulcers, arthritis, serositis, renal (proliferative GN), neurological (psychosis, seizures, cognitive), haematological (cytopenias - autoimmune), Coombs+ haemolysis. _Investigations_: ANA (sensitive); anti-dsDNA + low complement (specific, correlate with activity); anti-Sm (specific, diagnostic). Renal biopsy if proteinuria/active sediment. _Management_: hydroxychloroquine for all (reduces flares, improves survival), NSAIDs for arthralgia, corticosteroids for flares, immunosuppression (mycophenolate first-line for renal, also AZA, cyclophosphamide), biologics (rituximab, belimumab - anti-BAFF, anifrolumab - anti-IFN-α receptor); manage cardiovascular risk, avoid sun, contraception (avoid oestrogen), pregnancy planning. Antiphospholipid syndrome - separate but often co-exists [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Stem: A 30-year-old presents with worsening dyspnoea on exertion. CXR shows hilar lymphadenopathy and reticulonodular shadowing. Decompose sarcoidosis.

Model answer: _Sarcoidosis_ - multisystem granulomatous disease of unknown aetiology. Bimodal: 20-40 and 50-65; F slightly > M; African Americans most severe; Caucasians often resolve. Pathogenesis: aberrant T-cell-mediated response to unknown antigen → non-caseating granulomas (epithelioid macrophages, Th1 lymphocytes, multinucleate giant cells). _Clinical_ - 90% involve lungs: pulmonary infiltrates, hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy, dyspnoea, cough; staged 0-IV by CXR. Other organs: skin (erythema nodosum, lupus pernio - poor prognosis, scar sarcoid), eye (uveitis, dry eyes), neurosarcoid (cranial nerves - CN VII palsy, meningitis, parenchymal), cardiac (conduction blocks, sudden death, heart failure - often underdiagnosed; FDG-PET useful), liver/spleen, joints, hypercalcaemia (raised 1,25-D from granuloma macrophages), peripheral lymphadenopathy. _Löfgren syndrome_: erythema nodosum + bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy + arthritis + fever - excellent prognosis (often self-resolves). _Heerfordt syndrome_: anterior uveitis + parotid swelling + facial palsy + fever. _Investigations_: CXR; HRCT chest; serum ACE (raised in active disease but low specificity); calcium; tissue biopsy showing non-caseating granulomas (transbronchial, EBUS-guided lymph node, skin); exclude TB and lymphoma; PFTs (restrictive + reduced DLCO); echo + MRI for cardiac; LP for neuro. _Management_: many resolve spontaneously (~50% within 2 years). Steroid-sparing - methotrexate, azathioprine, leflunomide, hydroxychloroquine; biologics (infliximab) for refractory/neurosarcoid; rare lung transplant for end-stage. Long-term follow-up; multidisciplinary [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

QUESTION 12
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: A 50-year-old with prior haploidentical HSCT develops skin rash, diarrhoea, and jaundice. Decompose graft-versus-host disease.

Model answer: _Graft-versus-host disease (GVHD)_ - donor immune cells (T cells) recognise recipient tissues as foreign → inflammatory damage. Occurs after allogeneic HSCT (occasionally solid organ, blood transfusion in immunocompromised). _Acute GVHD_ (typically within 100 days, can be later): three target organs - (1) Skin (rash on palms, soles, face → desquamation in severe); (2) Liver (cholestatic - raised bilirubin, ALP); (3) GI (anorexia, nausea, vomiting, voluminous secretory diarrhoea, abdominal pain, ileus). Glucksberg grading (I-IV) by extent. _Risk factors_: HLA mismatch, female donor to male recipient, older donor/recipient, conditioning intensity, viral infections (CMV). _Pathophysiology_: 3 phases - (1) Tissue damage from conditioning + cytokine storm → APC activation; (2) T-cell activation - recipient APCs prime donor T cells; (3) Effector phase - cytotoxic T cells, NK cells, cytokines (TNF-α, IFN-γ) damage tissues. _Treatment_: high-dose corticosteroids first-line; if steroid-refractory - ruxolitinib (FDA 2019, JAK inhibitor); ECP (extracorporeal photopheresis); other immunosuppression. Prophylaxis: calcineurin inhibitors (ciclosporin, tacrolimus) + methotrexate; post-transplant cyclophosphamide (PTCy) - especially haploidentical. _Chronic GVHD_ (typically > 100 days): sclerodermatous skin, sicca-like (Sjögren features), oral, ocular, lung (BOS - bronchiolitis obliterans), liver, GI, joint contractures, immune dysfunction. Treatment - steroids + immunosuppression + ruxolitinib + ibrutinib (FDA 2017) + ECP + rehabilitation. _Graft-versus-tumour effect_ - related but beneficial; explains higher cure rates with allogeneic vs autologous in many haematological malignancies. Counterintuitive: complete absence of GVHD associated with higher relapse rates [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

QUESTION 13
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe vaccination principles. How do live, killed, subunit, and mRNA vaccines differ?

Model answer: _Vaccination_ - induces protective adaptive immunity without causing disease. Types: (1) _Live attenuated_ - weakened organism replicates in host; broad immunity (humoral + cellular), durable, often single dose (MMR, varicella, BCG, oral polio (OPV), yellow fever, rotavirus, intranasal influenza); contraindicated in immunocompromised, pregnancy. (2) _Inactivated/killed_ - whole organism killed; safer but weaker immunity; multiple doses + boosters (rabies, hepatitis A, IPV polio, whole-cell pertussis, Japanese encephalitis). (3) _Subunit/recombinant_ - purified components (Hep B surface antigen recombinant, HPV L1 capsid, varicella zoster glycoprotein in Shingrix - recombinant + adjuvant; very effective ~ 90% in adults including older); safe, multiple doses. (4) _Polysaccharide_ - encapsulated bacteria (PPV23 pneumococcal); T-independent, no memory in children < 2; weak. Stem: A 6-week-old presents with severe persistent infections, oral thrush, failure to thrive. Decompose severe combined immunodeficiency.

Stem: Synthesise this chapter: how do innate immunity, adaptive immunity, and immune disorders illustrate the design of the immune system?

Model answer: The immune system is one of physiology's most elegant systems - producing protective specificity from a finite genome through combinatorial mechanisms (VDJ recombination, somatic hypermutation, MHC polymorphism), balancing aggressive defence with self-tolerance, and integrating across all body systems. Design principles: (1) _Layered defence_ - barriers + innate + adaptive provide redundancy at different time scales; (2) _Pattern recognition_ - PRRs detect conserved microbial features (PAMPs); (3) _Self vs non-self_ discrimination through tolerance mechanisms (central thymic deletion + peripheral Treg + checkpoint receptors); (4) _Combinatorial diversity_ - VDJ + class-switch + somatic hypermutation generate ~10^11 antibody specificities from ~ 100 genes; (5) _Memory_ - long-lived T and B memory cells provide durable protection; (6) _Specialisation_ - T-cell subsets (Th1/2/17, Treg, CD8 cytotoxic, Tfh), antibody isotypes, complement pathways; (7) _Modulation_ - cytokines coordinate; checkpoints prevent excess; (8) _Spatial organisation_ - thymus (T development), bone marrow (B), lymph nodes/spleen (priming), tissues (effector). Disease patterns: (1) _Immunodeficiency_ (PID, AIDS, immunosuppression) - failure of one or more components → infections; (2) _Autoimmunity_ - tolerance failure → self-attack; (3) _Allergy_ - inappropriate IgE response to harmless antigen; (4) _Hypersensitivity_ (4 types) - inappropriately-targeted or excessive responses; (5) _Cancer_ - immune surveillance failure or escape; (6) _Transplantation_ - alloreactive responses; (7) _Inflammation_ - innate immunity dysregulation. Therapeutic frontier: (1) _Vaccines_ - one of medicine's greatest achievements (smallpox eradication, polio elimination, HPV cancer prevention; mRNA platform revolutionary); (2) _Immunoglobulin replacement_ (IVIG); (3) _Immunosuppression_ - corticosteroids, antimetabolites, calcineurin inhibitors; (4) _Biologics_ - anti-TNF, anti-IL-6, anti-IL-17, anti-CD20, anti-BAFF, anti-IFN, JAK inhibitors; (5) _Cell therapies_ - HSCT, CAR-T, regulatory T cells; (6) _Cancer immunotherapy_ - checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T, bispecifics, vaccines; (7) _Gene therapy_ - SCID, more emerging; (8) _Tolerance induction_ in trials. The immune system's complexity, beauty, and disease patterns all derive from the design challenge: detect anything foreign with high sensitivity while sparing self with high specificity, all from a finite genome. Modern immunology is one of the most impactful areas of medicine [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].
