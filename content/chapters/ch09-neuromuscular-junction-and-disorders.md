## Pass 1 — NMJ architecture and normal transmission (presynaptic, cleft, postsynaptic)

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The neuromuscular junction is best classified as which kind of synapse?

Correct answer: A chemical synapse using acetylcholine, with the postsynaptic site forming a specialised motor end-plate; ACh is synthesised in the cytoplasm of the motor terminal by choline acetyltransferase (ChAT) from acetyl-CoA and choline, packaged into vesicles by VAChT, released into the cleft, and rapidly hydrolysed by acetylcholinesterase concentrated in the basal lamina.

Distractors:

- "Electrical." — Reveals misconception: student names a fast direct-coupling synapse type. Correction: vertebrate skeletal NMJs are _chemical_ synapses; gap-junction-coupled electrical synapses are found in some CNS interneurons and cardiac muscle but not at the NMJ.
- "Adrenergic." — Reveals misconception: student conflates the somatic motor NMJ with sympathetic postganglionic synapses. Correction: somatic motor neurons release ACh at the NMJ; adrenergic transmission (noradrenaline) is the postganglionic sympathetic neurotransmitter.
- "Glutamatergic." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a CNS-fast-excitatory mechanism to the NMJ. Correction: CNS fast excitatory transmission uses glutamate; the NMJ uses ACh acting at nicotinic receptors.

Explanation: The vertebrate skeletal NMJ is a chemical synapse using ACh [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8; Berne & Levy ch.12]. The postsynaptic muscle membrane forms the motor end-plate, with deep junctional folds that house the nicotinic ACh receptors at their crests. ACh is synthesised in the cytoplasm of the motor terminal from acetyl-CoA and choline by **choline acetyltransferase (ChAT)**, packaged into vesicles by the **vesicular ACh transporter (VAChT)**, released into the cleft on Ca²⁺-triggered exocytosis, and rapidly hydrolysed by **acetylcholinesterase (AChE)** concentrated in the basal lamina. The breakdown product choline is recovered by the presynaptic Na⁺-coupled high-affinity choline transporter **CHT1**.

Hints:

1. The transmitter is acetylcholine.
2. The postsynaptic specialisation is the motor end-plate.
3. The postsynaptic receptor is nicotinic.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The postsynaptic nicotinic acetylcholine receptor at the _adult_ NMJ has a subunit composition of:

Correct answer: 2α + β + δ + ε (a heteropentamer; the embryonic γ subunit is replaced postnatally by ε, conferring different gating kinetics — briefer mean open time and slightly different ion handling).

Distractors:

- "α₂βγδ (the embryonic isoform)." — Reveals misconception: student picks the foetal subunit composition. Correction: the embryonic isoform contains γ instead of ε; γ is replaced by ε after birth as innervation matures.
- "α₅ (a homopentamer)." — Reveals misconception: student assumes a homopentameric assembly. Correction: the muscle nAChR is a heteropentamer with two α subunits and one each of β, δ, ε; not a homopentamer.
- "α₃β₂ (a neuronal subtype)." — Reveals misconception: student picks a CNS / autonomic neuronal nicotinic receptor composition. Correction: α3β2 and similar pentamers are neuronal types found in autonomic ganglia and CNS; the muscle NMJ uses 2α + β + δ + ε.

Explanation: The adult muscle nAChR is a pentamer of 2α + β + δ + ε [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8; Ganong 26e]. The embryonic isoform contains a γ subunit instead of ε; this is replaced postnatally as innervation matures, and the adult ε-form has different gating kinetics (briefer mean open time, higher single-channel conductance) and slightly different ion-handling. After **denervation** or in extrajunctional supersensitivity, the embryonic γ-form re-emerges throughout the sarcolemma — explaining the dangerous succinylcholine-induced hyperkalaemia in burns and prolonged immobility (the γ-form is everywhere, opens longer, releases more K⁺). Each ACh-binding site sits at the α / non-α interface; binding of two ACh molecules is required for full opening.

Hints:

1. Five subunits in total.
2. Two α subunits and one each of β, δ, plus a fifth.
3. The fifth subunit changes during postnatal maturation.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The synaptic cleft of the neuromuscular junction is approximately:

Correct answer: 50 nm wide — substantially wider than a typical CNS synaptic cleft (~20 nm); the NMJ cleft contains a specialised basal lamina rich in laminin, agrin, collagen IV, and acetylcholinesterase.

Distractors:

- "3 nm wide." — Reveals misconception: student picks the gap-junction inter-membrane distance. Correction: gap-junction inter-membrane distance is ~3 nm; chemical synaptic clefts are much wider (CNS ~20 nm; NMJ ~50 nm).
- "200 nm wide." — Reveals misconception: student overestimates the cleft. Correction: at ~200 nm transmitter diffusion would be too slow for fast synaptic transmission; the actual cleft is ~50 nm.
- "1 µm wide." — Reveals misconception: student picks an unphysiologically large value. Correction: 1 µm is the order of magnitude of an axon initial segment or small dendrite — not a synaptic cleft.

Explanation: The NMJ cleft is unusually wide (~50 nm) and contains a basal lamina rich in laminin, agrin, collagen IV, and acetylcholinesterase [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. ACh must diffuse this 50 nm to reach receptors at the crests of the junctional folds. The basal lamina also contains **agrin** (motor-neuron-derived), which orchestrates AChR clustering during NMJ formation via the muscle-side **MuSK / LRP4 / rapsyn** pathway. The cleft additionally houses **AChE** tethered by ColQ to the basal lamina — abnormalities in this tether produce a slow-channel-syndrome-like congenital myasthenic syndrome.

Hints:

1. Wider than a typical CNS cleft.
2. Contains the basal lamina with AChE.
3. ~50 nm.

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The "safety factor" of the normal neuromuscular junction means that:

Correct answer: Each end-plate potential is well above threshold so that one motor-neuron action potential reliably triggers one muscle action potential, even when transmission is reduced by frequency-related run-down — there is "spare capacity" in the system.

Distractors:

- "Each end-plate potential is just sub-threshold and requires summation." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a CNS-like sub-threshold pattern to the NMJ. Correction: at the NMJ, single APs reliably exceed threshold; summation is unnecessary in healthy transmission.
- "The NMJ has a high error rate." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the meaning of "safety factor". Correction: the high safety factor means _reliable_ transmission with very low error rate; reduced safety factor in disease produces failure.
- "Multiple motor neurons must fire simultaneously to depolarise a fibre." — Reveals misconception: student introduces convergent input. Correction: each muscle fibre is innervated by a single motor neuron (the motor unit); convergent input is not the architecture.

Explanation: Unlike most CNS synapses, the NMJ has a high safety factor (>1): a single motor-neuron AP releases far more transmitter than is needed to bring the muscle fibre to threshold, ensuring reliable 1:1 transmission [Berne & Levy ch.12; Costanzo — NMJ]. The safety factor depends on (i) abundant quantal release, (ii) high postsynaptic AChR density, (iii) deep junctional folds with voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in their depths to amplify the local depolarisation. Diseases that lower the safety factor toward 1 (myasthenia gravis, LEMS, congenital myasthenic syndromes) produce **fatigable weakness** because normal frequency-related decline in release is no longer hidden by the surplus. Drug-induced reductions in safety factor (aminoglycosides, magnesium, neuromuscular blockers) similarly produce weakness.

Hints:

1. The factor is greater than 1 in normal NMJ.
2. Reliable 1:1 transmission requires more than threshold.
3. NMJ disease lowers this factor toward 1, producing fatigability.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Each presynaptic action potential at the NMJ releases approximately how many ACh quanta (vesicles)?

Correct answer: 100–300 quanta per nerve impulse; each quantum (vesicle) contains ~5,000–10,000 ACh molecules — producing a large EPP (~30–40 mV depolarisation) well above the ~15-mV threshold for muscle AP generation (the molecular basis of the safety factor).

Distractors:

- "1." — Reveals misconception: student attributes single-vesicle release to the NMJ. Correction: a single quantum produces a miniature end-plate potential (mEPP, ~0.4 mV); this is far too small to depolarise the muscle to threshold.
- "10." — Reveals misconception: student picks a number too low for reliable transmission. Correction: ~10 quanta would produce ~4-mV EPP — still sub-threshold.
- "10,000." — Reveals misconception: student picks an unphysiologically large value, perhaps confusing molecules per vesicle with vesicles per AP. Correction: each vesicle has ~5,000–10,000 ACh molecules; 100–300 vesicles release per AP, giving ~5×10⁵–3×10⁶ ACh molecules per AP into the cleft.

Explanation: Roughly 100–300 quanta are released per nerve impulse at a normal NMJ; each quantum (synaptic vesicle) contains ~5,000–10,000 ACh molecules [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8; Guyton & Hall ch.7]. This produces a large end-plate potential (~30–40 mV depolarisation, well above the ~15-mV threshold for muscle action-potential generation) — the molecular basis of the safety factor. **Quantal release** is the foundational discovery of Bernard Katz: spontaneous mEPPs are single-vesicle fusion events; evoked EPPs are integer multiples of the mEPP amplitude. **Frequency-related run-down**: depletion of the readily releasable vesicle pool causes successive EPPs at high frequency to be slightly smaller — clinically silent in healthy NMJ but exposed when the safety factor is reduced (the basis of MG fatigability and the decremental EMG response).

Hints:

1. Each vesicle is one quantum.
2. The total release per AP is in the hundreds.
3. The total ACh molecules released is much larger because each vesicle has thousands.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The deep invaginations of the postsynaptic membrane at the NMJ — increasing surface area, concentrating AChRs at their crests, and housing voltage-gated Na⁺ channels at their depths — are called:

Correct answer: Junctional folds (also called postjunctional folds or secondary folds) — increase surface area; AChRs cluster at the crests opposite active zones; voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (Nav1.4) lie in the troughs and amplify the EPP into a propagating action potential.

Distractors:

- "Caveolae." — Reveals misconception: student names another type of membrane invagination. Correction: caveolae are small, flask-shaped membrane invaginations (caveolin-marked) involved in lipid raft signalling and endocytosis; they are not the NMJ structural feature.
- "T tubules." — Reveals misconception: student names the EC-coupling structure. Correction: T tubules are sarcolemmal invaginations that conduct the muscle AP into the fibre depths for Ca²⁺ release; they are unrelated to the NMJ post-synaptic surface.
- "Gap junctions." — Reveals misconception: student names electrical-coupling structures. Correction: gap junctions are connexon channels coupling adjacent cells; they are absent at the NMJ.

Explanation: Junctional (postjunctional, secondary) folds sharpen the local depolarisation [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. **AChR-channels are clustered (~10⁴/µm²) at the crests** opposite active zones — placing them where the Ca²⁺-triggered ACh release will arrive most concentrated. **Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (Nav1.4) are concentrated in the troughs** of the folds, where they amplify the EPP into a propagating action potential. **Loss of fold structure** in autoimmune NMJ disease (anti-AChR antibody-driven complement-mediated membrane damage) lowers the safety factor and is one of the contributors to weakness in myasthenia gravis. **Agrin / MuSK / LRP4 / rapsyn / DOK7** signalling is required for the development and maintenance of this specialised architecture; mutations in any of these genes can produce congenital myasthenic syndromes.

Hints:

1. AChRs cluster at the _crests_ of these folds.
2. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels lie in the _depths_.
3. The folds amplify and sharpen the EPP into an action potential.

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The motor-neuron-derived protein responsible for clustering nicotinic AChRs at the developing NMJ, signalling through the muscle-side LRP4–MuSK receptor complex, is:

Correct answer: Agrin — released from motor terminals; binds LRP4 on the muscle membrane; LRP4 activates MuSK (a receptor tyrosine kinase) → rapsyn-mediated AChR clustering. This same pathway is the target of autoantibodies in non-AChR-Ab-positive myasthenia gravis (anti-MuSK and anti-LRP4 disease).

Distractors:

- "Laminin." — Reveals misconception: student names a basal-lamina structural protein. Correction: laminin (especially laminin-α2) is a basal-lamina component required for NMJ formation but does not itself signal LRP4–MuSK clustering; agrin does.
- "Neuregulin." — Reveals misconception: student names a related signalling protein with a different role. Correction: neuregulin signals through ErbB receptors and contributes to Schwann cell development and AChR subunit expression but is not the principal AChR-clustering signal; that is agrin.
- "Sonic hedgehog." — Reveals misconception: student names a developmental morphogen unrelated to NMJ AChR clustering. Correction: SHH operates in earlier embryological patterning (limb, neural tube) but is not the NMJ-clustering signal.

Explanation: Agrin → LRP4 → MuSK → tyrosine kinase activation → rapsyn-mediated clustering of AChRs at the developing NMJ [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8; Ganong 26e]. This pathway is also the target of autoantibodies in non-AChR-antibody-positive myasthenia gravis: **anti-MuSK** and **anti-LRP4 antibodies** disrupt receptor maintenance and produce a clinically distinct subtype with bulbar prominence (especially anti-MuSK MG, which is poorly responsive to AChE inhibitors and often responds best to rituximab). **Congenital mutations** in RAPSN, AGRN, MUSK, LRP4, or DOK7 cause congenital myasthenic syndromes — early-onset fluctuating weakness with seronegative status, often responsive to combinations of pyridostigmine, salbutamol, ephedrine, or 3,4-diaminopyridine depending on subtype.

Hints:

1. The signal originates from the motor neuron.
2. The muscle-side receptor complex is LRP4–MuSK.
3. The end result is AChR clustering via rapsyn.

QUESTION 8
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: ACh action in the synaptic cleft is terminated primarily by:

Correct answer: Hydrolysis by acetylcholinesterase (AChE) anchored in the basal lamina — releasing choline (which is recovered by an Na⁺-coupled high-affinity choline transporter, CHT1, for re-synthesis of ACh by ChAT) and acetate.

Distractors:

- "Reuptake into the presynaptic terminal as intact ACh." — Reveals misconception: student applies the monoamine model to ACh. Correction: there is no transporter that takes up _intact_ ACh; cleft termination is by hydrolysis, with subsequent recovery of the breakdown product choline.
- "Diffusion away alone." — Reveals misconception: student underestimates active termination. Correction: diffusion alone would prolong ACh action far beyond the requirement for fast 1:1 transmission; AChE hydrolyses ACh within ~1 ms.
- "Endocytosis by glia." — Reveals misconception: student applies a glutamate / GABA termination strategy to ACh. Correction: at the NMJ, glial uptake is not the principal termination mechanism — AChE hydrolysis is.

Explanation: AChE hydrolyses ACh within ~1 ms; the released choline is recovered by the presynaptic CHT1 (high-affinity choline transporter) and re-used for ACh resynthesis by ChAT [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8; Costanzo — NMJ]. The brevity of cleft ACh ensures that single APs produce single muscle contractions and prevents desensitisation of postsynaptic receptors. **Anticholinesterases** (neostigmine, pyridostigmine, edrophonium, donepezil for Alzheimer disease) prolong ACh, raising EPP amplitude — therapeutically useful in myasthenia gravis and (for the centrally-acting agents) in dementia. **Organophosphate poisoning** (sarin, malathion, parathion) irreversibly inhibits AChE → cholinergic crisis (DUMBBELS muscarinic + nicotinic fasciculations and weakness); treated with atropine + pralidoxime (the latter regenerates AChE before "ageing" of the phosphate-enzyme bond).

Hints:

1. The terminating enzyme is in the basal lamina.
2. The breakdown product is choline.
3. CHT1 recovers choline for re-synthesis.

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: ACh-induced depolarisation at the motor end-plate is produced primarily by:

Correct answer: Na⁺ entry > K⁺ exit through the non-selective cation channel of the nicotinic AChR, with reversal potential ~0 mV; net inward Na⁺ current dominates at the resting membrane potential and depolarises the membrane toward 0 mV.

Distractors:

- "Cl⁻ entry through GABA-A-like channels." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a CNS inhibitory mechanism to the NMJ. Correction: Cl⁻ entry through GABA-A is the inhibitory mechanism in CNS; NMJ depolarisation uses nicotinic non-selective cation channels.
- "Ca²⁺ release from the SR." — Reveals misconception: student conflates the post-EPP excitation–contraction coupling step with the EPP itself. Correction: SR Ca²⁺ release happens _after_ the muscle action potential reaches the T tubule; the EPP itself is generated by Na⁺ / K⁺ flux through nAChR cation channels.
- "K⁺ entry through inward rectifiers." — Reveals misconception: student picks an inappropriate ion direction. Correction: inward rectifier K⁺ channels (Kir) help set the resting potential by allowing K⁺ to flow inward at very negative voltages; they do not generate the EPP.

Explanation: The nAChR is a non-selective cation channel. At the muscle resting potential of −90 mV, the driving force for Na⁺ entry (Vrest − ENa = ~−150 mV → strong inward force) is much larger than that for K⁺ exit (Vrest − EK ≈ 0–10 mV outward), so net inward Na⁺ current dominates and depolarises the membrane [Costanzo — NMJ; Guyton & Hall ch.7]. The reversal potential of the EPP is ~0 mV — between ENa and EK. The triggered local depolarisation reaches threshold (~−50 mV) and initiates a propagating muscle action potential. **Saltatory amplification**: voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in the depths of the junctional folds amplify the local current, ensuring that the EPP becomes a full muscle action potential.

Hints:

1. The channel is non-selective for cations.
2. The dominant ion at rest is Na⁺ inward.
3. Reversal potential is ~0 mV.

## Pass 2 — NMJ pharmacology — toxins, neuromuscular blockers, anticholinesterases

QUESTION 10
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Botulinum toxin produces flaccid paralysis by:

Correct answer: Cleaving SNARE proteins (SNAP-25 or synaptobrevin) in cholinergic presynaptic terminals, abolishing ACh release at the NMJ — Zn²⁺-dependent metalloprotease activity; flaccid paralysis with autonomic features (dry mouth, blurred vision, ileus, urinary retention) reflecting parallel cholinergic blockade.

Distractors:

- "Blocking postsynaptic AChRs." — Reveals misconception: student applies a curare-like postsynaptic mechanism. Correction: BoNT acts presynaptically on SNARE proteins, abolishing release; the postsynaptic AChR is intact.
- "Inhibiting acetylcholinesterase." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the wrong direction (toxin should produce _more_ ACh in the cleft if AChE were inhibited, not less). Correction: BoNT _prevents_ ACh release; it is not an AChE inhibitor.
- "Antagonising voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels." — Reveals misconception: student conflates BoNT mechanism with LEMS. Correction: LEMS antibodies attack presynaptic Cav2.1; BoNT cleaves SNAREs.

Explanation: Botulinum neurotoxin is a Zn²⁺-dependent metalloprotease. Different serotypes cleave specific SNAREs: BoNT/A and /E cleave SNAP-25; BoNT/B, /D, /F, /G cleave synaptobrevin; BoNT/C cleaves syntaxin and SNAP-25 [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8; Berne & Levy ch.12]. The toxin is highly selective for peripheral cholinergic terminals (NMJ, autonomic ganglia, parasympathetic postganglionic fibres). **Clinical use** exploits this: focal injections produce localised flaccid weakness or autonomic blockade (e.g., for dystonia, blepharospasm, hemifacial spasm, hyperhidrosis, chronic migraine, overactive bladder, cosmetic indications, achalasia). **Botulism** (foodborne, infant, wound, inhalational) presents with descending flaccid paralysis, autonomic dysfunction, and respiratory failure; treatment includes equine antitoxin (binds circulating but not internalised toxin) and supportive care for weeks-to-months until new SNARE synthesis and motor terminal sprouting restore function.

Hints:

1. The toxin is presynaptic, not postsynaptic.
2. The molecular targets are SNARE proteins.
3. Different serotypes cleave different SNAREs.

QUESTION 11
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: α-Bungarotoxin (the venom of the Taiwanese banded krait) produces paralysis by:

Correct answer: Irreversibly binding the postsynaptic nicotinic AChR α subunit, preventing ACh from gating the channel — acts at the same site as competitive non-depolarising blockers but with effectively irreversible kinetics; widely used as a research tool to label and quantify AChRs.

Distractors:

- "Inhibiting AChE." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the wrong direction of effect. Correction: AChE inhibition would _raise_ cleft ACh and _enhance_ signalling; α-bungarotoxin blocks the receptor.
- "Blocking presynaptic Ca²⁺ channels." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a LEMS-like mechanism to α-BTX. Correction: presynaptic Ca²⁺-channel blockade is the mechanism in LEMS and in Mg²⁺ excess; α-BTX is postsynaptic.
- "Cleaving SNAREs." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a botulinum-like mechanism. Correction: SNARE cleavage is the BoNT mechanism; α-BTX is a postsynaptic AChR antagonist.

Explanation: α-Bungarotoxin binds with very high affinity (and effectively irreversibly) to the muscle nicotinic AChR. Its labelled forms have been used as a research tool to quantify AChR density and to isolate the receptor for protein purification (the AChR was the first ligand-gated ion channel to be cloned, in part because of α-BTX-affinity-based isolation) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. The toxin's pharmacology established that the AChR is the molecular target of myasthenia gravis when injected animals exposed to AChR-extract developed AChR antibodies and an MG-like phenotype (experimental autoimmune myasthenia gravis, EAMG). **Note**: α-bungarotoxin does not bind α7 neuronal AChRs in the brain — there are different selective toxins for those (κ-bungarotoxin partly does). **Other postsynaptic toxins**: cobratoxin, conotoxins from cone snails — provided more receptor pharmacology tools.

Hints:

1. The toxin acts on the postsynaptic side.
2. Binding is virtually irreversible at the α subunit.
3. It is widely used as an AChR research label.

QUESTION 12
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: d-Tubocurarine (curare) produces neuromuscular blockade by:

Correct answer: Competitive (reversible) antagonism at the postsynaptic nicotinic AChR — reduces EPP amplitude; if the EPP falls below the muscle action-potential threshold, paralysis ensues. Reversal is achieved by anticholinesterases (raising cleft ACh to outcompete the antagonist).

Distractors:

- "Cleaving SNAREs." — Reveals misconception: student applies a BoNT-like mechanism. Correction: tubocurarine and modern non-depolarising blockers are _postsynaptic_ receptor antagonists; SNARE cleavage is BoNT.
- "Inhibiting AChE." — Reveals misconception: student names the _opposite_ class of agent (anticholinesterases reverse curare-induced blockade). Correction: AChE inhibitors raise cleft ACh and _reverse_ curare's action; they do not produce blockade.
- "Blocking voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a LEMS-like mechanism. Correction: presynaptic Ca²⁺-channel blockade is the LEMS / Mg²⁺ mechanism; curare and modern non-depolarising blockers are postsynaptic AChR antagonists.

Explanation: Tubocurarine and modern non-depolarising neuromuscular blockers (vecuronium, rocuronium, pancuronium, atracurium, cisatracurium) are competitive antagonists at the postsynaptic AChR [Costanzo — NMJ; Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. They reduce EPP amplitude; if EPP falls below the muscle action-potential threshold, paralysis ensues. **Reversal** is achieved by: (i) **anticholinesterases** (neostigmine, pyridostigmine) — raise cleft ACh to outcompete the antagonist (combined with glycopyrrolate or atropine to block muscarinic side effects); or (ii) for **rocuronium and vecuronium specifically**, **sugammadex** — a γ-cyclodextrin that encapsulates the steroidal blocker. **Atracurium** is unique in undergoing Hofmann elimination (spontaneous breakdown at body pH/temperature) — useful in renal/hepatic failure since elimination does not depend on those organs. **Pancuronium** has vagolytic effects (mild tachycardia). **Sites of differential sensitivity**: small fast muscles (eyes, face) are paralysed first; respiratory muscles (especially diaphragm) last.

Hints:

1. The mechanism is competitive (reversible) postsynaptic blockade.
2. The class is _non-depolarising_ NMJ blockers.
3. AChE inhibitors reverse the blockade by raising ACh.

QUESTION 13
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Succinylcholine (suxamethonium) produces neuromuscular blockade by:

Correct answer: Persistent depolarising agonism at the postsynaptic AChR — the channel opens, the muscle membrane stays depolarised, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels inactivate, and the muscle becomes inexcitable; visible fasciculations precede flaccid paralysis. Hydrolysed slowly by _plasma_ butyrylcholinesterase (not synaptic AChE).

Distractors:

- "Competitive (non-depolarising) AChR antagonism." — Reveals misconception: student picks the curare class. Correction: succinylcholine is _depolarising_ — it binds and _opens_ the AChR (an agonist), not blocks it (an antagonist).
- "SNARE cleavage." — Reveals misconception: student applies a BoNT-like mechanism. Correction: succinylcholine acts postsynaptically as an agonist; SNARE cleavage is BoNT.
- "AChE inhibition." — Reveals misconception: student picks an entirely different mechanism. Correction: succinylcholine is an AChR agonist; it is not an AChE inhibitor.

Explanation: Succinylcholine resembles two ACh molecules joined end-to-end. It binds and opens the AChR like ACh but is **not hydrolysed by AChE in the cleft** (it is hydrolysed by _plasma_ butyrylcholinesterase, slowly) [Costanzo — NMJ; Ganong 26e]. Persistent activation leads to sustained sarcolemmal depolarisation → Na⁺-channel inactivation → flaccid paralysis. Onset is rapid (~30–60 s) and duration brief (~5–10 min) — making it the agent of choice for **rapid-sequence intubation**. **Risks**: malignant hyperthermia (in RYR1-susceptible individuals — see Ch 7); **hyperkalaemia** (especially in burn / denervation / prolonged immobilisation patients — see Q14); **prolonged paralysis with pseudocholinesterase deficiency** (prolonged apnoea after dose; managed with continued mechanical ventilation until drug clears; reduced butyrylcholinesterase activity is genetic — dibucaine number test); **bradycardia** in repeat doses (pre-treatment with atropine can mitigate); **trismus** in some children. **Phase II block** with very high or prolonged doses can resemble non-depolarising block.

Hints:

1. The drug is a depolarising agonist (not an antagonist).
2. It is hydrolysed by plasma butyrylcholinesterase, not synaptic AChE.
3. Visible fasciculations precede paralysis.

**Both crises** require ICU admission and may need intubation. **Myasthenic crisis precipitants**: infection (most common), surgery, stress, pregnancy, electrolyte disturbances, drugs that worsen MG (aminoglycosides, β-blockers, magnesium, fluoroquinolones), undertreatment, immunosuppressant taper. **Cholinergic crisis precipitants**: pyridostigmine over-dosing, accidental organophosphate ingestion in MG patients, severe muscarinic side-effect predominance. **Modern practice** rarely uses edrophonium challenge — relies instead on clinical features, and the safer default is to withhold pyridostigmine, mechanically ventilate, and give IVIG / plasmapheresis.

1. The crises differ by whether ACh is _too low_ or _too high_.
2. Cholinergic crisis has muscarinic / DUMBBELS features; myasthenic crisis does not.
3. Both can require respiratory support.
