## Pass 1 — Comprehension and reasoning

QUESTION 1
Type: comprehension
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Explain why the resting membrane potential of a typical neuron sits close to the K⁺ equilibrium potential (~−95 mV) rather than to the Na⁺ equilibrium potential (~+65 mV), even though both ions have steep concentration gradients across the membrane.

Model answer: Each permeant ion attempts to drive the membrane potential toward its own equilibrium potential, but the _influence_ of each ion depends on its _permeability_ (or conductance), not just on its concentration gradient [Costanzo ch.1]. At rest, the membrane is much more permeable to K⁺ than to Na⁺. The dominant open channels at rest are _K⁺ leak channels_ (background K⁺ channels), which keep the membrane's K⁺ permeability high. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, by contrast, are nearly all closed at rest — so resting Na⁺ permeability is very low. Because K⁺ permeability dominates, the membrane potential is pulled close to EK; because Na⁺ permeability is low, Na⁺ has only a small influence on RMP. The membrane potential settles a few millivolts less negative than EK (~−70 to −80 mV instead of −95 mV) because the small but non-zero Na⁺ permeability pulls RMP slightly toward ENa. The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase contributes a few additional millivolts of negativity through its electrogenic 3:2 stoichiometry. The Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation captures the multi-ion contribution quantitatively: each ion's equilibrium potential is weighted by its permeability. The take-home: in physiology, _which ion controls the membrane potential at any given moment_ depends on which channels are open at that moment — not on which ion has the steeper concentration gradient.

Elaborative explanation: This insight generalises far beyond the resting potential. During the action potential upstroke, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open massively; Na⁺ permeability briefly exceeds K⁺ permeability; the membrane potential races toward ENa. During repolarisation, Na⁺ channels inactivate and K⁺ channels open; the K⁺ permeability dominates again; the membrane returns toward EK. The same principle explains the cardiac action potential (different mix of channels) and the slow pacemaker potentials (different again). The unifying lesson — _membrane potential follows the most permeable ion_ — is one of the most useful generalisations in physiology.

Hints:

1. Both ions have concentration gradients, but only one of them can move easily at rest.
2. What kind of channels are open at rest, and which ion do they conduct?
3. The membrane potential follows whichever ion has the highest permeability at that moment.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States that RMP follows the most permeable ion, not the one with the steepest gradient.
- Identifies K⁺ as the most permeable ion at rest (via K⁺ leak channels).
- Notes that Na⁺ permeability is low at rest (most voltage-gated Na⁺ channels closed).
- Explains that small Na⁺ and Cl⁻ permeabilities pull RMP slightly less negative than EK.
- Mentions or acknowledges the small electrogenic contribution of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase.

A Yellow answer correctly identifies K⁺ as the most permeable ion but does not articulate that this is _why_ RMP sits near EK rather than ENa, or omits the small Na⁺ permeability contribution.

A Red answer attributes RMP to concentration gradient alone, or claims RMP equals EK exactly, or attributes RMP primarily to the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase rather than to K⁺ permeability.

Common misconceptions:

- "The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase generates the resting potential." Correction: the pump _maintains the gradients_ on which RMP depends, but RMP itself is generated by K⁺ diffusion through K⁺ leak channels. The pump's electrogenic contribution is only a few mV.

QUESTION 2
Type: reasoning
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Explain how the Nernst equation predicts the equilibrium potential for a given ion, and why a _single_ ion's equilibrium potential is the membrane voltage at which the chemical and electrical driving forces on that ion are equal and opposite.

Model answer: The Nernst equation calculates the membrane potential at which a single permeant ion would be at _electrochemical equilibrium_ — no net flux across the membrane [Costanzo ch.1]. At body temperature (37 °C), it simplifies to: Ex = (60/z) × log₁₀(Ce/Ci), where Ex is the equilibrium potential for ion X (in mV), z is the ion's charge (+1 for Na⁺ and K⁺; +2 for Ca²⁺; −1 for Cl⁻), and Ce/Ci is the ratio of extracellular to intracellular concentrations.

The conceptual basis: when a membrane is selectively permeable to one ion, that ion will diffuse down its concentration gradient. Diffusion of a charged species creates a _diffusion potential_ — the moving charge sets up an electric field that opposes further diffusion. As more ions diffuse, the opposing electric field grows. Eventually, the field becomes large enough to exactly balance the chemical concentration gradient: chemical force pushes the ion one way; electrical force pulls it back; net flux is zero. The membrane potential at which this balance occurs is the _equilibrium potential_ for that ion.

For example, K⁺ is more concentrated inside the cell than outside (chemically wants to leave). As K⁺ leaves, the cell interior becomes negative (because positive charges leaving make the inside relatively negative). The negative interior electrically opposes further K⁺ outflow. At about −95 mV, the chemical gradient and electrical gradient on K⁺ are equal and opposite — K⁺ is at electrochemical equilibrium. This is EK.

The sign convention: by convention, membrane potentials are expressed as intracellular relative to extracellular. The Nernst equation gives the correct sign automatically when you apply it carefully: for K⁺ (more concentrated inside), Ce/Ci < 1, log of which is negative, so EK is negative. For Na⁺ (more concentrated outside), Ce/Ci > 1, log of which is positive, so ENa is positive.

The equation is fundamentally about a single ion. To predict the actual membrane potential when multiple ions are permeant simultaneously (as in the resting cell or during an action potential), the _Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation_ is needed — it weights each ion's equilibrium potential by its permeability.

Elaborative explanation: The Nernst equation is one of the most useful equations in physiology because it links a measurable concentration ratio to a measurable voltage. Clinically, this means changes in extracellular ion concentration (e.g., hyperkalaemia, hypocalcaemia) can be predicted to change the equilibrium potential and therefore the resting membrane potential — explaining why electrolyte derangements produce excitability changes. The equation also works backwards: measuring the reversal potential of a current at a particular membrane voltage can identify which ion is carrying that current (e.g., classical experimental dissection of synaptic currents).

Hints:

1. The equation is about _equilibrium_ — what does that mean in terms of net flux?
2. Two driving forces act on a charged ion across a membrane: chemical and electrical. What happens when they balance?
3. The equilibrium potential is the membrane voltage at which net flux is zero.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States the Nernst equation correctly (Ex = (60/z) × log₁₀(Ce/Ci) at body temperature).
- Explains that the equilibrium potential is where net flux of the ion is zero.
- Articulates the balance of chemical and electrical driving forces.
- Notes that the sign of Ex depends on the sign of the concentration gradient (more inside → negative Ex; more outside → positive Ex).
- Distinguishes Nernst (single ion) from Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz (multiple ions, actual RMP).

A Yellow answer states the equation and the equilibrium concept but does not articulate the chemical/electrical balance, or does not distinguish single-ion equilibrium from multi-ion membrane potential.

A Red answer treats Nernst as predicting the actual RMP rather than a single-ion equilibrium, or inverts the sign convention systematically, or attributes the equilibrium to ion concentration alone.

QUESTION 3
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Decompose the four phases of the typical neuronal action potential. For each phase, identify the ionic basis (which channels are open, which are closed) and the membrane potential range.

Model answer: The neuronal action potential has four sequential phases [Costanzo ch.1].

_Phase 1 — Resting potential (Em ≈ −70 to −80 mV)._ Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are _closed but available_ (activation gates closed; inactivation gates open). Voltage-gated K⁺ channels are mostly closed. K⁺ permeability is dominated by background K⁺ leak channels (always open), keeping the membrane potential close to EK. The membrane is at electrochemical near-equilibrium for K⁺ and Cl⁻; far from equilibrium for Na⁺ and Ca²⁺.

_Phase 2 — Upstroke / depolarisation (Em rises from −60 mV threshold to ~+30 mV)._ A depolarising stimulus brings the membrane to threshold. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channel activation gates open rapidly; Na⁺ rushes in down its electrochemical gradient (Em is far from ENa = +65 mV); the inward current depolarises the membrane further, opening more Na⁺ channels — a regenerative loop. The membrane potential rapidly approaches but doesn't quite reach ENa (the peak is typically +30 to +40 mV). Voltage-gated K⁺ channels begin to open with a delay, but at this point the upstroke is dominated by Na⁺ entry.

_Phase 3 — Repolarisation (Em falls from peak back toward RMP)._ Two events combine to repolarise the membrane: (a) Na⁺ channel _inactivation gates_ close (slower than activation), terminating the inward Na⁺ current. (b) Voltage-gated K⁺ channels are now fully open, producing a large outward K⁺ current that drives Em back toward EK. The combination of falling Na⁺ conductance and rising K⁺ conductance produces rapid repolarisation.

_Phase 4 — Hyperpolarising afterpotential (undershoot, Em transiently more negative than RMP)._ For a brief period after repolarisation, the K⁺ conductance remains higher than at rest (voltage-gated K⁺ channels close slowly); the membrane potential is therefore driven slightly closer to EK than the original RMP. Eventually, K⁺ channels close, K⁺ permeability returns to baseline, and the membrane depolarises slightly back to RMP.

The relationship between the phases and refractory periods: the _absolute refractory period_ overlaps phase 2 and most of phase 3 — Na⁺ channels are inactivated, no AP can fire. The _relative refractory period_ overlaps phase 4 — Na⁺ channels are recovering, but elevated K⁺ conductance means a stronger stimulus is needed to reach threshold.

Elaborative explanation: The same four-phase architecture, with variations, applies to skeletal muscle action potentials. Cardiac action potentials are different: ventricular myocytes have a _plateau phase_ (phase 2 in cardiac numbering) caused by Ca²⁺ entry through L-type Ca²⁺ channels, prolonging depolarisation; pacemaker cells (SA and AV nodes) use Ca²⁺ rather than Na⁺ for their upstroke and have spontaneous diastolic depolarisation (phase 4 in cardiac numbering) that drives them to threshold. Recognising the architectural difference between fast-response (Na⁺-driven) and slow-response (Ca²⁺-driven) action potentials is the foundation for understanding cardiac electrophysiology.

Hints:

1. Four phases, each defined by which ion channels are open or closed.
2. Trace the chronology: rest → depolarisation → repolarisation → afterhyperpolarisation.
3. Each phase has a distinct ionic basis — which ion dominates in each?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names all four phases (resting, upstroke/depolarisation, repolarisation, hyperpolarising afterpotential).
- For phase 1: identifies dominant K⁺ leak permeability; Na⁺ channels closed-but-available.
- For phase 2: identifies voltage-gated Na⁺ activation gates open; rapid Na⁺ entry.
- For phase 3: identifies Na⁺ inactivation + K⁺ channel opening for outward current.
- For phase 4: identifies persisting K⁺ conductance driving Em more negative than RMP.
- Optional: connects refractory periods to the phases.

A Yellow answer covers three of the four phases correctly but garbles one (commonly conflating phase 3 and 4, or omitting the hyperpolarising afterpotential).

A Red answer inverts the order of phases, attributes the upstroke to K⁺ rather than Na⁺, or claims the membrane potential reaches ENa fully.

QUESTION 4
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The voltage-gated Na⁺ channel has two functionally distinct gates: an _activation gate_ and an _inactivation gate_. Explain the kinetic difference between these two gates, the three states the channel can occupy, and how the kinetic difference shapes the action potential.

Model answer: The voltage-gated Na⁺ channel responsible for the action potential upstroke has two gates that respond to depolarisation but with very different kinetics [Costanzo ch.1].

_The two gates._ The _activation gate_ opens _quickly_ with depolarisation — within microseconds. The _inactivation gate_ closes _slowly_ with depolarisation — on a millisecond timescale. Both gates respond to the same stimulus (depolarisation) but in opposite directions and at different speeds. For Na⁺ to flow through the channel, both gates must be open simultaneously.

_The three states._

(1) _Closed but available_ — at the resting membrane potential, the activation gate is closed and the inactivation gate is open. The channel does not conduct, but it is "ready" to fire if depolarisation occurs.

(2) _Open_ — during the upstroke of the action potential, depolarisation has rapidly opened the activation gate; the inactivation gate has not yet closed (because it is slower); both gates are briefly open, and Na⁺ can flow through. This is a brief window — typically less than a millisecond.

(3) _Inactivated_ — at the peak of the action potential, the slow inactivation gate has now closed in response to the depolarisation. Even though the activation gate is still open, the inactivation gate blocks the pore. The channel does not conduct.

_How the kinetics shape the action potential._ The fast activation gate produces the rapid upstroke — when threshold is reached, activation gates open quickly, Na⁺ rushes in, and depolarisation is regenerative. The slow inactivation gate provides a built-in _off switch_ — even if the membrane stays depolarised, the inactivation gates close after a delay, terminating the upstroke. Without inactivation, the action potential would not terminate; the cell would remain at the Na⁺ equilibrium potential indefinitely. The temporal asymmetry of the two gates is therefore what makes the action potential brief and self-terminating rather than self-sustaining.

_Recovery._ For the channel to return from the inactivated state to the closed-but-available state, the membrane must repolarise back to the resting potential. Repolarisation re-opens the inactivation gates (they are coupled to membrane potential, just like the activation gates, but in opposite kinetics). Until this recovery is complete, no further action potential can fire — this is the molecular basis of the _absolute refractory period_.

Elaborative explanation: This three-state model (closed-but-available → open → inactivated) is one of the most important conceptual frameworks in cellular electrophysiology. It explains how a single channel type can produce a brief, self-terminating signal (the action potential), why the action potential cannot fire continuously even when stimulated continuously (refractory period), and why depolarising stimuli that develop slowly can fail to fire an AP at all (accommodation, because slow depolarisation closes inactivation gates before activation can occur). Drugs that bind preferentially to the inactivated state (e.g., lidocaine) selectively suppress channels that have been firing actively, providing the molecular basis for use-dependent block.

Hints:

1. Two gates, both controlled by voltage but with different speeds.
2. The fast one opens; the slow one closes; both are triggered by depolarisation.
3. The three states the channel can occupy reflect the four possible combinations of the two gates' positions (one combination — both closed — is uncommon).

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies activation gate as fast-opening, inactivation gate as slow-closing.
- Names the three states (closed-but-available, open, inactivated) and the position of each gate in each state.
- States that both gates must be open for Na⁺ to flow.
- Connects the kinetic asymmetry to the brief, self-terminating nature of the action potential.
- Connects the inactivated state to the absolute refractory period.

A Yellow answer correctly distinguishes the two gates and names the three states but does not articulate why the kinetic asymmetry matters for the AP shape.

A Red answer treats the two gates as functionally identical, or claims both open with depolarisation, or describes only two channel states (open / closed).

QUESTION 5
Type: comprehension
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Explain what is meant by the "all-or-none" property of the action potential. Once threshold is reached, why is the resulting AP of stereotyped amplitude regardless of stimulus strength? How is information about stimulus _intensity_ then encoded in the nervous system?

Model answer: The all-or-none property has two parts [Costanzo ch.1]. First, _the action potential either fires or it does not_ — there is a sharp threshold (~−60 mV) below which depolarisation simply dies away, and at or above which a full action potential is triggered. Second, _the action potential always has the same amplitude_ — it depolarises to approximately the same peak (~+30 mV) regardless of how strong the stimulus was, provided it was suprathreshold.

_Why the AP is stereotyped._ Once threshold is reached, the inward Na⁺ current through opening voltage-gated Na⁺ channels exceeds the outward K⁺ current. Depolarisation becomes self-sustaining — each new bit of depolarisation opens more Na⁺ channels, producing more inward current, producing more depolarisation. This regenerative loop drives the membrane potential rapidly toward ENa (+65 mV); the peak value depends on the equilibrium potentials and channel densities, _not_ on the stimulus that triggered the AP. The stimulus only needs to bring the membrane to threshold; from there, the cell's intrinsic properties determine the AP shape and amplitude. A stronger stimulus reaches threshold faster but produces an action potential of the same amplitude as a weaker (but still suprathreshold) stimulus.

_How intensity is encoded._ Two principal mechanisms.

(1) _Frequency coding (rate coding)._ A single neuron encodes stronger stimuli by firing action potentials at higher frequency. A weak stimulus may fire an AP every 100 ms (10 Hz); a strong stimulus may fire every 10 ms (100 Hz). The receiving neuron integrates the AP rate over time. The maximum firing frequency is limited by the absolute refractory period — typically a few hundred Hz at most.

(2) _Population coding (number of fibres)._ A stronger stimulus also recruits _more_ sensory receptors (and the corresponding neurons that innervate them). A light touch may activate just a few mechanoreceptors; firm pressure activates many. The brain integrates the number of active fibres in addition to the firing rate of each.

These two mechanisms together — frequency and recruitment — give the nervous system enormous dynamic range despite the all-or-none amplitude of each individual AP. The system encodes information through _temporal_ and _population_ patterns rather than through amplitude.

_Non-decremental propagation._ The all-or-none property also has a propagation consequence: each new region of axon membrane regenerates a full-size AP as the depolarisation reaches it. APs do not decay as they travel down the axon — the AP reaching the axon terminal is the same size as the AP at the cell body. This is what makes neurons reliable long-distance signal carriers.

Elaborative explanation: The all-or-none and non-decremental properties together solve a fundamental engineering problem: how to send signals over distances without amplitude decay. Passive electrical signals (graded potentials in dendrites and at synapses) decay rapidly; AP propagation is reliable because each AP is regenerated. The trade-off: the nervous system cannot use AP amplitude as an information channel; it must use frequency and recruitment instead. Recognising this design constraint is essential for understanding why motor units, sensory receptors, and neural codes are organised the way they are.

Hints:

1. "All-or-none" has two parts: (a) sharp threshold, (b) constant amplitude above threshold.
2. The amplitude is fixed because the regenerative loop drives Em to the same peak each time.
3. If amplitude can't change, what _can_ the nervous system change to encode stimulus strength?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines all-or-none: sharp threshold + stereotyped amplitude above threshold.
- Explains _why_ AP amplitude is stereotyped: regenerative Na⁺-channel loop, intrinsic properties of the cell.
- Articulates that amplitude does not encode stimulus strength.
- Names _both_ frequency coding (rate) and recruitment (population coding) as the mechanisms for encoding intensity.
- Optional: notes non-decremental propagation as a related consequence of the all-or-none property.

A Yellow answer correctly defines all-or-none and identifies frequency coding but omits population coding (or vice versa), or does not articulate why amplitude is stereotyped.

A Red answer claims amplitude varies with stimulus strength, or that information is encoded in AP amplitude, or denies the all-or-none property.

QUESTION 6
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A diabetic patient develops severe hyperkalaemia (plasma K⁺ 6.5 mmol/L) and presents with progressive muscle weakness. Decompose the molecular and cellular mechanism — from elevated ECF K⁺ to clinical weakness — and explain why the weakness is _paradoxical_ (intuitive expectation might be the opposite).

Model answer: Severe hyperkalaemia produces muscle weakness through a paradoxical mechanism that operates at the level of voltage-gated Na⁺ channel availability [Costanzo ch.1, Box 1.3].

_Step 1 — EK becomes less negative._ The K⁺ equilibrium potential is set by the K⁺ concentration ratio across the membrane (Nernst equation): EK = 60 × log₁₀(Ke/Ki). At normal Ke = 4 mmol/L, EK ≈ −95 mV. When Ke rises to 6.5 mmol/L, the gradient is reduced; EK becomes less negative — around −80 to −85 mV.

_Step 2 — RMP follows EK and depolarises._ Because the resting membrane is dominantly permeable to K⁺, the resting membrane potential tracks EK closely. As EK becomes less negative, RMP also becomes less negative — the cell _depolarises_ by several mV.

_Step 3 — The intuitive (but wrong) expectation._ Naively, one might expect this depolarisation to _increase_ excitability — the membrane potential is now closer to threshold (~−60 mV), so it would seem to take less stimulus to fire an action potential. This is what trips up most students.

_Step 4 — The actual consequence: Na⁺ channel inactivation._ Sustained depolarisation has a critical effect on voltage-gated Na⁺ channels: the _inactivation gates close_. Normally, at RMP, the inactivation gates are open (the channel is in the closed-but-available state). When the membrane is held at a depolarised level (as it is in chronic hyperkalaemia), the inactivation gates respond by closing. The channel cannot fire — even if depolarisation reaches threshold, the activation gates open but the inactivation gates remain closed, so no Na⁺ flows.

_Step 5 — Reduced excitability and clinical weakness._ With most Na⁺ channels in the inactivated state, very few are available to carry the inward Na⁺ current of the action potential upstroke. The cell becomes _less_ excitable, not more. In skeletal muscle, this manifests as muscle weakness — the muscle cannot fire action potentials reliably, so it cannot contract. The weakness is "paradoxical" because the membrane potential is closer to threshold, but the channel state has changed in a way that overrides the proximity-to-threshold benefit.

_Cardiac risk._ The same mechanism affects cardiac myocytes. Severe hyperkalaemia produces characteristic ECG changes (peaked T waves first, then prolonged PR, widened QRS, "sine wave" pattern, asystole) and is a medical emergency. The risk of fatal arrhythmia is what makes hyperkalaemia urgently treatable.

_Treatment logic._ Three approaches: (a) shift K⁺ back into cells using insulin + glucose (insulin activates Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase; glucose prevents iatrogenic hypoglycaemia), or with β2-agonists (which also stimulate the pump); (b) remove K⁺ from the body using loop diuretics, K⁺-binding resins, or dialysis; (c) stabilise membranes acutely with IV calcium gluconate (raises the threshold for AP firing, partially counteracting the inactivation).

Elaborative explanation: This question illustrates two general lessons. First, _physiology is not always intuitive_ — the depolarisation produced by hyperkalaemia "feels" like it should enhance excitability, but it doesn't. The mechanistic detail (Na⁺ channel inactivation) explains the paradox. Second, _the same molecular mechanism can fail in opposite directions_. Severe hypokalaemia (low ECF K⁺) makes EK _more_ negative, hyperpolarises the cell, makes it _harder_ to reach threshold, and also produces weakness — but for a different reason. Recognising both directions teaches students that excitability is a function of _both_ the proximity of RMP to threshold _and_ the availability of Na⁺ channels.

Hints:

1. Trace the chain: high ECF K⁺ → EK changes → RMP changes → Na⁺ channel state changes → excitability changes.
2. The intuitive expectation about excitability is wrong. Why?
3. Sustained depolarisation closes a specific gate. What happens to AP firing when that gate is closed?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Connects high ECF K⁺ to less-negative EK (via Nernst equation).
- Explains that RMP follows EK and depolarises.
- Identifies the _paradox_: depolarisation should bring closer to threshold (intuitive) but instead reduces excitability.
- Explains the mechanism: sustained depolarisation closes Na⁺ channel inactivation gates.
- Connects fewer available Na⁺ channels to inability to fire AP and clinical weakness.
- Optional: mentions cardiac risk and / or treatment principles.

A Yellow answer traces the chain through to inactivation but does not articulate the paradox, or fails to connect channel state to clinical weakness.

A Red answer claims hyperkalaemia hyperpolarises (rather than depolarises), or attributes weakness to direct K⁺ inhibition of muscle contraction, or denies that the mechanism is at the channel level.

QUESTION 7
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 32-year-old woman presents with episodes of blurred vision, double vision, and progressive leg weakness over several years. MRI shows multiple white-matter lesions; visual evoked potentials show prolonged latency. Decompose the underlying pathophysiology — from immune attack on myelin to clinical features and electrophysiologic findings — and explain why visual evoked potentials are a sensitive functional test.

Model answer: This is a classical presentation of multiple sclerosis (MS), the most common demyelinating disease of the central nervous system [Costanzo ch.1, Box 1.4]. The pathophysiology unfolds through a chain that begins at the level of myelin sheath integrity.

_Step 1 — Autoimmune demyelination._ MS is characterised by an autoimmune attack on CNS myelin (and the oligodendrocytes that produce it). Activated T cells cross the blood-brain barrier and recognise myelin antigens; cytokines and other inflammatory mediators damage the myelin sheath; the axon may initially be spared but is exposed and vulnerable. Lesions ("plaques") develop in the white matter — characteristically scattered in _space_ (multiple anatomic locations) and in _time_ (occurring as discrete episodes over years).

_Step 2 — Loss of myelin's electrical insulation._ Normally, myelin acts as an electrical insulator — it greatly increases membrane resistance and decreases membrane capacitance along the internodal axon. This forces depolarising currents to flow rapidly down the axon interior to the next node of Ranvier (where Na⁺ channels are concentrated), enabling fast saltatory conduction. With myelin destroyed, membrane resistance falls — current "leaks out" across the demyelinated axon segment instead of flowing efficiently along it.

_Step 3 — Slowed or failed conduction._ The leakage of current produces two effects: (a) the _length constant_ (distance over which the depolarisation can spread without decaying) decreases, so local currents may not reach the next node of Ranvier with enough amplitude to depolarise it to threshold; and (b) when conduction does occur, it is much _slower_ because more current is needed to overcome the leakage. In severe demyelination, conduction can fail entirely.

_Step 4 — Clinical features by lesion location._ The clinical presentation depends on which white-matter tracts are affected.

- _Optic nerve / chiasm:_ optic neuritis with blurred vision, eye pain on movement, central scotoma. Often the first MS symptom.
- _Brainstem:_ internuclear ophthalmoplegia (impaired conjugate eye movement on lateral gaze, due to medial longitudinal fasciculus demyelination); dysarthria; vertigo.
- _Spinal cord:_ sensory symptoms (paraesthesiae, numbness), motor weakness, gait ataxia, bladder/bowel dysfunction.
- _Cerebellum and connections:_ ataxia, intention tremor.

_Step 5 — Visual evoked potentials as a functional test._ Visual evoked potentials (VEPs) measure the electrical response of the visual cortex to a flashing or pattern-reversing visual stimulus. The latency from stimulus to cortical response (typically ~100 ms in normal subjects, the "P100") is exquisitely sensitive to optic-pathway demyelination. Even when MRI shows no optic-nerve lesion, VEPs frequently show prolonged latency, reflecting slowed conduction in subclinically demyelinated optic-pathway fibres. This makes VEPs a useful tool for confirming optic-pathway involvement and for documenting subclinical demyelination — particularly useful diagnostically when imaging is equivocal.

_Treatment principles._ Acute relapses are treated with high-dose corticosteroids to reduce inflammation. Disease-modifying agents (interferon-beta, glatiramer acetate, monoclonal antibodies such as natalizumab and ocrelizumab) reduce relapse frequency by modulating the autoimmune attack. Symptomatic management addresses spasticity, fatigue, bladder dysfunction, etc.

Elaborative explanation: MS illustrates how a single biophysical defect (loss of myelin's insulating function) produces a wide range of clinical phenotypes depending on lesion location. The architectural lesson — that myelin is essential for fast saltatory conduction, and that its loss produces conduction failure or slowing — generalises to peripheral nervous system demyelinating diseases as well (Guillain-Barré syndrome, chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy, hereditary neuropathies). Recognising the unifying mechanism makes the diverse clinical presentations of demyelinating diseases comprehensible rather than disparate.

Hints:

1. The disease attacks the myelin sheath. What does that do to membrane resistance and capacitance?
2. The biophysical change has two consequences for conduction: amplitude and speed. Both are affected.
3. VEPs measure latency from visual stimulus to cortical response. What does a prolonged latency reflect?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies MS as autoimmune demyelination of CNS axons.
- Explains how loss of myelin reduces membrane resistance and disrupts saltatory conduction.
- Articulates the consequence: slowed or failed conduction.
- Names at least two specific clinical syndromes related to lesion location (optic neuritis, brainstem signs, spinal cord, cerebellar).
- Explains how VEPs detect slowed conduction in the optic pathway (prolonged latency reflects slowed conduction).

A Yellow answer covers most of the above but does not articulate the biophysical mechanism (membrane resistance and current leakage) clearly, or omits the explanation of VEPs.

A Red answer attributes MS to neuronal degeneration, or to peripheral nerve disease, or claims demyelination doesn't affect conduction velocity.

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Lidocaine is administered subcutaneously before a minor procedure. Explain how lidocaine's _use-dependent_ (state-dependent) block of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels accomplishes selective local anaesthesia of pain pathways while sparing motor function and consciousness.

Model answer: Lidocaine is a small lipophilic molecule that blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels [Costanzo ch.1; Berne ch.1]. Its clinical selectivity arises from two principles: _use-dependent block_ and _fibre-size selectivity_.

_Use-dependent (state-dependent) block._ Lidocaine binds preferentially to voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in their _open_ or _inactivated_ states. Quiescent channels (in the closed-but-available state, the dominant state at rest) bind lidocaine poorly. The drug's affinity for the channel depends on the channel's conformation: when the channel is firing (transitioning rapidly between open and inactivated states), lidocaine binds and accumulates; when the channel is quiescent, the drug dissociates. The clinical consequence: neurons that are actively firing accumulate lidocaine in their Na⁺ channels and become blocked; neurons that are quiescent or firing slowly accumulate less drug and remain functional.

_Why this benefits local anaesthesia._ When a small amount of lidocaine is injected near a nerve, all fibres in the nerve are exposed. But the fibres that are most actively firing — typically the small-diameter A-delta and C fibres carrying acute and slow pain (which are activated continuously by the painful procedure) — accumulate the drug fastest and are blocked first. Larger motor fibres (Aα), proprioceptive fibres (Aβ), and myelinated cold-touch fibres are firing less actively and are blocked more slowly. The patient therefore experiences pain relief before significant motor block — which is the desirable clinical outcome (the patient stays still, the surgeon can operate, and pain is controlled).

_Fibre-size selectivity._ Independently of use-dependence, lidocaine more readily blocks small-diameter fibres than large-diameter fibres. Small fibres have shorter internodal distances and more nodes per unit length within the lidocaine diffusion zone; the cumulative Na⁺-channel blockade therefore reaches a critical level (where conduction fails) more easily in small fibres. Pain fibres (A-delta and C) are predominantly small; motor fibres (Aα) are large. The fibre-size effect adds to the use-dependence: small + actively firing pain fibres are blocked first.

_Why systemic effects are limited._ Lidocaine injected subcutaneously stays mostly local — it diffuses around the injection site, blocks nearby nerves, and is slowly absorbed into the systemic circulation. Once absorbed, it is rapidly metabolised by the liver (cytochrome P450). Plasma levels are far too low to affect distant nerves or the brain (consciousness is preserved). However, _intravenous_ or excessive lidocaine causes systemic effects: CNS toxicity (perioral numbness, dizziness, agitation, seizures) and cardiovascular toxicity (hypotension, bradycardia, arrhythmias). These effects also reflect Na⁺ channel block — but in tissues where it is no longer therapeutic.

_The same principle in antiarrhythmic use._ Lidocaine is a class IB antiarrhythmic (intravenous use). The same use-dependence makes it selectively block ischaemic cardiac myocytes (which are partially depolarised and have many channels in the inactivated state) over normal myocytes — useful in suppressing ischaemic ventricular arrhythmias.

Elaborative explanation: Use-dependent block is one of the most elegant principles in pharmacology — the drug binds preferentially to the disease-state conformation of its target. The same principle applies to several other drug classes: class IC antiarrhythmics (flecainide), some anti-epileptics (phenytoin, carbamazepine), and even some antipsychotics. Recognising use-dependence as a general design strategy explains why these drugs have selectivity for active disease processes rather than all neuronal activity equally.

1. Lidocaine binds the Na⁺ channel in two of its three conformational states. Which two?
2. Pain fibres are firing actively during a painful procedure. What state are their channels in?
3. The combination of use-dependence and fibre-size selectivity favours blockade of small actively-firing pain fibres.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies lidocaine's target as voltage-gated Na⁺ channels.
- Explains use-dependent block: drug binds preferentially to open or inactivated channels.
- Articulates that actively firing fibres accumulate the drug faster.
- Notes fibre-size selectivity (small fibres blocked more easily than large).
- Explains the clinical consequence: pain pathways blocked preferentially.
- Optional: mentions systemic absorption being limited (consciousness preserved) or class IB antiarrhythmic use.

A Yellow answer correctly identifies the target and use-dependence but does not articulate fibre-size selectivity, or does not connect the mechanism to clinical selectivity.

A Red answer attributes lidocaine's effect to GABA receptors (hypnotic effect), or claims it works on K⁺ channels, or denies the use-dependence.

Stem: A diner consumes pufferfish (fugu) at a restaurant where the chef has not properly removed the toxin-containing organs. Within 30 minutes, the diner develops perioral numbness, ascending muscle weakness, and dyspnoea. Explain the mechanism of toxicity, the clinical sequence, and why no specific antidote exists.

Model answer: The toxin involved is _tetrodotoxin (TTX)_, a small natural compound concentrated in the liver, ovaries, and skin of pufferfish (Tetraodontidae) — and notably also in some newts, octopuses, and other organisms [Costanzo ch.1].

_Mechanism of toxicity._ TTX binds with extraordinary specificity and high affinity to the _extracellular pore_ of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels. The bound molecule physically occludes the pore, preventing Na⁺ from entering the cell. With Na⁺ channels blocked, the upstroke of the action potential cannot occur — depolarising stimuli reach threshold but no inward Na⁺ current is generated, so the membrane simply repolarises without firing. The block is reversible — TTX eventually unbinds — but during the time of exposure, conduction through affected nerves and muscles is abolished.

_Clinical sequence._ The progression of symptoms follows a stereotyped pattern based on which nerves and tissues are affected first.

_Stage 1 (within 30 minutes):_ perioral numbness (lips, tongue, face) — small sensory fibres are most accessible to the toxin via mucous membranes, and these fibres are blocked first. Light-headedness, headache, salivation.

_Stage 2 (30 minutes to a few hours):_ ascending paralysis. Larger sensory and motor fibres are blocked. The patient develops difficulty walking, weak grip, generalised weakness. Speech becomes slurred. Motor weakness ascends from peripheral muscles toward central muscles.

_Stage 3:_ respiratory paralysis. Diaphragm and intercostal muscles are affected. The patient cannot breathe adequately and develops hypoxaemia and hypercapnia. This is the lethal step — without ventilatory support, the patient dies of respiratory failure.

_Stage 4:_ cardiovascular collapse. Cardiac myocytes also depend on voltage-gated Na⁺ channels for their (fast-response) action potentials; cardiac TTX block produces hypotension and bradyarrhythmias.

_Notable feature._ Consciousness is _preserved_ until very late, because TTX does not readily cross the blood-brain barrier. The patient is awake and aware while becoming progressively paralysed — a particularly distressing experience.

_Why no specific antidote._ TTX binds the Na⁺ channel pore directly; there is no enzymatic detoxification system in humans (unlike, say, snake venoms, which are proteins that can be neutralised by anti-venom antibodies). No drug exists that specifically displaces TTX from the channel. Treatment is therefore entirely _supportive_: maintain the airway, provide mechanical ventilation, support cardiac function. Patients who survive the first 24 hours typically recover fully because TTX eventually unbinds and is excreted. Mortality is high if respiratory support is unavailable; with intensive care, survival rates are much improved.

_Clinical pearl._ The same toxin (TTX) and a related toxin (saxitoxin, from dinoflagellates, causing paralytic shellfish poisoning) are used in research as the standard reference Na⁺-channel blockers. They were instrumental in the experimental dissection of action potentials by Hodgkin and Huxley.

Elaborative explanation: TTX poisoning is an instructive clinical case for understanding the action potential — every clinical feature follows directly from voltage-gated Na⁺ channel blockade. The ascending paralysis pattern (small fibres first) reflects fibre-size selectivity (similar to the principle behind lidocaine's selectivity, but in reverse — TTX affects small fibres first because they have smaller channels with easier access). The preservation of consciousness reflects the blood-brain barrier's exclusion of TTX. The lack of an antidote reflects the simple physical occlusion mechanism — there is no enzyme to inhibit, no metabolic pathway to bypass.

1. The toxin selectively blocks one type of voltage-gated channel.

3. The sequence of symptoms (perioral numbness → ascending weakness → respiratory failure) reflects which fibres are blocked first.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

Stem: A patient on long-term loop diuretic therapy (furosemide) develops hypokalaemia (plasma K⁺ 2.8 mmol/L) and complains of muscle weakness and fatigue. ECG shows U waves and prolonged QT. Explain how _low_ extracellular K⁺ — the opposite direction from hyperkalaemia — produces excitability changes that also impair muscle and cardiac function.

Model answer: Hypokalaemia, like hyperkalaemia, alters cellular excitability — but through a different mechanism, in the opposite direction [Costanzo ch.1]. The clinical features (muscle weakness, ECG changes) reflect the consequences for skeletal and cardiac excitability.

_Step 1 — EK becomes more negative._ When extracellular K⁺ falls (Ke = 2.8 mmol/L), the K⁺ concentration ratio across the membrane becomes steeper (Ki/Ke is larger). The Nernst-predicted EK becomes more negative — for example, around −105 to −110 mV (vs the normal −95 mV).

_Step 2 — RMP follows EK and hyperpolarises._ Because the resting membrane is dominantly K⁺-permeable, the RMP becomes more negative — the cell hyperpolarises by several mV.

_Step 3 — Threshold becomes harder to reach._ With a more negative RMP, more depolarisation is needed to bring the cell to threshold (which remains at ~−60 mV regardless of RMP). Excitability is therefore _reduced_ by simply increasing the gap between RMP and threshold.

_Step 4 — Action potential changes._ Several additional effects compound the basic excitability problem:

- Voltage-gated K⁺ channels' behaviour depends on intracellular K⁺ concentration; in hypokalaemia, _cellular_ K⁺ may also be reduced (over time), which slows the K⁺ outward current responsible for repolarisation.
- The action potential is therefore prolonged (longer duration), particularly noticeable in cardiac myocytes where it manifests as QT prolongation on the ECG.
- Cardiac repolarisation is delayed, predisposing to _triggered activity_ and arrhythmias including torsades de pointes.

_Clinical features._

_Skeletal muscle:_ weakness and fatigue. Muscle cells have a wider RMP-to-threshold gap and require stronger stimuli to fire. Severe hypokalaemia can produce frank paralysis (rare but serious — flaccid quadriparesis from extreme hypokalaemia).

_Cardiac muscle and ECG changes:_

- _U waves_ (small wave following T wave) — represent delayed repolarisation of Purkinje fibres and ventricular muscle.
- _T-wave flattening or inversion._
- _Prolonged QT interval_ (delayed repolarisation).
- _Increased risk of arrhythmias_, particularly atrial and ventricular premature beats and torsades de pointes.

_Smooth muscle:_ hypokalaemia can cause ileus (paralytic) and orthostatic hypotension.

_Why loop diuretics cause hypokalaemia._ Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) inhibit the Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ symporter (NKCC2) in the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle. This prevents Na⁺ reabsorption in the loop; Na⁺ is delivered to the distal nephron at higher rates. The increased distal Na⁺ delivery activates ENaC-mediated Na⁺ reabsorption in the collecting duct, which generates a lumen-negative potential that drives K⁺ secretion through ROMK channels into the urine. Volume contraction also activates RAAS, with aldosterone further increasing distal Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺ secretion. The combined effect: substantial K⁺ loss in the urine over days to weeks. Concurrent magnesium loss contributes to refractory hypokalaemia (Mg²⁺ deficiency impairs Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase function and ROMK gating; replacing K⁺ alone may not work without Mg²⁺ replacement).

_Treatment._ Replace K⁺ orally or intravenously (intravenous replacement carries risk of acute hyperkalaemia if too fast). Consider concurrent K⁺-sparing diuretic (spironolactone, eplerenone, amiloride). Replace magnesium if also low. Treat the underlying cause (review diuretic indication; consider dose reduction or alternative therapy).

_The general principle._ Both hyperkalaemia and hypokalaemia impair excitability — but through opposite mechanisms. Hyperkalaemia depolarises the cell and inactivates Na⁺ channels. Hypokalaemia hyperpolarises the cell and increases the gap to threshold. Both extremes are dangerous; plasma K⁺ must be maintained within a narrow range (3.5–5.3 mmol/L) for normal cellular function.

Elaborative explanation: This question complements the hyperkalaemia question (Pass 1, Q6 in the MCQ set; Pass 2, Q6 in this set) by illustrating that the same regulated variable (plasma K⁺) can fail in either direction with predictable consequences. Recognising the "two extremes" pattern is useful for many other regulated variables — Na⁺ (hyper- and hyponatraemia both produce neurological symptoms via different mechanisms), Ca²⁺ (hypocalcaemia produces tetany; hypercalcaemia causes weakness and cardiac effects), pH (acidosis and alkalosis both have characteristic ECG and CNS effects). The unifying lesson — _deviation in either direction from a regulated set-point produces dysfunction_ — is the basic logic of homeostasis.

2. The mechanism for the cardiac changes involves _both_ a hyperpolarised RMP and a slower repolarisation.
3. Why does loop diuretic use cause K⁺ loss? Trace the nephron mechanism.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Articulates the consequence: increased gap between RMP and threshold, reducing excitability.

Stem: A first-year medical student claims: "The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase generates the resting membrane potential — without it, the cell would have no resting potential at all." Identify the misconception, explain the correction, and clarify the actual role of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase in establishing and maintaining the RMP.

Model answer: The misconception is that the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase _directly generates_ the RMP through its electrogenic 3:2 stoichiometry [Costanzo ch.1]. The correction has two parts.

_The pump's actual role._ The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase establishes and maintains the _ionic concentration gradients_ on which the RMP depends — high intracellular K⁺ (~120 mmol/L) and low intracellular Na⁺ (~14 mmol/L). It does this by hydrolysing ATP to actively pump 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per cycle. Without continuous pump activity, the gradients dissipate slowly (over hours, due to slow leak through other channels), and eventually the K⁺ diffusion that generates the RMP is no longer possible. So the pump is _necessary_ for the RMP — but it does not generate the potential directly.

_The actual generator of the RMP._ The RMP is generated by _K⁺ diffusion_ through K⁺ leak channels. The mechanism: K⁺ is much more concentrated inside than outside, so it tends to diffuse outward (down its concentration gradient). Because K⁺ is positively charged, its outward diffusion leaves the cell interior negative (positive charges leaving make the interior relatively negative). The negative interior produces an electric field that opposes further K⁺ outflow. At about −95 mV (the K⁺ equilibrium potential, EK), the chemical gradient and electrical gradient on K⁺ are equal and opposite; net K⁺ flux stops. The membrane potential settles near this value because K⁺ permeability dominates at rest. This is the RMP — and it is generated by _passive_ K⁺ diffusion, not by active pumping.

_Why the pump's electrogenic effect is small._ The 3:2 stoichiometry means 1 net positive charge leaves the cell per ATP cycle. This is a small outward current that contributes a few millivolts of additional negativity to the RMP — _but only a few millivolts_. The dominant determinant of RMP is the K⁺ diffusion potential (~−95 mV); the pump's electrogenic contribution is a minor adjustment (~3–5 mV). This is why inhibiting the pump (e.g., with ouabain) produces a small immediate depolarisation (a few mV), with a much larger depolarisation appearing only later as the gradients dissipate.

_A useful analogy._ Think of a battery (the K⁺ gradient) and a charger (the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase). The battery is what stores the energy and provides the voltage; the charger maintains the battery so it can keep providing voltage. The voltage _itself_ comes from the battery, not the charger. If you disconnect the charger, the battery still provides voltage for a long time before it runs down. If you destroy the battery (eliminate the K⁺ gradient), no amount of charging will produce voltage immediately. The K⁺ gradient is the battery; the pump is the charger; the RMP is the voltage.

_The student's correction._ The student should adopt this two-layer mental model:

1. _The pump establishes the gradient._ Without the pump (over hours), no gradient.
2. _The K⁺ leak channels generate the voltage._ Without K⁺ leak channels (acutely), no RMP, even if the gradient is still intact.

Both components are necessary. The pump is upstream and slow; the K⁺ leak channels are downstream and fast. The acute generator of the RMP is K⁺ diffusion.

Elaborative explanation: This misconception is one of the most common in cellular physiology, and it persists because the pump's electrogenicity does contribute _something_ to the RMP. The deeper conceptual point is that _most cellular electrical phenomena depend on diffusion of ions through channels, with the pumps maintaining the gradients in the background_. This applies not only to the RMP but to the action potential (driven by Na⁺ and K⁺ channels), to synaptic potentials (driven by ligand-gated channels), and to many regulatory phenomena. Pumps are essential, but they are infrastructure — the moment-to-moment electrical action happens at channels.

Hints:

1. Two layers: the pump and the K⁺ leak channels. Each does a different job.
2. The pump establishes the _gradient_; what generates the _voltage_?
3. The pump's electrogenic contribution is small. What is the dominant generator of RMP?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the misconception: the pump is treated as the direct generator of RMP.
- Corrects: the pump _maintains the gradients_; K⁺ diffusion through K⁺ leak channels _generates the voltage_.
- Explains the mechanism of K⁺ diffusion-generated potential (K⁺ leaves the cell, leaving negative charge behind, until the diffusion potential balances further K⁺ outflow).
- Notes that the pump's electrogenic contribution is small (a few mV), not the dominant contributor.
- Optional: gives the analogy of battery (gradient) vs charger (pump).

A Yellow answer corrects the misconception but does not articulate the K⁺ diffusion mechanism that actually generates the RMP, or does not quantify the pump's electrogenic contribution as small.

A Red answer agrees with the student, or attributes the RMP to a single source (either pump alone or channels alone) without acknowledging the layered architecture.

Stem: Two main strategies increase action potential conduction velocity along an axon: increasing axon diameter and adding myelin. Explain the _biophysical mechanism_ by which each strategy works, why the two strategies are complementary rather than redundant, and how invertebrates and vertebrates have favoured different strategies.

Model answer: Conduction velocity in nerve fibres depends on the _cable properties_ — how far and how fast a depolarisation can spread passively along the axon before it must be regenerated [Costanzo ch.1]. Two cable properties are central: the _length constant_ (λ, the distance over which depolarisation decays to 37% of its peak) and the _time constant_ (τ, the time for the membrane to reach 63% of its final voltage in response to current).

_Strategy 1 — Increased axon diameter._ The internal (axoplasmic) resistance Ri is inversely proportional to the cross-sectional area (A = πr²). A larger axon has lower Ri — current flows more easily down the axon interior. Length constant λ ∝ √(Rm/Ri), so reducing Ri increases λ. With a longer length constant, the depolarising current from one regenerative site can spread further before decaying below the threshold needed to depolarise the next site. Conduction velocity therefore increases with axon diameter.

_Strategy 2 — Myelination._ Myelin is a lipid insulator wrapped around the axon. It increases membrane resistance (Rm) — current can't leak out across the myelinated regions — and decreases membrane capacitance (Cm) — less charge needs to be moved to depolarise the membrane. Both effects contribute. Higher Rm increases λ (current is forced to flow rapidly down the axon interior rather than leaking out across the membrane); lower Cm decreases τ (the membrane depolarises faster in response to inward current). The net effect: depolarising currents from one node of Ranvier travel rapidly through the high-Rm, low-Cm internodal region to the next node, where the action potential is regenerated. The action potential effectively "jumps" from node to node — _saltatory conduction_.

_Why the two strategies are complementary, not redundant._ They work through different cable parameters. Diameter affects Ri (internal resistance); myelin affects Rm and Cm (membrane resistance and capacitance). The two effects multiply: a large myelinated axon has both a low Ri and a high Rm/low Cm, achieving the longest length constant and the shortest time constant — the fastest possible conduction. The largest myelinated mammalian motor axons (~20 µm diameter) conduct at ~100 m/s. Without diameter increase, the same myelin alone produces less velocity; without myelin, the same diameter alone produces less velocity. The combination is what biology has selected for fast vertebrate motor and sensory pathways.

_Invertebrate vs vertebrate strategies._ Invertebrates lack myelin (with rare exceptions). To achieve fast conduction, they have evolved _very large_ axons — most famously the _squid giant axon_ (~1 mm in diameter), which serves the rapid escape response by jet propulsion. Conduction velocity in the squid giant axon is impressive (~25 m/s) but is achieved at enormous metabolic and structural cost — a single axon takes up substantial body volume.

Vertebrates have evolved myelin, allowing fast conduction in much smaller axons (a 5–10 µm myelinated axon can match the squid giant axon's velocity). This allows the vertebrate nervous system to pack many fast-conducting fibres into the small spaces available — millions of axons in a peripheral nerve trunk, organised in topographic order. Without myelin, a vertebrate nervous system would be impossibly bulky.

_The metabolic economy of myelin._ In addition to speed, myelin also reduces the metabolic cost of maintaining ionic gradients. Action potentials regenerate only at nodes of Ranvier, so the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase needs to restore ions only at the nodes — not along the entire axonal membrane. This drastically reduces the ATP cost per action potential, freeing up energy for other neural functions.

Elaborative explanation: This question illustrates how evolution can solve the same engineering problem (fast conduction) through multiple independent mechanisms, each with its own trade-offs. Vertebrate myelination is one of the major adaptive innovations that allowed vertebrate nervous systems to become large and complex — without myelin, mammals could not have the millions of fast-conducting fibres that underlie sensorimotor integration, conscious experience, and complex behaviour. The architectural lesson — _fast electrical signalling requires either large diameter or insulation, and ideally both_ — generalises to other engineered electrical systems (e.g., transmission lines, where both diameter and insulation matter for fast signal transmission).

Hints:

1. Two cable properties matter: length constant (how far) and time constant (how fast).
2. Diameter affects internal resistance; myelin affects membrane resistance and capacitance.
3. Invertebrates use one strategy; vertebrates the other (or both).

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines length constant and time constant (or explains them in words).
- For diameter: explains that larger axons have lower internal resistance, increasing length constant.
- For myelin: explains that myelin increases membrane resistance and decreases membrane capacitance, increasing length constant and decreasing time constant.
- Articulates that the two strategies affect different cable parameters and are therefore complementary.
- Notes the invertebrate strategy (large unmyelinated axons, e.g., squid giant axon) vs vertebrate strategy (myelinated axons of moderate diameter).

A Yellow answer covers diameter and myelin but does not articulate the cable-property mechanism, or does not contrast invertebrate and vertebrate strategies.

A Red answer treats the two strategies as competitive rather than complementary, or attributes conduction velocity to a different mechanism (e.g., Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activity).

Stem: Compare the _neuronal_ (fast-response) action potential and the _cardiac slow-response_ action potential (e.g., in SA node pacemaker cells). For each, identify the dominant ion driving the upstroke, the channel responsible, and the functional consequence of the difference for cellular behaviour.

Model answer: The neuronal and cardiac slow-response action potentials differ fundamentally in the ion that drives the upstroke, with profound functional consequences.

_Neuronal (fast-response) action potential._ The upstroke is driven by _inward Na⁺ current_ through _voltage-gated Na⁺ channels_ [Costanzo ch.1]. The activation gates open within microseconds of depolarisation reaching threshold; Na⁺ rushes in down its electrochemical gradient (Em is far from ENa = +65 mV); the upstroke is _very fast_ (rate of rise ~500 V/s). The entire action potential lasts only 1–2 ms in nerve and skeletal muscle. The fast response is also seen in cardiac atrial and ventricular muscle and in the Purkinje conducting system.

Functional consequences of the fast response:

- _Rapid conduction velocity_ — fast upstrokes spread quickly via local currents.
- _Brief refractory period_ — allows high firing rates (up to ~1000 Hz in some neurons).
- _All-or-none with sharp threshold_ — clear binary signalling.

_Cardiac slow-response action potential._ The upstroke is driven by _inward Ca²⁺ current_ through _L-type voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels_ (also called dihydropyridine receptors). This action potential occurs in pacemaker cells of the SA and AV nodes, where voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are essentially absent or non-functional. The Ca²⁺ activation gates open _much more slowly_ than Na⁺ activation gates, so the upstroke is _slow_ (rate of rise ~1–10 V/s). The peak depolarisation reaches a less positive value because the Ca²⁺ equilibrium potential (~+120 mV) drives the membrane only as far as the channels' kinetics allow.

Functional consequences of the slow response:

- _Slow conduction velocity_ — the action potential takes longer to develop, so it spreads more slowly. The AV node deliberately exploits this to delay atrial-to-ventricular conduction, allowing the atria time to empty before ventricular contraction begins.
- _Longer refractory period_ — Ca²⁺ channel inactivation kinetics are slower, prolonging the refractory period and limiting the maximum heart rate.
- _Spontaneous diastolic depolarisation_ — pacemaker cells exhibit a slow phase 4 depolarisation between action potentials, driven by the funny current (If, a mixed Na⁺/K⁺ current activated by hyperpolarisation) and progressively closing K⁺ channels. This automaticity is what makes pacemaker cells fire spontaneously without external stimulation.

_Pharmacological implications._ Drugs targeting the two channel classes have different effects:

- Voltage-gated Na⁺ channel blockers (lidocaine, class I antiarrhythmics): primarily affect fast-response cells (atrial and ventricular muscle, Purkinje fibres). Used to suppress ventricular arrhythmias.
- Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem — class IV antiarrhythmics): primarily affect slow-response cells (SA and AV nodes). Used to slow heart rate and AV nodal conduction (treating supraventricular tachycardia, atrial fibrillation rate control).

This selectivity is one reason that antiarrhythmic drug classification (Vaughan Williams classification) is so important clinically — different arrhythmias arise from different cell types with different action potential mechanisms, and the drugs that target each are accordingly different.

_The Ca²⁺-driven upstroke in other contexts._ Ca²⁺ as the upstroke driver is not exclusive to cardiac pacemaker cells. Smooth muscle action potentials (where they occur) are typically Ca²⁺-driven. Some neuronal action potentials in dendrites are also Ca²⁺-driven. The unifying lesson: the ion that drives the upstroke is determined by which voltage-gated channel is most abundant in that cell — Na⁺ for fast neuronal/skeletal/atrial-ventricular cardiac; Ca²⁺ for slow SA/AV nodal and smooth muscle.

Elaborative explanation: This comparison illustrates how the same architectural framework (an action potential as a voltage-dependent regenerative response) can be implemented with different ions, producing different functional properties. The cardiac SA node's slow-response architecture is not a bug — it is a feature. Slow upstroke means slow conduction; slow conduction at the AV node means atrioventricular delay; AV delay means coordinated atrial-then-ventricular contraction; coordinated contraction means efficient cardiac pumping. Similarly, spontaneous diastolic depolarisation in pacemaker cells means automaticity; automaticity means the heart doesn't need external pacing. Recognising the design logic of each tissue's action potential mechanism is essential for cardiac and smooth muscle physiology.

Hints:

1. Two action potential types, two different upstroke ions.
2. The fast type uses the same ion as in nerves; the slow type uses a different ion.
3. The slow type allows for things the fast type cannot (e.g., spontaneous firing).

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies Na⁺ via voltage-gated Na⁺ channels as the fast-response upstroke driver.
- Identifies Ca²⁺ via L-type voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels as the slow-response upstroke driver.
- Notes the kinetic difference (Ca²⁺ channel activation is slower).
- Connects the slow-response architecture to functional advantages (slow conduction at AV node, automaticity in pacemaker cells).
- Optional: mentions pharmacological selectivity (Na⁺ blockers vs Ca²⁺ blockers).

A Yellow answer correctly identifies the two ions but does not articulate the functional consequences (slow conduction, AV delay, automaticity).

A Red answer claims both action potentials are driven by the same ion, or attributes pacemaker automaticity to a different mechanism (e.g., spontaneous Na⁺ channel activation).

Stem: Both _hyperkalaemia_ (K⁺ 6.5 mmol/L) and _hypokalaemia_ (K⁺ 2.8 mmol/L) impair excitability and produce muscle weakness — but through opposite mechanisms. Compare the two scenarios in terms of effect on RMP, mechanism of impaired excitability, and characteristic ECG changes.

Model answer: Both extremes of plasma K⁺ disturb cellular excitability through Nernst-equation-driven changes in EK and RMP — but the direction and the dominant mechanism differ [Costanzo ch.1].

_Hyperkalaemia (K⁺ 6.5 mmol/L)._

_Effect on RMP._ Higher Ke reduces the K⁺ gradient. EK becomes less negative (~−80 to −85 mV vs normal −95 mV). RMP follows EK and depolarises (becomes less negative).

_Mechanism of impaired excitability._ Sustained depolarisation closes the inactivation gates on voltage-gated Na⁺ channels. With most channels inactivated, the cell cannot mount a normal action potential upstroke even if threshold is reached. _Excitability is reduced because too few Na⁺ channels are available._

_ECG changes (in approximate order of severity)._

- _Peaked, narrow T waves_ (the earliest and most characteristic finding) — reflects accelerated repolarisation.
- _Shortened QT interval_ (early).
- _PR prolongation_ — slowed atrioventricular conduction.
- _Loss of P waves_ — sinoatrial node failure to depolarise.
- _Widened QRS complex_ — slowed intraventricular conduction.
- _"Sine wave" pattern_ — fusion of widened QRS into the T wave.
- _Ventricular fibrillation or asystole_ — terminal event.

_Hypokalaemia (K⁺ 2.8 mmol/L)._

_Effect on RMP._ Lower Ke increases the K⁺ gradient. EK becomes more negative (~−100 to −110 mV vs normal −95 mV). RMP follows EK and hyperpolarises (becomes more negative).

_Mechanism of impaired excitability._ The wider gap between RMP and threshold means more depolarisation is required to fire an action potential. Additional effects: low cellular K⁺ slows voltage-gated K⁺ channel function, prolonging repolarisation; cardiac action potential is lengthened. _Excitability is reduced because the threshold is harder to reach, and repolarisation is delayed._

_ECG changes._

- _Flattened or inverted T waves._
- _Prominent U waves_ (small wave following the T wave; reflects delayed repolarisation of Purkinje fibres / late ventricular tissue).
- _Prolonged QT interval_ — actually a "QU" interval as the U wave fuses with the T wave; reflects delayed repolarisation.
- _ST segment depression._
- _Increased risk of arrhythmias_, particularly torsades de pointes (polymorphic ventricular tachycardia secondary to QT prolongation), atrial and ventricular premature beats.

_The two scenarios compared._

| Feature            | Hyperkalaemia                          | Hypokalaemia                                           |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| EK                 | Less negative                          | More negative                                          |
| RMP                | Depolarised                            | Hyperpolarised                                         |
| Mechanism          | Na⁺ channel inactivation               | Wider RMP-threshold gap; slowed repolarisation         |
| Characteristic ECG | Peaked T waves; widened QRS; sine wave | U waves; flat/inverted T; prolonged QT                 |
| Arrhythmia type    | Bradyarrhythmias; asystole             | Tachyarrhythmias; torsades de pointes                  |
| Clinical urgency   | Life-threatening                       | Life-threatening (especially with QT-prolonging drugs) |

_The unifying lesson._ Both extremes of plasma K⁺ produce excitability dysfunction — but through different mechanisms with different ECG signatures and different arrhythmia risks. The body normally maintains plasma K⁺ within a narrow range (3.5–5.3 mmol/L) precisely because deviations in either direction are dangerous. The clinical management of K⁺ derangements requires identifying which direction is the problem (the ECG changes give immediate clues) and treating accordingly.

_Treatment principles._

For hyperkalaemia: shift K⁺ into cells (insulin + glucose; β2-agonists), remove K⁺ from body (loop diuretics, K⁺-binding resins, dialysis), stabilise membranes (IV calcium gluconate to raise the threshold and partially counteract Na⁺ channel inactivation).

For hypokalaemia: replace K⁺ orally or intravenously (slowly to avoid acute hyperkalaemia); replace concurrent magnesium if low (Mg²⁺ deficiency causes refractory hypokalaemia by impairing K⁺ retention); treat the underlying cause (review diuretic indication; consider K⁺-sparing diuretic).

Elaborative explanation: This question reinforces the general principle that a regulated variable can fail in either direction, with predictable mechanistic and clinical consequences. The same logic applies to other regulated variables — Na⁺, Ca²⁺, pH. Recognising the two extremes pattern is one of the most useful diagnostic frameworks in clinical practice. Both hyperkalaemia and hypokalaemia are classic CBME competencies, and the underlying mechanisms (Na⁺ channel inactivation; RMP-threshold gap) are foundational physiology that ties together excitability, the Nernst equation, and clinical electrolyte derangement.

Hints:

1. Both extremes affect excitability, but through opposite changes in RMP.
2. Hyperkalaemia depolarises the cell; hypokalaemia hyperpolarises it.
3. The mechanism of weakness differs: one is about channel availability; the other is about the gap to threshold.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Optional: includes treatment principles or the unifying "two extremes" lesson.

A Yellow answer covers both scenarios at a basic level but does not clearly distinguish the two mechanisms of impaired excitability, or does not name the characteristic ECG changes for each.

Stem: Drawing on transport, RMP, and action potential physiology, explain the cellular consequences of severe ATP depletion (e.g., during prolonged ischaemia). Trace the cascade from pump failure through gradient collapse, loss of excitability, Ca²⁺ overload, and ultimately cell death.

Model answer: Severe ATP depletion produces a stereotyped cellular collapse — the _ischaemic cellular cascade_ — that integrates transport physiology, ionic gradients, and excitability [Costanzo ch.1; Berne ch.1].

_Step 1 — Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase failure._ The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase consumes 25–40% of resting cellular ATP. When ATP falls (within minutes of significant ischaemia), the pump's activity falls in proportion. Active extrusion of Na⁺ slows; active import of K⁺ slows.

_Step 2 — Ionic gradients collapse._ Without the pump, Na⁺ leaks slowly into the cell through background channels; K⁺ leaks slowly out. Over minutes to tens of minutes (depending on tissue and severity), the steep gradients (intracellular Na⁺ ~14 mmol/L, K⁺ ~120 mmol/L) begin to dissipate. Intracellular Na⁺ rises; intracellular K⁺ falls; extracellular K⁺ also rises (locally), which has additional consequences (see below).

_Step 3 — RMP depolarises._ As EK becomes less negative (because Ki/Ke decreases — both because Ki falls and Ke rises), the RMP follows EK and depolarises. The cell loses its negative resting potential.

_Step 4 — Excitability fails._ Sustained depolarisation closes Na⁺ channel inactivation gates (the same mechanism as in hyperkalaemia). Even if the membrane reaches threshold, no Na⁺ channels are available to fire an action potential. Neurons cannot transmit signals; cardiac myocytes cannot contract; smooth muscle cannot maintain tone.

_Step 5 — Secondary active transport collapses._ The Na⁺ gradient that powers Na⁺-coupled symporters (SGLT, Na⁺-amino acid carriers, NHE-1) is now flat. Glucose uptake fails. Amino acid uptake fails. pH regulation via NHE-1 fails. The cell becomes acidotic from continued anaerobic metabolism (lactate accumulation) without effective H⁺ extrusion.

_Step 6 — Ca²⁺ overload._ Three Ca²⁺-extrusion mechanisms fail simultaneously:

- _PMCA_ (plasma membrane Ca²⁺-ATPase) cannot pump without ATP.
- _SERCA_ (sarco/endoplasmic reticulum Ca²⁺-ATPase) cannot pump without ATP.
- _NCX_ (Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger) cannot extrude Ca²⁺ because the Na⁺ gradient is gone; under conditions of high intracellular Na⁺ and depolarised membrane potential, NCX may even _reverse_ — bringing Ca²⁺ in instead of out.

Cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ rises explosively — orders of magnitude. This is the critical step in ischaemic cellular damage.

_Step 7 — Ca²⁺-activated damage._ Elevated cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ activates several damaging enzymes:

- _Phospholipases_ (especially PLA₂) — damage membrane lipids; release arachidonic acid for inflammatory mediators; cause membrane permeability changes.
- _Proteases_ (calpains) — cleave cytoskeletal and other proteins; release reactive oxygen species.
- _Endonucleases_ — fragment DNA, contributing to apoptotic / necrotic cell death.

_Step 8 — Cell volume failure._ Without Na⁺ extrusion, intracellular Na⁺ rises and water follows osmotically. Cells _swell_ (cellular oedema). Severe swelling damages membranes physically (necrotic blebbing).

_Step 9 — Mitochondrial damage._ Ca²⁺ overload also affects mitochondria. The mitochondrial permeability transition pore opens, causing depolarisation of the inner mitochondrial membrane, swelling, and release of cytochrome c. This commits the cell to apoptosis (or necrosis if energy depletion is severe enough that orderly apoptosis cannot proceed).

_Step 10 — Cell death._ If reperfusion does not occur within a critical window (varies by tissue: brain ~5 min, heart ~30 min, kidney several hours), the cell dies. Reperfusion can paradoxically cause additional damage ("reperfusion injury") through reactive oxygen species generation and Ca²⁺ overload from the suddenly-restored Na⁺ gradient.

_Therapeutic implications._

_Restoring ATP rapidly_ is the single most important intervention. In stroke and MI, this means restoring blood flow (thrombolysis, mechanical thrombectomy, percutaneous coronary intervention). The earlier the better — every minute of ischaemia produces additional cell death.

_Reducing metabolic demand_ during ischaemia can extend survival time. Hypothermia (e.g., post-cardiac-arrest cooling) slows metabolism and reduces ATP consumption. Some tissues benefit from glucose/insulin support to maintain anaerobic metabolism temporarily.

_Limiting Ca²⁺ overload._ Preventing or attenuating intracellular Ca²⁺ rise — for example, with calcium-channel antagonists in some settings — has been investigated but with limited clinical success.

_The unifying lesson._ The cellular ischaemic cascade illustrates how _all_ major cellular functions ultimately depend on ATP. The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase is the fulcrum: its failure brings down ionic gradients, excitability, secondary active transport, Ca²⁺ regulation, and cell volume in parallel. Recognising this dependency is essential for understanding ischaemic disease and the rationale for early reperfusion therapy.

Elaborative explanation: This integrative question ties together transport physiology, RMP and action potentials, secondary active transport, and cellular pathology. The student who can decompose ischaemic damage in this way demonstrates mastery of the chapter — recognising that excitability, transport, and cell survival are not separate topics but a single integrated system that fails in parallel when ATP is lost. The ischaemic cascade is the molecular substrate of stroke, myocardial infarction, acute kidney injury, and many other clinical syndromes; understanding it links cellular physiology directly to bedside clinical reasoning.

Hints:

1. ATP failure has a single primary target — which transporter?
2. Trace the cascade: pump → gradients → excitability → secondary active transport → Ca²⁺ → damage.
3. Why does Ca²⁺ overload occur? Three Ca²⁺-extrusion mechanisms must all fail.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase as the primary target of ATP depletion.
- Traces the chain: pump failure → ionic gradient collapse → RMP depolarisation → Na⁺ channel inactivation → loss of excitability.
- Explains the parallel failure of secondary active transport (SGLT, NHE, NCX).
