- "Highest in venules" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Velocity, flow, and cross-sectional area**:

- Volumetric flow (Q) is constant through serial vessels (continuity).
- Velocity (v) = flow ÷ cross-sectional area (v = Q / A).
- Therefore: velocity is **inversely proportional** to total cross-sectional area at each level.

**Velocities at different levels**:

- **Aorta** — Highest velocity (~30–40 cm/s); smallest total CSA (~3–5 cm²).
- **Arterioles** — Velocity falls (cross-sectional area sums up).
- **Capillaries** — Lowest velocity (~0.05 cm/s); largest total CSA (~5,000 cm²); allows time for exchange (diffusion of O₂, CO₂, glucose, amino acids, fluid).
- **Venules / veins** — Velocity rises again as vessels merge.
- **Venae cavae** — High velocity (~10–15 cm/s); intermediate CSA.

**Clinical relevance**:

- Slow capillary flow optimises diffusion.
- High aortic velocity → potential for turbulence (Reynolds number) and audible bruit / murmur.

(Berne & Levy, 7e; Costanzo, 7e — Ch.4.)

Correct answer: 2000 — Re = ρvD/η (density × velocity × diameter / viscosity); above ~2000 flow becomes turbulent → audible bruit/murmur, higher resistance, energy loss; turbulence promoted by ↑ velocity (anaemia, AS, AVF), ↓ viscosity (anaemia), large diameter, irregular surfaces (atherosclerosis), bifurcations

Explanation: **Reynolds number and turbulent flow**:

**Equation**: Re = ρ × v × D / η

- ρ = blood density.
- v = velocity.
- D = vessel diameter.
- η = blood viscosity.

**Threshold**:

- Re <2000: laminar (parabolic velocity profile, layers slide smoothly, audible silence).
- Re >2000: turbulent (eddies, energy loss, audible bruit / murmur).

**Conditions favouring turbulence (Re ↑)**:

- ↑ Velocity — High flow states: exercise, AVF, aortic regurgitation, anaemia, severe AS (high-velocity jet).
- ↓ Viscosity — Anaemia (low haematocrit reduces viscosity).
- ↑ Diameter — Aneurysm, post-stenotic dilation.
- Irregular surface — Atherosclerotic plaques.
- Bifurcations and branch points.

**Clinical correlations**:

- **Aortic stenosis** — High-velocity jet through narrowed orifice → turbulent ejection murmur radiating to carotids.
- **Anaemia** — Increased CO + low viscosity → flow murmur (functional, "innocent" murmur).
- **Carotid bruit** — Turbulent flow at stenosis.

**Korotkoff sounds** during BP measurement also reflect turbulent flow during cuff deflation.

Explanation: **Local autoregulation of blood flow**:

**Definition**: The ability of a vascular bed to maintain relatively constant blood flow despite changes in perfusion pressure.

**Range**: MAP ~60–150 mmHg in most tissues (cerebral, coronary, renal); shifted right in chronic hypertension.

**Mechanisms**:

1. **Myogenic (Bayliss) response** — Vascular smooth muscle constricts in response to stretch (rising BP) and relaxes when distending pressure falls. Mediated by stretch-activated cation channels and Ca²⁺ entry.

- **Brain** — PaCO₂ dominant; very tight autoregulation.
- **Heart** — Adenosine and metabolic dominant; high baseline O₂ extraction → flow-dependent.
- **Kidney** — Myogenic + tubuloglomerular feedback.
- **Skeletal muscle (active)** — Metabolic dominant; functional sympatholysis.

**Failure of autoregulation**:

- Below ~60 mmHg MAP — flow falls linearly with pressure → ischaemia.
- Above ~150 mmHg MAP — breakthrough vasodilation, vasogenic oedema (PRES).
- Disrupted in stroke penumbra, severe hypertension, sepsis.

Explanation: **Endothelium-derived vasodilators**:

**NO** (chief mediator):

- Synthesised from L-arginine + O₂ by **eNOS** (endothelial NO synthase) with cofactor BH4.
- Stimulated by shear stress, ACh (M3), bradykinin, histamine, ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺.
- Diffuses to smooth muscle → activates soluble guanylyl cyclase → cGMP → PKG → ↓ Ca²⁺, K⁺ channel opening → vasodilation.
- Also antiplatelet and anti-leukocyte adhesion.
- Half-life seconds (rapidly inactivated by haemoglobin and reactive oxygen species).

**Prostacyclin (PGI₂)**:

- Endothelial COX-2 product.
- Activates Gs → cAMP → PKA → vasodilation.
- Antiplatelet (counterbalances thromboxane A₂ from platelets — basis of aspirin's selective antiplatelet effect at low dose).

**EDHF (endothelium-derived hyperpolarising factor)**:

- Heterogeneous (epoxyeicosatrienoic acids, K⁺, H₂O₂, gap-junction signal).
- Hyperpolarises smooth muscle via Ca²⁺-activated K⁺ channel opening.
- Important in resistance vessels and microcirculation.

**Endothelial dysfunction**:

- Reduced NO bioavailability (oxidative stress; uncoupled eNOS) is central to atherosclerosis, HTN, diabetic microvascular complications, septic vasodilation paradox.
- Detected by impaired flow-mediated dilation (FMD).

**Pharmacology (NO/cGMP pathway)**:

- **Nitrates** — supply exogenous NO.
- **PDE5 inhibitors** (sildenafil) — prevent cGMP breakdown.
- **sGC stimulators** (riociguat) — direct sGC activation.
- **Statins** — pleiotropic ↑ NO bioavailability.

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.20; Costanzo, 7e.)

Stem: The Starling forces governing capillary fluid exchange are summarised by the equation:

Correct answer: Net filtration = K_f × [(P_c - P_i) - σ(π_c - π_i)]; capillary hydrostatic (drives filtration), interstitial hydrostatic (opposes), plasma oncotic (opposes filtration), interstitial oncotic (drives filtration); σ = reflection coefficient (1 in tight capillaries, near 0 in leaky)

- "Net filtration = K_f × (π_c - π_i)" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Net filtration = P_c alone" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Net filtration = π_c alone" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Starling forces and Starling-Landis equation**:

J_v = K_f × [(P_c − P_i) − σ(π_c − π_i)]

Where:

- **J_v** = net filtration rate.
- **K_f** = filtration coefficient (vessel permeability × surface area).
- **P_c** = capillary hydrostatic pressure (drives filtration outward).
- **P_i** = interstitial hydrostatic pressure (~ −1 to 0 mmHg; opposes outward filtration when negative, becoming favouring once tissue is oedematous).
- **π_c** = plasma colloid osmotic pressure (~25–28 mmHg; opposes filtration / favours reabsorption).
- **π_i** = interstitial colloid osmotic pressure (~3–8 mmHg; favours filtration).
- **σ** = reflection coefficient (1 = impermeable to protein → tight capillary; 0 = freely permeable → ineffective oncotic gradient).

**Modern revision (Levick & Michel)**:

- Most capillaries filter throughout their length under normal conditions; reabsorption mainly occurs in venules and through the **endothelial glycocalyx** (subglycocalyx oncotic pressure is the relevant value, not interstitial).
- Net excess filtration is returned to circulation by lymphatics (~2–4 L/day).

**Capillary hydrostatic gradient**:

- Arteriolar end: P_c ~32 mmHg → favours filtration.
- Venular end: P_c ~15 mmHg → reduced filtration; some net reabsorption in older models.

**Plasma oncotic pressure**: ~25–28 mmHg; principally from **albumin** (~70%); globulins contribute remainder.

(Costanzo, 7e — Ch.4.)

Explanation: **Blood-brain barrier (BBB)**:

**Structural elements**:

- **Endothelial cells** with extensive tight junctions (claudins, occludin, ZO proteins).
- **No fenestrations**.
- **Few pinocytotic vesicles**.
- **Pericytes** wrapping the endothelium.
- **Astrocyte foot processes** ensheathing the capillary.
- Thicker basement membrane.

**Selective transport**:

- **GLUT1** for glucose.
- **L-system** for large neutral amino acids.
- **Specific transporters** for ions, vitamins, nucleosides.
- **P-glycoprotein** and **MRP** efflux pumps actively pump out many xenobiotics.

**Permeability**:

- **Lipid-soluble** molecules (anaesthetics, ethanol, caffeine, nicotine, steroids) cross freely.
- **Water-soluble** molecules generally excluded unless transported.
- Chemotherapeutic drugs often poorly cross BBB → CNS sanctuary for tumours; intrathecal therapy needed for some.

**Circumventricular organs (CVOs)**:

- Areas with **leaky** BBB (fenestrated capillaries):
  - Area postrema (chemoreceptor trigger zone for vomiting).
  - Subfornical organ, OVLT (osmoregulation, thirst).
  - Median eminence (hypothalamic-pituitary connection).
  - Pineal gland.
- Allow brain to sense blood composition and release hormones to circulation.

**BBB disruption**:

- Stroke (within hours; vasogenic oedema).
