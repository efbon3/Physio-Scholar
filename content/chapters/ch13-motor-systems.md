## Pass 1 — Spinal motor control: motor units, reflexes, CPGs, UMN vs LMN

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Sherrington's "final common pathway" for motor output to skeletal muscle refers to:

Correct answer: The α-motor neuron in the ventral horn of the spinal cord (or in motor cranial nerve nuclei) — the ultimate target of all descending and reflex inputs onto skeletal muscle, integrating ~10,000–200,000 presynaptic terminals to produce a frequency-coded command via the neuromuscular junction.

Distractors:

- "The corticospinal tract." — Reveals misconception: student picks the descending pathway that drives the final common pathway. Correction: the corticospinal tract is one of many inputs _to_ the α-motor neuron; the α-motor neuron itself is the final common pathway.
- "The cerebellum." — Reveals misconception: student picks a coordinating structure. Correction: the cerebellum modulates motor output via thalamus and brainstem nuclei but does not directly innervate skeletal muscle; the α-motor neuron does.
- "The basal ganglia." — Reveals misconception: student picks an action-selection structure. Correction: basal ganglia gate movement via thalamocortical loops; they do not innervate skeletal muscle directly. The final common pathway remains the α-motor neuron.

Explanation: The α-motor neuron is the only output route from the CNS to skeletal muscle [Guyton & Hall ch.55]. Approximately 10,000–200,000 presynaptic terminals converge on it from corticospinal, rubrospinal, vestibulospinal, reticulospinal, propriospinal, and sensory afferent sources, plus inhibitory interneurons. The α-motor neuron integrates this input and produces a frequency-coded command via its NMJ to muscle. Loss of α-motor neurons (poliomyelitis, ALS) produces flaccid paralysis with atrophy and fasciculations — distinguishable from upper motor neuron lesions (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign).

Hints:

1. The pathway must be the _last_ neuron before skeletal muscle.
2. It receives inputs from many descending and reflex sources.
3. Loss of this neuron produces flaccid paralysis with atrophy.

Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F

Stem: The Henneman size principle states that during graded contraction, motor units are recruited in order of:

Correct answer: Increasing size — small slow Type I (fatigue-resistant) motor units recruited first, larger fast Type IIa next, and largest Type IIx motor units last as more force is required; force grading uses both recruitment (more units) and rate coding (higher firing frequency).

Distractors:

- "Random." — Reveals misconception: student denies an orderly recruitment principle. Correction: motor units are recruited in a highly orderly fashion based on size; this is not random.
- "Largest first." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the principle. Correction: small motor units are recruited _first_ because they have higher input resistance and lower threshold; large units are recruited last for high-force tasks.
- "Reverse order during fatigue." — Reveals misconception: student picks an exception that does not apply. Correction: with sustained submaximal effort, fatiguing units may be replaced by other small units (rotation), but the size principle's basic recruitment order is preserved during graded contraction.

Explanation: Small α-motor neurons have higher input resistance and lower threshold, so they recruit first at any given synaptic drive [Guyton & Hall ch.55]. As force increases, progressively larger and faster motor units are recruited. The advantage is that most-used low-force tasks (postural, fine control) are handled by fatigue-resistant Type I units, while rapid-fatiguing Type IIx units are spared for occasional bursts. Force grading combines recruitment (more motor units) and rate coding (higher firing rates within recruited units). The principle is robust across muscles and is one of the bedrock organising rules of motor physiology.

Hints:

1. Recruitment order is orderly, not random.
2. Small precedes large because of input resistance.
3. Type I units come first; Type IIx units last.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The myotatic (stretch) reflex — exemplified by the patellar tendon tap — is best described as:

Correct answer: A monosynaptic reflex with Ia afferent → α-motor neuron in the spinal cord → quadriceps contraction (latency ~30 ms), with concurrent Ia inhibitory interneuron activation producing reciprocal inhibition of antagonist muscles; tests the integrity of L2–L4 segments.

Distractors:

- "Polysynaptic pathway with multiple interneurons." — Reveals misconception: student treats the stretch reflex as polysynaptic. Correction: the myotatic reflex is the prototypical _monosynaptic_ reflex — Ia afferent synapses directly on the α-motor neuron of the same muscle.
- "Cortical processing." — Reveals misconception: student involves the cortex. Correction: the stretch reflex is purely spinal; it operates without cortical input, although descending pathways modulate its gain.
- "Cerebellar timing." — Reveals misconception: student involves the cerebellum. Correction: the cerebellum modulates voluntary movement and reflex gain but is not part of the basic reflex arc.

Explanation: The stretch reflex is the simplest reflex with the minimal neural circuit [Guyton & Hall ch.55; Berne & Levy ch.9]. Pathway: muscle spindle stretch → Ia afferent → ventral horn → α-motor neuron of the same muscle → muscle contraction. The brevity (~30 ms) makes it useful clinically — areflexia indicates peripheral nerve, dorsal root, ventral root, motor neuron, or muscle disease, while hyperreflexia indicates UMN lesion (loss of cortical inhibition). The test concurrently activates Ia inhibitory interneurons that inhibit the antagonist muscle (hamstrings during a knee-jerk) — reciprocal inhibition that allows clean limb movement.

Hints:

1. Only one synapse separates afferent from motor output.
2. The reflex tests several spinal segments at once.
3. Reciprocal inhibition of antagonist muscles is part of the same circuit.

Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The Golgi tendon organ reflex (inverse stretch reflex) involves:

Correct answer: Activation of Ib afferents from GTOs by muscle tension → inhibitory interneurons in the spinal cord → inhibition of homonymous α-motor neurons (autogenic inhibition) → muscle relaxes; protects against excessive force and underlies the clasp-knife phenomenon in spasticity.

Distractors:

- "Excitation of homonymous motor neurons." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the sign of the reflex. Correction: the GTO reflex is _inhibitory_ on its own muscle — autogenic inhibition; excitation of homonymous motor neurons is the spindle stretch reflex.
- "Direct cortical inhibition." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cortical mechanism. Correction: the GTO reflex is spinal, mediated by Ib afferents and inhibitory interneurons; cortical input is not required.
- "Cerebellar feedback." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cerebellar mechanism. Correction: the cerebellum modulates motor output but the inverse stretch reflex is a spinal Ib reflex, not a cerebellar feedback.

Explanation: The GTO senses muscle force in series with the tendon [Guyton & Hall ch.55]. When tension becomes high, Ib afferents fire, activating inhibitory interneurons that inhibit the homonymous α-motor neurons, and the muscle relaxes — protecting against tendon rupture and excessive force. The clasp-knife phenomenon in spasticity demonstrates this in slow motion: passive stretch initially encounters resistance (hyperactive spindle stretch reflex), then suddenly gives way as GTO inhibition kicks in — like a folding penknife. The reflex is modulated by descending pathways during voluntary movement.

Hints:

1. The GTO sits in series with muscle, not in parallel.
2. The reflex is inhibitory on its own muscle.
3. In spasticity, the giving-way at high stretch is the GTO doing its job.

Type: application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The flexor (withdrawal) reflex with crossed extension:

Correct answer: A polysynaptic reflex in which a nociceptive afferent from one limb activates ipsilateral flexors and inhibits ipsilateral extensors, while the crossed limb shows the opposite pattern — extensor activation and flexor inhibition — preserving posture during withdrawal (e.g., stepping on a tack pulls up the affected leg while the other leg supports body weight).

Distractors:

- "Affects only the stimulated limb." — Reveals misconception: student denies the crossed component. Correction: the crossed extension is essential for postural support during withdrawal; both ipsilateral and contralateral patterns are part of the same reflex.
- "Always inhibits all extensors." — Reveals misconception: student misses the contralateral facilitation. Correction: ipsilateral extensors are inhibited but contralateral extensors are _facilitated_ to support body weight.
- "Requires cortical control." — Reveals misconception: student adds cortical input. Correction: the crossed extension reflex operates without cortical input but is modulated by descending pathways during voluntary actions.

Explanation: The flexor withdrawal with crossed extension is a beautiful example of integrated spinal reflex [Guyton & Hall ch.55]. Nociceptive input drives multiple interneurons that activate ipsilateral flexors and inhibit ipsilateral extensors while doing the opposite on the contralateral side, preventing fall during withdrawal. Latency is ~50–100 ms because the circuit is polysynaptic. The reflex operates without cortical input but is modulated by descending pathways during voluntary action, and shares circuits with the spinal locomotor central pattern generator.

Hints:

1. Withdraw on the painful side, support on the other.
2. The reflex is polysynaptic, not monosynaptic.
3. Posture is maintained by activating the contralateral extensors.

Stem: Renshaw cells in the ventral horn are best described as:

Correct answer: Inhibitory interneurons that receive excitatory collaterals from α-motor neurons and inhibit those same and adjacent α-motor neurons (recurrent inhibition); they use glycine, help stabilise motor output, and synchronise muscle activity.

Distractors:

- "Sensory afferents." — Reveals misconception: student picks an afferent role. Correction: Renshaw cells are interneurons within the spinal cord, not primary sensory afferents from the periphery.
- "Excitatory interneurons." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the sign. Correction: Renshaw cells are _inhibitory_ (glycinergic) — they suppress, not excite, the motor neurons they contact.
- "Glial cells." — Reveals misconception: student denies the neuronal identity. Correction: Renshaw cells are neurons (interneurons), not glia.

Explanation: Renshaw cells receive excitatory axon collaterals from α-motor neurons and fire to inhibit (via glycine) the same and neighbouring α-motor neurons — negative feedback that stabilises motor output [Guyton & Hall ch.55]. Tetanus toxin selectively cleaves SNARE proteins in inhibitory interneurons (including Renshaw and Ia inhibitory interneurons), abolishing inhibition and producing unopposed motor neuron firing → spastic paralysis ("lockjaw" and opisthotonus). Strychnine blocks postsynaptic glycine receptors and produces a similar pattern. The clinical relevance is direct: protecting glycinergic inhibition prevents catastrophic motor over-activity.

Hints:

1. The cell receives a _collateral_ from a motor neuron and feeds back negatively.
2. Its transmitter is glycine, not GABA.
3. Loss of its function (tetanus, strychnine) produces spastic motor disinhibition.

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: γ-Motor neurons innervate:

Correct answer: Intrafusal muscle fibres of muscle spindles — by contracting the polar regions of intrafusal fibres, they "reset" spindle sensitivity during voluntary contraction, preventing the spindle from going slack and stopping its signal; α–γ co-activation keeps spindle sensitivity matched to changing extrafusal length.

Distractors:

- "Extrafusal muscle fibres." — Reveals misconception: student picks the workhorse fibres of muscle. Correction: extrafusal muscle is innervated by α-motor neurons and produces force; γ-motor neurons innervate intrafusal fibres of the spindle.
- "Smooth muscle." — Reveals misconception: student picks visceral muscle. Correction: smooth muscle is innervated by autonomic neurons; γ-motor neurons are somatic and innervate intrafusal fibres.
- "Cardiac muscle." — Reveals misconception: student picks heart muscle. Correction: cardiac muscle is innervated by autonomic fibres modulating intrinsic pacemakers; γ-motor neurons do not innervate heart.

Explanation: γ-motor neurons (smaller and slower than α) project from the ventral horn to intrafusal fibres [Guyton & Hall ch.55; Berne & Levy ch.9]. During voluntary contraction, descending commands activate both α (extrafusal — produce force) and γ (intrafusal — maintain spindle sensitivity) motor neurons — α–γ co-activation. Without γ activation, the spindle would slacken during contraction and stop signalling. The descending input typically contains α–γ co-activation patterns, allowing the spindle to continue reporting deviations even as muscle length changes. Selective loss of γ disrupts spindle signalling and produces ataxic-like impairment of feedback control.

Hints:

1. The motor neurons that innervate spindles are smaller than those innervating extrafusal fibres.
2. They contract the polar ends of intrafusal fibres.
3. Their job is to keep the spindle from going slack during voluntary contraction.

Stem: Central pattern generators (CPGs) for rhythmic activities such as locomotion are located:

Correct answer: Within the spinal cord — networks of interneurons in the lumbar enlargement (legs) and cervical enlargement (arms) that produce alternating flexor/extensor activation patterns even without descending input or sensory feedback, demonstrated in spinal-transected animals; descending and sensory inputs modulate CPG output.

Distractors:

- "In the cerebral cortex only." — Reveals misconception: student locates CPGs in cortex. Correction: cortex _modulates_ locomotion but does not generate the basic rhythmic pattern; the cord can produce stepping after spinal transection.
- "In the cerebellum only." — Reveals misconception: student picks the cerebellum. Correction: the cerebellum coordinates and refines movement but is not the rhythm generator; the rhythm originates in spinal cord interneurons.
- "In the brainstem only." — Reveals misconception: student picks the brainstem. Correction: the brainstem houses CPGs for _respiration_, _swallowing_, and _mastication_, but locomotor CPGs reside in the spinal cord.

Explanation: Sherrington and Graham Brown showed that decerebrate cats can produce rhythmic stepping with treadmill stimulation alone — no cortical or cerebellar input required [Guyton & Hall ch.55; Berne & Levy ch.9]. CPGs in the spinal cord generate the basic rhythmic motor patterns; descending and sensory inputs modulate them. Each pair of CPGs (one for each limb) coordinates with the others through propriospinal interneurons. Clinically, spinal cord injury patients can sometimes regain walking with intensive treadmill training, likely engaging surviving CPG circuits and reorganising remaining descending input — the basis of robot-assisted gait training. Other CPGs include brainstem (respiration, swallowing, mastication), spinal cord (scratching, locomotion), and nucleus retroambiguus (vocalisation).

Hints:

1. The pattern persists after spinal transection, ruling out a cortical generator.
2. Each limb has its own CPG.
3. Descending pathways and sensory feedback shape, but do not generate, the rhythm.

QUESTION 9
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Lower motor neuron (LMN) lesions produce:

Correct answer: Flaccid paralysis with muscle atrophy, fasciculations, fibrillations on EMG, areflexia, and a normal or downgoing plantar reflex — because the affected α-motor neuron is the final common pathway, and its loss removes spinal reflexes' target.

Distractors:

- "Spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign." — Reveals misconception: student gives the _upper_ motor neuron syndrome. Correction: those features characterise UMN lesions; LMN lesions produce flaccidity, atrophy, fasciculations, and areflexia.
- "Mild weakness only." — Reveals misconception: student understates LMN findings. Correction: LMN lesions produce a syndrome with multiple stigmata (atrophy, fasciculations, areflexia), not isolated mild weakness.
- "Cortical sensory loss." — Reveals misconception: student adds a cortical sensory deficit. Correction: LMN lesions affect motor output to muscle; cortical sensory loss is an unrelated cortical phenomenon.

Explanation: LMN syndrome features include flaccidity (no tone, no reflexes), atrophy (rapid denervation atrophy in days to weeks), fasciculations (spontaneous twitches from denervation supersensitivity in muscle), fibrillations on EMG, areflexia (loss of reflex arc target), and absence of Babinski sign (the corticospinal tract is intact in pure LMN lesions, so the plantar response is absent or downgoing) [Guyton & Hall ch.55–56; Ganong]. Causes include motor neuron disease (ALS — also has a UMN component), poliomyelitis, peripheral nerve lesions, radiculopathy, neuromuscular junction disease (myasthenia, LEMS — usually with preserved deep tendon reflexes), and severe myopathies. The contrast with UMN lesions (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski) is the bedrock distinction in localising motor lesions.

Hints:

1. The α-motor neuron is the final common pathway.
2. Its loss removes both voluntary movement and reflex target.
3. Atrophy and fasciculations are hallmarks of denervation.

Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Upper motor neuron (UMN) lesions produce (after the spinal shock period resolves):

Correct answer: Spasticity (velocity-dependent hypertonia, "clasp-knife"), hyperreflexia, Babinski sign (extensor plantar response), clonus, loss of fine motor control, and a "pyramidal pattern" weakness (upper limb extensors and lower limb flexors weaker than antagonists), without atrophy or fasciculations — reflecting loss of cortical inhibition over intact spinal motor neurons.

Distractors:

- "Flaccid paralysis with atrophy." — Reveals misconception: student gives the LMN pattern. Correction: flaccidity and atrophy characterise LMN lesions; UMN lesions produce spasticity and hyperreflexia, with motor neurons themselves intact.
- "Pure motor weakness with normal reflexes." — Reveals misconception: student strips out the reflex changes. Correction: hyperreflexia is a defining UMN feature; normal reflexes do not fit the UMN syndrome.
- "Sensory loss only." — Reveals misconception: student picks a sensory pattern. Correction: UMN lesions are _motor_ — weakness, spasticity, hyperreflexia; sensory loss is a separate finding from sensory pathway lesions.

Explanation: UMN syndrome features (after spinal shock resolves) include spasticity (clasp-knife pattern), hyperreflexia (loss of cortical inhibition over spinal reflexes), Babinski sign (pathognomonic of corticospinal tract dysfunction in adults; normal in infants <1 year), clonus (repetitive rhythmic stretch reflex), loss of fine motor control (distal finger movements particularly), pyramidal pattern weakness, and absence of atrophy or fasciculations [Guyton & Hall ch.55; Ganong]. Causes include stroke, MS, trauma to spinal cord or brain, cerebral palsy, brain tumour, and hereditary spastic paraparesis. **Spinal shock**: acutely after cord transection all reflexes are absent (flaccid) for days to weeks; recovery brings the typical UMN spasticity through neural plasticity restoring reflex function in the absence of descending modulation.

Hints:

1. Reflexes increase, not disappear (after the shock period).
2. The plantar response goes upward in adults.
3. There is no atrophy and no fasciculations because the motor neuron is intact.

## Pass 2 — Descending pathways, motor cortex, brainstem motor systems

QUESTION 11
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The corticospinal (pyramidal) tract:

Correct answer: Originates in motor cortex (Betz cells of M1 layer V, with contributions from S1, premotor, and SMA), descends through the posterior limb of the internal capsule, the cerebral peduncle of the midbrain, the pons, and the pyramid of the medulla, where ~90% decussates at the medullary pyramids to form the lateral corticospinal tract; the remaining ~10% stays uncrossed as the anterior corticospinal tract.

Distractors:

- "Decussates in the spinal cord." — Reveals misconception: student picks the spinothalamic decussation pattern. Correction: corticospinal decussates at the _medullary pyramids_ (caudal medulla); the spinal-cord decussation belongs to the spinothalamic tract.
- "Originates in the brainstem." — Reveals misconception: student picks a brainstem origin. Correction: corticospinal fibres originate in cortex (M1, premotor, SMA, S1); brainstem-origin tracts include rubrospinal, vestibulospinal, reticulospinal, and tectospinal.
- "Stays ipsilateral." — Reveals misconception: student denies decussation. Correction: ~90% of corticospinal fibres cross at the medullary pyramids — explaining contralateral hemiparesis after a unilateral cortical lesion.

Explanation: The corticospinal tract is the major voluntary motor pathway [Guyton & Hall ch.56; Berne & Levy ch.9]. About 30% of fibres synapse directly on α-motor neurons (monosynaptic, supporting fine distal motor control of fingers), and ~70% synapse on interneurons. The lateral CST controls distal limb muscles (especially the hand); the anterior CST controls proximal trunk muscles. Decussation at the medullary pyramids explains why a unilateral cortical lesion produces contralateral hemiparesis. Direct monosynaptic projections to fingertip motor neurons are an evolutionary expansion in primates and underpin the dexterity of the human hand.

Hints:

1. The cortical origin includes M1 plus several other cortical areas.
2. The crossing point is in the medulla, not the cord.
3. The decussated bundle is the _lateral_ corticospinal tract.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: The motor homunculus (in M1, primary motor cortex, precentral gyrus) shows:

Correct answer: Disproportionate representation — hand (especially fingers), face/lips, and tongue have the largest cortical area, while trunk and proximal limbs are small; the map is somatotopic, with face most lateral, hand and arm in the middle, trunk near the vertex, and lower limb most medial (extending into the paracentral lobule).

Distractors:

- "Equal cortical area for each body part." — Reveals misconception: student denies cortical magnification. Correction: motor cortex has highly non-uniform representation reflecting fine motor control needs; equal area is wrong.
- "Random arrangement." — Reveals misconception: student denies somatotopy. Correction: the motor homunculus is precisely somatotopic, mapped systematically by Penfield through cortical stimulation.
- "Same as somatosensory homunculus exactly." — Reveals misconception: student equates the two homunculi. Correction: motor and sensory homunculi are similar but not identical; M1 has expanded representation for hand, lips, and tongue (fine motor control), while S1 is biased toward sensory density.

Explanation: Penfield's mapping of motor cortex (1937) revealed the motor homunculus — disproportions reflecting fine motor control needs (precision pinch with fingertips; fine speech articulation with face and tongue) [Guyton & Hall ch.56]. Movement representation in M1: face laterally, hand and arm in the middle, trunk near vertex, leg medially in paracentral lobule extending onto the medial surface. Adjacent premotor cortex (lateral area 6) serves movement planning, especially externally cued movements; the supplementary motor area (medial area 6) serves internally cued/sequenced movements. Lesions of M1 produce contralateral hemiparesis with characteristic distribution depending on location — face-arm hemiparesis from MCA stroke, leg hemiparesis from ACA stroke, and the classical face-arm-leg pattern from internal capsule stroke.

Hints:

1. The cortex devotes more area to body parts requiring fine motor control.
2. Face is lateral; foot is medial in the paracentral lobule.
3. The representation is large for fingers, lips, and tongue.

Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: The internal capsule is a critical white matter highway because:

Correct answer: It carries corticospinal, corticobulbar (to brainstem), and thalamocortical fibres in a tightly packed region; small lesions (lacunar strokes) of the posterior limb produce dense contralateral hemiparesis ("pure motor stroke") because all motor descending fibres are concentrated there.

Distractors:

- "Carrying only sensory fibres." — Reveals misconception: student narrows the internal capsule to a sensory channel. Correction: the internal capsule carries motor (corticospinal, corticobulbar) plus sensory thalamocortical fibres; it is a mixed white-matter highway.
- "Found in the brainstem." — Reveals misconception: student misplaces the internal capsule. Correction: the internal capsule lies between thalamus medially and basal ganglia laterally, in the cerebral hemisphere — not in the brainstem.
- "Carrying only ascending pathways." — Reveals misconception: student strips out descending traffic. Correction: corticospinal and corticobulbar are _descending_; the internal capsule is bidirectional.

Explanation: The internal capsule lies between the thalamus medially and the basal ganglia laterally [Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Ganong]. The anterior limb carries frontopontine fibres and thalamic projections; the genu carries corticobulbar fibres (face); the posterior limb carries corticospinal fibres (arm anteriorly, leg posteriorly) plus thalamocortical sensory fibres. Lacunar strokes of the internal capsule (small subcortical infarcts in deep penetrating arteries from hypertension and small-vessel disease) produce highly characteristic syndromes: pure motor hemiparesis (most common — contralateral face-arm-leg involvement; no sensory loss; no aphasia), pure sensory stroke (thalamic involvement), sensorimotor stroke (combined), ataxic hemiparesis (cerebellar pathway involvement), and dysarthria-clumsy hand syndrome. MRI confirms small subcortical infarct.

Hints:

1. The structure sits between the thalamus and basal ganglia.
2. Corticospinal, corticobulbar, and thalamocortical fibres all squeeze through.
3. A small lacune here can mimic a large cortical stroke clinically.

QUESTION 14
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The brainstem motor pathways include:

Correct answer: Rubrospinal (red nucleus → contralateral cord; flexor muscles of distal limbs, largely vestigial in primates), reticulospinal (medullary and pontine reticular formation → cord; postural control and locomotion modulation), vestibulospinal (vestibular nuclei → cord; postural reflexes), and tectospinal (superior colliculus → cord; head/eye orientation toward stimuli).

Distractors:

- "None." — Reveals misconception: student denies brainstem motor pathways exist. Correction: multiple brainstem-origin descending motor pathways operate in parallel with corticospinal — these evolved earlier and remain critical for posture, balance, locomotion, and orientation.
- "Only the reticulospinal." — Reveals misconception: student narrows to one pathway. Correction: there are at least four major brainstem motor pathways (rubrospinal, reticulospinal, vestibulospinal, tectospinal), each with distinct functions.
- "Only the corticospinal." — Reveals misconception: student conflates corticospinal with brainstem-origin pathways. Correction: corticospinal originates in cortex; the brainstem-origin pathways are separate and parallel.

Explanation: The brainstem provides multiple parallel descending motor pathways that evolved before the corticospinal tract and remain critical for postural control, balance, locomotion, and basic motor functions [Guyton & Hall ch.56; Berne & Levy ch.9]. Rubrospinal — from red nucleus; influences flexor muscle tone and limb fine motor control; largely vestigial in primates as corticospinal has taken over fine control. Vestibulospinal — lateral (excites antigravity extensors for postural support) and medial (head-neck orientation). Reticulospinal — medullary (inhibits spinal reflex activity) and pontine (excites antigravity extensors). Tectospinal — orients head and eyes to visual or auditory stimuli. These pathways explain decerebrate rigidity (lesion below red nucleus, above vestibular nuclei: unopposed extensor tone, extension of all four limbs) and decorticate rigidity (lesion above red nucleus: flexor posturing of upper limbs from preserved rubrospinal flexor drive, extensor of lower limbs).

Hints:

1. Four major pathways arise from named brainstem nuclei.
2. Three contribute to extensor tone; one controls flexors.
3. Decerebrate and decorticate posturing localise lesions along this axis.

QUESTION 15
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Acute spinal cord transection produces "spinal shock":

Correct answer: A period of complete flaccid areflexia below the lesion lasting days to weeks, thought to result from sudden loss of descending tonic facilitation; with time, neural plasticity restores reflex function so that UMN signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski) gradually develop, and "completeness" of cord injury cannot be accurately assessed until shock resolves.

Distractors:

- "Immediate hyperreflexia." — Reveals misconception: student attributes UMN-syndrome features to acute injury. Correction: acutely, all reflexes below the lesion are _absent_ (spinal shock); hyperreflexia develops weeks later as plasticity restores reflex function without descending modulation.
- "Cortical changes only." — Reveals misconception: student locates the phenomenon in cortex. Correction: spinal shock is a _spinal_ phenomenon — sudden loss of descending facilitation onto cord motor neurons.
- "Permanent flaccidity." — Reveals misconception: student treats shock as the final state. Correction: spinal shock is transient (days to weeks); flaccidity gives way to spasticity with time as the cord adapts.

Hints:

1. Acutely, the cord below the lesion is silent — not hyperactive.
2. The shock resolves over days to weeks.
3. Final assessment of injury completeness must wait until shock has resolved.

Correct answer: Caudate nucleus + putamen (together = striatum) + globus pallidus (internal/external segments) + subthalamic nucleus + substantia nigra (pars compacta and pars reticulata) — interconnected through direct (movement-promoting) and indirect (movement-suppressing) pathways under dopaminergic modulation from substantia nigra pars compacta.

- "Cerebellum and thalamus." — Reveals misconception: student picks coordinating structures rather than basal ganglia. Correction: cerebellum and thalamus are separate from the basal ganglia, though they are part of the broader motor network.
- "Just the substantia nigra." — Reveals misconception: student narrows basal ganglia to one nucleus. Correction: the substantia nigra is one component; the basal ganglia also include striatum, globus pallidus, and subthalamic nucleus.

Explanation: Basal ganglia organisation centres on inputs (mainly to the striatum, from cortex, thalamus, and substantia nigra), an internal circuitry of direct and indirect pathways, and outputs (via GPi/SNr to thalamus and back to cortex) [Berne & Levy ch.10; Guyton & Hall ch.58; Boron & Boulpaep ch.16]. **Direct pathway** (movement-promoting): cortex → striatum → GPi → thalamus → cortex; striatum inhibits GPi, GPi normally inhibits thalamus, so striatal activation disinhibits thalamus → movement. **Indirect pathway** (movement-suppressing): cortex → striatum → GPe → STN → GPi → thalamus → cortex; net effect is more inhibition of thalamus → less movement. **Substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc)** dopamine modulates striatum: D1 receptors activate the direct pathway (excitatory); D2 receptors inhibit the indirect pathway (also activating movement). Loss of indirect pathway components (Huntington's, hemiballismus) → uncontrolled movements (chorea, ballism).

Hints:

1. The striatum (caudate + putamen) is the major input nucleus.
2. Direct pathway promotes movement; indirect pathway suppresses it.
3. Dopamine from SNc tilts the balance toward movement.

- "Essential tremor." — Reveals misconception: student picks a tremor disorder. Correction: essential tremor is rhythmic oscillation, not sustained twisting/posturing; dystonia is sustained or intermittent muscle contraction with abnormal postures.
- "Parkinsonism." — Reveals misconception: student picks a hypokinetic syndrome. Correction: parkinsonism features bradykinesia, rigidity, and resting tremor; dystonia is a hyperkinetic disorder with sustained postures.
- "Cerebellar ataxia." — Reveals misconception: student picks an ataxia syndrome. Correction: cerebellar ataxia presents with uncoordinated movement and gait; dystonia presents with sustained posturing without ataxia.

Explanation: Dystonia is classified as primary (genetic — DYT1 gene encoding torsinA in early-onset generalised dystonia, plus other DYT loci) or secondary (drug-induced from neuroleptic exposure, tardive dystonia, or levodopa; post-stroke or post-traumatic; cervical dystonia/torticollis; blepharospasm; oromandibular dystonia; occupational dystonias such as writer's cramp and musician's dystonia) [Berne & Levy ch.10; Ganong]. Pathophysiology involves abnormal basal ganglia output and sensorimotor integration, with co-contraction of agonist and antagonist muscles. Treatment: botulinum toxin injection is first-line for focal dystonia (cervical, blepharospasm, oromandibular) — temporarily blocks NMJ release of ACh in the targeted muscle; oral options include anticholinergics (trihexyphenidyl, benztropine), baclofen, benzodiazepines, and tetrabenazine; deep brain stimulation (GPi-DBS) is effective for refractory generalised dystonia, especially DYT1; intrathecal baclofen for some severe cases. Dopa-responsive dystonia (Segawa disease) is a critical not-to-miss diagnosis — childhood-onset dystonia with dramatic response to small doses of levodopa.

Hints:

1. The movements are sustained or intermittent twisting postures.
2. Focal forms (cervical, blepharospasm) are common.
3. Botulinum toxin is first-line for focal dystonia.

QUESTION 29
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Essential tremor differs from Parkinsonian tremor in that it is:

Correct answer: A postural and action tremor (worsens with arms outstretched and during voluntary movement) rather than a resting tremor; bilateral and often hand/head; improves with alcohol; treated with propranolol, primidone, gabapentin, or topiramate, and with DBS or focused ultrasound for refractory cases.

Distractors:

- "A unilateral resting tremor that disappears with movement." — Reveals misconception: student gives the Parkinsonian pattern. Correction: that pattern fits Parkinson's tremor; essential tremor is _postural and action_, not resting.
- "Worse with alcohol intake." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the alcohol response. Correction: essential tremor characteristically _improves_ (transiently) with alcohol — a useful diagnostic clue, though not a treatment.
- "Always associated with rigidity and bradykinesia." — Reveals misconception: student includes parkinsonian features. Correction: essential tremor lacks rigidity and bradykinesia; coexistence with parkinsonism suggests a different or evolving diagnosis.

Explanation: Essential tremor is the most common adult movement disorder [Ganong]. It is a postural and action (kinetic) tremor — worse with arms outstretched and during voluntary movement (writing, holding a cup) — and is typically bilateral, involving the hands, head, and sometimes voice. The tremor often improves transiently with alcohol (a useful diagnostic clue but not a treatment recommendation, given alcohol's harms). It contrasts with Parkinsonian tremor, which is a resting tremor that improves with movement, is typically unilateral at onset, and is accompanied by rigidity and bradykinesia. Treatment: first-line oral agents are propranolol (β-blocker) and primidone (anticonvulsant); second-line gabapentin and topiramate. For refractory cases, deep brain stimulation of the ventral intermediate nucleus of the thalamus (VIM-DBS) and MR-guided focused ultrasound thalamotomy provide durable benefit. A genetic predisposition is common (autosomal dominant in many families).

Hints:

1. The tremor occurs during posture and action, not at rest.
2. Alcohol transiently improves the tremor.
3. Propranolol and primidone are first-line drugs.

QUESTION 30
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: A patient develops sudden violent flinging movements of one arm and leg on one side. The most likely lesion is:

Correct answer: A lacunar infarct of the contralateral subthalamic nucleus (STN) producing hemiballismus — the loss of STN excitation of GPi reduces GPi inhibition of the thalamus, releasing involuntary large-amplitude flinging movements; usually improves over weeks and treated with dopamine antagonists or VMAT2 inhibitors.

Distractors:

- "Cerebellar haemorrhage." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cerebellar lesion. Correction: cerebellar lesions cause ataxia and dysmetria, not violent flinging movements; hemiballismus localises to the contralateral STN.
- "Cortical motor stroke." — Reveals misconception: student picks a corticospinal lesion. Correction: cortical motor stroke produces _weakness_ (hemiparesis), not violent flinging movements; hemiballismus is a basal ganglia phenomenon.
- "Spinal cord lesion." — Reveals misconception: student picks a cord lesion. Correction: cord lesions produce paresis and reflex changes, not large-amplitude proximal flinging movements; hemiballismus is supraspinal.

Explanation: Hemiballismus is sudden, violent, large-amplitude flinging movements of one limb or hemibody, usually from a contralateral STN stroke (lacunar infarct in hypertensive patients) [Berne & Levy ch.10; Ganong]. Pathophysiology: the STN normally provides excitatory drive to GPi via the indirect pathway; loss of STN drive reduces GPi inhibition of the thalamus, disinhibiting thalamocortical drive and producing involuntary movement of the contralateral limbs. The disease typically improves over weeks; treatment with dopamine antagonists (haloperidol, risperidone) or VMAT2 inhibitors (tetrabenazine) reduces the movements while spontaneous resolution occurs. The condition illustrates that overactivity of the direct pathway _or_ underactivity of the indirect pathway produces hyperkinetic disorders, while the opposite produces hypokinetic syndromes (Parkinson's).

Hints:

1. The movements are sudden, violent, and proximal, affecting one side of the body.
2. The classical lesion is in the contralateral subthalamic nucleus.
3. Many cases improve over weeks with supportive treatment.

Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 30-year-old woman presents with episodes of subacute weakness, numbness, optic neuritis, and incoordination over several months; MRI shows multiple white-matter plaques. The most likely diagnosis is:

Correct answer: Multiple sclerosis (MS) — a demyelinating disease producing episodes of upper motor neuron weakness, sensory loss, optic neuritis, brainstem features, and cord lesions, with MRI plaques disseminated in space and time; treated acutely with high-dose IV methylprednisolone and long-term with disease-modifying therapies.

Distractors:

- "Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis." — Reveals misconception: student picks a motor neuron disease. Correction: ALS produces UMN+LMN signs without sensory loss, optic neuritis, or relapsing-remitting course; MS fits the clinical pattern described.
- "Parkinson's disease." — Reveals misconception: student picks a hypokinetic disorder. Correction: PD presents with bradykinesia, rigidity, and resting tremor in older adults; MS presents with relapsing UMN/sensory/optic deficits in younger patients.
- "Stroke." — Reveals misconception: student picks an acute vascular event. Correction: stroke is acute, focal, and typically vascular-territorial; MS produces multiple lesions disseminated in space and time, often with optic neuritis as a presenting feature.

Explanation: Multiple sclerosis is a chronic demyelinating disease of the CNS, presenting most often in young adults with relapsing-remitting episodes of UMN weakness, sensory loss, optic neuritis, internuclear ophthalmoplegia (MLF lesion), cerebellar features, bladder dysfunction, and Lhermitte's sign [Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Ganong]. MRI shows multiple white-matter plaques disseminated in space (multiple CNS regions) and time (lesions of different ages, with and without contrast enhancement); CSF shows oligoclonal IgG bands without serum match. Acute relapse is treated with high-dose IV methylprednisolone (and plasma exchange in steroid-refractory cases). Disease-modifying therapies include β-interferons, glatiramer, oral agents (dimethyl fumarate, fingolimod, teriflunomide, cladribine, siponimod, ozanimod), and high-efficacy biologics (natalizumab, ocrelizumab, ofatumumab, alemtuzumab) for active disease. Subtypes include relapsing-remitting (most common), primary progressive, and secondary progressive. Symptomatic management addresses spasticity (baclofen, tizanidine), neuropathic pain (gabapentin, pregabalin), bladder dysfunction, fatigue, and depression.

Hints:

1. Onset is in young adulthood, often female.
2. Lesions are multifocal and accumulate over time on MRI.
3. Optic neuritis is a frequent presenting feature.

QUESTION 32
Type: application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Post-stroke spasticity in the upper limb is best managed by:

Correct answer: A multidisciplinary approach — physical therapy (range of motion, stretching, splinting/casting), oral agents (baclofen, tizanidine, dantrolene, gabapentin), botulinum toxin injection for focal spasticity (commonly elbow flexors, wrist flexors, gastrocnemius), and intrathecal baclofen pump for severe spasticity unresponsive to oral treatment.

Distractors:

- "High-dose IV methylprednisolone." — Reveals misconception: student picks an acute MS-relapse treatment. Correction: methylprednisolone treats acute MS relapse, not post-stroke spasticity, which is managed with PT plus targeted antispasmodics and chemodenervation.
- "Dopamine agonists." — Reveals misconception: student picks a Parkinson's drug. Correction: dopamine agonists treat Parkinson's bradykinesia, not post-stroke spasticity; spasticity management uses GABAergic agents and botulinum toxin.
- "Surgical decompression." — Reveals misconception: student picks an acute neurosurgical option. Correction: surgical decompression treats raised ICP or cord compression in acute stages; chronic spasticity is managed pharmacologically and with rehabilitation.

Explanation: Post-stroke spasticity arises from loss of cortical inhibition over spinal reflex circuits, with hyperactive stretch reflexes producing velocity-dependent hypertonia, often with a "clasp-knife" pattern; if untreated it leads to contractures, pain, hygiene problems, and impaired function [Boron & Boulpaep ch.16; Ganong]. A multidisciplinary approach is the standard. Physical therapy (range of motion, stretching, splinting/casting, serial casting, tone-inhibiting positioning) is the foundation. Oral agents include baclofen (GABA-B agonist), tizanidine (α₂ agonist), dantrolene (muscle-acting on ryanodine receptor), and gabapentin. Botulinum toxin injection is used for focal spasticity (commonly into elbow flexors, wrist flexors, finger flexors, plantarflexors, gastrocnemius), reducing tone for ~3 months; repeated injections help, with simultaneous PT to remodel the joint and muscle. For severe generalised spasticity unresponsive to oral therapy, an intrathecal baclofen pump delivers drug directly into the CSF and can transform function. Selective dorsal rhizotomy is reserved for cerebral palsy in selected children. The overall goal is functional gain (e.g., easier dressing, hygiene, transfers), not just tone reduction.

Hints:

1. The mechanism is loss of cortical inhibition over spinal reflexes.
2. PT plus pharmacology plus chemodenervation is the standard combination.
3. Botulinum toxin is the workhorse for focal limb spasticity.
