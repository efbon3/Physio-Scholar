QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Describe the structure of haemoglobin and explain how its sigmoidal O₂ dissociation curve serves O₂ transport.

Model answer: HbA structure: Tetramer of 2α + 2β globin chains. Each subunit has a haem prosthetic group with Fe²⁺ centre that reversibly binds one O₂ molecule. Total: 4 O₂ per Hb tetramer.

Conformational states:

- T (tense) state — Low O₂ affinity. Fully deoxygenated.
- R (relaxed) state — High O₂ affinity. Oxygenated.
- Cooperative binding: first O₂ binding promotes T→R transition, increasing affinity for subsequent O₂. Hill coefficient ~2.7.

Sigmoidal curve significance:

- Upper plateau (high PO₂, ~60–100 mm Hg): saturation near 100% even with modest PaO₂ variability — robust O₂ loading in lungs.
- Steep middle (~20–60 mm Hg): small PO₂ changes produce large saturation changes — efficient unloading at tissues. P50 ~26.7 mm Hg.
- Lower portion: residual saturation maintains O₂ delivery even at venous PO₂.

Modulators (right shift = decreased affinity, more tissue O₂):

- Increased CO₂ (Bohr).
- Decreased pH (Bohr).
- Increased temperature.
- Increased 2,3-BPG.
- Adapt to active tissues with high metabolism.

Left shift (increased affinity, less tissue O₂):

- Decreased temperature.
- Decreased CO₂, increased pH.
- Decreased 2,3-BPG.
- Carboxyhaemoglobin (CO).
- Methaemoglobin.
- Fetal Hb (essential for placental O₂ transfer).

Pathological:

- Severe hypoxaemia (PaO₂ <60) drops on the steep portion → desaturation accelerates.
- Pulse oximetry reads SaO₂ effectively only at >75% (above ~30 mm Hg PaO₂).
- ABG measures PaO₂ directly.

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.29; Guyton & Hall, 14e, Ch.41.)

QUESTION 2
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Trace the steps from EPO release to mature RBC, explaining marrow regulation of erythropoiesis.

Model answer: 1. Hypoxia (low tissue PO₂) → renal interstitial cells stabilise HIF-α. 2. HIF-1/2 activates EPO gene → EPO secretion. 3. EPO travels to bone marrow. 4. EPO-R activation on erythroid progenitors → JAK2/STAT5 signalling. 5. Proliferation + differentiation at multiple stages:

- HSC → CMP → MEP → BFU-E (early) → CFU-E (late) → proerythroblast.
- Erythroblast stages: basophilic → polychromatic → orthochromatic.

6. Nuclear extrusion during late erythroblast → reticulocyte.
7. Reticulocyte release to blood; matures over 1–2 days.
8. Mature RBC circulates ~120 days; recycled by spleen macrophages.

Required cofactors:

- Iron — Haem synthesis. Dietary intake ~10–20 mg/day, ~10% absorbed. Recycled from senescent RBCs.
- B12 / folate — DNA synthesis.
- Other haematopoietic factors — IL-3, SCF, GM-CSF.

Time course:

- EPO surge (e.g., post-haemorrhage) → reticulocytosis in 3–5 days.
- Full Hb recovery from acute blood loss in ~6–8 weeks (assuming adequate iron).

Pharmacological augmentation:

- Recombinant EPO (epoetin α/β) for CKD anaemia, chemotherapy-induced anaemia.
- Darbepoetin (longer half-life, weekly/biweekly dosing).
- HIF stabilisers (roxadustat, daprodustat) — newer oral options.

Limits to erythropoiesis:

- Iron deficiency (~30% in pregnant women globally).
- B12/folate deficiency.
- Marrow infiltration (leukaemia, metastasis).
- Aplastic anaemia (idiopathic, drugs, toxins).
- CKD (low EPO).
- Anaemia of chronic disease (cytokine-mediated suppression + hepcidin).

(Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.34; Berne & Levy, 7e.)

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe sickle cell disease (HbSS) in detail. What are the major acute and chronic complications, and how is each managed?

Model answer: _Sickle cell disease_ - autosomal recessive haemoglobinopathy. HBB gene mutation (Glu6Val on β chain) -> HbS (β6-Val); homozygous HbSS = sickle cell anaemia; compound heterozygotes - HbSC, HbS-β-thalassaemia. HbS polymerises in deoxygenated state -> RBCs sickle (irreversible after multiple cycles); sickled cells are stiff, less deformable, adhere to endothelium, occlude microvasculature, are destroyed by spleen (extravascular haemolysis). Chronic intravascular and extravascular haemolysis + vaso-occlusion + endothelial dysfunction + chronic inflammation. Sickle trait (HbAS) is asymptomatic with rare exceptions (renal medullary infarction, splenic infarction at high altitude, exercise-induced, contraindication for high-altitude flying without supplemental O2). Trait offers heterozygote advantage against P. falciparum malaria - explains high prevalence in endemic regions. Acute complications: (1) _Vaso-occlusive crisis (VOC) / pain crisis_ - severe pain in bones, limbs, back, chest, abdomen; multifactorial - hypoxia, dehydration, infection, cold, stress, altitude. Pain may be excruciating; the chronic experience of pain shapes life. Management: aggressive opioid analgesia (often need PCA; do not undertreat - opioid stigma is a documented healthcare disparity), hydration (avoid overload), warmth, oxygen if hypoxic, treat any underlying infection. (2) _Acute chest syndrome_ - new pulmonary infiltrate + fever/respiratory symptoms; multifactorial (infection - chlamydia, mycoplasma, viral; fat embolism; pulmonary microvascular sickling; hypoventilation from chest pain). Leading cause of mortality in SCD. Management - empirical antibiotics covering atypicals, oxygen, analgesia, exchange transfusion (rapid removal of HbS; especially severe or rising Hb above ~ 90 with falling SaO2), bronchodilators, incentive spirometry. (3) _Stroke_ - 11% of HbSS by age 20; ischaemic in children, haemorrhagic in adults. Transcranial Doppler (TCD) screening for high cerebral artery velocity (> 200 cm/s) in children identifies high stroke risk. Chronic transfusion programme (every 3-4 weeks to keep HbS < 30%) reduces stroke risk significantly. New treatments may modify paradigm. (4) _Splenic sequestration_ - blood pooled in spleen; rapid drop in Hb, hypovolaemia; emergency transfusion; splenectomy considered if recurrent. (5) _Aplastic crisis_ - parvovirus B19 infection -> transient red cell aplasia; rapid Hb drop without retic rise; supportive transfusion until recovery (typically 7-10 days). (6) _Priapism_ - prolonged painful erection from sickling in cavernous tissue; ischaemic priapism is medical emergency - aspiration, intracavernosal phenylephrine, exchange transfusion. (7) _Acute multi-organ failure_ - severe; ICU support; exchange transfusion. Chronic complications: (1) _Functional asplenia_ - by ~ 5 years from autoinfarction; _risk of overwhelming sepsis_ with encapsulated organisms (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, N. meningitidis, Salmonella - osteomyelitis). Vaccinations + lifelong antibiotic prophylaxis (penicillin V in childhood; some advocate lifelong). (2) _Avascular necrosis_ of femoral and humeral heads - bone infarction; hip/shoulder replacement often needed; pain management. (3) _Retinopathy_ - especially HbSC; proliferative; laser treatment. (4) _Renal_ - hyposthenuria (concentrating defect from inner medullary sickling), papillary necrosis, FSGS, CKD, RCC (medullary type) risk. (5) _Pulmonary hypertension_ - chronic hypoxia, intravascular haemolysis (free Hb scavenges NO), chronic emboli; rising mortality risk (echo screening). (6) _Cholelithiasis_ - pigment stones from chronic haemolysis. (7) _Leg ulcers_ - chronic, painful; hard to heal. (8) _Bone changes_ - dactylitis ("hand-foot syndrome" - first presentation often), osteomyelitis (Salmonella, S. aureus), AVN, osteopenia. (9) _Pregnancy complications_ - higher maternal/fetal mortality; increased crises; transfusion may be needed. (10) _Iron overload_ from chronic transfusion - chelation needed. (11) _Cardiopulmonary_ - cardiomegaly, increased CO from chronic anaemia. (12) _Cognitive impairment_ - silent infarcts, anaemia chronic. (13) _Psychological burden_ - chronic disease, pain, healthcare interactions. Management overview: (1) _Disease-modifying therapy_: (a) _Hydroxyurea_ (the mainstay since 1995) - increases HbF (which doesn't sickle); reduces VOC, ACS, mortality; consider in all symptomatic SCD from infancy; usually well-tolerated. (b) _L-glutamine_ (Endari) - reduces oxidative damage; modest VOC reduction. (c) _Crizanlizumab_ (anti-P-selectin) - reduces VOC. (d) _Voxelotor_ - HbS oxygenation modifier; reduces haemolysis (evidence on clinical events evolving). (e) _Disease-modifying transfusion_ - exchange transfusion for stroke prevention, severe disease, ACS; avoid simple transfusion which raises viscosity. (f) _Curative therapies_: HSC transplant (sibling-matched best; > 85% cure for selected); haploidentical and unrelated donor approaches improving access; _gene therapy_ (FDA 2023 - exa-cel CRISPR-based and lovo-cel gene addition for severe SCD) - landmark advances; access remains limited globally. (g) Splenectomy in selected (recurrent sequestration). (2) _Prevention_: vaccinations (pneumococcal, Hib, meningococcal, influenza, COVID, hepatitis), antibiotic prophylaxis (penicillin V in childhood), folic acid, hydration, avoidance of triggers (cold, dehydration, altitude, hypoxia). (3) _Multidisciplinary care_ - haematologist, primary care, pain specialist, psychologist, social work, dental, transition planning from paediatric to adult care. (4) _Newborn screening_ - heel-prick blood spot in many countries; allows early intervention from infancy. (5) _Genetic counselling_ - autosomal recessive; carrier screening for at-risk populations; pre-conception/prenatal counselling. Outcomes: Median life expectancy in modern care has improved (40s-50s in resource-rich settings; substantially less in low-resource settings). Hydroxyurea reduces mortality. Gene therapy may transform prognosis for those with access. The disease has substantial healthcare disparities globally, and within countries [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: SCD is one of the most studied genetic diseases in medicine. Hydroxyurea has been disease-modifying since 1995. Recent gene therapy approvals are landmark advances. The opioid stigma in SCD is a notable failing of medicine - patients in pain crises are often undertreated due to misplaced concerns about addiction. Healthcare disparities in SCD are profound globally and within countries.

Hints:

1. HbS polymerises in deoxy state - sickling - vaso-occlusion + haemolysis.
2. Acute: VOC, ACS, stroke, sequestration, aplastic crisis, priapism.
3. Chronic: AVN, retinopathy, renal, PH, leg ulcers, asplenia.
4. Hydroxyurea + transfusion + gene therapy.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names HbS molecular defect and polymerisation.
- Lists ≥ 4 acute complications with management.
- Lists ≥ 4 chronic complications.
- Names hydroxyurea as disease-modifying mainstay.
- Mentions HSCT and gene therapy as curative options.

A Yellow answer covers SCD broadly but misses key complications or therapies.

A Red answer treats SCD as just chronic anaemia.

Common misconceptions:

- "Sickle pain is overstated.": Correction: Vaso-occlusive crises are severe; opioid stigma drives undertreatment - a documented healthcare disparity.
- "Hydroxyurea works for everyone.": Correction: Highly effective for most but ~ 25% are non-responders; some refuse for fear of side effects; access limited in low-resource settings.

Stem: Describe iron metabolism in detail. How are absorption, transport, storage, and recycling regulated?

Model answer: Iron is essential for haem synthesis (RBC, mitochondrial cytochromes), DNA synthesis (ribonucleotide reductase), oxygen transport. Total body iron ~ 3-4 g (man) / 2-3 g (woman). Distribution: ~ 70% in haemoglobin, ~ 25% in storage (ferritin/hemosiderin in liver, spleen, marrow), ~ 5% in myoglobin and enzymes. _Daily turnover_ ~ 25 mg (mostly recycling from senescent RBCs by macrophages); intake needs ~ 1-2 mg/day to balance losses (~ 1 mg/day from skin shedding, GI cell turnover, sweat, plus menstrual ~ 10-30 mg/cycle in women). Pregnancy adds ~ 1000 mg over 9 months. _Absorption_: in proximal small intestine (duodenum, upper jejunum). Dietary iron in two forms: (1) _Haem iron_ (~ 30% bioavailable; meat, fish, poultry) - absorbed by haem carrier protein 1 (HCP1) at apical enterocyte membrane; (2) _Non-haem iron_ (~ 5-15% bioavailable; plants, supplements) - reduced from Fe³⁺ to Fe²⁺ by duodenal cytochrome b (DCYTB; vitamin C enhances reduction); transported across apical membrane by divalent metal transporter 1 (DMT1). Inside enterocyte: stored as ferritin (lost when cell sloughs in 2-3 days) OR exported across basolateral membrane by _ferroportin_ (the only known cellular iron exporter); coupled with hephaestin (oxidises Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ for transferrin loading). _Hepcidin_ (peptide hormone made by liver, master regulator) binds ferroportin -> internalisation and degradation -> reduced iron absorption + reduced macrophage iron release. Hepcidin is _upregulated_ by iron stores (BMP6/SMAD signalling), inflammation (IL-6 → STAT3 → hepcidin - explains anaemia of chronic disease/inflammation), HFE/TFR2 signalling. Hepcidin is _downregulated_ by iron deficiency, hypoxia, increased erythropoietic demand (erythroferrone secreted by erythroblasts). Mutations in HFE -> hepcidin deficiency -> hereditary haemochromatosis (HFE mutation, autosomal recessive, common in Northern Europeans). _Transport_: iron loaded onto _transferrin_ (a glycoprotein, binds 2 Fe³⁺ atoms; saturation normally ~ 30%); transferrin saturation = serum Fe / total iron-binding capacity (TIBC). Transferrin distributes iron to all tissues. _Cellular uptake_: via transferrin receptor 1 (TFR1) -> receptor-mediated endocytosis -> endosomal acidification (releases Fe³⁺ from transferrin) -> reduction to Fe²⁺ -> DMT1 transports out of endosome -> incorporated into ferritin (storage), haem (mitochondria - ALA synthase, ferrochelatase), or other proteins. Apo-transferrin recycles to surface. TFR1 is regulated by cellular iron - upregulated when low (more receptors to capture iron), downregulated when high. _Storage_: ferritin (cytoplasmic iron-protein complex; up to 4500 Fe atoms per molecule; soluble; readily mobilised). Hemosiderin (insoluble breakdown of ferritin; iron less readily mobilisable; accumulates in iron overload). Ferritin levels in serum reflect storage iron - clinically useful (low in deficiency, raised in overload, raised in inflammation as acute-phase reactant - context required). _Recycling_: senescent RBCs phagocytosed by splenic and hepatic macrophages -> haem oxygenase releases Fe + biliverdin + CO; iron stored as ferritin or exported via ferroportin to plasma transferrin -> available for new erythropoiesis. ~ 25 mg/day cycles - extremely efficient. _Disorders_: (1) _Iron deficiency_ - blood loss (most common - GI, menstrual), inadequate intake, malabsorption, pregnancy. Microcytic hypochromic anaemia. Investigate cause; treat with oral or IV iron. (2) _Iron overload_ - hereditary (HFE haemochromatosis - C282Y/H63D mutations - most common monogenic disease in Northern Europeans; ~ 1 in 200-300 homozygous; not all develop disease; raised TSAT > 50%, raised ferritin; treat with venesection - simple, effective; phlebotomy until target ferritin, then maintenance), juvenile haemochromatosis (HJV, hepcidin gene mutations; severe), TFR2 mutations; secondary - chronic transfusion (thalassaemia, MDS, sickle cell, AA), African dietary iron overload, alcoholism. Iron deposits in liver (cirrhosis, HCC), heart (dilated cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias), pancreas (diabetes), pituitary (hypogonadism), skin ("bronze diabetes"), joints (arthropathy). Treat - venesection or chelation (deferasirox - oral once daily; deferoxamine - SC infusion overnight; deferiprone). (3) _Anaemia of chronic disease/inflammation_ - hepcidin upregulated by IL-6, blocking iron absorption and macrophage release; functional iron deficiency for erythropoiesis despite adequate stores. Treat underlying cause; iron of limited use unless deficient as well. (4) _Sideroblastic anaemia_ - failure to incorporate iron into haem; ringed sideroblasts on marrow biopsy (Prussian blue); inherited (X-linked - ALAS2) or acquired (alcohol, chloramphenicol, isoniazid, lead, copper deficiency, MDS-RS). Treatment: vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) for some inherited; transfusion + chelation; treat underlying. (5) _Aceruloplasminaemia_ - rare; iron accumulates in brain, liver, pancreas; movement disorder. (6) _Atransferrinaemia_ - rare; iron not transported; severe anaemia + iron overload. Therapeutic frontier: hepcidin agonists (rusfertide for iron overload disorders, polycythaemia vera; in trials), TMPRSS6 inhibitors (raise hepcidin), ferroportin antagonists (block iron release - vamifeport in early trials). Iron metabolism is one of the most beautifully integrated systems - sensors (HFE, TfR2), signal (BMP6/SMAD, IL-6/STAT3, erythroferrone), regulator (hepcidin), executor (ferroportin), storage (ferritin), transport (transferrin) all coordinated. Disorders illustrate failure of specific components [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Iron metabolism is a highly integrated regulatory system. The hepcidin-ferroportin axis is the master regulator. Disorders span deficiency (most common anaemia globally) to overload (hereditary haemochromatosis is the most common monogenic disease in Northern Europeans). Modern iron biology has transformed understanding of anaemia of inflammation and produced therapeutic targets.

Hints:

1. Hepcidin = master regulator; binds ferroportin -> reduced absorption + macrophage release.
2. Transport on transferrin; cellular uptake via TFR1.
3. Recycling from senescent RBCs ~ 25 mg/day.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names DMT1 and HCP1 for absorption.
- Identifies hepcidin and ferroportin as master regulators.
- Distinguishes HFE haemochromatosis (deficient hepcidin) from secondary overload.
- Names anaemia of chronic disease via IL-6/hepcidin axis.
- Mentions venesection vs chelation for iron overload.

A Yellow answer covers iron handling but misses regulation.

A Red answer treats iron metabolism as passive.

Common misconceptions:

- "All iron deficiency is from low intake.": Correction: Most adult iron deficiency is from blood loss; intake matters more in children, pregnancy, vegetarians.
- "Hereditary haemochromatosis is rare.": Correction: It is the most common monogenic disease in Northern Europeans (~ 1 in 200-300 homozygous; many under-diagnosed).

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe haemoglobin structure-function in detail. How does it support O2 transport and CO2/H+ handling?

Model answer: _Haemoglobin_ is a tetramer (~ 64 kDa) of two α and two β chains; HbA = α2β2 (97% of adult Hb). Each globin subunit carries one haem (Fe + protoporphyrin IX); each Hb tetramer binds up to 4 O2. Adult Hb composition: HbA (α2β2, 97%); HbA2 (α2δ2, 2-3%); HbF (α2γ2, < 1%). Fetal: HbF dominant; switch to HbA in first 6 months. Hb concentration: 130-180 g/L (man), 120-160 g/L (woman). RBC contains ~ 250 million Hb molecules; ~ 1 billion O2 binding sites per RBC. _Haem_ - protoporphyrin IX with central Fe²⁺; reversibly binds O2 (oxygenation, not oxidation; Fe stays Fe²⁺). Met-Hb (Fe³⁺) cannot bind O2 - met-Hb reductase recovers. O2 binding induces conformational changes propagated through tetramer (cooperativity). _Cooperativity_ - each O2 binding increases affinity of remaining sites - basis of sigmoidal O2-Hb dissociation curve. Tense (T) state - low affinity, deoxy. Relaxed (R) state - high affinity, oxy. Transition described by allosteric model (Monod-Wyman-Changeux). P50 (PaO2 at which Hb is 50% saturated) = ~ 27 mm Hg in normal adult. Curve shifts: _Right shift_ - reduced affinity (offloads O2 more readily) - increased CO2, H⁺ (Bohr effect), 2,3-BPG (binds in central cavity of deoxy-Hb), temperature, exercise; physiologically appropriate in tissues. _Left shift_ - increased affinity - decreased CO2, H⁺, 2,3-BPG, temperature; HbF (does not bind 2,3-BPG well -> higher O2 affinity facilitates placental transfer); CO binding (very high affinity, stabilises Hb in left-shifted state - shifts curve left and reduces P50). At lung (PaO2 100 mm Hg): Hb ~ 97-98% saturated. At tissues (PvO2 40 mm Hg): Hb ~ 75% saturated; ~ 5 mL O2/100 mL blood released per pass. Vigorous exercise can extract more (PvO2 down to 15-20). O2 capacity = 1.34 × Hb (g/dL) × SaO2 - the dissolved fraction is small (~ 0.3 mL/dL at 100 mm Hg). _CO2 transport_ - three forms: (a) Bicarbonate (~ 70%) - CO2 + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 (catalysed by RBC carbonic anhydrase) ⇌ HCO3⁻ + H⁺; HCO3⁻ exits via Cl⁻/HCO3⁻ exchange (Band 3); H⁺ buffered by deoxyHb (Haldane effect). In tissues, the chloride shift moves Cl⁻ in and HCO3⁻ out; reverses in lungs. (b) Carbamino compounds (~ 23%) - CO2 binds amino terminals of globin; affinity higher in deoxyHb (Haldane). (c) Dissolved (~ 7%). _Haldane effect_ - deoxyHb binds CO2 and H⁺ more readily than oxyHb; supports CO2 uptake in tissues, release in lungs. _Bohr effect_ - increased CO2 / H⁺ shifts curve right - Hb releases O2 more readily; supports O2 delivery to metabolically active tissues. _Buffering_ - Hb is the major intracellular buffer; histidine residues with pKa near physiological pH; deoxyHb is a better buffer than oxyHb (helps in tissues where CO2 generated). Imidazole buffering. _Disorders of haemoglobin_: (1) _Sickle cell_ - HbS β6Glu→Val; polymerises in deoxy state. (2) _Thalassaemia_ - quantitative defect in α or β. (3) _HbE_ - β26Glu→Lys; common SE Asia. (4) _HbC_ - β6Glu→Lys; haemolysis less severe than SS; HbSC milder than SS but with retinopathy. (5) _Methaemoglobinaemia_ - Fe³⁺ instead of Fe²⁺; cannot bind O2; left-shifts remaining haems. Causes: oxidant drugs (dapsone, primaquine, nitrites, lidocaine, sulfonamides), nitrates (well-water nitrates in infants), inherited NADH-cytochrome b5 reductase deficiency, HbM (unstable). Symptoms: cyanosis (chocolate-brown blood, normal PaO2), dyspnoea, headache, fatigue, confusion, seizures at high levels. Treat: discontinue oxidant; methylene blue 1-2 mg/kg IV (reduces Fe³⁺ via NADPH-MetHb reductase); contraindicated in G6PD (use ascorbic acid). (6) _Carboxyhaemoglobinaemia_ - CO binds haem with ~ 200x affinity of O2; functional anaemia + left shift. Sources: smoke, exhaust, faulty heaters, suicide attempts. Symptoms: cherry-red or normal skin (vs blue in hypoxia), headache, confusion, seizures, coma; pulse oximetry falsely normal (cannot distinguish O2-Hb from CO-Hb); arterial co-oximetry needed. Treat: 100% O2 (reduces CO half-life from ~ 5 h to ~ 90 min), hyperbaric O2 in severe (CO half-life ~ 25 min; reduces neurological sequelae). (7) _Cyanide poisoning_ - binds cytochrome oxidase, blocking electron transport; cells cannot use O2 despite normal Hb. Sources: industrial, smoke, amygdalin (laetrile), nitroprusside infusion. Symptoms: rapid; almond breath; bright red venous blood; lactic acidosis. Treat: hydroxocobalamin (binds CO to form cyanocobalamin, B12; preferred), sodium thiosulfate (provides sulphur for rhodanese to detoxify); historically nitrites induce metHb (binds CN⁻) but problematic. (8) _Polycythaemia_ - raised Hb; primary (PV - JAK2 V617F) or secondary (hypoxia from altitude, COPD; tumour - EPO secretion; dehydration); risk of thrombosis. (9) _Anaemia_ - reduced Hb; many causes. Modern haemoglobinopathy diagnosis: Hb electrophoresis, HPLC, isoelectric focusing, genetic testing. Newborn screening for SCD/thalassaemia in many countries. Hb structure-function is one of the most studied paradigms in molecular medicine [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: Hb is a marvellous evolved structure for O2 transport, CO2 carriage, and acid-base buffering. Cooperativity, the Bohr effect, and the Haldane effect together make O2 delivery efficient at lungs and tissues. Disorders span quantitative (thalassaemia), qualitative (sickle, methaemoglobin), and toxic (CO, cyanide). Modern diagnosis and therapy build on this foundation.

Hints:

1. Tetramer α2β2 with cooperative O2 binding.
2. Bohr (CO2/H+ -> right shift) and Haldane (deoxy binds CO2/H+) effects.
3. Curve shifts: 2,3-BPG, temperature, pH, CO/met-Hb.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names Hb tetramer structure with α and β chains.
- Identifies cooperativity, Bohr, and Haldane effects.
- Lists ≥ 3 factors shifting O2-Hb curve right.
- Names CO and metHb effects on Hb function.
- Mentions ≥ 3 disorders of Hb structure or function.

A Yellow answer covers Hb basics but misses Bohr/Haldane or specific disorders.

A Red answer treats Hb as passive O2 carrier.

Common misconceptions:

- "Hb just carries O2.": Correction: Hb also carries ~ 23% of CO2 (carbamino), buffers H⁺, and has cooperative kinetics that match physiological needs.
- "Pulse oximetry detects all Hb dysfunction.": Correction: Pulse oximetry cannot distinguish O2-Hb from CO-Hb; needs co-oximetry to detect carboxyhaemoglobinaemia.

Stem: Describe G6PD deficiency. What triggers and clinical features matter?

Model answer: _Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency_ - X-linked (males more affected); most common enzyme deficiency worldwide (~ 400 million); high prevalence in Africa, Mediterranean, Middle East, Southeast Asia (heterozygote advantage against falciparum malaria). Avoid haemolysis-inducing drugs (especially before prescribing sulfonamides, dapsone, primaquine, methylene blue). Newborn screening reduces fatal haemolysis. Most affected individuals are otherwise healthy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: G6PD deficiency illustrates how a single enzyme deficiency creates conditional disease - asymptomatic until oxidant exposure. Recognition before prescribing oxidant drugs prevents haemolytic crises. The malaria heterozygote advantage explains the geographical distribution.

Stem: Describe paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH). What is its pathophysiology and how is it managed?

Model answer: _PNH_ - acquired clonal HSC mutation in _PIGA_ (X-linked) -> defective glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchor on cell surfaces -> deficient CD55 (DAF) and CD59 (MIRL) on RBCs -> uncontrolled complement-mediated intravascular haemolysis. Triad: (1) _Intravascular haemolysis_ - dark urine (haemoglobinuria, especially morning - 'paroxysmal nocturnal'), pallor, jaundice, fatigue, dyspnoea; chronic pulmonary hypertension, oesophageal spasm, abdominal pain, dysphagia (smooth muscle dystonia from NO scavenging by free Hb); chronic kidney disease from chronic Hb-induced injury; (2) _Thrombosis_ - hepatic veins (Budd-Chiari), splanchnic, cerebral, ileal, dermal, deep limb veins; bizarre sites; mortality risk; possibly free-Hb-mediated NO depletion + complement-mediated platelet activation; (3) _Bone marrow failure_ - PNH is associated with aplastic anaemia (~ 50% of AA have small PNH clone; ~ 15% of AA develop overt PNH); cytopenias variable. Diagnosis: flow cytometry of peripheral blood - CD55 and CD59 deficiency on RBCs; FLAER (fluorescent aerolysin variant - detects GPI-AP deficiency on granulocytes/monocytes); urine haemosiderin; classic Ham acidified-serum test (historical); negative direct Coombs (intravascular haemolysis but not antibody-mediated). Management: (1) _Eculizumab_ (anti-C5 monoclonal antibody) - blocks terminal complement; transformative since 2007 - reduces haemolysis, transfusion dependency, thrombosis risk; risk of meningococcal infection (vaccinate Neisseria meningitidis B, A/C/W/Y; antibiotic prophylaxis); IV every 2 weeks. (2) _Ravulizumab_ - longer-acting analogue; IV every 8 weeks. (3) _Iptacopan_ - oral C5 inhibitor (newer; FDA 2023). (4) _Pegcetacoplan_ - C3 inhibitor (FDA 2021); useful for breakthrough haemolysis on C5 inhibition. (5) _Crovalimab_ - newer C5 inhibitor (Q4 weekly). (6) Allogeneic HSCT - curative for severe PNH or PNH-AA; risks of GVHD, infection. (7) Anticoagulation for thrombosis (DOAC or warfarin). (8) Iron and folate supplementation; transfusion support. (9) AA management for marrow failure component (ATG + ciclosporin, eltrombopag). Vaccinations critical before complement inhibitors. Outcome: massively improved with complement inhibitors; PNH was previously a slowly fatal disease but is now manageable. Diagnosis often delayed - consider in unexplained haemolysis, especially with cytopenia, thrombosis at unusual sites, marrow failure, or bone marrow with hypoplasia [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: PNH illustrates the consequences of complement dysregulation on host cells. The triad of haemolysis + thrombosis + cytopenia is distinctive. Eculizumab (2007) and successors are paradigm-changing - blocking complement at C5 transforms outcomes. Newer C3 inhibitors and oral agents continue to improve management.

3. Eculizumab transforms outcomes.

Stem: Describe the warm and cold autoimmune haemolytic anaemias. How are they distinguished and treated?

Model answer: _Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia (AIHA)_ - antibody-mediated haemolysis of RBCs. Two main types: (1) _Warm AIHA_ - IgG antibodies optimal at 37°C; bind RBCs -> phagocytosed by splenic macrophages (extravascular); spherocytes on film; positive direct antiglobulin test (DAT/Coombs) - IgG +/- C3. Causes: idiopathic (~ 50%), lymphoproliferative (CLL, lymphoma - increasingly common), autoimmune (SLE, scleroderma, RA, Sjögren), drugs (methyldopa - rare now; rare with newer drugs), HIV, EBV, post-transplant. Clinical: chronic anaemia + jaundice + splenomegaly; fatigue, pallor; severe cases - heart failure, neurological. Treatment: (1) _Corticosteroids_ - prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day, gradually tapered after response; ~ 75-80% respond; (2) _Rituximab_ (anti-CD20) - 2nd line; depletes B cells; effective in refractory; (3) _Splenectomy_ - removes major site of haemolysis; consider if steroid-refractory or steroid-dependent; vaccinations + antibiotic prophylaxis; (4) Other immunosuppressants - cyclophosphamide, azathioprine, mycophenolate; (5) Folic acid; (6) Transfusion - challenging because of incompatibility; find 'least incompatible' RBCs; warm blood to body temperature; cautious dose. (2) _Cold AIHA_: Two subtypes - (a) Cold agglutinin disease (CAD) - chronic IgM-mediated cold agglutinins; bind RBCs at low temperatures; intravascular haemolysis (complement-mediated MAC; IgM activates complement very efficiently); cold agglutinins on film. Causes: idiopathic (clonal lymphoproliferative disorder), Mycoplasma pneumoniae (typical age), EBV, lymphoma. Treatment: keep warm (avoid cold exposure; transfusions through warmer); rituximab + bendamustine first-line (better than rituximab alone); sutimlimab (anti-C1s, FDA 2022) - blocks classical complement, transformative; eculizumab in selected. (b) Paroxysmal cold haemoglobinuria (PCH) - rare; biphasic anti-P IgG (Donath-Landsteiner antibody); binds RBCs in cold, complement activates on warming; intravascular haemolysis. Classically associated with viral illnesses (children) or syphilis (rare). Treatment: warmth + supportive; usually self-limiting. Drug-induced haemolysis: several mechanisms - (a) Immune complex ("bystander" - eg, ceftriaxone); (b) Drug-adsorption (eg, penicillin); (c) Autoimmune (eg, methyldopa). Identify the drug and discontinue. Diagnosis principles: (1) Confirm haemolysis (retic, LDH, bilirubin, haptoglobin, blood film); (2) Direct antiglobulin test (DAT) - positive indicates antibody/complement-coated RBCs - IgG (warm AIHA), IgG + C3 (warm), C3 only (cold AIHA, drug-induced); (3) Indirect Coombs - serum antibodies; (4) Cold agglutinin titre, thermal amplitude (CAD); (5) Underlying disease workup (lymphoproliferative, autoimmune, infection). Pearls: spherocytes can appear in both HS and AIHA; DAT distinguishes (positive in AIHA, negative in HS). Cold AIHA may have RBC clumping at room temperature - warm sample to 37°C before processing. Newer treatments are reshaping cold AIHA management - sutimlimab a paradigm shift [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: AIHA divides usefully into warm (IgG, extravascular, steroid-responsive) and cold (IgM, intravascular, complement-mediated, sutimlimab-responsive). DAT pattern (IgG vs C3) distinguishes biochemically. Modern treatments span steroids, rituximab, and complement inhibition.

Hints:

1. Warm AIHA = IgG, extravascular, spherocytes, steroid-responsive.
2. Cold AIHA = IgM/C3, intravascular, sutimlimab-responsive.
3. DAT distinguishes IgG vs C3 patterns.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names corticosteroids first-line for warm; rituximab second-line.
- Names sutimlimab as recent advance for cold AIHA.
- Identifies common causes of both types.
- Notes transfusion challenges (least incompatible).

A Yellow answer covers AIHA but misses subtype distinction or new treatments.

Stem: Describe haemolytic disease of the newborn. How is it prevented and treated?

Model answer: _Haemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn (HDFN)_ - immune-mediated haemolysis caused by maternal antibodies crossing placenta and attacking fetal RBCs. Most clinically important: anti-RhD (most severe historically); anti-Kell (severe; can suppress erythropoiesis as well); other Rh (anti-c, anti-E, anti-C, anti-e); MNS, Duffy, Kidd, ABO. _Pathophysiology_: (1) Sensitisation - first pregnancy with RhD-positive fetus (or transfusion of RhD-positive blood) -> fetal RBCs leak into maternal circulation (especially at delivery, antepartum bleeding, miscarriage, amniocentesis, abdominal trauma) -> RhD-negative mother produces anti-D IgG. (2) Subsequent pregnancy - if fetus RhD-positive, maternal IgG anti-D crosses placenta -> attacks fetal RhD-positive RBCs -> haemolysis -> fetal anaemia -> hyperbilirubinaemia (cleared by mother in utero); severe -> erythroblastosis fetalis -> hydrops fetalis (cardiac failure, ascites, pleural effusion, pericardial effusion, oedema) -> intrauterine death. After birth, neonatal jaundice + risk of _kernicterus_ (bilirubin deposits in basal ganglia, brainstem -> athetoid cerebral palsy, deafness, gaze paresis, dental enamel dysplasia; preventable with phototherapy + exchange transfusion). Mothers most at risk: RhD-negative + RhD-positive partner + RhD-positive fetus. _Prevention_: (1) _Anti-D immunoglobulin (Rho(D) immune globulin, Rhogam)_ - given to RhD-negative mothers: (a) Routine antenatal at ~ 28 weeks; (b) Within 72 h of any potentially sensitising event - delivery of RhD-positive baby (confirmed by cord blood), miscarriage, abortion, antepartum haemorrhage, amniocentesis, CVS, abdominal trauma, ECV, sensitising procedures, intrauterine death. Mechanism: passive antibodies bind fetal RBCs in maternal circulation, masking from immune system before active immunisation can occur. Standard dose 300 mcg or weight-adjusted (Kleihauer-Betke test for fetomaternal haemorrhage volume). (2) Pre-conception counselling - identify at-risk couples; first-baby risk (Kell, ABO different from Rh - some immunity from prior exposure). _Antenatal care_: (1) ABO and RhD typing in early pregnancy; (2) Antibody screening; (3) If antibodies detected - serial maternal antibody titres; if rising or > 1:16 (anti-D) - middle cerebral artery peak systolic velocity (MCA-PSV) Doppler (now replaces invasive amniocentesis in most cases); (4) If MCA-PSV elevated > 1.5 MoM - cordocentesis to confirm fetal anaemia and intrauterine transfusion. _Intrauterine transfusion (IUT)_ - O RhD-negative, ABO-compatible, irradiated, leucoreduced, CMV-negative blood given via cordocentesis under ultrasound guidance; can repeat every 2-3 weeks until delivery; transformative for severe cases. _Postnatal management_: (1) Cord blood - blood group, DAT, Hb, bilirubin; (2) Phototherapy for hyperbilirubinaemia (450 nm light - converts bilirubin to water-soluble lumirubin and isomers); (3) Exchange transfusion for severe (typically bilirubin > 350 micromol/L or rapidly rising; or anaemia at birth); (4) IVIG can reduce severity in some cases; (5) Late anaemia - continued haemolysis from residual maternal antibodies; transfusion may be needed; (6) Long-term follow-up for neurodevelopmental outcomes (reduced now with anti-D prophylaxis). _Modern outcomes_: HDFN-related mortality has fallen ~ 95% since anti-D prophylaxis; intrauterine transfusion has transformed severe affected pregnancies. RhD-negative mothers can have successful pregnancies with appropriate care. Persistent challenges: anti-Kell sensitisation (no equivalent prophylaxis); other antibody-mediated HDFN; access to anti-D in low-resource settings. Future: cell-free fetal DNA testing for fetal RhD status from maternal blood (allows targeted prophylaxis - given only if fetus RhD-positive); some countries already use [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18; Costanzo ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: HDFN is one of the great public health success stories - anti-D prophylaxis has dramatically reduced disease. Modern management combines screening, MCA-PSV monitoring, intrauterine transfusion, and postnatal phototherapy/exchange transfusion. Cell-free fetal DNA testing for RhD status promises further refinement.

Hints:

1. RhD-negative mother + RhD-positive fetus -> sensitisation -> haemolysis.
2. Anti-D prophylaxis at 28 weeks + after sensitising events.
3. MCA-PSV Doppler + intrauterine transfusion for affected pregnancy.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names HDFN mechanism (maternal IgG anti-D crossing placenta).
- Identifies anti-D immunoglobulin and its timing (28 weeks + post-event).
- Names MCA-PSV Doppler as non-invasive surveillance.
- Mentions intrauterine transfusion for severe cases.
- Notes neonatal management (phototherapy, exchange transfusion).

A Yellow answer covers HDFN but misses prophylaxis schedule or new techniques.

A Red answer treats this as a rare historical disease.

Common misconceptions:

- "Anti-D prophylaxis is given to all pregnant women.": Correction: Only RhD-negative mothers; cell-free fetal DNA testing may further target prophylaxis to those carrying RhD-positive fetuses.
- "HDFN is only Rh-related.": Correction: Multiple antibodies can cause - anti-Kell, anti-c, anti-E, ABO mismatch in some cases; modern care screens for many.

Stem: Describe chronic leukaemia and lymphoma. How are CML, CLL, and major lymphomas distinguished and treated?

Model answer: _Chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML)_ - clonal myeloproliferation; defining BCR-ABL fusion (Philadelphia chromosome - t(9;22)) -> constitutively active tyrosine kinase. Three phases: chronic (~ 85%), accelerated, blast crisis. Clinical: incidental finding (raised WCC), constitutional symptoms (weight loss, sweats, fatigue), splenomegaly (often massive). Diagnosis: FBC (raised WCC with myeloid left shift, basophilia, eosinophilia, mild thrombocytosis); blood film (granulocytic series); BCR-ABL by PCR or FISH or cytogenetics. _Treatment_: imatinib (first generation TKI, 2001 - revolutionised CML; now available generically); second-generation (dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib) - faster response, useful first-line in higher-risk; third-generation (ponatinib) - active against T315I resistance mutation; asciminib (allosteric BCR-ABL inhibitor; FDA 2021). Goal: deep molecular response (BCR-ABL transcript reduction). Some patients achieve treatment-free remission after years of deep response. Median survival now > 20 years, approaches normal in good responders. HSCT reserved for refractory/blast crisis. _Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL)_ / _small lymphocytic lymphoma (SLL)_ - same disease different presentation; clonal mature B cells. Clinical: incidental lymphocytosis, lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, B symptoms; immune dysfunction (recurrent infections, autoimmune cytopenias - AIHA, ITP), Richter transformation to aggressive lymphoma (~ 5-10%), hypogammaglobulinaemia. Diagnosis: persistent absolute lymphocytosis > 5 x 10^9/L of clonal B cells; flow cytometry (CD5+, CD19+, CD23+, CD20 dim, kappa or lambda restricted - typical immunophenotype); blood film (small mature lymphocytes, smudge cells); risk stratification by IGHV mutational status (mutated favourable; unmutated less favourable), FISH (del 17p TP53 worst; del 13q best; trisomy 12; del 11q ATM), TP53 mutations, beta-2 microglobulin. Staging - Rai or Binet. Treatment: "watch and wait" for asymptomatic; treat when symptomatic, cytopenias, large nodes, B symptoms. Options: (1) BTK inhibitors (ibrutinib first-generation; acalabrutinib, zanubrutinib - more selective, better tolerated) - paradigm-changing; covalent and now non-covalent (pirtobrutinib for resistance); (2) BCL-2 inhibitor (venetoclax) - often combined with rituximab/obinutuzumab; fixed-duration; (3) Anti-CD20 antibodies (rituximab, obinutuzumab); (4) Older: chemoimmunotherapy (FCR, BR) - now largely replaced by targeted agents; (5) HSCT in selected; (6) CAR-T cell in trials/early use. Long survival often achieved. Lymphomas - classified as Hodgkin (HL) or non-Hodgkin (NHL). _Hodgkin lymphoma_ - bimodal (young adults; older); painless lymphadenopathy (often cervical), B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss > 10%), pruritus, alcohol-induced node pain. Histology: Reed-Sternberg cells in inflammatory background. Subtypes: classical (nodular sclerosing most common, mixed cellularity, lymphocyte-rich, lymphocyte-depleted); nodular lymphocyte-predominant (different biology). Staging: Ann Arbor (I-IV) ± B symptoms. Treatment: ABVD (adriamycin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine) + radiation in early stage; eBEACOPP for advanced; brentuximab vedotin (anti-CD30 ADC) and PD-1 inhibitors (nivolumab, pembrolizumab) revolutionised relapsed/refractory; cure rates 80-90%. _Non-Hodgkin lymphoma_ - heterogeneous; B (~ 90%) or T cell. Major subtypes: (a) _Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL)_ - most common aggressive NHL; rapidly enlarging masses; treat R-CHOP (rituximab + cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisolone); newer R-pola-CHP (with polatuzumab vedotin); CAR-T cell for r/r (axicabtagene, tisagenlecleucel, lisocabtagene); cure rates 60-70%. (b) _Follicular lymphoma_ - indolent; painless lymphadenopathy; histological grading; watch and wait or treat when symptomatic; rituximab ± bendamustine, R-CHOP; transformation to aggressive lymphoma in 30-40% over years; median survival decades. (c) _Burkitt lymphoma_ - very aggressive; fastest doubling time of any tumour; jaw mass (endemic, Africa) or abdominal/marrow (sporadic, US); MYC translocation; treat intensive chemo + rituximab + intrathecal; high cure with prompt treatment; tumour lysis risk. (f) T-cell lymphomas - more aggressive; harder to treat; PTCL, ALCL (often ALK+), cutaneous (mycosis fungoides, Sezary). (g) HIV-associated, EBV-driven (Burkitt, post-transplant lymphoproliferative). Classification by WHO continues to evolve with molecular subtyping. Modern lymphoma management is increasingly genotype-driven and immunotherapy-rich [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Chronic leukaemias and lymphomas illustrate how molecular targeted therapy revolutionised oncology. CML with imatinib (2001) and CLL with BTK inhibitors (2014+) transformed prognosis. Lymphomas show how risk-stratified and increasingly immune-based therapy (ADCs, CAR-T, checkpoint inhibitors) achieves cure for many.

3. Lymphomas: HL (Reed-Sternberg) vs NHL (DLBCL most common); CHOP-based + targeted/CAR-T.

Stem: Describe multiple myeloma and plasma cell disorders.

Model answer: _Plasma cell dyscrasias_ - clonal expansion of plasma cells producing monoclonal protein (paraprotein, M-protein). Spectrum: MGUS -> SMM -> MM -> plasma cell leukaemia. _MGUS_ (monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance) - present in 1-3% over 50; benign monoclonal protein (< 30 g/L); no end-organ damage; ~ 1% per year progression to MM; surveillance only. _Smouldering myeloma (SMM)_ - intermediate; M-protein > 30 g/L or ≥ 10% plasma cells but no CRAB; some 'high-risk SMM' criteria now indicate early treatment. _Multiple myeloma (MM)_: clonal plasma cells in marrow + monoclonal protein + end-organ damage (CRAB). Median age 65-70 years. Clinical (CRAB criteria): (1) _Calcium_ - hypercalcaemia (osteoclast activation); (2) _Renal_ - cast nephropathy (light chain in tubules), hypercalcaemia, amyloid, hyperviscosity, NSAIDs, IV contrast; (3) _Anaemia_ - marrow infiltration; (4) _Bone_ - lytic lesions ('punched-out' on plain X-ray), pathological fractures, vertebral compression, osteoporosis, severe bone pain. Plus immunoparesis (low normal Ig - infections), recurrent infections (especially encapsulated organisms), hyperviscosity (rarely), neuropathy, weight loss, cord compression. Diagnosis: SLiM-CRAB criteria (any one): S (marrow plasma cells ≥ 60%), Li (light chain ratio ≥ 100), M (≥ 1 focal MRI lesion); plus CRAB. Investigations: serum + urine protein electrophoresis + immunofixation (M-band, light chain identification); free light chain ratio; quantitative immunoglobulins; calcium, renal function, beta-2 microglobulin, albumin (ISS staging), LDH (R-ISS); skeletal survey or whole-body MRI/CT/PET (MRI most sensitive for marrow lesions); bone marrow biopsy + flow cytometry + cytogenetics (high-risk: t(4;14), t(14;16), del 17p, gain 1q21). Subtypes: IgG (50%), IgA (25%), IgD (rare), IgM (Waldenström macroglobulinaemia - distinct disease), light chain only (15%), nonsecretory (rare). (4) _CAR-T cell_ - BCMA-directed (idecabtagene vicleucel, ciltacabtagene) - high response rates in r/r MM; FDA 2021-2022. (5) _Bispecifics_ (BCMA, GPRC5D, FcRH5) - teclistamab, talquetamab, elranatamab - new (2022-2023); promising even in heavily pretreated. (6) _Bone disease_: bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid, pamidronate) or denosumab (RANKL inhibitor; better in renal impairment); radiation for symptomatic lesions; vertebroplasty/kyphoplasty; orthopaedic. (7) _Renal_: hydration, avoid NSAIDs and contrast, treat MM aggressively, plasma exchange in cast nephropathy (controversial). (8) _Infection prevention_: vaccinations, IVIG in selected hypogammaglobulinaemia, prophylactic antivirals/antifungals during therapy. (9) _Hyperviscosity_: plasma exchange. Plasma cell dyscrasias have been transformed by new therapies; survival has more than doubled in 2 decades [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Multiple myeloma illustrates how clonal plasma cells produce devastating multi-organ disease through paraprotein and direct effects (CRAB). AL amyloidosis is a related entity requiring careful diagnosis.

Stem: Describe coagulation disorders and bleeding diatheses. How is each diagnosed and managed?

Model answer: _Bleeding disorders_ classified by mechanism: platelet vs coagulation factor vs vascular. Platelet disorders cause mucocutaneous bleeding (petechiae, gum, GI, mucosal); factor deficiencies cause deep tissue bleeding (haemarthroses, intramuscular, retroperitoneal, delayed). Distinguishing pattern is important. (1) _Haemophilia A_ - X-linked recessive; factor VIII deficiency; ~ 1/5000 male births; severity by FVIII level (severe < 1%, moderate 1-5%, mild > 5%). Severe presents in infancy with haemarthroses (knees, elbows, ankles), intramuscular bleeding, prolonged bleeding after circumcision, intracranial bleeding rare but devastating. Chronic haemarthroses -> haemophilic arthropathy. Diagnosis: prolonged aPTT (corrects on mixing), normal PT, low FVIII, normal vWF. Management: (a) _Factor replacement_ - recombinant FVIII for bleeds and prophylaxis; (b) _Long-acting factor concentrates_ (efmoroctocog alfa, others); (c) _Emicizumab_ - bispecific antibody mimicking FVIII function (binds factor IXa and X, replacing FVIII activity); revolutionary - subcutaneous, less frequent dosing; effective in severe HA + inhibitors; (d) _Gene therapy_ - valoctocogene roxaparvovec (FDA 2022 for severe HA) - AAV-based FVIII gene transfer; durability and safety being established; (e) _Inhibitors_ - alloantibodies to FVIII develop in ~ 30% of severe HA after exposure; treat with bypassing agents (FEIBA, recombinant FVIIa) or emicizumab; immune tolerance induction. (f) Comprehensive haemophilia centre care - dental, surgical planning, exercise, education, counselling. (2) _Haemophilia B_ (Christmas disease) - factor IX deficiency; same X-linked pattern; clinical features similar to HA. Treatment: factor IX concentrate; etranacogene dezaparvovec (gene therapy FDA 2022). (3) _von Willebrand disease (vWD)_ - most common inherited bleeding disorder (~ 1% prevalence; clinically significant ~ 0.1%). vWF normally: (a) bridges platelets to subendothelial collagen via GPIb-IX-V (primary haemostasis); (b) carries factor VIII (extends its half-life). Defects: type 1 (~ 75%; partial quantitative deficiency; AD); type 2 (qualitative; subtypes 2A, 2B, 2M, 2N); type 3 (severe; absent vWF; AR; rare). Clinical: mucocutaneous bleeding (heavy menstrual bleeding common - HMB; epistaxis; GI; surgical), more rarely deep bleeding. Diagnosis: vWF antigen, ristocetin cofactor activity (vWF:RCo - functional), FVIII activity, vWF multimer analysis. Management: (a) _Desmopressin (DDAVP)_ - releases vWF from Weibel-Palade bodies; useful in type 1 and 2A; trial first; (b) _vWF/FVIII concentrate_ - for severe cases or types unresponsive to DDAVP; (c) Antifibrinolytics (tranexamic acid) - mucosal bleeding; (d) Hormonal - HMB (OCP, levonorgestrel-IUD); (e) Avoid antiplatelets/NSAIDs. (4) _Vitamin K-dependent factor deficiency_ - vitamin K essential for γ-carboxylation of factors II, VII, IX, X, protein C, S. Deficiency: dietary (rare in adults; common in newborns - hence vitamin K injection at birth), malabsorption (cholestasis, antibiotics, bile-acid sequestrants), warfarin therapy. Prolonged PT and aPTT; treat with vitamin K (oral if mild; IV - slow; SC less reliable). (5) _Liver disease_ - reduced synthesis of all factors except VIII; combined coagulopathy; thrombocytopenia (hypersplenism); platelet dysfunction; reduced fibrinogen. Vitamin K does not correct (liver synthetic failure). FFP for active bleeding/procedure. PCC. (6) _Thrombocytopenia_ - causes: (a) Reduced production - marrow infiltration, aplastic, MDS, B12/folate, alcohol, drugs; (b) Increased destruction - immune (ITP - IgG against GPIIb/IIIa or GPIb-IX; idiopathic or secondary to viral, drugs, autoimmune); thrombotic microangiopathy (TTP, HUS, DIC, HELLP); drug-induced (heparin -> HIT antibodies to PF4-heparin -> paradoxical thrombosis; not bleeding); (c) Sequestration - hypersplenism. _ITP_ - common; isolated thrombocytopenia; bone marrow normal. Treatment: corticosteroids first-line (prednisolone or dexamethasone pulse); IVIG for rapid effect; anti-D (in RhD+ non-splenectomised); rituximab; TPO-receptor agonists (eltrombopag, romiplostim, avatrombopag); fostamatinib (SYK inhibitor); splenectomy in refractory. _HIT_ - typically 5-10 days after heparin start; thrombocytopenia + thrombosis; antibodies to PF4-heparin complex; stop all heparin; alternative anticoagulation (fondaparinux, argatroban, bivalirudin); transition to DOAC or warfarin with bridging; do not give platelets unless bleeding (worsens thrombosis). (7) _Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)_ - see Ch 16. (8) _Other rare_ - factor V, VII, X, XI, XIII deficiencies; afibrinogenaemia; α2-antiplasmin deficiency. (9) _Drug-induced bleeding_ - antiplatelets, anticoagulants. Bleeding history is essential (mucosal vs deep, age of onset, family history, drug history, surgical history); investigation includes FBC, blood film, coagulation screen (PT, aPTT, fibrinogen, mixing studies if prolonged), bleeding time (rarely now), platelet function tests (PFA-100), specific factor assays, vWF assays, viscoelastic tests (TEG, ROTEM). Modern haemophilia care has been revolutionised by emicizumab and gene therapy - patients now have prospects of normal life expectancy and minimal joint damage [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Bleeding disorders span genetic (haemophilia A/B, vWD), acquired (DIC, liver disease, HIT, ITP), and drug-induced. Pattern recognition by site (mucocutaneous vs deep) is informative. Modern haemophilia therapy with emicizumab and gene therapy is transformative; ITP has multiple effective treatments.

Hints:

1. Platelet vs factor disorders - different bleeding patterns.
2. Haemophilia A/B - factor replacement; emicizumab for HA; gene therapy 2022.
3. vWD - DDAVP, vWF concentrate.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Distinguishes platelet from coagulation bleeding patterns.
- Names haemophilia A vs B and their factor deficiencies.
- Identifies vWF function and vWD subtypes.
- Names ≥ 3 ITP treatments.
- Mentions HIT as paradoxical thrombosis.

A Yellow answer covers some disorders but misses pattern recognition or new therapies.

Stem: Describe thrombotic disorders. How are venous and arterial thrombosis distinguished and managed?

Model answer: _Thrombotic disorders_ - inappropriate clot formation; major cause of mortality globally. Distinguish venous from arterial. (1) _Venous thromboembolism (VTE)_: (a) DVT (deep vein thrombosis) - usually lower limb (calf, popliteal, femoral, iliac); presents with leg swelling, pain, warmth, erythema; can extend or embolise; (b) PE (pulmonary embolism) - sudden dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, tachycardia; massive PE - shock, syncope, RV failure; sub-massive PE - RV strain on echo or troponin/BNP elevation; non-massive - smaller PE without compromise; (c) Other: portal vein, splenic vein (often paraneoplastic), cerebral venous sinus, retinal, renal, mesenteric. _Virchow triad_: stasis, hypercoagulability, endothelial injury. Risk factors: stasis (immobilisation, surgery, long flights, paralysis), hypercoagulability (cancer - especially adenocarcinoma; pregnancy/postpartum; OCP/HRT; obesity; smoking; nephrotic syndrome; PNH; thrombophilia inherited - factor V Leiden ~ 5% Caucasians, prothrombin G20210A, antithrombin/protein C/S deficiency; antiphospholipid syndrome - lupus anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-β2-GP1; severe inflammation; COVID-19), endothelial injury (surgery, trauma, central venous catheters, IBD, vasculitis). Diagnosis: (a) Clinical scores (Wells DVT, Wells PE, Geneva, PERC); (b) D-dimer - sensitive but not specific; useful to rule out in low-pretest-probability; (c) Imaging - DVT: compression ultrasound; iliac/IVC: CT or MR venography; PE: CT pulmonary angiography (gold standard) or VQ scan (if contrast contraindicated, pregnancy); (d) Investigate cause: cancer screening if unprovoked age-appropriate; thrombophilia workup in selected (recurrent, young, unusual sites, family history, autoimmune features) - check factor V Leiden, prothrombin, antithrombin/protein C/S, antiphospholipid panel, JAK2 mutation; (e) Echo for RV strain in PE. Treatment: (a) _Anticoagulation_: (i) Initial - DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban - now first-line for most VTE; convenient, no monitoring; specific reversal agents andexanet alfa, ciraparantag) or LMWH (enoxaparin) for 5-10 days then warfarin (older approach), or LMWH alone (cancer-associated VTE - LMWH historically but DOACs now equivalent in many trials); (ii) Duration - 3-6 months for provoked; longer or indefinite for unprovoked, recurrent, ongoing risk; (iii) UFH for haemodynamic compromise / massive PE / renal impairment; (iv) Pregnancy - LMWH (warfarin and DOACs contraindicated). (b) _Thrombolysis_ - massive PE with shock or sub-massive with RV dysfunction (catheter-directed); contraindications - recent surgery, bleeding, ICH; bleeding risk significant. (c) _Surgical thrombectomy_ - in selected. (d) _IVC filter_ - for proximal DVT/PE when anticoagulation contraindicated; retrievable filters preferred. (e) _Compression stockings_ for symptomatic relief (less for prevention now). (f) Treat underlying (cancer; antiphospholipid syndrome - lifelong anticoagulation). (2) _Arterial thrombosis_: (a) Coronary - acute MI; (b) Cerebral - ischaemic stroke; (c) Peripheral - acute limb ischaemia, mesenteric ischaemia; (d) Splanchnic. Mechanism: endothelial injury (atherosclerosis - plaque rupture; vasculitis) + platelet activation + flow disturbance + hypercoagulability. Risk factors: atherosclerotic - hypertension, diabetes, smoking, dyslipidaemia, age, family history, hyperhomocysteinaemia; AF (cardioembolic stroke); valvular disease; PFO with paradoxical embolism; cocaine/amphetamine; APLS. Diagnosis: clinical + imaging (angiography, CT, MR). Treatment: (a) Reperfusion - urgent: PCI for STEMI, thrombolysis or thrombectomy for stroke (see Ch 10), surgical embolectomy for limb ischaemia; (b) _Antiplatelets_ (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor for ACS post-PCI; aspirin + clopidogrel after stroke - dual short-term, then monotherapy; aspirin alone for primary prevention in selected); (c) _Anticoagulation_ - for AF (DOACs preferred over warfarin in non-valvular AF), valvular (warfarin only); (d) Statin; (e) Cardiovascular risk factor management. (3) _Antiphospholipid syndrome (APLS)_ - autoimmune; antibodies (lupus anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-β2-GP1) + clinical (venous or arterial thrombosis, pregnancy morbidity - recurrent miscarriage, preterm severe pre-eclampsia/IUGR, fetal death); paradoxical aPTT prolongation that does not correct on mixing. Treatment: anticoagulation indefinite (warfarin preferred for triple-positive arterial APLS - DOACs _less effective_; warfarin still standard); aspirin in pregnancy + LMWH; treat underlying (lupus). Catastrophic APLS - multiple thrombosis, organ failure - immediate immunosuppression + plasma exchange. (4) _Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT)_ - paradoxical thrombosis; see Ch on bleeding. (5) _Thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA)_ - TTP (ADAMTS13 deficiency; pentad MAHA + thrombocytopenia + AKI + neurological + fever; emergency plasma exchange + steroids + caplacizumab), HUS (E. coli O157 - typical; complement-mediated atypical - eculizumab), DIC, HELLP, malignant hypertension. _Thrombophilia_ - inherited or acquired tendency to thrombosis; varying penetrance. Modern anticoagulation has been transformed by DOACs (factor Xa inhibitors and dabigatran) - more convenient, no monitoring, specific reversal, broadly first-line for most indications [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Thrombotic disorders span venous (DVT/PE) and arterial (MI, stroke). Mechanisms differ - venous reflects Virchow triad with hypercoagulability prominent; arterial primarily atherothrombotic with platelet activation. Anticoagulation has been revolutionised by DOACs. APLS is a critical diagnosis with specific implications.

Hints:

1. Venous (DVT/PE) - Virchow triad; LMWH/DOAC.
2. Arterial (MI/stroke) - atherothrombosis; antiplatelets + statins.
3. APLS - autoimmune; warfarin for arterial, especially triple-positive.

- Identifies APLS criteria and warfarin preference.

Stem: Describe how bone marrow transplantation is used in haematological diseases. What are the principles, indications, and complications?

Model answer: _Haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT)_ - replacement of haematopoietic system; can be curative for haematological malignancies and inherited disorders. Two major types: (1) _Autologous_ - patient's own HSCs; harvested before high-dose therapy, returned after; supports recovery from intensive chemo/radiation but does not introduce new immunity; cannot exploit graft-versus-tumour effect; lower TRM (< 5%). (2) _Allogeneic_ - donor HSCs; donor immune cells provide graft-versus-tumour effect but risk GVHD; higher TRM (10-30% depending on age, donor matching, intensity). Donor sources: HLA-identical sibling (best; ~ 25% of patients have one), matched unrelated donor (MUD; through registries), haploidentical (parent or sibling 50% match; growing in importance with post-transplant cyclophosphamide reducing GVHD), umbilical cord blood (off-the-shelf; less HLA-restricted; smaller cell dose - usually for paediatric or smaller adults). Sources of HSCs: bone marrow harvest, peripheral blood (after G-CSF mobilisation - now most common), cord blood. Indications: (1) _Allogeneic_: high-risk acute leukaemia (AML, ALL) in first remission or beyond; relapsed/refractory leukaemia/lymphoma; MDS (intermediate-high risk); aplastic anaemia (matched sibling preferred; younger patients); inherited bone marrow failure (Fanconi, dyskeratosis congenita); inherited haemoglobinopathies (sickle cell with sibling-matched donor; β-thalassaemia major); inherited metabolic/storage diseases (some); severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID); chronic granulomatous disease; Wiskott-Aldrich; some inherited platelet disorders. (2) _Autologous_: multiple myeloma; relapsed Hodgkin lymphoma; relapsed DLBCL; some aggressive lymphomas; AL amyloidosis; testicular cancer; some autoimmune diseases (refractory MS, scleroderma - investigational/specialised). Process: (1) _Pre-transplant assessment_ - performance status, organ function, comorbidities, infections, psychological readiness; HLA typing; (2) _Conditioning regimen_ - chemotherapy (cyclophosphamide, busulfan, fludarabine, melphalan, thiotepa) ± total body irradiation; goal: eradicate disease, suppress recipient immune system to allow engraftment, create marrow space (myeloablative or reduced-intensity); (3) _Stem cell infusion_ - relatively simple bedside procedure; (4) _Engraftment_ - 2-4 weeks for neutrophils, longer for platelets; supportive care during nadir critical - infection, bleeding, transfusion support; (5) _Post-transplant care_ - monitoring for engraftment, infection, GVHD (allo); immunosuppression (allo); reassessment of disease. Complications: (1) _Acute (first 100 days)_: (a) _Cytopenia/infections_ - bacterial (especially during neutropenic period), fungal (Aspergillus, Candida, Pneumocystis), viral (CMV reactivation - prophylaxis with letermovir; HSV, VZV, BK virus, adenovirus, EBV-PTLD); (b) _Mucositis_ - severe; pain, infection risk; analgesia, palifermin in selected; (c) _Sinusoidal obstruction syndrome (VOD)_ - liver toxicity from busulfan/conditioning; weight gain, ascites, hyperbilirubinaemia; defibrotide treatment; (d) _Engraftment syndrome_ - fever, rash, capillary leak around engraftment; steroids; (e) _Graft failure_ - rare; (f) _Acute GVHD_ (allo only) - donor T cells attack recipient organs (skin - rash; liver - cholestasis; gut - diarrhoea); grading I-IV; treatment - steroids first-line, then ruxolitinib (FDA 2019); 30-50% incidence; major morbidity and mortality. (2) _Late (after 100 days)_: (a) _Chronic GVHD_ (allo) - sclerodermatous, sicca-like; multi-organ; complex management - steroids + ruxolitinib + ibrutinib + extracorporeal photopheresis; (b) _Infections_ - encapsulated organisms, viral reactivation, opportunistic; long-term prophylaxis; (c) _Late effects of conditioning_ - cataracts, endocrine (thyroid, growth), gonadal failure (infertility), cardiomyopathy (anthracycline), pulmonary, renal; (d) _Secondary malignancies_ - radiation-induced; therapy-related MDS/AML; (e) _Relapse of underlying disease_. Survival: depends on disease, age, donor matching, conditioning intensity, comorbidities. Modern HSCT outcomes have improved with reduced-intensity conditioning, better supportive care, post-transplant cyclophosphamide for GVHD prophylaxis (especially haploidentical), targeted GVHD therapies (ruxolitinib). Some haematological malignancies are now considered curable with HSCT in selected patients - testament to the power of graft-versus-tumour and replacement of malignant haematopoiesis [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: HSCT is one of the most powerful therapies in haematology, capable of curing many otherwise fatal diseases. The complexity (donor selection, conditioning, engraftment, GVHD management) requires specialised centres. Modern advances - haploidentical donors, reduced-intensity conditioning, ruxolitinib for GVHD - have dramatically broadened access and improved outcomes.

Hints:

1. Auto = patient's own; allo = donor (sibling/MUD/haplo/cord).
2. Conditioning + infusion + engraftment + immunosuppression.
3. GVHD - acute (first 100 days) and chronic (later).

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Distinguishes autologous from allogeneic HSCT.
- Names ≥ 3 indications for allo HSCT.
- Identifies acute and chronic GVHD with management.
- Mentions infection risks at different stages.
- Notes graft-versus-tumour effect.

A Yellow answer covers HSCT but misses GVHD or specific indications.

A Red answer treats HSCT as a single procedure.

Common misconceptions:

- "HSCT is the same as a blood transfusion.": Correction: It involves replacing the entire haematopoietic system with stem cells from donor or self; complex pre/post care; high stakes.
- "All HSCT requires sibling donor.": Correction: Multiple sources now: matched sibling (best), unrelated, haploidentical, cord blood; haploidentical with post-transplant Cy has dramatically expanded access.

Stem: Describe the design principles of erythrocytes and oxygen transport. How do the shape, size, lifespan, and biochemistry serve their function?

Model answer: _RBC design_ is exquisitely matched to function. Structural features: (1) _Biconcave disc shape_ - 7-8 μm diameter, 2 μm thick at edges, 1 μm thick at centre. Maximises surface area: volume ratio (~ 140 μm² for ~ 90 μm³ - much more than a sphere of same volume); enables rapid gas diffusion; deformability for capillary passage (~ 3 μm capillaries). Cytoskeleton (spectrin, ankyrin, band 3, actin) maintains shape and elasticity. (2) _Anucleate_ - matures with nucleus extruded; provides space for haemoglobin (~ 250 million Hb molecules per cell). Cannot synthesise new proteins; cannot divide; cannot repair DNA damage. (3) _No mitochondria_ - depends on glycolysis for ATP; doesn't compete with own O2 cargo. (4) _Hb-rich cytoplasm_ - ~ 33 g/dL Hb; high concentration but supersaturation point lowers in deoxy (basis of sickle polymerisation). (5) _Membrane lipid bilayer + cytoskeleton_ - flexible yet stable. (6) _Surface antigens_ - blood groups (ABO, Rh, others), but minimal HLA - allows transfusion across donors without major HLA matching for RBCs. (7) _Glycophorin_ - sialic acid carriers; influences interactions with parasites (P. falciparum invasion). Functional features: (1) _O2 transport_ - main function; cooperative binding via Hb; sigmoidal curve. ~ 5 mL O2 delivered per 100 mL blood per pass at rest; up to 15-20 with vigorous exercise. (2) _CO2 transport_ - dissolved (~ 7%), bicarbonate (~ 70%), carbamino (~ 23%). (3) _Acid-base buffering_ - Hb is the major intracellular buffer. (4) _NO scavenging_ - free Hb (intravascular haemolysis) scavenges NO -> vasoconstriction and platelet activation; explains pulmonary hypertension in chronic haemolysis (sickle, PNH, thalassaemia, mechanical heart valves). (5) _Lifespan ~ 120 days_ - balance between durability and accumulating damage; Heinz body inclusions in oxidant stress. (6) _Senescence sensors_ - aged RBCs lose CD47, expose phosphatidylserine -> macrophage clearance in spleen. Why these design choices: (1) Anucleate maximises Hb capacity per cell; (2) Biconcave maximises diffusion area; (3) No mitochondria preserves O2 cargo; (4) Cooperativity allows efficient loading and unloading; (5) Bohr/Haldane effects optimise O2/CO2 exchange. Trade-offs: (1) Limited reparability - any damage shortens lifespan; (2) Cannot adapt to chronic stress; (3) Vulnerable to oxidative damage (G6PD deficiency); (4) Vulnerable to membrane defects (HS, HE); (5) Vulnerable to Hb defects (haemoglobinopathies). Comparative biology: cetacean and seal RBCs have higher MCV - reduced viscosity advantage; high-altitude camelids (llama, vicuña) have higher Hb-O2 affinity (left-shifted) for low-PaO2 environments; bird RBCs are nucleated (less efficient O2 carriers per cell); fish/amphibian RBCs are nucleated. Mammalian anucleate design represents one extreme of evolutionary optimisation. Disorders illustrate design failures: haemoglobinopathies (qualitative or quantitative Hb), membrane (HS, HE), enzyme (G6PD, PK), production (anaemias), survival (haemolysis), turnover (DIC, mechanical, autoimmune). Therapy targets each level: O2 delivery (transfusion, EPO), Hb structure (hydroxyurea for HbF; voxelotor for HbS oxygenation; gene therapy for HBB), recycling (chelation for iron overload), production (EPO, EPO mimetics, hepcidin agonists/antagonists in trials), senescence (delayed-storage RBCs in development). Design principle synthesis: each RBC is a miniature O2-delivery machine optimised through evolution for the specific demands of mammalian metabolism. Disorders reveal the elegance of the solution and the cost when any component fails [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: RBC design is one of the most beautifully optimised structures in physiology - shape, size, biochemistry, and lifespan all matched to O2-delivery function. Disorders reveal the elegance of the design through failures - each level (Hb, membrane, enzyme, production, lifespan) has its dedicated diseases. Modern therapies target specific levels with increasing precision.

2. Each design feature serves O2 delivery efficiency.
3. Disorders reveal design failures.

QUESTION 18
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Synthesise erythrocyte physiology. How do RBC structure, Hb function, EPO regulation, and iron metabolism collectively support oxygenation, and what design principles emerge?

Model answer: Erythrocyte physiology integrates four major axes - structural (cell), functional (Hb), regulatory (EPO), substrate (iron) - to produce reliable, scalable O2 delivery. (1) _Structural axis_ - biconcave anucleate disc maximises Hb cargo and diffusion surface; flexible cytoskeleton allows passage through capillaries; lifespan 120 days balanced between durability and accumulating damage. (2) _Functional axis (Hb)_ - cooperative O2 binding via tetrameric quaternary structure; sigmoidal curve fits physiological PaO2 range; Bohr/Haldane effects optimise tissue O2 unloading and CO2 carriage; right shift with exercise/CO2/H+/2,3-BPG/heat increases delivery; left shift with HbF facilitates placental transfer; CO and metHb pathologically left-shift. (3) _Regulatory axis (EPO)_ - kidney peritubular cells sense O2 (via HIF-2α); EPO release proportional to need; bone marrow erythropoiesis scaled to demand; can increase 6-8 fold from baseline; failure (CKD) causes anaemia treatable with recombinant EPO. (4) _Substrate axis (iron)_ - hepcidin/ferroportin master regulator; recycling efficient (~ 25 mg/day); deficiency or overload both cause clinical disease; multiple therapies targeting different points in iron handling. Cross-axis integration: (a) _EPO + iron_ - efficient erythropoiesis requires both; renal anaemia treated with EPO + iron; iron deficiency without EPO drive is suboptimal; HIF stabilisers (vadadustat) coordinate both. (b) _Hb + iron_ - haemoglobin synthesis requires iron; thalassaemia and SCD treated with iron management among other strategies. (c) _RBC + Hb_ - membrane defects (HS) shorten Hb-laden cell lifespan; haemoglobinopathies damage cell mechanics. (d) _Recycling + production_ - macrophage clearance feeds iron back to marrow for new RBCs; chronic haemolysis can outpace recycling efficiency. Examples of integrated function: (a) _Altitude adaptation_ - hypoxia at altitude -> kidney HIF-2α -> EPO -> increased erythropoiesis -> rise in RBC mass over weeks; if iron-sufficient, polycythaemia for O2 carrying; coupled with right-shift of Hb curve via 2,3-BPG. (b) _Chronic haemolysis_ (sickle, thalassaemia) - shortened RBC lifespan -> compensatory increased EPO and erythropoiesis; iron overload from increased absorption + transfusions. (c) _Pregnancy_ - plasma volume rises faster than RBC mass -> dilutional anaemia; iron demand increased; physiological. (d) _Renal failure_ - reduced EPO + iron dysregulation + uraemic toxins -> anaemia; treat with EPO, iron, dialysis. (e) _Anaemia of chronic disease_ - inflammation -> hepcidin up -> reduced iron release; functional iron deficiency for erythropoiesis. (f) _Chronic transfusion_ - iron overload + alloimmunisation; chelation needed. Design principles: (1) _Specialised cell for specialised function_ - RBC architecture optimised for O2 delivery; (2) _Cooperative biochemistry_ - Hb cooperativity and Bohr/Haldane effects; (3) _Hierarchical regulation_ - EPO at top, iron at base, marrow expansion as plant; (4) _Recycling for efficiency_ - iron and Hb both extensively recycled; (5) _Surplus capacity_ - 6-8 fold expansion possible; (6) _Multiple sensors and signals_ - HIF, hepcidin, erythroferrone, EPO all interact; (7) _Robustness with vulnerability_ - simple, optimised cell vulnerable to specific failures (oxidant stress, complement, parasites). Disorders reveal design: each clinical syndrome (anaemia, polycythaemia, haemoglobinopathy, haemolysis, iron disorders) maps to a specific design failure. Modern therapy: each axis has dedicated treatments - replacing missing component (EPO, iron, transfusion, factor VIII), modifying Hb (hydroxyurea, voxelotor, gene therapy), modulating regulation (hepcidin agonists/antagonists, HIF stabilisers), targeting cellular destruction (eculizumab, sutimlimab). Pedagogical synthesis: erythrocyte physiology is one of the clearest examples in physiology of how an integrated system delivers a critical function (oxygenation) through multiple coordinated axes, each with specific regulators, and how disease maps cleanly to specific failure points. Modern haematology builds on this framework with increasingly precise therapy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.18].

Elaborative explanation: Erythrocyte physiology synthesises cell biology, biochemistry, endocrinology, and metabolism to deliver O2. Each axis (structure, Hb, EPO, iron) has dedicated regulators and clinical disorders. Modern haematology targets each level with increasingly precise therapy. The chapter integrates a vast body of clinical and basic science.

3. Each disease maps to specific design failure.
