QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the five phases of the ventricular cardiac action potential and explain how each phase contributes to function.

Model answer: (0) Rapid depolarisation - voltage-gated Nav1.5 opens; Na⁺ in (~ +30 mV); fast spike. (1) Initial repolarisation - Ito (transient outward K⁺) - notch. (2) Plateau (~ 200 ms) - inward Cav1.2 (L-type Ca²⁺) balanced by outward delayed-rectifier K⁺ (IKr, IKs); Ca²⁺ in supports CICR for contraction; long plateau prevents tetanus. (3) Repolarisation - Cav1.2 inactivates; IKr/IKs dominate; membrane returns to RMP. (4) Resting potential (~ -90 mV) - IK1 (inward rectifier) sets RMP; cardiomyocytes do not depolarise spontaneously (atria/ventricle). SA/AV nodes have no phase 4 stable rest - funny current (If, HCN channels) drives spontaneous diastolic depolarisation; reach threshold → automatic firing. Ca²⁺ enters during phase 0 of nodal cells (slow); not Na⁺. ECG correlates: P (atrial depol), QRS (ventricular phase 0), T (phase 3 repol). Drugs target specific channels: Class I Na (lidocaine, flecainide), Class III K (amiodarone, sotalol), Class IV Ca (verapamil, diltiazem). Long QT (genetic or drug-induced) prolonged repolarisation → torsades [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the conduction system - SA node to ventricles. How does the electrical pacemaker hierarchy work?

Model answer: _Conduction system_: SA node (right atrium near SVC) → atrial muscle and Bachmann bundle → AV node (interatrial septum) → bundle of His → bundle branches (right + left, left further to anterior + posterior fascicles) → Purkinje fibres → ventricular muscle apex-to-base. _Pacemaker hierarchy_ (intrinsic rates): SA 60-100/min; AV junction 40-60/min; ventricular Purkinje 20-40/min. Faster pacemaker dominates; if it fails, lower escapes. _AV node_ delays signal ~ 100 ms (allows atrial-to-ventricular filling). _Conduction velocity_: SA/AV slow (0.05 m/s - Ca²⁺-dependent phase 0); atria/ventricles fast (1 m/s); Purkinje very fast (2-4 m/s) - rapid synchronous ventricular contraction. _Autonomic modulation_: sympathetic increases rate (β1 → cAMP → If, ICaL up), AV conduction; parasympathetic decreases rate (M2 → Gi → reduced cAMP, K⁺ channels open hyperpolarising); resting heart rate dominated by vagal tone. Disorders: SA dysfunction (sick sinus - bradycardia, pauses, tachy-brady); AV blocks (1st - PR > 200 ms; 2nd Mobitz I - progressive PR; Mobitz II - dropped beat; 3rd complete AV dissociation - emergency, escape rhythm); fascicular blocks; bundle branch blocks; Wolff-Parkinson-White (accessory pathway); pacemaker indications [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

3. Sympathetic up; vagal down.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe cardiac autonomic control. How do sympathetic and parasympathetic systems regulate heart rate, contractility, and conduction?

Model answer: Cardiac autonomic regulation: (1) _Sympathetic_ (via SNS T1-T4) - postganglionic NA on β1 → Gs → cAMP → PKA → phosphorylation of: (a) HCN channels (faster If diastolic depolarisation, ↑ rate); (b) Cav1.2 (more open, ↑ Ca²⁺ entry, ↑ contractility, ↑ AV conduction); (c) Phospholamban (reduces SERCA inhibition → faster Ca²⁺ reuptake, ↑ relaxation rate = lusitropy); (d) Troponin I (reduces Ca²⁺ sensitivity, faster relaxation); (e) Ryanodine receptor (more Ca²⁺ release). Net: ↑ HR, ↑ contractility, ↑ relaxation, ↑ conduction. β2 secondary; α1 mild positive inotropy + vasoconstriction. (2) _Parasympathetic_ (vagus, predominantly atrial - SA, AV; sparse to ventricles) - ACh on M2 → Gi → reduced cAMP + K⁺ channels open (G-protein-gated K⁺ - GIRK) → hyperpolarisation. Net: ↓ HR (negative chronotropy), ↓ AV conduction (negative dromotropy), ↓ atrial contractility; minimal direct ventricular effect. _Resting tone_: vagal dominant (resting HR < intrinsic 100/min). Reciprocal modulation (typically). Adrenal medulla adds circulating adrenaline (β1, β2, α). Drugs: β-blockers (negative chronotropy, inotropy), atropine (parasympathetic block - ↑ HR), digoxin (vagal-enhancing + Na/K-ATPase inhibition), ivabradine (HCN inhibitor - ↓ HR without inotropic effect). Heart rate variability (HRV) reflects autonomic balance; reduced in heart failure, diabetic autonomic neuropathy, post-MI - mortality marker [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe excitation-contraction coupling in cardiac muscle. How does it differ from skeletal muscle?

Model answer: Cardiac ECC = CICR (Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release). Sequence: (1) AP propagates via gap junctions in intercalated disc to next cell; (2) Phase 0 depolarisation; (3) Phase 2 plateau - Cav1.2 (DHPR) at T-tubule opens (~ 25% of contractile Ca²⁺); (4) Trigger Ca²⁺ binds RyR2 across ~ 12 nm dyadic gap → opens RyR2 → SR Ca²⁺ release (~ 75%, calsequestrin-stored); (5) Cytosolic Ca²⁺ rises 10⁻⁷ → 10⁻⁶ M → binds troponin C → tropomyosin shifts → cross-bridge cycle. Relaxation: SERCA2a (regulated by phospholamban - β1-PKA reduces inhibition for faster reuptake = positive lusitropy); NCX (3 Na : 1 Ca; reverse mode possible); PMCA. _Skeletal muscle ECC_: mechanical coupling - DHPR (Cav1.1) directly mechanically opens RyR1 across triad; doesn't require Ca²⁺ entry. Differences: cardiac graded (more trigger Ca²⁺ → larger transient, modulable by β-adrenergic), skeletal all-or-none; cardiac slower; cardiac at dyad (Z line), skeletal at triad (A-I junction). _Clinical relevance_: digoxin (Na/K-ATPase inhibition raises intracellular Na → reduced NCX-mediated Ca²⁺ extrusion → ↑ SR load → ↑ contractility); calcium channel blockers (reduce Cav1.2 entry → ↓ contractility); CPVT - RyR2 mutations cause Ca²⁺ leak → triggered arrhythmias [Boron & Boulpaep ch.9].

3. β-adrenergic modulates cardiac, not skeletal.

Stem: Describe ECG basics - waves, intervals, axis, common abnormalities.

Model answer: _ECG waveform_: P (atrial depol), QRS (ventricular depol), T (ventricular repol), U (?Purkinje repol). _Intervals_: PR (0.12-0.20 s; AV nodal conduction), QRS (< 0.12 s; ventricular activation), QT (≤ 0.44 s, varies with rate; corrected QTc by Bazett), ST (isoelectric normally; elevation = injury, depression = ischaemia), RR (heart rate). _Lead system_: 3 limb leads (I, II, III), 3 augmented (aVR, aVL, aVF), 6 chest (V1-V6). Frontal plane axis: lead I + aVF quadrants - normal -30 to +90; LAD < -30 (LV hypertrophy, LBBB); RAD > +90 (RV hypertrophy, RBBB, posterior MI). _Common abnormalities_: STEMI (ST elevation in contiguous leads, reciprocal depression - emergency PCI/thrombolysis); NSTEMI (ST depression, T inversion); old MI (Q waves); LV hypertrophy (Sokolow-Lyon: SV1 + RV5/V6 > 35 mm); LBBB (QRS > 120, broad notched R V5/V6); RBBB (QRS > 120, RSR' V1, slurred S V5/V6); long QT (drug-induced or congenital - torsades risk); AF (irregularly irregular, no P waves); atrial flutter (sawtooth F waves, often 2:1 conduction); SVT (regular narrow complex, ~ 150-200); VT (regular wide complex - emergency); VF (chaotic, no organised activity - emergency); WPW (delta wave, short PR, slurred QRS upstroke); AV blocks (1st PR > 200, 2nd Mobitz I/II, 3rd dissociation); BBBs; pericarditis (saddle ST elevation in all leads); hyperkalaemia (peaked T → wide QRS → sine wave); pulmonary embolism (S1Q3T3 - rare, sinus tach common); Brugada syndrome (V1-V3 ST elevation, sudden death). Reading systematically: rate, rhythm, axis, intervals, P, QRS, ST, T waves [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe atrial fibrillation - mechanism, classification, and management.

Model answer: _Atrial fibrillation (AF)_ - most common sustained arrhythmia. Mechanism: re-entry circuits + ectopic foci (often pulmonary vein), atrial fibrosis, electrical and structural remodelling ('AF begets AF'). Risk factors: age (> 65 most cases), HTN, IHD, valvular (mitral), HFrEF/HFpEF, hyperthyroidism, alcohol, sleep apnoea, obesity, T2DM, family history (some monogenic), CKD, post-cardiac surgery. _Classification_: paroxysmal (< 7 days), persistent (> 7 days, electrical/pharmacological cardioversion to revert), long-standing persistent (> 1 year), permanent (no further attempts at SR). Valvular vs non-valvular (mitral stenosis or mechanical valve = valvular). Clinical: palpitations, dyspnoea, fatigue, dizziness, syncope; many asymptomatic (incidentally found); presents with stroke or HF. ECG: irregularly irregular, no discernible P, fibrillatory waves. _Risks_: thromboembolism (LA thrombus from stasis - left atrial appendage usually); stroke risk varies (CHA₂DS₂-VASc score: CHF, HTN, Age ≥ 75 (2), DM, prior Stroke (2), Vascular disease, Age 65-74, Sex female - ≥ 2 in men, ≥ 3 in women → anticoagulation indicated); HF (rate-related cardiomyopathy, loss of atrial kick). _Management_: Rate vs rhythm control (RACE, AFFIRM showed no mortality difference for most; rhythm control may be better in younger/symptomatic - EAST-AFNET 4): Rate - β-blockers, non-DHP CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem), digoxin; target HR < 110 lenient or < 80 strict (depends). Rhythm - DC cardioversion if unstable; antiarrhythmics (flecainide, amiodarone, dronedarone, sotalol); catheter ablation (pulmonary vein isolation) - especially paroxysmal/persistent, less successful in long-standing. _Anticoagulation_: DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran) preferred non-valvular AF; warfarin valvular or CKD severe; HAS-BLED score for bleeding risk (counsel, not preclude). Ablation reduces AF burden but anticoagulation continued based on stroke risk. Address modifiable risk factors (BP, weight, alcohol, sleep apnoea - LEGACY trial). LAA closure (Watchman) for patients unable to anticoagulate [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe ventricular arrhythmias - VT, VF, torsades. How are they recognised and managed?

Model answer: _Ventricular tachycardia (VT)_: ≥ 3 consecutive ventricular beats, > 100/min. Sustained > 30 s or haemodynamic compromise. Causes: structural heart disease (post-MI scar most common - re-entry around scar), cardiomyopathy, channelopathy (LQTS, Brugada, CPVT, ARVC), drugs, electrolytes (K, Mg). Monomorphic (single morphology, often scar-related re-entry) vs polymorphic (multiple morphologies; consider torsades, ischaemia). Diagnosis: wide QRS regular tachycardia; capture beats, fusion beats, AV dissociation favour VT over SVT with aberrancy. Management: (1) _Unstable_ - immediate DC cardioversion (synchronised); pulseless VT defib + CPR. (2) _Stable_ - amiodarone or lidocaine IV; reverse triggers (electrolytes, ischaemia, drugs). (3) Long-term: ICD if survived sudden cardiac death or high risk (ischaemic CM with EF ≤ 35% post-MI ≥ 40 days, NICM EF ≤ 35% NYHA II-III); β-blockers; antiarrhythmics; catheter ablation. _Ventricular fibrillation (VF)_: chaotic, disorganised; cardiac arrest; immediate defibrillation + CPR + ACLS. _Torsades de pointes_: polymorphic VT with twisting QRS axis on a background of long QT. Causes: congenital LQTS (KCNQ1, KCNH2, SCN5A; Romano-Ward AD; Jervell-Lange-Nielsen AR + deafness), drug-induced (many - antiarrhythmics, antibiotics - macrolides, fluoroquinolones; antipsychotics; antidepressants; methadone; antifungals; check QTDrugs.org); electrolyte (low K, Mg, Ca); bradycardia; structural. Recognise: long QT (> 500 ms concerning) + polymorphic VT. Management: (1) Magnesium sulphate IV (regardless of Mg level) - first-line; (2) Stop offending drug; (3) Correct electrolytes (K, Mg); (4) Pacing (overdrive) or isoproterenol if bradycardia-dependent; (5) DC cardioversion if unstable. Prevention: avoid QT-prolonging drug combinations; β-blocker (esp. nadolol) for LQTS; ICD for high-risk LQTS or post-aborted SCD. _Brugada syndrome_: SCN5A mutation (loss-of-function); type 1 ECG (V1-V2 ST elevation, downsloping); risk of polymorphic VT/VF and SCD; ICD if symptomatic. _CPVT_: RyR2 (or CASQ2) mutations; exercise/emotion-induced bidirectional VT in absence of structural disease; β-blocker (nadolol), ICD, sympathectomy. Modern arrhythmology: gene panel testing in unexplained SCD; ICD selection; ablation; medical optimisation [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 65-year-old presents with chest pain, ST elevation in II, III, aVF. Decompose acute MI management.

Model answer: _ST-elevation MI (STEMI)_: ECG ST elevation in ≥ 2 contiguous leads + appropriate symptoms = emergency. Inferior MI (II, III, aVF) usually right coronary; check right-sided leads (V4R) for RV infarct (preload-dependent - cautious nitrates, fluids). Diagnosis: clinical + ECG + troponin (rises 3-6h, peaks 24h, falls 7-14 days). Differential: aortic dissection (CT immediately if suspicion), pericarditis, PE, oesophageal. _Reperfusion - door-to-balloon < 90 min if PCI available, door-to-needle < 30 min if thrombolysis_. (1) Primary PCI - preferred when available; mortality benefit. (2) Thrombolysis - if PCI not available within 120 min from first medical contact; tenecteplase or alteplase; contraindications - active bleeding, recent surgery/stroke/trauma, severe HTN, etc. _Adjunctive therapy_: (a) DAPT - aspirin loading 300 mg + ticagrelor 180 mg (or prasugrel 60 mg post-PCI; clopidogrel 600 mg if older or higher bleeding risk); continue 12 months (longer/shorter individualised). (b) Anticoagulation acute - heparin (UFH or LMWH) or bivalirudin during PCI. (c) Statin high-dose (atorvastatin 80 mg). (d) ACE-inhibitor (or ARB) - reduces remodelling. (e) β-blocker - reduces ischaemia, mortality. (f) Pain - opioids (morphine - watch hypotension, nausea); nitrates (avoid in RV infarct). (g) Oxygen if SpO2 < 90% (not routine - excess O2 may harm). _Complications_: arrhythmia (VT/VF early - cardioverter, lidocaine; AV blocks especially inferior MI - atropine, pacing); LV failure / cardiogenic shock (Killip class IV; vasopressors, IABP, ECMO); mechanical (papillary muscle rupture - acute MR; VSD - new murmur day 3-7; free wall rupture - tamponade); recurrent ischaemia; pericarditis (Dressler late). Long-term: cardiac rehabilitation, lifestyle (smoking, diet, exercise), DAPT 12 months, lifelong aspirin + statin + β-blocker + ACE-I, address risk factors aggressively, defibrillator if EF ≤ 35% post 40 days [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

3. Inferior MI - watch for RV (avoid nitrates), AV block.

Stem: A 75-year-old with palpitations and irregular pulse, ECG shows AF. Decompose AF stroke prevention.

Model answer: _AF stroke prevention_: stratify risk → anticoagulate appropriately. (1) Confirm diagnosis - 12-lead ECG; consider extended monitoring if paroxysmal suspected. (2) Determine valvular vs non-valvular - mitral stenosis or mechanical valve = warfarin; otherwise = non-valvular. (3) _CHA₂DS₂-VASc score_: CHF (1), HTN (1), Age ≥ 75 (2), DM (1), prior Stroke/TIA/embolism (2), Vascular disease (1), Age 65-74 (1), Sex (female 1 if other risk factors). Anticoagulation indicated: men ≥ 2, women ≥ 3 (or ≥ 1 in male, ≥ 2 in female with non-sex risk factor; modern guidance simplified). 0 - none; 1 (men)/2 (women, all non-sex points) - consider; ≥ 2 (or 3) - DOAC. (4) _Bleeding risk_: HAS-BLED score (HTN, Abnormal renal/hepatic, Stroke, Bleeding, Labile INR, Elderly, Drugs/alcohol; ≥ 3 high bleeding risk - counsel and monitor, NOT preclude anticoagulation in most cases unless reversible factor). (5) _Choice_: DOACs preferred non-valvular AF (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran) - similar/better stroke prevention, less ICH, no monitoring; warfarin if valvular/severe CKD/triple-positive APLS. (6) _Special situations_: pregnancy - LMWH; severe CKD CrCl < 15 - apixaban/warfarin (some evidence for apixaban in dialysis); high bleeding risk despite need - LAA closure (Watchman device). (7) Address modifiable risk factors: BP control, weight loss, alcohol moderation, sleep apnoea treatment - reduces AF burden (LEGACY). (8) Rate or rhythm control as secondary - β-blockers, non-DHP CCBs, digoxin (rate); flecainide, amiodarone, dronedarone (rhythm); ablation. (9) Cardioversion - if recent onset and unstable, immediate; if stable, anticoagulate ≥ 3 weeks before or TOE-guided. Continue anticoagulation after rhythm restoration based on CHA₂DS₂-VASc. (10) Reversal: idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet for Xa; PCC + vit K for warfarin. Emerging: cabozantinib reduces AF, mendelian randomization shows weight loss reduces AF [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

QUESTION 10
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 60-year-old with progressive dyspnoea, leg swelling, and orthopnoea. Decompose heart failure with reduced ejection fraction.

Model answer: _HFrEF_ - EF ≤ 40%; clinical syndrome of cardiac dysfunction. Causes: ischaemic (most common - post-MI), hypertensive, idiopathic dilated, valvular (chronic regurgitation), tachycardia-induced, alcoholic, peripartum, viral myocarditis, infiltrative (amyloid, sarcoid), genetic (titin, MYH7, etc), arrhythmogenic, iron overload. Pathophysiology: contractile dysfunction → ↓ CO → neurohormonal activation (RAAS, sympathetic, ADH) → fluid retention + vasoconstriction → initially compensates but worsens long-term (LV remodelling, fibrosis, dysfunction). Symptoms: fatigue, exercise intolerance, dyspnoea on exertion, orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea, peripheral oedema, RUQ discomfort (hepatic congestion), nocturia. Signs: raised JVP, S3, displaced apex, basal crackles, hepatomegaly, ascites, oedema, cool peripheries (low CO). Diagnosis: BNP/NT-proBNP raised; ECG (LBBB common, prior MI, LVH); CXR (cardiomegaly, pulmonary oedema, Kerley B lines); echo (EF, chamber sizes, wall motion, valvular, RV function, diastolic function). Cause workup: troponin, iron studies, TFT, ANA if young, viral serology, cardiac MRI for cause, coronary angiography. Management - guideline-directed medical therapy ('the four pillars' for all HFrEF unless contraindicated): (1) ACE-I or ARB (or ARNI - sacubitril/valsartan); (2) β-blocker (carvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate, nebivolol); (3) MRA (spironolactone, eplerenone); (4) SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) - mortality reduction even non-diabetic. Plus: loop diuretics (furosemide) for symptom relief - not mortality. _ARNI_ - replace ACE-I/ARB if tolerated; greater mortality benefit (PARADIGM-HF). _Vericiguat_ (sGC stimulator) for worsening HF. _Devices_: CRT (biventricular pacing) if EF ≤ 35% + LBBB + QRS ≥ 130 ms; ICD primary prevention if EF ≤ 35% NYHA II-III; combination CRT-D. _Advanced therapies_: LVAD (bridge or destination), heart transplant. _Lifestyle_: salt restriction, fluid restriction in advanced, exercise rehabilitation (cardiac rehab improves outcomes), weight, smoking cessation, alcohol moderation, vaccinations. _Acute decompensation_: IV diuretics, oxygen, NIV, vasodilators if BP allows, inotropes if cardiogenic shock. Modern HFrEF management has dramatically improved outcomes [Boron & Boulpaep ch.21].

Stem: Synthesise this chapter: how do cardiac electrophysiology, conduction, and arrhythmias interact, and what design principles emerge?
