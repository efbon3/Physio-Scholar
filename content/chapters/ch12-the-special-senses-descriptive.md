Stem: Describe the complete sequence of events in rod photoreceptor phototransduction. Why do photoreceptors hyperpolarise rather than depolarise in response to light?

Model answer: Phototransduction in rods is one of the most studied G-protein cascades in biology. The full sequence:

1. Dark state:

- Rods continuously synthesise cGMP via guanylyl cyclase.
- cGMP keeps CNG (cyclic nucleotide-gated) cation channels open in the outer segment plasma membrane.
- Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ enter through these channels — the dark current.
- Membrane potential remains relatively depolarised (~−40 mV).
- This tonic depolarisation drives continuous glutamate release at the photoreceptor's axon terminal (synapse with bipolar cells).

2. Light absorption:

- Photon absorbed by 11-cis-retinal chromophore in rhodopsin (a 7-transmembrane GPCR).
- Retinal isomerises to all-trans-retinal.
- This conformational change activates rhodopsin (now called metarhodopsin II).

3. G-protein activation:

- Activated rhodopsin catalyses GDP/GTP exchange on transducin (Gαt).
- One activated rhodopsin can activate ~500 transducin molecules — providing the first stage of amplification.

4. cGMP-PDE activation:

- GTP-bound Gαt activates cGMP phosphodiesterase (PDE6) by displacing its inhibitory γ subunit.
- One activated PDE6 hydrolyses many cGMP molecules per second — second stage of amplification.

5. cGMP fall:

- Cytosolic cGMP concentration drops rapidly.
- CNG channels close because cGMP is no longer bound.
- Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ entry stops.

6. Hyperpolarisation:

- With reduced cation entry but continued K⁺ efflux through other channels, membrane potential becomes more negative (~−70 mV).
- Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels at the synaptic terminal close.
- Glutamate release decreases.

7. Bipolar cell response:

- ON-bipolar cells have mGluR6 (metabotropic glutamate receptors); glutamate normally hyperpolarises them. Reduced glutamate → ON-bipolar cells depolarise → activate.
- OFF-bipolar cells have ionotropic glutamate receptors; glutamate normally depolarises them. Reduced glutamate → OFF-bipolar cells hyperpolarise → silenced.
- Different ganglion cell types receive ON or OFF input, creating the parallel ON/OFF pathways.

8. Recovery (light → dark transition):

- Rhodopsin kinase phosphorylates active rhodopsin → arrestin binds → rhodopsin inactivated.
- GTPase activating proteins (GAPs) accelerate Gαt's intrinsic GTPase activity → Gαt-GDP → reactivates PDE6 inhibitor.
- Guanylyl cyclase activity increases (Ca²⁺ falls because no Ca²⁺ entry; low Ca²⁺ activates GC) → cGMP rises again.

Stem: Trace the visual pathway from retina to primary visual cortex. How does each visual field defect map to a specific anatomical lesion?

Model answer: The visual pathway has a remarkably orderly anatomical organisation that allows clinical localisation of lesions from visual field testing alone.

The pathway (from periphery to cortex):

1. Retinal photoreceptors (rods + cones) → bipolar cells → ganglion cells.

2. Retinal ganglion cell axons form the optic nerve. ~1 million axons per nerve.

3. Optic chiasm — Major decussation site. Nasal retinal fibres (carrying images of the temporal visual field) cross to the contralateral side. Temporal retinal fibres (carrying nasal visual field) remain ipsilateral.

4. Optic tract — Each tract now carries information from the contralateral visual hemifield.

5. Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of the thalamus — Six layers, magnocellular (motion), parvocellular (colour, fine detail), koniocellular (blue-yellow).

6. Optic radiations — Wide white-matter tract from LGN to V1.

- Inferior optic radiations (Meyer's loop) — Pass through temporal lobe → represent superior visual field.
- Superior optic radiations — Pass through parietal lobe → represent inferior visual field.

7. Primary visual cortex (V1) — Calcarine sulcus, occipital lobe. Tonotopically organised representation of contralateral visual hemifield.

Optic nerve (anterior to chiasm) — Monocular blindness of the affected eye.

Stem: Describe the molecular and cellular basis of cochlear function. How is frequency analysed, and why are outer hair cells (OHCs) called "active mechanical amplifiers"?

Model answer: The cochlea is a remarkable organ — performing real-time spectral decomposition of acoustic signals while simultaneously amplifying low-level sounds. Key features:

1. Tonotopic organisation of the basilar membrane:

The basilar membrane has graded mechanical properties:

- Base (near oval window): narrow, stiff → resonant at high frequencies (~20 kHz).
- Apex: wide, compliant → resonant at low frequencies (~20 Hz).

When sound enters via stapes oscillating on oval window:

- A travelling wave propagates along basilar membrane.
- The wave grows in amplitude as it travels.
- It reaches maximum amplitude at the frequency-specific resonant location, then decays beyond.

This produces frequency analysis (spectral decomposition) into spatial location — the place principle of frequency coding.

Tonotopy is preserved at every subsequent stage:

- Cochlear nerve (each fibre tuned to a characteristic frequency).
- Cochlear nuclei.
- Superior olive.
- Inferior colliculus.
- Medial geniculate.
- Primary auditory cortex (Heschl's gyrus).

2. Hair cell architecture:

Inner hair cells (IHCs):

- ~3,500 in single row.
- Pear-shaped.
- Flask-shaped synaptic ribbons release glutamate at extraordinary rates (~1000 vesicles/s sustainable).
- ~10–20 afferent CN VIII fibres per IHC.
- ~95% of CN VIII afferents synapse on IHCs.
- These are the primary sensors — true auditory transducers.

Outer hair cells (OHCs):

- ~12,000 in 3 rows.
- Cylindrical.
- ~10% of afferents synapse on them; mostly receive efferent input (from medial olivocochlear bundle).
- These are active amplifiers rather than primary sensors.

3. Mechanoelectrical transduction:

In response to basilar membrane vibration:

- Stereocilia of hair cells deflect against the tectorial membrane.
- Deflection toward the tallest stereocilium opens mechanotransduction channels (likely TMC1/TMC2) in shorter stereocilia.
- Channels are opened by tip links (myosin VIIa, USH1C, USH2A protein complex) connecting adjacent stereocilia tip-to-side.
- K⁺ enters from endolymph (high K⁺, +80 mV endocochlear potential drives entry).
- Ca²⁺ also enters.
- Hair cell depolarises → glutamate release at IHC base → CN VIII afferent firing.

Mutations in tip-link proteins (cadherin-23, protocadherin-15) cause Usher syndrome (hearing loss + retinitis pigmentosa).

Model answer: Answer key (summary):

QUESTION 7
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Why are special senses called "special" — what distinguishes them from somatic and visceral senses?

Model answer: Answer key (summary):

Special senses (vision, hearing, taste, smell, vestibular) are mediated by:

- Specialised peripheral receptors in dedicated end organs (eye, ear, tongue, nose).
- Cranial nerve afferents (CN I-VIII) directly to brainstem nuclei.
- Largely centralised processing with multiple parallel pathways.
- Conscious perception of complex multidimensional information.

Distinguished from somatic senses (skin, joint, muscle) which use:

- Receptors distributed throughout the body.
- Spinal nerve afferents.
- Pathway through dorsal columns or spinothalamic.

And visceral senses which:

- Are largely subconscious.
- Carry homeostatic and reflex information.
- Project to NTS and autonomic nuclei.

The "special" designation reflects the evolutionary investment in dedicated, sophisticated sensory organs for accessing distant information (vision/hearing) and for chemical assessment of food and air (taste/smell). These provide critical survival and behavioural information that requires elaborate neural processing.

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 60-year-old presents with painless central visual loss in the right eye over weeks. Fundoscopy shows drusen and pigmentary changes at the macula. Decompose this presentation.

Model answer: This is _age-related macular degeneration (AMD)_ — the leading cause of central visual loss in older adults in developed countries. Two forms: (1) _Dry (atrophic) AMD_ — ~85% of cases; drusen (extracellular deposits between RPE and Bruch's membrane), RPE atrophy, gradual progression; treatment limited (AREDS vitamin formulation: vitamins C, E, zinc, copper, lutein, zeaxanthin slows progression in moderate disease). (2) _Wet (neovascular) AMD_ — ~15% of cases; choroidal neovascularisation breaks through Bruch's membrane → subretinal haemorrhage, exudate, fibrosis; rapid central vision loss; treatment with intravitreal anti-VEGF agents (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab, faricimab) has revolutionised outcomes. Risk factors: age, smoking, family history, hypertension, white ethnicity, complement factor H polymorphisms. Clinical features: central scotoma, distorted vision (metamorphopsia — straight lines look wavy on Amsler grid), reduced visual acuity. Investigation: dilated fundoscopy, optical coherence tomography (OCT) for retinal architecture, fluorescein angiography for neovascularisation. Peripheral vision and navigation are preserved [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: AMD illustrates why the macula (high cone density, foveal pit, watershed blood supply) is uniquely vulnerable to age-related and oxidative stress. Anti-VEGF therapy demonstrates that understanding pathophysiology (VEGF-driven choroidal neovascularisation) directly produces effective treatment.

Hints:

1. Distinguish dry vs wet AMD by mechanism and treatment.
2. Drusen + pigmentary change at macula is dry AMD.
3. Anti-VEGF therapy revolutionised wet AMD outcomes.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names AMD as the diagnosis with central scotoma and metamorphopsia.
- Distinguishes dry (drusen, atrophy) from wet (neovascular) forms.
- Names anti-VEGF agents for wet AMD and AREDS vitamins for dry AMD.
- Identifies risk factors (age, smoking, family history).

A Yellow answer covers AMD but misses the wet/dry distinction or anti-VEGF therapy.

- "AMD causes total blindness": Correction: AMD causes _central_ vision loss; peripheral vision is preserved, allowing navigation.
- "All AMD is treatable with anti-VEGF": Correction: Anti-VEGF helps _wet_ AMD; dry AMD is managed with AREDS vitamins, lifestyle, and visual aids.

QUESTION 9
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: A 65-year-old diabetic with progressive blurred vision has bilateral lens opacities on slit-lamp. Decompose cataract.

Model answer: _Cataract_ — opacification of the crystalline lens; the world's leading cause of reversible blindness. Pathophysiology: lens proteins (crystallins) progressively oxidised, glycated, and aggregate; lens fibres disorganise; transparency lost. Risk factors: ageing (most common cause; ~60% of those over 70), diabetes (sorbitol accumulation via aldose reductase → osmotic damage), corticosteroids, UV exposure, smoking, trauma, congenital (e.g., galactosaemia). Types: nuclear, cortical, posterior subcapsular (steroid- and diabetes-associated). Clinical features: gradual painless visual loss, glare, halos, reduced contrast sensitivity, second-sight phenomenon (refractive shift toward myopia in nuclear cataract). Treatment: surgical phacoemulsification with intraocular lens (IOL) implantation — among the most successful operations in medicine; visual rehabilitation often complete. Toric IOLs correct astigmatism; multifocal IOLs reduce reading-glasses dependence [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: Cataract is a structural failure of lens transparency. Modern phacoemulsification with IOL implantation has transformed it from a major cause of blindness to a routine outpatient procedure with rapid visual recovery — a triumph of physiology-driven surgical design.

Hints:

1. Lens proteins lose transparency from oxidation/aggregation.
2. Diabetes accelerates via aldose reductase/sorbitol pathway.
3. Phacoemulsification + IOL is curative.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names cataract and the lens-protein opacification mechanism.
- Identifies ageing and diabetes as major risk factors.
- Names phacoemulsification + IOL as definitive treatment.
- Mentions ≥ 2 specific risk factors beyond ageing.

A Yellow answer covers cataract but misses the diabetic mechanism or surgical treatment.

A Red answer confuses cataract with refractive error.

Common misconceptions:

- "Cataract causes pain": Correction: Cataract is _painless_; pain suggests other pathology (uveitis, acute glaucoma, corneal disease).
- "All visual loss in elderly is cataract": Correction: Differential includes AMD, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy; comprehensive eye exam is essential.

QUESTION 10
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 25-year-old presents with sudden monocular visual loss accompanied by pain on eye movement. Fundoscopy is normal. Decompose this presentation.

Model answer: This is _optic neuritis_ — most often idiopathic or as a presenting feature of multiple sclerosis. Pathophysiology: inflammation of the optic nerve (typically retrobulbar — fundoscopy normal because the optic disc is not directly inflamed; if anterior, the disc is swollen — papillitis). Mechanism: T-cell-mediated autoimmune demyelination, often anti-MOG or anti-AQP4 (NMO spectrum) when not classical MS. Clinical features: subacute monocular vision loss over hours-days; pain on eye movement (~90%); reduced visual acuity, colour vision (red desaturation), contrast sensitivity, central scotoma; relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD — Marcus Gunn pupil) — paradoxical dilation of the affected pupil with swinging-flashlight test because afferent input is weaker; Uhthoff phenomenon — symptoms worsen with heat. Investigation: MRI brain + orbits with contrast (T2 hyperintensity, gadolinium enhancement of the optic nerve; MS plaques in periventricular white matter); visual evoked potentials (delayed); CSF (oligoclonal bands in MS); aquaporin-4 and MOG antibodies (NMOSD differential). Treatment: high-dose IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day for 3 days (followed by oral taper) — accelerates recovery; does not change long-term visual outcome but reduces MS relapse rate short-term. ~60% recover good acuity; permanent colour and contrast deficits common; recurrence common. Long-term: ~50% develop MS over 15 years [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: Optic neuritis is the classic example of how an inflammatory CNS demyelinating lesion produces a striking clinical picture mappable to the affected pathway. RAPD is a powerful bedside sign of unilateral optic nerve disease; Uhthoff phenomenon links demyelination to thermal sensitivity of conduction.

Hints:

1. Sudden monocular vision loss + pain on eye movement.
2. RAPD on swinging-flashlight test.
3. Often a first presentation of MS.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names optic neuritis and links to MS.
- Identifies the RAPD as a key bedside sign and explains its mechanism.
- Names MRI as the key investigation.
- Names methylprednisolone as the treatment.
- Mentions MS conversion risk over years.

A Yellow answer makes the diagnosis but misses the RAPD or the MS link.

A Red answer treats this as a refractive error or cataract.

Common misconceptions:

- "Pain on eye movement is typical of all optic disease": Correction: Pain on eye movement is _characteristic_ of retrobulbar optic neuritis; cataract, glaucoma, and AMD are typically painless.
- "Steroids cure optic neuritis": Correction: Steroids accelerate recovery but do _not_ improve final visual outcome; they may modestly reduce short-term MS relapses.

Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply

Stem: A 70-year-old with poorly controlled hypertension presents with sudden painless complete monocular vision loss. Fundoscopy shows retinal pallor and a cherry-red spot. Decompose this presentation.

Model answer: This is _central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO)_ — an ophthalmic emergency analogous to ischaemic stroke, producing inner-retinal infarction. Pathophysiology: occlusion of the central retinal artery (a branch of the ophthalmic artery, itself a branch of the internal carotid artery) by embolus (most common — carotid plaque, cardiac source) or thrombosis. The inner two-thirds of the retina (ganglion cells, inner nuclear layer) lose their blood supply and infarct within ~90 minutes; the outer retina (photoreceptors) is supplied by the choroid and survives. Cherry-red spot: the fovea is thin and the underlying intact choroid shows through against the surrounding pale infarcted retina. Clinical: sudden painless visual loss; severely reduced acuity (count fingers or worse); RAPD; pale retina; cherry-red spot; arteriolar 'box-car' segmentation. Investigation: ESR/CRP urgently to exclude _giant cell arteritis_ (GCA) — a treatable cause; carotid Doppler; echocardiogram; vascular risk assessment. Acute treatment: ocular massage (may dislodge embolus), reduce IOP (acetazolamide, mannitol), anterior chamber paracentesis, IV thrombolysis within 4.5 h has been considered (controversial); typically poor visual outcome regardless. Most important: this is a _stroke_ — full neurovascular workup, 30-50% develop other vascular events; secondary prevention with antiplatelets, statin, BP control, and treatment of any underlying source [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: CRAO is the eye's equivalent of ischaemic stroke. Even when the eye outcome is poor, the patient is at high risk of brain stroke — workup and secondary prevention are essential. Giant cell arteritis must be excluded (different urgent treatment).

1. Sudden painless monocular blindness + cherry-red spot.
2. Inner retinal infarction; outer retina spared by choroid.
3. Treat as a stroke — workup and secondary prevention.

A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names CRAO and the mechanism (embolic occlusion of central retinal artery).
- Explains the cherry-red spot anatomically.
- Names GCA as a critical differential to exclude with ESR/CRP.
- Mentions stroke workup and secondary prevention.
- Notes the typically poor visual prognosis.

A Yellow answer makes the diagnosis but misses the GCA differential or stroke workup.

A Red answer treats this as optic neuritis or AMD.

QUESTION 12
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 65-year-old presents with progressive painless visual field loss. Tonometry: IOP 28 mm Hg. Cup-to-disc ratio 0.8 (asymmetric). Decompose primary open-angle glaucoma.

Model answer: _Primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG)_ — the second most common cause of irreversible blindness worldwide. Pathophysiology: chronic optic neuropathy from progressive retinal ganglion cell loss; raised IOP is the principal modifiable risk factor (normal 10-21 mm Hg; consistently > 21 in glaucoma, but normal-tension glaucoma exists with normal IOP). Mechanism of raised IOP: aqueous humour produced by ciliary body epithelium drains through the trabecular meshwork at the iridocorneal angle; in POAG, the meshwork stiffens/blocks, raising IOP. The angle is _open_ on gonioscopy (vs angle-closure glaucoma where the iris physically blocks drainage). Optic nerve damage: raised IOP and impaired axonal transport at the lamina cribrosa → ganglion cell death → optic disc cupping (cup-to-disc ratio rises) and progressive visual field loss starting peripherally (arcuate scotoma, nasal step), eventually involving central vision. Clinical: typically asymptomatic until late; bilateral but often asymmetric; family history common; African ethnicity higher risk; older age. Investigation: tonometry (IOP), gonioscopy (open angle), fundoscopy and OCT (cup-to-disc ratio, RNFL thickness), automated visual field testing (Humphrey perimetry — arcuate defects, nasal steps, paracentral scotomas). Treatment: lower IOP. (1) Topical drops — first-line: prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost, travoprost) increase uveoscleral outflow; β-blockers (timolol) reduce aqueous production; α2 agonists (brimonidine), carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide). (2) Selective laser trabeculoplasty (SLT). (3) Surgical: trabeculectomy, MIGS (minimally invasive glaucoma surgery), tube shunt. Adherence is critical; most blindness from glaucoma is preventable with screening + therapy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: POAG is silent until late and irreversible once optic nerve fibres are lost. Screening (tonometry, fundoscopy) in those over 40 (especially with family history, African ethnicity, or diabetes) is the cornerstone of preventing glaucoma blindness.

Hints:

1. Open angle + raised IOP + optic disc cupping.
2. Asymmetric cupping is highly suspicious.
3. Lower IOP with prostaglandin analogues first-line.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names POAG and the mechanism (trabecular meshwork dysfunction).
- Distinguishes open-angle from closed-angle.
- Identifies cup-to-disc ratio as a key sign.
- Names ≥ 2 IOP-lowering drug classes (prostaglandin analogues, β-blockers).
- Mentions surgical/laser options for refractory cases.

A Yellow answer covers POAG but misses the open vs closed angle distinction or specific drug classes.

A Red answer confuses POAG with acute angle-closure.

Common misconceptions:

- "All glaucoma has raised IOP": Correction: Normal-tension glaucoma has IOP < 21 but progressive optic neuropathy; ~25% of glaucoma.
- "Symptoms warn of glaucoma early": Correction: POAG is asymptomatic until late; screening is essential — symptoms appear after irreversible damage.

QUESTION 13
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 50-year-old with vertigo, hearing loss, and tinnitus has episodes lasting hours that recur over years. Decompose Ménière disease.

Model answer: _Ménière disease_ — episodic inner-ear disorder defined by the triad of vertigo, fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss, and tinnitus, often with aural fullness. Pathophysiology: endolymphatic hydrops — distension of the membranous labyrinth (cochlear duct, saccule, utricle) by excess endolymph due to impaired absorption at the endolymphatic sac. Mechanical distortion and rupture of Reissner membrane mix endolymph (high K⁺) with perilymph (low K⁺), depolarising hair cells and producing acute symptoms; recovery over hours-days. Clinical: episodic vertigo (20 min - 24 h, with N/V); fluctuating low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss progressing over years; roaring tinnitus; aural fullness; +/- drop attacks (Tumarkin crises). Diagnosis: AAO-HNS criteria (≥ 2 spontaneous vertigo episodes ≥ 20 min; audiometrically documented low- to mid-frequency SNHL in the affected ear; fluctuating aural symptoms; other causes excluded). Investigation: pure-tone audiometry, MRI to exclude vestibular schwannoma. Management: (1) Lifestyle — low-salt diet (~1.5 g/day), reduce caffeine and alcohol, manage stress; (2) Diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide + amiloride) reduce endolymph volume; (3) Acute episode — antiemetics (prochlorperazine), antihistamines (cinnarizine), benzodiazepines short-term; (4) Refractory: intratympanic gentamicin (chemical labyrinthectomy — selectively damages vestibular hair cells, sparing hearing), intratympanic steroids, endolymphatic sac decompression, vestibular nerve section, or labyrinthectomy. Hearing aids for SNHL [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: Ménière disease illustrates the consequences of disrupted endolymph homeostasis. The mechanical distortion-rupture-recovery cycle explains the episodic nature; chronic hydrops explains the progressive hearing loss. Treatment hierarchy targets the underlying pathophysiology.

Hints:

1. Vertigo + SNHL + tinnitus + aural fullness.
2. Endolymphatic hydrops is the proposed mechanism.
3. Low-salt diet + diuretics first-line.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names Ménière disease and the triad.
- Names endolymphatic hydrops as the mechanism.
- Identifies the AAO-HNS criteria for diagnosis.
- Names ≥ 2 management modalities (low-salt + diuretics; intratympanic gentamicin/steroids).
- Mentions audiometry as essential investigation.

A Yellow answer covers Ménière but misses the hydrops mechanism or specific treatments.

A Red answer confuses Ménière with BPPV or vestibular neuritis.

Common misconceptions:

- "All vertigo is Ménière": Correction: Differential is broad — BPPV (brief positional), vestibular neuritis (acute prolonged, no hearing loss), labyrinthitis (with hearing loss but acute self-limiting), vestibular migraine, schwannoma.
- "Surgery is first-line": Correction: Surgery is reserved for refractory disease; lifestyle, diuretics, and intratympanic therapy precede it.

QUESTION 14
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Why is the visual system organised as it is — with retinotopy, parallel pathways (M and P), V1 columnar architecture, and dorsal/ventral streams? What functional benefits do these design features confer?

Model answer: The visual system illustrates several deep design principles. (1) _Retinotopy_ preserves spatial relationships from retina → LGN → V1 → higher areas. Benefit: spatial computations (motion, depth, edge detection) can be done locally without complex remapping; lesions produce predictable visual field defects. (2) _Parallel pathways_ — magnocellular (M) and parvocellular (P) and koniocellular (K) streams from retina through LGN to V1 carry different information types. M (large RFs, transient response, achromatic): motion and depth (fast). P (small RFs, sustained response, red-green): form and colour. K: blue-yellow colour. Benefit: simultaneous processing of multiple visual attributes without temporal serialisation. (3) _V1 columnar architecture_ — ocular dominance columns (input from each eye), orientation columns (preferred line orientation), blob/interblob (colour vs form). Benefit: local proximity of related computations; efficient wiring; supports binocular fusion and stereopsis. (4) _Dorsal vs ventral streams_ — dorsal (V1 → MT → parietal): "where/how" — motion, action, spatial relationships. Ventral (V1 → V4 → IT → temporal): "what" — object recognition, faces, colour. Benefit: division of labour between perception-for-action (dorsal) and perception-for-recognition (ventral). Patient evidence: dorsal lesions → optic ataxia (cannot reach for visible objects); ventral lesions → visual agnosia (can see but not recognise). (5) _Hierarchical processing_ — V1 simple cells (oriented edges) → complex cells (motion-invariant edges) → V2/V4 (more complex shapes) → IT (objects, faces). Benefit: progressively abstract representations; supports perceptual constancies (recognising an object across rotations, sizes, lighting). (6) _Cortical magnification_ — disproportionate cortical area for fovea (high-acuity reading and face recognition). Benefit: high-resolution analysis where attention is directed; matches the high cone density of the fovea. (7) _Top-down modulation_ — attention, expectation, and context modulate activity even at V1. Benefit: efficient processing biased by behavioural relevance [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15; Berne & Levy ch.7].

Elaborative explanation: These design features are not accidental — they reflect evolutionary solutions to the visual system's core problem: extracting useful information from a 2D array of photons in real time. The brain divides the problem into parallel, hierarchical, retinotopic, attention-modulated computations. Visual neuroscience provides perhaps the clearest example of how brain architecture matches computational demands.

Hints:

1. Retinotopy + parallel + columnar + streams + magnification + top-down.
2. Each principle solves a specific computational problem.
3. Lesions reveal the architecture (optic ataxia, agnosia, achromatopsia).

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies retinotopy and its computational benefit.
- Distinguishes M and P pathways with their information types.
- Names dorsal (where/how) and ventral (what) streams.
- Mentions ≥ 2 V1 columnar features (ocular dominance, orientation).
- Connects each design feature to a functional benefit.
- Cites lesion evidence (optic ataxia, agnosia).

A Yellow answer covers some design features but misses the hierarchical or stream architecture.

A Red answer treats vision as a single linear pathway.

Common misconceptions:

- "Vision is a single pathway from eye to V1.": Correction: Vision involves parallel streams, hierarchical areas, and dorsal/ventral specialisations beyond V1.
- "The fovea is just a small spot.": Correction: The fovea has disproportionate cortical magnification (~ 50% of V1 representing the central ~ 10° of the visual field).

QUESTION 15
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 40-year-old presents with sudden vertigo, nausea, and gait instability that has lasted for 3 days. Hearing is preserved. Decompose vestibular neuritis vs labyrinthitis.

Model answer: _Vestibular neuritis_ — acute viral or post-viral inflammation of the vestibular nerve (often vestibulocochlear nerve; viral reactivation, often HSV-1, in the vestibular ganglion). Hearing is _preserved_ (vs labyrinthitis where the cochlea is also inflamed). Clinical: acute spontaneous vertigo with nausea/vomiting lasting 1-3 days, gradually improving over weeks; spontaneous nystagmus (horizontal-rotatory, fast phase away from affected side); gait instability; head-impulse test positive (corrective saccades when head turned toward affected side - VOR failure). No hearing loss, no tinnitus, no aural fullness, no neurological signs (vs central vertigo from stroke/MS). HINTS examination (Head Impulse, Nystagmus pattern, Test of Skew) distinguishes peripheral from central: peripheral - positive head-impulse, unidirectional horizontal nystagmus, no skew deviation; central (suspect stroke/cerebellar/brainstem) - negative head-impulse (VOR intact), bidirectional or vertical nystagmus, present skew deviation. _Labyrinthitis_ - similar but with cochlear involvement (sensorineural hearing loss + tinnitus); same management. Investigation: MRI brain if any central features or risk factors; audiometry if labyrinthitis; ENT/neurology referral. Management: (1) Acute - antiemetics (prochlorperazine, ondansetron), vestibular sedatives (diazepam, meclizine) for the first 24-72 hours only - prolonged use _delays_ vestibular compensation; (2) Brief course of corticosteroids (methylprednisolone) may speed recovery; (3) Vestibular rehabilitation therapy from day 3-5 promotes central compensation; (4) Avoid prolonged bed rest. Recovery: most patients improve over weeks; ~10-30% have persistent imbalance requiring vestibular rehab; recurrence rare. Distinction from BPPV: BPPV is brief (< 1 minute) positional vertigo from otoconia in semicircular canals; treated with Epley manoeuvre [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: Acute vertigo is a classic clinical decompositional challenge: peripheral (benign, self-limiting) vs central (potentially catastrophic). HINTS bedside examination is more sensitive than early MRI for posterior circulation stroke. Vestibular rehabilitation harnesses central compensation - the brain's ability to recalibrate balance using vision and proprioception when vestibular input is asymmetric.

Hints:

1. Acute prolonged vertigo + preserved hearing = vestibular neuritis.
2. HINTS exam distinguishes peripheral from central.
3. Avoid prolonged vestibular sedatives - delays compensation.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names vestibular neuritis vs labyrinthitis on hearing involvement.
- Describes the HINTS exam and its components.
- Identifies head-impulse positivity as a peripheral sign.
- Names early antiemetics + brief sedatives + vestibular rehab.
- Mentions BPPV differential (brief positional).

A Yellow answer covers neuritis but misses the HINTS exam or central differential.

A Red answer treats this as Ménière or BPPV.

Common misconceptions:

- "All acute vertigo is benign": Correction: Always exclude central causes (cerebellar/brainstem stroke); HINTS exam and red flags (headache, neuro signs, age, vascular risk) are essential.
- "Bed rest accelerates recovery": Correction: Bed rest _delays_ vestibular compensation; early mobilisation and vestibular rehab promote recovery.

QUESTION 16
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Compare and contrast the molecular and pathway organisation of taste and smell. Why are they so different despite both being chemical senses?

Model answer: Both _chemoreceptive_ but architecturally very different. _Taste_: (1) Receptor cells - specialised epithelial cells in taste buds on tongue (fungiform, foliate, circumvallate papillae), soft palate, pharynx, epiglottis; (2) Five basic tastes - sweet, bitter, sour, salty, umami; receptor mechanisms vary: salty (Na+ via ENaC), sour (H+ via PKD2L1 and OTOP1 channels), sweet/umami/bitter (GPCR-mediated, T1R and T2R families); (3) Innervation - facial nerve (CN VII, anterior 2/3), glossopharyngeal (CN IX, posterior 1/3), vagus (CN X, epiglottis); (4) Central pathway - solitary tract nucleus -> VPM thalamus -> primary gustatory cortex (insula); ipsilateral; (5) Limited diversity - only 5 basic tastes generate the full gustatory experience (with retronasal smell). _Smell_: (1) Receptor cells - olfactory sensory neurons in olfactory epithelium (roof of nasal cavity); these are _bipolar neurons_ with cilia bearing odorant receptors and central axons - unique among sensory systems; turnover throughout life from basal stem cells - one of the few sites of adult neurogenesis; (2) Receptor mechanism - ~400 functional human odorant receptor genes (largest gene family in the genome); each neuron expresses one type; combinatorial coding - each odorant activates multiple receptors, each receptor responds to multiple odorants - generates ~10,000+ discriminable smells; (3) Pathway - axons pass through cribriform plate to olfactory bulb (glomerular convergence); mitral cells project to piriform cortex (primary olfactory cortex), amygdala (emotional), entorhinal cortex (memory), orbitofrontal cortex (conscious perception); (4) Unique features - only sensory modality bypassing thalamus on its way to cortex; closely linked to limbic system explaining emotional and memory potency of smells (Proust effect); ipsilateral. Functional differences: smell can be exquisitely specific and connect to memory/emotion (limbic projections); taste contributes mostly to flavour (~80% of "taste" is actually retronasal smell). Why so different? Smell evolved for distance chemodetection (foraging, predator/mate detection) - needs high diversity and direct limbic access for behavioural drive; taste evolved for ingestion gating (toxin avoidance, nutrient identification) - needs fewer categories with rapid reflexive responses (gag, salivation). Clinical: anosmia (post-viral, COVID-19, neurodegeneration - early Parkinson, Alzheimer); ageusia (rare without anosmia); zinc and B12 deficiency affect both; smoking dulls both [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: The taste-smell contrast illustrates how evolutionary pressures shape sensory architecture. Smell - distance, diversity, behavioural - has 400 receptors, direct limbic access, lifelong neurogenesis. Taste - ingestion gating - has 5 categories with thalamic relay. Anosmia in COVID-19 highlighted how olfactory dysfunction can be a sensitive early sign of viral or neurodegenerative disease.

Hints:

1. Taste: 5 basics, GPCR/channel mix, thalamic relay.
2. Smell: 400 receptors, combinatorial code, bypasses thalamus.
3. Both bypass differently from each other; clinically distinct.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the 5 basic tastes and their receptor mechanisms.
- Identifies the ~400 olfactory receptors and combinatorial coding.
- Notes that olfactory bipolar neurons turn over throughout life.
- Identifies olfaction's bypass of thalamus and limbic connections.
- Connects evolutionary roles to architecture differences.
- Mentions COVID-19 anosmia or neurodegeneration link.

A Yellow answer covers basic features but misses the evolutionary/limbic rationale.

A Red answer treats taste and smell as undifferentiated chemoreception.

Common misconceptions:

- "Most flavour is taste": Correction: ~80% of perceived flavour is actually retronasal smell; pinch the nose during eating - flavour disappears.
- "Olfactory neurons live forever": Correction: Olfactory sensory neurons turn over from basal stem cells - one of the few adult neurogenesis sites; this allows recovery after viral injury.

QUESTION 17
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: A 30-year-old with sudden unilateral hearing loss after a respiratory illness has audiometry showing severe SNHL on the right. Decompose sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL).

Model answer: _Sudden sensorineural hearing loss_ - a medical emergency defined as ≥ 30 dB SNHL across ≥ 3 contiguous frequencies developing within 72 hours. Pathophysiology: most cases idiopathic (~85-90%); proposed mechanisms - viral cochleitis (HSV, VZV, post-COVID), microvascular occlusion of the cochlear (labyrinthine) artery, autoimmune cochleitis, perilymphatic fistula, intracochlear membrane rupture. Clinical: acute unilateral hearing loss; tinnitus very common (~80%); aural fullness; vertigo (~30-40%) - if vertigo prominent, consider vestibulocochlear involvement (e.g., labyrinthitis, vestibular schwannoma). Critical bedside test: _Weber and Rinne tuning fork tests_ - in unilateral SNHL, Weber lateralises to the _unaffected_ ear; Rinne is positive (AC > BC) in the affected ear (vs conductive loss where it lateralises to the affected ear and Rinne is negative). Investigation: pure-tone audiometry (urgent - within 72 h to confirm); MRI of internal auditory canals to exclude vestibular schwannoma (~ 1-3% of SSNHL has occult schwannoma); blood tests (FBC, ESR, autoimmune screen, syphilis, Lyme if relevant). Treatment - controversial but standard: (1) High-dose oral or intratympanic corticosteroids - prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day for 7-10 days then taper, OR intratympanic dexamethasone (avoids systemic side effects; useful as salvage therapy if oral fails); (2) Hyperbaric oxygen (some evidence within 14 days); (3) Antivirals (controversial - aciclovir, valaciclovir); (4) Hearing aid or cochlear implant if recovery incomplete. Time-critical: _treatment within 7-14 days_ offers best chance of recovery; ~50-65% recover at least partially without treatment, but irreversible damage occurs if untreated. Always investigate for vestibular schwannoma even if recovery occurs [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: SSNHL illustrates the urgency principle in sensory neuroscience: the cochlear hair cells are post-mitotic and irrecoverable once damaged. The 7-14 day window for steroid treatment makes prompt diagnosis essential. The same Weber/Rinne reasoning that underlies bedside diagnosis of acute hearing loss also distinguishes conductive from sensorineural causes more generally.

Hints:

1. Hearing loss > 30 dB across 3+ frequencies in < 72 h.
2. Weber lateralises to unaffected ear; Rinne positive in affected.
3. Treat within 7-14 days with corticosteroids; investigate for schwannoma.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names SSNHL with the specific definition.
- Distinguishes SNHL from conductive on Weber/Rinne.
- Names urgent audiometry + MRI for vestibular schwannoma exclusion.
- Names corticosteroids (oral or intratympanic) as primary therapy.
- Mentions the 7-14 day treatment window.

A Yellow answer covers SSNHL but misses the time window or schwannoma exclusion.

A Red answer treats this as conductive hearing loss or earwax.

Common misconceptions:

- "All sudden hearing loss is from earwax or infection": Correction: SSNHL is sensorineural and can be permanent; urgent audiometry distinguishes from conductive causes; treat within 14 days.
- "Steroids are not effective": Correction: Although evidence is mixed, prompt steroid therapy is the standard of care; recovery rates are higher with treatment, especially when started early.

QUESTION 18
Type: synthesis
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Synthesise the chapter: what design principles emerge from the special senses, and what do clinical syndromes teach about each system?

Model answer: Special senses share several design principles. (1) _Specialised receptor cells_ - photoreceptors, hair cells, taste buds, olfactory neurons - each transduces a specific physical or chemical stimulus into membrane potential changes via specific molecular machinery (rhodopsin, mechanically gated channels, GPCRs). (2) _Sensory transduction is amplification + adaptation_ - small stimuli (single photons) are amplified to membrane potential changes; large or sustained stimuli are dampened by adaptation (calcium-driven feedback, receptor downregulation, cortical filtering). (3) _Topographic mapping_ - retinotopy (vision), tonotopy (audition), somatotopy (touch) - preserves spatial relationships in cortical representation, supporting computational efficiency and predictable lesion patterns. (4) _Parallel processing_ - M/P streams in vision, fast/slow afferents in pain, multiple pathways in audition - allows simultaneous extraction of different stimulus features. (5) _Cortical hierarchy and specialisation_ - primary sensory cortex (V1, A1, S1) extracts elementary features; higher areas integrate into perception, action (dorsal stream), or recognition (ventral stream). (6) _Ipsilateral and contralateral mixing_ - vision: contralateral hemifield to each hemisphere; audition: bilateral cortical projection; somatosensation: contralateral after decussation. (7) _Top-down modulation_ - attention, expectation, and context shape early sensory processing. (8) _Limbic integration_ - especially smell and pain - explains emotional/motivational dimensions. (9) _Sensory loss is highly localising_ - the cortex magnifies high-acuity inputs, and specific lesions produce predictable visual field defects, audiograms, and dermatomal patterns. Clinical syndromes mapped to physiology: _Vision_ - cataract (lens), AMD (macula), glaucoma (optic nerve), retinal detachment (photoreceptor-RPE), diabetic retinopathy (microvasculature), optic neuritis (optic nerve, MS), CRAO (vascular). _Audition_ - SSNHL (cochlea/auditory nerve), Ménière (endolymphatic hydrops), presbycusis (high-frequency cochlear loss with age), conductive loss (otosclerosis, otitis media), vestibular schwannoma (CN VIII at cerebellopontine angle). _Vestibular_ - BPPV (otoconia), vestibular neuritis (CN VIII), Ménière (hydrops), central vertigo (stroke/MS - HINTS positive). _Olfaction_ - post-viral anosmia (COVID-19), neurodegenerative anosmia (Parkinson, Alzheimer - early sign). _Taste_ - rare in isolation; usually with anosmia. Pedagogical insight: each clinical syndrome is a controlled lesion that reveals what the affected component does. The same logic across all special senses - specialised receptor + dedicated pathway + cortical processing + clinical patterns of failure - illustrates the integrative power of physiology in neurology, ENT, and ophthalmology [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15].

Elaborative explanation: The special senses are the neuroscience case study of how to extract information from the environment efficiently and reliably. Each modality solves slightly different physical problems (photons vs sound vs molecules) with shared computational strategies (specialised transducers + parallel processing + hierarchical cortex). Clinical neurology of the special senses is essentially applied physiology of these design features.

Hints:

1. Specialised receptors -> dedicated pathways -> cortical hierarchy.
2. Topographic maps + parallel processing + top-down modulation.
3. Clinical syndromes localise to specific design failures.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Articulates ≥ 4 design principles (specialised receptors, topographic maps, parallel processing, hierarchy).
- Maps ≥ 3 clinical syndromes per modality to the affected design feature.
- Identifies the role of cortical magnification and attention.
- Connects the chapter's clinical syndromes to underlying physiology.
- Synthesises across modalities rather than treating each in isolation.

A Yellow answer covers individual senses but misses the unifying design principles.

A Red answer treats the chapter as a list of unrelated facts.

Common misconceptions:

- "The senses are unrelated systems": Correction: Despite different transduction mechanisms, the senses share computational architecture - parallel pathways, hierarchical cortex, topographic mapping, top-down modulation.
- "Sensory deficits are minor compared to motor": Correction: Sensory loss produces profound disability and quality-of-life impact; vision and hearing loss are leading causes of disability worldwide.
