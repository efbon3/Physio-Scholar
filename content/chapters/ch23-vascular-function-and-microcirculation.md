Correct answer: Stores systolic pressure energy in elastic recoil → maintains continuous flow during diastole; loss with arterial stiffening (aging, atherosclerosis) → wide pulse pressure, isolated systolic hypertension

Explanation: The aorta acts as a pressure reservoir ("Windkessel" = air chamber). During systole, ~50% of stroke volume flows to periphery; the rest distends the aorta. During diastole, elastic recoil maintains flow. This converts pulsatile cardiac output into more continuous peripheral flow.

Explanation: **Endothelial functions**:

- "Direct activation of α1-adrenergic receptors" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Inhibition of endothelin" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **NO/cGMP pathway**:

- **eNOS** in endothelium constitutively synthesises NO from L-arginine (with cofactor BH4); activated by shear stress, ACh (M3), bradykinin, histamine, increased intracellular Ca²⁺.

- **sGC stimulators** (riociguat) — direct sGC activation independent of NO; for PAH and CTEPH.
- **Statins** — pleiotropic ↑ eNOS expression and NO bioavailability beyond LDL lowering.

Stem: The earliest detectable lesion of atherosclerosis is the:

Correct answer: Fatty streak — subendothelial accumulation of LDL-laden macrophages (foam cells), already detectable in adolescents; progresses over decades to fibrofatty plaque with smooth muscle proliferation, lipid core, fibrous cap, and calcification; plaque rupture/erosion → atherothrombosis → MI/stroke

- "Mature fibrofatty plaque" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Vasa vasorum hypertrophy" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.
- "Aortic dissection" — Reveals misconception: this option does not match the canonical answer; review the Explanation for the distinguishing rationale. Correction: see correct answer above for the precise concept.

Explanation: **Atherosclerosis — pathogenesis (response-to-injury hypothesis)**:

1. **Endothelial dysfunction** — Risk-factor-driven (HTN, smoking, diabetes, dyslipidaemia, obesity, oxidative stress) → reduced NO, increased adhesion molecule expression.
2. **LDL deposition and oxidation** — LDL passes into intima; oxidised LDL recruits and activates macrophages.
3. **Foam cell formation** — Macrophages take up oxLDL via scavenger receptors (CD36, SR-A) → "fatty streak" (visible in adolescent aortas).
4. **Smooth muscle migration and proliferation** — Migrate from media into intima; secrete ECM (fibrous cap).
5. **Plaque maturation** — Necrotic lipid core, fibrous cap, neovascularisation, calcification.
6. **Plaque rupture / erosion** — Thin fibrous cap, large lipid core, inflammation → exposure of subendothelial collagen and tissue factor → thrombosis → ACS / stroke.

**Risk factors**: HTN, smoking, diabetes, dyslipidaemia (↑LDL, ↓HDL, ↑Lp(a)), obesity, family history, age, male sex, sedentary lifestyle, CKD, inflammation (CRP).

**Treatment**:

- Lifestyle (Mediterranean diet, exercise, smoking cessation).
- **Statins** (HMG-CoA reductase inhibition) → LDL <70 mg/dL or <55 mg/dL in established CV disease.
- **Ezetimibe**, **PCSK9 inhibitors** (alirocumab, evolocumab), **bempedoic acid**, **inclisiran** (siRNA) — if LDL goal not met.
- **Antiplatelet** — Aspirin in established CV disease; clopidogrel after PCI.
- **BP and glycaemic control**.

(Berne & Levy, 7e; Costanzo, 7e.)

Correct answer: During diastole — myocardial compression of intramural vessels obstructs flow during systole; flow resumes when ventricular wall relaxes; LV more dependent than RV; coronary perfusion pressure ≈ aortic diastolic − LVEDP

Explanation: **Coronary circulation**:

**Anatomy**: Right and left coronary arteries arise from the aortic root.

- **Left main → LAD** (anterior wall, anterior septum, apex; "widow-maker" if proximally occluded) + **LCx** (lateral wall).
- **RCA** → posterior wall, inferior wall, RV. Supplies SA node in ~60% (right-dominant) and AV node in ~90%.

**Phasic flow**:

- LV — Almost no flow during systole (myocardium compresses subendocardial vessels). Flow predominantly during diastole.
- RV — Continuous flow (lower RV pressure does not occlude vessels).

**Coronary perfusion pressure** = Aortic DBP − LVEDP. Reduced in:

- Tachycardia (less time in diastole).
- Aortic stenosis (high LV pressure during systole).
- Hypotension or aortic insufficiency (low DBP).
- Increased LVEDP (HF).

**Regulation**:

- Adenosine (from ATP breakdown during ischaemia) is a major coronary vasodilator.
- NO endothelial; sympathetic effect attenuated by metabolic vasodilation.
- Coronary "steal" — Vasodilator dilates normal vessels; flow diverted from poststenotic territories that are already maximally dilated.

**Subendocardium most vulnerable** to ischaemia (highest wall stress, lowest perfusion pressure).

(Berne & Levy, 7e; Costanzo, 7e — Ch.4.)
