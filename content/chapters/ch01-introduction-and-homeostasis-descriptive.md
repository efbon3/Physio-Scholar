Authored in three passes (comprehension and reasoning → clinical decomposition → integration and pathophysiology), then deduplicated. Tier 1 target was 15 questions; final count is 15. Each question is independent — students encounter them in unpredictable order through spaced repetition. The Socratic progression visible in the pass structure is _for the author_, not for the student.

The self-grading checklist is the load-bearing element. Students compare their submitted answer against the model answer and the checklist and rate themselves Green / Yellow / Red.

## Pass 1 — Comprehension and reasoning

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Explain in your own words what _homeostasis_ means and why its maintenance is essential for cell survival.

Model answer: Homeostasis is the maintenance of nearly constant conditions in the body's internal environment — the extracellular fluid (ECF) — within narrow operating ranges [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1; Ganong ch.1]. It is not perfect constancy; instead, regulated variables (ions, nutrients, gases, waste products, temperature, pH) are held within tight ranges around set points. The 19th-century French physiologist Claude Bernard (writing in the 1860s) recognised that cells in the body do not contact the external world directly; they live in an internal fluid environment he called the _milieu intérieur_. Walter Cannon coined the term "homeostasis" in 1926 — extending Bernard's concept and naming the regulatory mechanisms that defend the constancy of this environment [SOURCE DISAGREEMENT: Guyton 14e Ch. 1 dates the coining to 1929, citing Cannon's *Physiological Reviews* article; the primary literature shows the term was first published by Cannon in 1926 in *American Journal of Physiology* — the earlier date is historically defensible and is used here]. Homeostasis is essential for cell survival because cells require specific concentrations of ions (for electrical excitability and transport gradients), specific pH (for enzyme function), specific osmolality (for cell volume), and specific concentrations of oxygen, glucose, and other substrates (for metabolism). If these conditions deviate beyond the survivable range, cellular function collapses — enzymes denature, gradients fail, transport breaks down — and the cell, then the organ, then the organism, dies.

Elaborative explanation: The body's homeostatic mechanisms span the entire physiological hierarchy: respiratory control of CO₂ and O₂; renal control of water, electrolytes, and acid-base balance; cardiovascular control of perfusion pressure; endocrine control of fuel substrates and calcium; and many others. Disease is fundamentally a disruption of homeostasis, and pathophysiology is the study of how regulatory mechanisms fail or are overwhelmed. Recognising homeostasis as the central organising concept is what allows a student to see physiology as a coherent discipline rather than a collection of disconnected facts.

Hints:

1. The concept describes how the body keeps its internal environment stable in spite of external variation.
2. Think about what cells need to function properly — and what would happen if those conditions varied widely.
3. The term combines Greek roots meaning "similar" (homeo-) and "standing" (-stasis).

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines homeostasis as the maintenance of nearly constant (not perfectly constant) conditions in the internal environment.
- Identifies the _internal environment_ as the extracellular fluid (and may reference Bernard's _milieu intérieur_).
- States that variables are kept within narrow ranges around set points, not at fixed values.
- Explains _why_ this constancy matters for cells: specific ion concentrations for electrical activity and transport, specific pH for enzymes, specific osmolality for cell volume, specific substrates for metabolism.

A Yellow answer is missing one of these but has a mostly-correct mental model — e.g., defines homeostasis correctly and gives examples of regulated variables but does not articulate why the constancy matters at the cellular level.

A Red answer treats homeostasis as perfect constancy, confuses it with metabolism or with the cell's internal environment (cytoplasm), or fails to connect it to cell survival.

Common misconceptions:

- "Homeostasis means the body keeps everything perfectly constant.": Correction: homeostasis maintains variables within narrow ranges, not at fixed points; some residual variation always persists.
- "Homeostasis is about the inside of cells.": Correction: homeostasis is about the _extracellular_ fluid in which cells live — the _milieu intérieur_.

QUESTION 2
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Why does the body rely on negative feedback as the primary architecture for homeostatic control rather than positive feedback?

Model answer: Negative feedback opposes deviations in a regulated variable, returning it toward a set point — the system therefore _stabilises_. Positive feedback amplifies deviations, driving the variable further from set point — the system therefore _destabilises_ and runs away [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1]. Stability is exactly what homeostasis requires: cells need predictable concentrations of ions, nutrients, and gases, which only a stabilising architecture can deliver. Most homeostatic mechanisms in the body — temperature regulation, arterial pressure control via the baroreflex, blood glucose homeostasis, acid-base balance — therefore use negative feedback. Positive feedback is _not_ absent from physiology, but it is reserved for specific tasks where rapid amplification is the goal: the upstroke of a nerve action potential (Na⁺ channels open more Na⁺ channels), the clotting cascade (activated factors generate more activated factors), and parturition (cervical stretch triggers stronger contractions). Each of these useful positive-feedback loops has a built-in termination event (Na⁺ channel inactivation; vessel sealed; baby delivered) and sits inside a larger negative-feedback context (membrane potential, blood volume, reproductive cycle). When positive feedback lacks a termination — as in heatstroke or decompensated haemorrhage — it becomes a vicious cycle that ends only with death or external intervention.

Elaborative explanation: The architectural choice between positive and negative feedback is not arbitrary. Engineering control theory predicts the same: stable systems use negative feedback; oscillators and amplifiers use positive feedback. Biology has independently arrived at the same answer, because survival demands stable internal conditions, with brief amplifying events deployed only when speed is essential.

Hints:

1. What outcome does each architecture produce — stability or amplification?
2. Which outcome does cell survival require?
3. Where in the body is each architecture useful, and why?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines negative feedback as opposing the deviation, producing stability.
- Defines positive feedback as amplifying the deviation, producing instability.
- Explicitly connects "stability" to the survival requirement of cells / tissues.
- Notes that positive feedback is not absent — it is used in specific physiological contexts (e.g., action potential, clotting, parturition).
- States that useful positive feedback always has a termination event and sits inside a larger negative-feedback context.

A Yellow answer correctly contrasts the two architectures and identifies stability as the goal but omits the role of positive feedback as a deliberate choice for specific tasks, or fails to mention the termination requirement.

A Red answer claims positive feedback is "always pathological" or that homeostasis "always uses negative feedback exclusively" — either ignores the genuine physiological roles of positive feedback.

QUESTION 3
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A pharmacology student asks: arterial pH varies by less than 0.1 units in a healthy person, but systolic blood pressure can vary by 20–30 mm Hg with posture and activity. Why does the body regulate pH so much more tightly than pressure?

Model answer: The tightness of regulation reflects how sensitive the body's molecular machinery is to a given variable. Proteins — including every enzyme — depend on protonation states of charged side chains; even small pH changes alter enzyme conformation and activity dramatically [Guyton ch.1]. A pH shift of 0.4 units (from 7.4 to 7.0 or 7.8) approaches the boundaries of survivable enzyme function and would cripple metabolism, ion transport, and oxygen delivery. The body therefore regulates pH with very tight gain — buffering, respiratory compensation, and renal compensation work together to keep pH within ±0.05 of 7.4 under normal conditions. Arterial pressure, by contrast, can vary considerably because each critical organ (brain, heart, kidney) has its own _autoregulation_ that maintains constant blood flow across a wide range of mean arterial pressures. The body therefore tolerates pressure variation as long as autoregulation can compensate. Quantitatively, this difference in tightness is captured by the _gain_ of each control system: the temperature-control system has a gain of about −33 (very tight); the baroreceptor pressure control system has a gain of only about −2 [Guyton ch.1]. Higher gain = tighter regulation = smaller residual error after a perturbation.

Elaborative explanation: The pH–pressure contrast is a useful general principle: the tighter the regulation, the more sensitive the downstream machinery to small deviations in that variable. Calcium is similarly tightly regulated (because it controls neuromuscular excitability); plasma sodium is tightly regulated (because it controls cell volume via osmolality); plasma potassium is tightly regulated (because it controls resting membrane potential). Body weight, by contrast, is loosely regulated because it does not have an analogous moment-to-moment cellular consequence.

Hints:

1. What does pH do to enzyme function compared to what pressure does to organ blood flow?
2. Each organ has a mechanism that buffers it against pressure changes — what is that mechanism called?
3. Express the concept quantitatively using the "gain" of a control system.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Connects pH sensitivity to enzyme function (proteins are pH-sensitive at the molecular level).
- Notes that pressure variation is tolerable because critical organs autoregulate their blood flow.
- Mentions or uses the concept of _gain_ (tightness of regulation).
- Optional: cites the comparative gain values (temperature-control gain ≈ −33 vs baroreceptor gain ≈ −2) or another quantitative anchor.

A Yellow answer states that pH must be tight because of enzymes, but does not articulate why pressure can vary (autoregulation) or does not introduce the quantitative concept of gain.

A Red answer claims pressure is _also_ tightly regulated and varies less than 5 mm Hg, or that pH varies as much as pressure, or treats both variables symmetrically.

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Compare _negative feedback_, _positive feedback_, and _feed-forward_ control as architectures for physiological regulation. For each, describe the timing of the response relative to the deviation in the regulated variable, and give one specific physiological example.

Model answer: Negative feedback responds _after_ a deviation has occurred and acts to _oppose_ the deviation, returning the variable toward its set point. Example: a rise in arterial pressure activates baroreceptors, which signal the medulla to slow the heart and dilate vessels, returning pressure toward normal [Guyton ch.1]. Positive feedback also responds _after_ a deviation has occurred but acts to _amplify_ the deviation. Example: in the upstroke of a nerve action potential, a small depolarisation opens voltage-gated Na⁺ channels; Na⁺ entry depolarises further; further depolarisation opens more channels — a self-reinforcing loop terminated by Na⁺ channel inactivation. Feed-forward control acts ahead of the full deviation — it anticipates the size of an expected change and pre-emptively engages the corrective response. Example: when food is detected and nutrients enter the gut, incretin hormones (GIP, GLP-1) augment insulin release _early_ during nutrient absorption, before plasma glucose reaches its post-prandial peak, blunting the size of that peak [Sherwood ch.1; Guyton ch.1]. The three architectures are complementary: feed-forward reduces the magnitude of an expected deviation, negative feedback corrects whatever deviation does occur, and positive feedback is reserved for specific tasks where speed of amplification matters. _Adaptive control_ layers on top of feed-forward — when a feed-forward command produces a small error, the next feed-forward command is adjusted to reduce that error, making it a kind of "delayed negative feedback."

Elaborative explanation: The three architectures together explain how the body achieves both stability _and_ responsiveness. Pure negative feedback alone would produce unnecessarily large excursions; pure feed-forward alone would fail when predictions are inaccurate; adaptive control improves prediction over time. Recognising this layered architecture is essential for understanding complex regulatory systems like glucose homeostasis, motor control, and ventilatory regulation.

Hints:

1. Two of the three architectures respond _to_ a deviation; one acts _before_ the deviation.
2. Of the two reactive architectures, one opposes the deviation and one amplifies it.
3. Identify a familiar example for each: the baroreflex, the action potential, and a meal-related hormone response.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Negative feedback: response _after_ deviation, _opposes_ it (with one example).
- Positive feedback: response _after_ deviation, _amplifies_ it (with one example).
- Feed-forward: response _before_ deviation, _anticipates_ it (with one example).
- States that the three architectures are complementary, not exclusive.

A Yellow answer correctly defines all three but gets the timing wrong for one (e.g., calls feed-forward a "fast negative feedback" rather than anticipatory) or omits an example.

A Red answer conflates two architectures (e.g., treats feed-forward and positive feedback as the same) or misidentifies the timing for two of the three.

QUESTION 5
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A first-year medical student asks why the textbook calls the extracellular fluid the "internal environment" rather than just "the fluid around cells." Explain the conceptual significance of Bernard's term and what the term emphasises about how cells live.

Model answer: Claude Bernard's 19th-century insight — captured in the term _milieu intérieur_ — was that cells in the body do not contact the external world (air, food, water, dirt) directly. They are insulated from it by tissues and by the extracellular fluid that bathes them. The cell's actual environment is the ECF, not the outside world. Calling the ECF the "internal environment" emphasises three things [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1]. First, the ECF is the buffer between cells and external variation — its composition can be defended against fluctuations in environmental temperature, water intake, dietary salt, atmospheric oxygen, and so on. Second, the ECF is the medium through which all exchange occurs: oxygen and nutrients reach cells via the ECF, and waste products leave via the ECF. Third, the central question of physiology becomes "how does the body keep the ECF composition constant?" — and homeostatic mechanisms are precisely the answer to that question. Bernard's concept is therefore the philosophical foundation of regulatory physiology: rather than studying the body system by system in isolation, physiology becomes the integrated study of how the body's organ systems each contribute to defending the constancy of the _milieu intérieur_.

Elaborative explanation: Bernard's insight reframed physiology. Before him, study tended to focus on individual organs and their functions. After him, organ functions are understood as contributions to the larger task of maintaining the ECF — the lungs supply O₂ and remove CO₂, the kidneys regulate water and ions, the GI system supplies nutrients and removes solid waste, the cardiovascular system distributes the ECF, and the endocrine and nervous systems coordinate them all. This integrated view is exactly the framework Walter Cannon then formalised as "homeostasis."

Hints:

1. Cells in your tissues do not actually touch the outside world. So what _do_ they touch?
2. The term emphasises that the ECF is buffered against external variation — that buffering is the body's job.
3. Bernard's term is in French and means literally "interior environment."

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the term as Bernard's _milieu intérieur_ (and may translate as "internal environment").
- States that cells do not contact the external world directly — the ECF is their actual environment.
- Explains that the term emphasises the _buffering_ of cells from external variation.
- Connects the concept to physiology's central organising question: how do organ systems maintain ECF constancy.

A Yellow answer correctly identifies the term and the basic idea but does not articulate why the framing matters — i.e., does not connect to the integrated view of physiology that follows.

A Red answer treats the ECF as "just the fluid around cells" without the conceptual significance, or attributes the term to Cannon, or describes it as the _intracellular_ fluid.

## Pass 2 — Clinical decomposition

QUESTION 6
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A trauma patient has bled 2 litres of blood. Decompose the cardiovascular events that follow, explain how a positive-feedback vicious cycle can develop, and contrast this scenario with what happens after a smaller (1 litre) bleed.

Model answer: A 2-litre haemorrhage produces an initial chain of negative events: blood volume falls, venous return falls, end-diastolic volume falls, stroke volume and cardiac output fall, and arterial pressure falls. The body's negative-feedback compensations — the baroreceptor reflex, sympathetic vasoconstriction, the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system, ADH release, and venoconstriction — engage immediately to defend pressure and perfusion. With a 1-litre bleed, these compensations succeed: pressure is maintained adequately, coronary blood flow is preserved, and the heart continues to pump. But once losses exceed the compensatory capacity, a positive-feedback vicious cycle develops [Guyton ch.1]. Falling cardiac output reduces coronary blood flow, which weakens the heart muscle. The weakened heart pumps even less, which drops cardiac output further, which reduces coronary flow more, which weakens the heart further. Each cycle reinforces the previous one. This is the architectural shift from compensated shock (still inside negative feedback) to decompensated shock (a positive-feedback collapse). Without intervention, the cycle terminates in cardiac arrest. The clinical implication is that haemorrhage has a "tipping point": before the tip, the body's negative-feedback compensations handle the loss; beyond the tip, only external intervention (fluid resuscitation, blood products, surgical haemostasis) can interrupt the positive-feedback collapse.

Elaborative explanation: The same architectural principle — a positive-feedback collapse layered onto a previously stable negative-feedback system — explains many lethal clinical syndromes: cardiogenic shock after large MI, sepsis-induced cardiovascular collapse, and end-stage congestive heart failure. Recognising the transition from compensated to decompensated state is the basis of trauma triage and the urgency of timely resuscitation.

Hints:

1. Map out the chain: blood lost → what falls → what does that do to the heart's own blood supply?
2. At what point does the body's normal stabilising response (negative feedback) become overwhelmed?
3. The architectural classification of the spiraling decompensation is the same as the architecture of the action-potential upstroke — though the consequence here is harmful.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the initial chain: volume loss → venous return ↓ → stroke volume ↓ → cardiac output ↓ → pressure ↓.
- Names at least two compensatory negative-feedback mechanisms (baroreflex, RAAS, ADH, sympathetic vasoconstriction).
- Identifies the positive-feedback element: low cardiac output → low coronary flow → weaker heart → lower cardiac output.
- Contrasts the 1-litre bleed (compensation succeeds) with the 2-litre bleed (compensation overwhelmed).
- States that the architectural shift is from compensated shock (negative feedback intact) to decompensated shock (positive feedback collapse).

A Yellow answer correctly maps the chain and identifies the compensations but does not explicitly identify the architectural shift to positive feedback, or does not contrast the two scenarios.

A Red answer treats both bleeds as the same, or labels the entire process as negative feedback failure without identifying the positive-feedback collapse, or omits the role of coronary perfusion in the cycle.

QUESTION 7
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Explain step-by-step how severe heatstroke (core temperature 42°C) becomes a pathological positive-feedback loop. Why does the body fail to cool itself, and what does the architecture of this failure tell us about the difference between _pathological_ and _useful_ positive feedback?

Model answer: Heatstroke begins with a thermal load that overwhelms the body's normal cooling mechanisms (sweating, cutaneous vasodilation, behavioural cooling). Once core temperature rises above ~41–42°C, the temperature-regulating neurons of the hypothalamus — which are themselves temperature-sensitive — begin to malfunction [Sherwood ch.1; Guyton ch.1]. A damaged hypothalamic centre produces a weaker cooling response: less sweat output, less vasodilation, less behavioural drive to seek cooling. With the cooling response weakened, core temperature rises further, which further damages the hypothalamic centre, which further reduces cooling, and so on. This is a positive-feedback vicious cycle: each turn of the loop makes the next turn worse. The architecture is identical to the haemorrhagic decompensation in Question 6 and to the action-potential upstroke — except that in heatstroke, there is _no built-in termination event_. In useful positive feedback, the loop ends because something stops the stimulus: Na⁺ channels inactivate; the vessel is sealed; the baby is delivered. In heatstroke, nothing within the loop stops the cycle — the regulator damaged by the deviation cannot regulate, so the deviation grows unchecked. Survival therefore requires _external_ interruption: rapid cooling with cold IV fluids, ice baths, or evaporative cooling, before cellular damage from temperatures above ~42°C destroys neural and other tissue irreversibly.

Elaborative explanation: The contrast between useful and pathological positive feedback is a load-bearing concept in pathophysiology. Many lethal clinical states (decompensated shock, heatstroke, sepsis-induced multiorgan failure, malignant hyperthermia) share the same architecture: a normally stable negative-feedback system is pushed past a tipping point, after which positive feedback dominates and progresses to death without external intervention. Recognising this architectural pattern guides clinical urgency.

Hints:

1. What component of the cooling system is itself damaged by high temperature?
2. Each cycle of the loop must produce something that worsens the next cycle. What is that something?
3. Compare the termination of this loop with the termination of useful positive-feedback loops — what is missing here?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Names the loop chain: hyperthermia → hypothalamic damage → less cooling → more hyperthermia.
- Identifies the architectural classification: pathological positive feedback (vicious cycle).
- Contrasts with useful positive feedback by noting that useful loops have a built-in termination event whereas heatstroke does not.
- States that interruption requires external action (rapid cooling).
- Optional: notes the cellular damage threshold (~42°C) and its irreversibility.

A Yellow answer correctly identifies the cycle but does not explicitly contrast pathological vs useful positive feedback, or does not state that interruption requires external action.

A Red answer treats heatstroke as failure of a negative-feedback system without identifying the active positive-feedback architecture, or claims the cycle is self-terminating without intervention.

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A diabetic patient on insulin therapy is found to have a plasma glucose of 35 mg/dL. Explain why the brain — rather than the heart, kidneys, or skeletal muscles — manifests dysfunction first, and what symptoms are expected.

Model answer: The brain depends almost exclusively on glucose for energy under acute conditions [Guyton ch.1]. Three features of brain metabolism explain its vulnerability to hypoglycaemia: (1) the brain has very limited stores of glycogen, so it cannot buffer itself against a falling glucose supply for more than a few minutes; (2) under acute hypoglycaemia the brain has limited capacity to switch to alternative substrates such as ketones, because ketone-utilising machinery requires hours to days to upregulate; and (3) the brain consumes a large fraction of the body's resting glucose flux despite being only 2% of body weight, so any drop in glucose supply hits it disproportionately. The heart and skeletal muscle, by contrast, can use fatty acids; the kidney has its own gluconeogenic capacity. As plasma glucose falls below about half its normal value, neuronal function deteriorates progressively: mental irritability and confusion appear first, then tremor and sweating (these are autonomic counter-regulatory signs, not directly cerebral), and at very low values seizures and loss of consciousness follow [Guyton ch.1]. Severe and prolonged hypoglycaemia can cause permanent neuronal injury. The clinical implication is that hypoglycaemia is a neurological emergency — rapid restoration of blood glucose with oral or IV glucose is the standard treatment, and delay can cause permanent damage.

Elaborative explanation: This is one reason insulin therapy in diabetes carries such close monitoring requirements. Unlike the slow damage of chronic hyperglycaemia, hypoglycaemia produces immediate neurological dysfunction and is the most acute risk of insulin or sulfonylurea use. Patients are typically taught to recognise early autonomic signs (sweating, tremor, palpitations) so they can self-treat before neuroglycopenic symptoms (confusion, seizures) appear.

Hints:

1. Which organ has the highest glucose dependence per unit mass?
2. Why can other organs (heart, muscle) tolerate hypoglycaemia better — what alternative fuels do they use?
3. Symptom progression follows the brain's degree of dysfunction — what are the early symptoms vs the late ones?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the brain's near-exclusive dependence on glucose (under acute conditions).
- States at least one of: limited cerebral glycogen / inability to switch rapidly to alternative substrates / disproportionate glucose consumption.
- Contrasts with at least one other organ that can use alternative substrates (heart/muscle = fatty acids).
- Names symptoms in approximate progression: irritability/confusion → tremor → seizures → coma.
- Optional: links to clinical implication (rapid glucose administration is the intervention).

A Yellow answer states the brain depends on glucose and lists symptoms but does not articulate _why_ the brain is more vulnerable than other organs (does not contrast with alternative-substrate use).

A Red answer claims the heart or kidneys are equally vulnerable, or attributes hypoglycaemic symptoms to organs other than the brain, or omits the substrate-dependence argument.

QUESTION 9
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A child presents with severe watery diarrhoea, marked thirst, dry mucous membranes, weakness, and decreased urine output. Apply the principles of homeostatic compensation to explain what the body is "trying to do" in this clinical picture and why those compensations can themselves become harmful if losses are not replaced.

Model answer: Severe diarrhoea produces continuous loss of water, sodium, potassium, and bicarbonate from the gut. The dominant disturbance is ECF volume contraction (and frequently a loss of bicarbonate that produces metabolic acidosis). The body responds with several layered negative-feedback compensations [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1]. The baroreceptor reflex senses falling pressure and activates the sympathetic nervous system: heart rate rises, vessels constrict, and arterial pressure is defended. The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system activates: aldosterone drives sodium and water reabsorption in the distal nephron, reducing renal water loss (manifesting clinically as decreased urine output). ADH (vasopressin) is secreted in response to falling ECF volume and rising plasma osmolality, further reducing renal water loss and producing intense thirst. These compensations together attempt to maintain perfusion. But the compensations have costs: the high aldosterone wastes potassium (compounding the gut potassium loss), driving hypokalaemia, which causes weakness and predisposes to arrhythmias. The reduced urine output limits the body's ability to excrete ongoing acid load, contributing to acidosis. If losses continue uncorrected, the compensations are eventually overwhelmed and decompensated hypovolaemic shock develops. The clinical priority is therefore _replacement_ of fluid and electrolytes (oral rehydration solution or IV fluids) — the compensations buy time but cannot solve the problem.

Elaborative explanation: This is a perfect example of Guyton's general point that homeostatic compensations can be both adaptive (in the short term) and harmful (in the long term). The body's response to diarrhoea is the same as its response to haemorrhage in many ways — both involve ECF volume contraction — and both share the same compensatory chain (baroreflex, RAAS, ADH). Recognising the unifying principle helps a student understand why the management — fluid and electrolyte replacement — is similar across very different aetiologies.

Hints:

1. What is the dominant homeostatic disturbance in diarrhoea — is it volume loss, electrolyte loss, or both?
2. What three regulatory systems (one neural, two hormonal) defend against ECF volume contraction?
3. Each compensation has a side-effect that the body must tolerate. What does aldosterone do to potassium?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the dominant disturbance: ECF volume contraction (with electrolyte loss).
- Names at least two compensatory systems: baroreflex/sympathetic, RAAS, ADH.
- Explains the compensatory goal: maintain perfusion / arterial pressure.
- Identifies at least one harmful side-effect of compensation (e.g., aldosterone-driven potassium loss → hypokalaemia → weakness; reduced urine output limits acid excretion).
- States that compensation buys time but does not solve the underlying problem — fluid and electrolyte replacement is required.

A Yellow answer correctly names the compensations and the goal but does not articulate the harmful side-effects, or does not connect the clinical features (weakness, decreased urine) to specific compensations.

A Red answer treats the diarrhoea as primary electrolyte derangement without recognising volume contraction, or claims the body's response is purely passive (no compensations).

QUESTION 10
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Decompose the baroreceptor reflex into the three architectural components of a homeostatic control system: receptor, integrator (control centre), and effector. Identify the regulated variable and describe how each component contributes to the corrective response when arterial pressure rises above set point.

Model answer: The baroreceptor reflex is a textbook example of a negative-feedback control system. Its regulated variable is _arterial pressure_ [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1]. The receptors are stretch-sensitive nerve endings in the walls of the carotid sinus and the aortic arch — they fire at a baseline rate at normal pressure and increase their firing rate when arterial wall stretch increases. The integrator (control centre) is the cardiovascular centre in the medulla oblongata, which receives baroreceptor afferent input and integrates it with input from chemoreceptors and higher brain regions. The effectors are the heart and the blood vessels: the heart's rate and contractility are modulated by sympathetic and parasympathetic outflow, and the vessels' diameter is modulated by sympathetic outflow. When arterial pressure rises above set point, baroreceptors fire more rapidly. The medulla responds by _decreasing_ sympathetic outflow and _increasing_ parasympathetic (vagal) outflow. The result: heart rate slows, contractility falls, and blood vessels dilate. Stroke volume and total peripheral resistance both fall, so cardiac output and resistance both fall, and arterial pressure falls back toward set point. The reverse occurs when pressure falls — baroreceptors fire less, the medulla increases sympathetic and decreases parasympathetic, and heart rate, contractility, and vasoconstriction all rise to push pressure back up. The reflex is therefore architecturally complete: it senses, integrates, acts, and the action _opposes_ the disturbance — the defining feature of negative feedback.

Elaborative explanation: Decomposing a familiar reflex into receptor / integrator / effector is a generalisable thinking tool. The same decomposition works for body temperature regulation (cutaneous and core thermoreceptors → preoptic hypothalamus → sweat glands, skin vessels, skeletal muscle), for blood glucose regulation (pancreatic β-cells → β-cell signalling apparatus → multiple peripheral effectors), and for many other systems. Practising this decomposition is one of the most useful study habits in physiology.

Hints:

1. The regulated variable is what the system is trying to keep constant. What variable is it?
2. Where in the body does the system _sense_ this variable? Where does it _act_?
3. The integrator is between sensing and acting — where is it anatomically?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the regulated variable as arterial pressure.
- Names the receptors: baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch.
- Names the integrator: medullary cardiovascular centres.
- Names the effectors: heart (rate and contractility) and blood vessels.
- Describes the response logic for at least one direction (rise or fall): how the autonomic outflow changes and how the effector response moves pressure back toward set point.

## Pass 3 — Integration and pathophysiology

QUESTION 11
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A patient with chronic kidney disease (CKD) develops sustained hypertension. Argue both sides: explain how this hypertension is _initially_ a homeostatic compensation, and how it becomes a contributor to ongoing pathology over time.

Model answer: Healthy kidneys excrete sodium and water in proportion to intake, maintaining sodium balance through _pressure natriuresis_ — the higher the renal perfusion pressure, the more sodium and water are excreted [Guyton ch.1]. In CKD, nephron loss reduces the kidneys' ability to excrete a normal sodium load at normal arterial pressures. The body's response is to raise arterial pressure: at the higher pressure, the remaining nephrons can excrete enough sodium to balance intake. This is the compensation — sodium balance is restored at the cost of an elevated arterial pressure. In the short term, this compensation is necessary for survival; without it, sodium and water would accumulate, expanding ECF volume to the point of pulmonary oedema and heart failure. In the long term, however, the elevated pressure damages the renal vasculature: glomeruli are exposed to higher hydrostatic pressures, basement membranes thicken, additional nephrons are lost, and the kidneys' filtering and excretory capacity falls further. As more nephrons are lost, even more pressure is required to maintain sodium balance — a slow vicious cycle. The elevated pressure also damages other organs: left ventricular hypertrophy, accelerated atherosclerosis, retinopathy, and increased stroke risk. The hypertension is therefore _both_ a homeostatic compensation (without which the patient cannot maintain sodium balance) and a long-term pathological driver (because high pressure itself causes organ damage). This dual nature is the core idea of pathophysiology.

Elaborative explanation: Many chronic diseases follow this pattern. In heart failure, the body's compensatory neurohormonal activation (sympathetic, RAAS) initially supports cardiac output but accelerates myocardial remodelling and worsens long-term prognosis. In COPD, hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction redirects flow away from poorly ventilated alveoli but in chronic disease causes pulmonary hypertension. Recognising the compensation–pathology duality is essential to understanding why interventions like ACE inhibitors (in heart failure and CKD) or β-blockers (in heart failure) — which reduce the very compensations the body is mounting — are paradoxically beneficial.

Hints:

1. Why must arterial pressure rise in CKD? What renal mechanism requires the higher pressure?
2. The kidneys are both _causing_ the high pressure (by failing) and _damaged_ by the high pressure. Sketch this loop.
3. The compensation has a benefit and a cost — what is each?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- States the kidney's normal role: pressure natriuresis to balance sodium.
- Explains why pressure must rise in CKD (to restore sodium balance through impaired kidneys).
- Identifies the compensation as initially adaptive (necessary for sodium balance / survival).
- Lists at least two long-term costs of the elevated pressure (renal damage / vascular damage / cardiac hypertrophy / stroke risk).
- Connects the scenario to the broader concept that homeostatic compensations can become long-term trade-offs.

A Yellow answer correctly explains either the compensation side or the pathology side but not both, or fails to connect the scenario to the broader principle.

A Red answer treats hypertension as purely pathological with no compensatory role, or treats it as purely compensatory without acknowledging long-term damage, or attributes the hypertension to a non-renal cause.

QUESTION 12
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The cardiac action potential, the clotting cascade, and parturition are three classical examples of physiologically useful positive feedback. What architectural feature do they share, and what specifically protects each from becoming a runaway cycle?

Model answer: All three are positive-feedback loops in which a deviation in some variable triggers a response that _amplifies_ the deviation rapidly, accomplishing a task that requires speed [Guyton ch.1]. In the action potential, an initial small depolarisation opens voltage-gated Na⁺ channels; Na⁺ entry depolarises further; further depolarisation opens more channels — a self-reinforcing loop that produces the rapid upstroke. In the clotting cascade, vessel injury exposes tissue factor and activates a small amount of thrombin; thrombin activates more clotting factors, which generate more thrombin — the cascade amplifies a small initial signal into a large clot quickly. In parturition, descent of the fetal head stretches the cervix; cervical stretch sends afferent signals that increase oxytocin release and uterine contractions; stronger contractions push the head against the cervix more, increasing stretch — and so on. The shared architecture is amplification with a clear physiological purpose that requires speed (depolarisation, vessel sealing, delivery). Each loop has a specific _built-in termination event_ that prevents runaway: the action potential ends when voltage-gated Na⁺ channels inactivate (a time- and voltage-dependent process intrinsic to the channel itself); clotting ends when the vessel is sealed (no more tissue factor exposed) and is further bounded by natural anticoagulants (antithrombin III, protein C/S); parturition ends when the baby is delivered and the cervical stretch stimulus is removed. In each case, the positive-feedback loop sits inside a larger negative-feedback context: membrane potential is held within bounds; blood volume is maintained within bounds; the reproductive cycle returns to its quiescent state. When the termination event fails or is absent — as in heatstroke or in pathological clot extension producing thrombosis — positive feedback becomes a vicious cycle.

Elaborative explanation: Memorising the three examples without understanding the shared architecture is shallow. The deeper insight — that useful positive feedback always amplifies a deviation rapidly and always has a termination — is the lens that lets a student recognise positive-feedback architecture in less familiar settings (sneezing reflex, cough reflex, certain hormonal cascades like LH surge for ovulation).

Hints:

1. What is happening to the regulated variable in each of the three examples — is it being opposed or amplified?
2. Each loop must end somehow, otherwise it would run away. What ends the action potential? The clot? Labour?
3. What general statement can you make about the relationship between useful positive feedback and overall negative feedback in physiology?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the common architectural feature: amplification of a deviation.
- Identifies the common purpose: a process requiring speed of amplification.
- For each of the three examples, names the specific termination event:
  - Action potential: voltage-dependent inactivation of Na⁺ channels (and/or activation of K⁺ channels).
  - Clotting: vessel sealed (no more tissue factor exposed) and/or natural anticoagulants limit propagation.
  - Parturition: delivery removes the cervical stretch stimulus.
- States the principle: useful positive feedback always sits inside a larger negative-feedback context.

A Yellow answer correctly identifies the architecture and most termination events but misses one or names the termination vaguely (e.g., "the loop just stops" rather than naming the specific mechanism).

A Red answer treats the three examples as having different architectures, or claims positive feedback is always pathological, or says the loops simply run out of substrate (true for none of the three).

QUESTION 13
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Explain what _gain_ means as a property of a homeostatic control system. Why does the body have different gains for different regulated variables, and what does a higher absolute gain magnitude indicate about the system?

Model answer: Gain is a quantitative measure of how effectively a control system corrects a deviation. It is defined as the _correction_ divided by the residual _error_, with sign — Gain = correction / error [Guyton ch.1]. The negative sign that always accompanies gain in a negative-feedback system reflects that the correction is in the _opposite direction_ of the disturbance — the architectural defining feature of negative feedback. A higher _absolute_ gain magnitude means the system makes a larger correction relative to the residual error — equivalently, it leaves a smaller proportion of the deviation uncorrected. Tighter regulation. In Guyton's worked example (14e Ch. 1), transfusing blood into a person with non-functional baroreceptors raised arterial pressure from 100 to 175 mm Hg; with intact baroreceptors, pressure rose only to 125 mm Hg. The system corrected −50 mm Hg out of a +75 mm Hg disturbance, leaving +25 mm Hg of residual error. Gain = −50 / +25 = −2 [SOURCE DISAGREEMENT: other Guyton-family resources, including the *Guyton & Hall Physiology Review*, use different illustrative numbers for this calculation; the values used here are taken directly from the Guyton & Hall textbook 14e Ch. 1 worked example]. The temperature control system, by contrast, has a gain of approximately −33: the same fractional disturbance in core temperature would be corrected to a much smaller residual error. Different variables have different gains because they have different physiological tolerances. Core temperature is regulated tightly because metabolic enzymes are extremely temperature-sensitive — a few degrees of deviation cripples cellular function, so evolution has produced a high-gain system. Arterial pressure tolerates wider variation because the critical organs (brain, heart, kidney) autoregulate their blood flow across a range of pressures, so a less tight system suffices. Gain is therefore not a universal constant but a system-specific design parameter matched to the tolerance of the regulated variable.

Elaborative explanation: Gain explains why some clinical disturbances are immediately life-threatening (a 0.4-pH-unit shift, a 7°C temperature rise) while others are tolerable for hours (moderate pressure swings, even moderate sodium swings). Comparing gains across variables is also useful in pharmacology and pathophysiology: drugs that affect the gain of a regulatory system (β-blockers reducing the baroreflex's adrenergic limb; SGLT-2 inhibitors lowering the threshold for renal glucose excretion) change how robust the regulated variable is to perturbation.

Hints:

1. Gain is one quantity divided by another. The numerator captures how much the system corrected; the denominator captures how much error remained.
2. Why does the negative sign matter? What does it tell you about the _direction_ of the correction relative to the disturbance?
3. Why would the body invest more "regulatory capacity" in some variables than others? Think about the consequences of a deviation in each.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines gain as correction divided by error (with sign).
- Notes that the negative sign indicates the correction is in the _opposite direction_ of the disturbance.
- States that higher absolute gain = tighter regulation = smaller residual error.
- Explains why different variables have different gains (different physiological sensitivity / tolerance).
- Optional: gives a comparison example (temperature ≈ −33 vs baroreflex ≈ −2) or works through the Guyton baroreceptor calculation.

A Yellow answer correctly defines gain but does not articulate why different variables have different gains, or describes gain as binary (present/absent) rather than quantitative.

A Red answer confuses gain with response speed (a separate property), or claims all systems have the same gain, or describes gain as a positive number (missing the sign).

QUESTION 14
Type: misconception-targeted
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A first-year medical student says: "Negative feedback maintains the body in a perfectly constant state — every regulated variable stays exactly at its set point." Identify the misconception, explain why it is wrong, and use either the concept of _gain_ or a specific physiological example to support your correction.

Model answer: The misconception is that negative feedback achieves _perfect_ control — that every regulated variable sits exactly at its set point. The correction: negative feedback minimises but cannot eliminate variation [Guyton ch.1]. Some residual error always persists after the system has acted. The reason is structural: a negative-feedback loop only generates a corrective signal _when there is an error to correct_. If the error were truly zero, there would be nothing to drive the response. In practice, every regulated variable oscillates within a narrow band around its set point, with the size of the band determined by the system's gain. A high-gain system (temperature control, gain ≈ −33) keeps the band very narrow; a lower-gain system (baroreflex, gain ≈ −2) keeps the band wider. Even high-gain systems leave a residual error: in Guyton's worked baroreceptor example, the system corrected most of the pressure rise but left +25 mm Hg of residual deviation from the original set point. Specific physiological examples that demonstrate the principle: arterial pressure varies by 20–30 mm Hg with posture and activity in healthy people; blood glucose rises briefly above fasting after a meal before insulin pulls it back; body temperature varies by ~1°C over the day with circadian rhythm. None of these are signs of regulatory failure — they are the _normal operation_ of negative-feedback systems with finite gain. The student's correct mental model should be: regulated variables are kept _within narrow ranges around_ their set points, not _exactly at_ them.

Elaborative explanation: This misconception leads to a deeper one — that any deviation from a textbook "normal value" is pathological. A more sophisticated clinical understanding accepts that physiological variables vary normally within ranges, and that disease is signalled either by values _outside_ the normal range or by _patterns_ of variation that differ from normal (e.g., loss of normal circadian temperature variation; failure of post-prandial glucose to return to fasting). The concept of gain is also the foundation for understanding why drugs that _increase_ the gain of a regulatory system (e.g., baroreflex sensitisers) tighten regulation, and why disease states that _reduce_ gain (e.g., autonomic neuropathy in diabetes) widen normal variation.

Hints:

1. Think about what would have to be true for the body to keep a variable _exactly_ constant. Is that achievable with feedback?
2. What does the gain of a control system tell you about the residual error after correction?
3. Find a familiar example of a regulated variable that _does_ vary measurably even in healthy people.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Identifies the misconception: that homeostasis means perfect constancy.
- Corrects: negative feedback minimises but cannot eliminate variation; some residual error always persists.
- Explains _why_ error persists (the system needs error to generate a response).
- Uses gain or a specific physiological example to support the correction.
- Restates the correct mental model: variables are kept _within narrow ranges_, not _exactly at_ set points.

A Yellow answer states the misconception is wrong but does not explain _why_ error persists structurally, or fails to provide a concrete example.

A Red answer agrees with the student's statement, or claims the body achieves perfect constancy "in theory," or uses a poor example (e.g., something that is not a regulated variable).

QUESTION 15
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Explain how feed-forward control complements negative feedback in maintaining a regulated variable. Use the physiological example of glucose homeostasis after a meal to anchor your explanation, and describe what would happen to post-prandial glucose excursions if feed-forward control were absent.

Model answer: Negative feedback acts _after_ a deviation has occurred; feed-forward acts _before_ the deviation, in anticipation [Sherwood ch.1; Guyton ch.1]. The two work as complementary layers: feed-forward reduces the magnitude of an expected deviation, and negative feedback corrects whatever residual deviation actually occurs. Glucose homeostasis is a useful worked example. As nutrients enter the gut after a meal, incretin hormones — GIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide) and GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) — are released by intestinal endocrine cells. These incretins augment insulin secretion from pancreatic β-cells _early_ during the absorptive phase, before plasma glucose reaches its post-prandial peak. By the time glucose absorption is complete, insulin concentrations have already risen substantially, and tissues (especially liver and skeletal muscle) are primed to take up the incoming glucose. The feed-forward limb therefore reduces the _size_ of the post-prandial glucose excursion rather than abolishing it. The negative-feedback limb then handles whatever residual rise occurs: rising glucose itself directly stimulates β-cell insulin secretion, which drives glucose uptake until plasma glucose returns toward fasting. Without feed-forward, the post-prandial glucose excursion would be much larger. Plasma glucose would rise higher and stay elevated longer, requiring a more aggressive (and delayed) negative-feedback insulin response. This is exactly what happens in patients with reduced incretin response (a feature of type 2 diabetes) — post-prandial hyperglycaemia is exaggerated, and incretin-targeting drugs (DPP-4 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists) work in part by restoring this feed-forward anticipation. Feed-forward also supports adaptive control: the brain learns from the size of past errors and refines the next feed-forward signal, making future anticipations more accurate — a form of "delayed negative feedback" [Guyton ch.1].

Elaborative explanation: The architectural relationship — feed-forward reduces excursion magnitude, negative feedback corrects residual — is general. Motor control uses the same combination: the cerebellum sends feed-forward commands based on the intended movement, while spinal reflexes provide negative-feedback correction during execution. Recognising the layered architecture is one of the most powerful frameworks in physiology and explains why systems whose feed-forward is impaired (diabetic gastroparesis affecting incretin release; cerebellar ataxia affecting motor feed-forward) appear "uncoordinated" even when their negative-feedback components are intact.

Hints:

1. Two architectures are at work, but they have different timing relative to the deviation. What is each doing?
2. In glucose homeostasis, name a hormone that is released _before_ glucose has risen. What triggers its release?
3. What would happen to the size of the post-prandial glucose peak if the anticipatory limb were missing?

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Defines feed-forward as anticipatory (acting _before_ the deviation).
- Defines negative feedback as reactive (acting _after_ the deviation).
- Explains how feed-forward reduces the magnitude of the excursion.
- Uses glucose homeostasis as the worked example: incretin hormones (GIP/GLP-1), released in response to gut nutrients, augment insulin secretion _early_ during absorption — reducing the size of the post-prandial glucose excursion rather than abolishing it.
- States what would happen without feed-forward: larger and longer post-prandial glucose excursions, more aggressive late insulin response (and clinical implication if known: this is what happens in T2DM).

A Yellow answer correctly distinguishes the two architectures but does not articulate the example clearly (e.g., does not name incretins or does not connect the example to clinical consequence).

A Red answer treats feed-forward and negative feedback as synonymous, or claims feed-forward is unnecessary in a healthy person, or describes the example incorrectly (e.g., says insulin only rises after glucose).
