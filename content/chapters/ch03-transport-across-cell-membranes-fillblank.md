## Pass 1 — Passive transport: diffusion, channels, aquaporins

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Each functional aquaporin in the plasma membrane is assembled from four AQP monomers; this oligomeric arrangement is called a \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: homotetramer

Accepted variants:

- homotetramer
- tetramer
- AQP tetramer
- homo-tetramer

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "dimer" or "trimer" → "Almost — but the number of monomers per assembled aquaporin is one greater than that. Think four."
- "heterotetramer" → "The four monomers are _identical_ — so the prefix should reflect that."

Explanation: Each aquaporin monomer has six membrane-spanning domains and a central water-conducting pore [Berne ch.1]. Four identical monomers assemble in the plasma membrane to form a homotetramer, with each monomer functioning as an independent water channel. The tetramer is the functional unit found in the membrane; the four pores conduct water at very high rates (~10⁹ molecules per second per channel) while excluding ions and most solutes.

Hints:

1. The number of monomers per assembled aquaporin equals the number of fingers on one hand minus one.
2. The four subunits are _identical_ — this matters for the prefix.
3. Combine "homo-" (same) with the Greek prefix for "four."

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The aquaporin isoform that is inserted into the apical membrane of the renal collecting duct in response to ADH (vasopressin) and increases water reabsorption is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: AQP2

Accepted variants:

- AQP2
- aquaporin-2
- aquaporin 2
- AQP-2

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "AQP1" → "AQP1 is constitutively expressed in many tissues including the renal proximal tubule and descending limb of the loop of Henle, but the _ADH-regulated_ isoform in the collecting duct is a different number."
- "AQP3" or "AQP4" → "AQP3 and AQP4 are also expressed in the renal collecting duct, but on the _basolateral_ membrane (not apical). The ADH-regulated apical AQP has a different number."

Explanation: AQP2 is the ADH-regulated water channel inserted into the apical membrane of principal cells in the renal collecting duct [Berne ch.1]. ADH binds V2 receptors on the basolateral membrane, raises cellular cAMP, and triggers translocation of AQP2-containing vesicles to the apical membrane. With AQP2 in place, water flows down its osmotic gradient from the hypotonic tubular fluid into the hypertonic medullary interstitium — concentrated urine is produced. Mutations in _AQP2_ (or in the V2 receptor) cause nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, in which the collecting duct cannot respond to ADH and dilute urine is excreted in large volumes.

Hints:

1. The number is one of the lowest aquaporin numbers — early discovered, well-studied.
2. The apical membrane of the principal cell needs an aquaporin that _moves_ in and out under hormonal control.
3. The number is between 1 and 5.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Open pores such as aquaporins conduct water at approximately \_\_\_ molecules per second per channel.

Canonical answer: 10⁹ (one billion)

Accepted variants:

- 10⁹
- 10^9
- 10 to the 9th
- ~10⁹
- one billion
- 1,000,000,000

Tolerance: ±1 order of magnitude (10⁸ to 10¹⁰ all grade Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "10⁶" → "That is the rate for _gated channels_, not open pores. Open pores are about three orders of magnitude faster."
- "10²" or "10³" → "That is the rate for _carriers_ and _ATP-dependent transporters_. Pores are much faster."

Explanation: The transport rate hierarchy is striking [Berne ch.1, Table 1.3]: open pores (e.g., aquaporins, mitochondrial outer-membrane porins) conduct up to ~10⁹ molecules per second; gated channels conduct 10⁶ to 10⁸ per second when open; solute carriers (uniporters, symporters, antiporters) cycle at 10² to 10⁴ per second; ATP-dependent transporters cycle at the same lower range. The hierarchy reflects the underlying mechanism — pores are simply open conduits, channels gate, and carriers/pumps must undergo conformational change with each cycle.

Hints:

1. The fastest class of transporters can conduct molecules at this rate.
2. Pores are several orders of magnitude faster than carriers.
3. The number is one followed by nine zeros.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: In carrier-mediated transport, the rate plateaus at a maximum value when all binding sites are saturated. This maximum is called the transport maximum, abbreviated \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: Tm

Accepted variants:

- Tm
- T-max
- Tmax
- T(max)
- transport maximum

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "Vmax" → "Vmax is the analogous concept in _enzyme kinetics_. The transport-physiology equivalent uses a different abbreviation."
- "Km" → "Km is the substrate concentration at half-maximal _enzyme_ velocity. The transport-physiology maximum uses a different abbreviation."

Explanation: Tm (transport maximum) is the rate of carrier-mediated transport when all binding sites on the carrier are occupied [Costanzo ch.1]. Above the substrate concentration that produces Tm, transport rate does not increase further — additional substrate cannot find binding sites. The kinetics resemble Michaelis-Menten enzyme kinetics; Tm is the transport analogue of Vmax. Tm-limited reabsorption underlies clinical phenomena such as the renal glucose threshold (glucose appears in urine when filtered load exceeds Tm) and the renal phosphate threshold.

Hints:

1. The abbreviation is two characters — a capital letter and a lowercase letter.
2. The first character matches the first letter of "transport"; the second matches the first letter of "maximum."
3. The concept is analogous to Vmax in enzyme kinetics.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The ratio of a solute's solubility in oil relative to its solubility in water is called the \_\_\_ coefficient and predicts how readily the solute crosses the lipid bilayer.

Canonical answer: partition

Accepted variants:

- partition
- Partition
- partition coefficient

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "diffusion" → "Different concept — the diffusion coefficient (D) describes a solute's mobility in solution, captured by the Stokes-Einstein equation. The ratio of oil-to-water solubility is named differently."
- "permeability" → "Permeability (P) is the _combined_ property — it includes the solute's oil:water solubility, its diffusion coefficient, and the membrane thickness. The oil:water solubility alone has its own name."

Explanation: The partition coefficient (K) describes a solute's relative solubility in oil compared to water [Costanzo ch.1]. It predicts how easily the solute will dissolve in the hydrophobic core of the lipid bilayer and therefore how rapidly it will cross by simple diffusion. Nonpolar solutes (gases, steroids, lipid-soluble drugs) have high partition coefficients and cross the membrane easily; polar solutes (ions, sugars, amino acids) have low partition coefficients and cross poorly. The partition coefficient enters Fick's law via permeability: P = KD/Δx.

Hints:

1. The word means "division" or "splitting" — referring to a solute dividing between two phases.
2. The two phases are hydrophobic (oil) and hydrophilic (water).
3. Eight letters; begins with "p" and ends in "n."

## Pass 2 — Active transport: pumps and exchangers

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: For each ATP molecule hydrolysed, the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase extrudes \_\_\_ Na⁺ ions from the cell.

Canonical answer: 3

Accepted variants:

- 3
- three

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "2" → "Two is the number of _K⁺ ions imported_ per cycle, not Na⁺ extruded. The two stoichiometries differ by one — that's what makes the pump electrogenic."
- "1" → "The pump moves more than one Na⁺ per cycle. Try a number that is one greater than the number of K⁺ ions imported."

Explanation: With each cycle of ATP hydrolysis, the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase moves 3 Na⁺ ions out of the cell and 2 K⁺ ions into the cell [Berne ch.1]. The 3:2 stoichiometry is asymmetric, making the pump _electrogenic_ — net positive charge leaves the cell each cycle, contributing slightly to the negative resting membrane potential. The pump is the principal mechanism that establishes and maintains the steep Na⁺ and K⁺ gradients across the plasma membrane; these gradients in turn power secondary active transport and excitable-cell function.

Hints:

1. The number is one greater than the number of K⁺ ions imported per cycle.
2. The two stoichiometries together sum to five.
3. The pump is _electrogenic_ — there is a net outward charge per cycle.

- alpha
- α-subunit
- alpha subunit

QUESTION 10
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX) on the cardiac myocyte plasma membrane has a stoichiometry of \_\_\_ Na⁺ ions exchanged for one Ca²⁺ ion.

Canonical answer: 3

Accepted variants:

- 3
- three
- 3:1
- 3 to 1

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "2" → "Two is the K⁺ stoichiometry of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. NCX has a different ratio — and notably one that is _electrogenic_."
- "1" → "Equimolar exchange would not be electrogenic. NCX exchanges more than one Na⁺ per Ca²⁺."

Explanation: The Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX) couples movement of 3 Na⁺ ions in one direction to 1 Ca²⁺ ion in the opposite direction [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. Under normal conditions in cardiac myocytes (Na⁺ low intracellularly, Ca²⁺ low intracellularly, membrane near resting potential), the exchanger runs in "forward" mode: 3 Na⁺ in, 1 Ca²⁺ out. The 3:1 stoichiometry makes NCX electrogenic — net positive charge moves into the cell when Ca²⁺ is being extruded. Inhibition of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase by cardiac glycosides raises intracellular Na⁺, which reduces the driving force for NCX-mediated Ca²⁺ extrusion, raising intracellular Ca²⁺ and increasing cardiac contractility.

Hints:

1. The Na⁺ stoichiometry is the same as the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase Na⁺ stoichiometry per cycle.
2. NCX is electrogenic, so the ratio is not 1:1.
3. The number is the same as the number of Na⁺ ions extruded per Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase cycle.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: CFTR (cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator) is a member of the \_\_\_ family of transporters — a class of ATP-dependent transporters defined by ATP-binding domains.

Canonical answer: ABC (ATP-binding cassette)

Accepted variants:

- ABC
- ATP-binding cassette
- ABC transporter
- ATP binding cassette

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "P-type" or "P-type ATPase" → "P-type ATPases include the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase and SERCA. CFTR is a different ATP-dependent transporter class."
- "V-type" → "V-type ATPases (vacuolar) acidify endosomes and lysosomes. CFTR is a different class."

Explanation: ABC (ATP-binding cassette) transporters are a large family of ATP-dependent transporters found in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes [Berne ch.1]. They are characterised by amino-acid domains that bind ATP (the ABC domains). Humans have at least seven subgroups and over 40 specific transporters. CFTR is one of the more unusual members: rather than transporting molecules through itself, it functions as an _ATP-gated chloride channel_ and also regulates other transporters (e.g., the epithelial Na⁺ channel ENaC). Other notable ABC transporters include P-glycoprotein (multidrug resistance protein, exports many drugs from cancer cells) and the bile salt export pump.

Hints:

1. The acronym has three letters, one of which is "B."
2. Each letter stands for a word: ATP, binding, cassette.
3. The first three letters of the alphabet, in order.

Stem: The most common disease-causing mutation in CFTR — accounting for the majority of cystic fibrosis cases — is the deletion of phenylalanine at position 508, abbreviated \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: F508del

Accepted variants:

- F508del
- ΔF508
- delta-F508
- DeltaF508
- ΔPhe508
- delF508
- p.Phe508del

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "G551D" or "R117H" → "Those are CFTR mutations seen in cystic fibrosis but they are far less common than the dominant deletion at residue 508."
- "F508" alone → "Almost — F508 names the residue, but the _deletion_ must be indicated. Add 'del' or use the Δ prefix."

Explanation: F508del (also written ΔF508) is a deletion of the codon for phenylalanine at position 508 of CFTR [Berne ch.1]. The deletion causes the protein to misfold during synthesis in the endoplasmic reticulum; the misfolded protein is identified by the ER quality-control system, retained, and degraded — so very little F508del-CFTR ever reaches the plasma membrane. The result is loss of CFTR-mediated chloride conductance on epithelial apical membranes (airways, sweat glands, pancreas, intestine, vas deferens), with the clinical phenotype of cystic fibrosis. Modulator drugs such as lumacaftor (a "corrector" that helps the misfolded protein traffic to the membrane) and ivacaftor (a "potentiator" that increases channel gating once the protein is in place) restore some function and represent a major therapeutic advance.

Hints:

1. The mutation is a _deletion_ of a single amino acid — which one?
2. The position number is between 500 and 510.
3. The amino acid deleted is phenylalanine (one-letter code F).

## Pass 3 — Vesicular transport, clinical anchors, and concentrations

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Receptor-mediated endocytosis is initiated at specialised regions of the plasma membrane known as coated pits, which are coated on their cytoplasmic face by the protein \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: clathrin

Accepted variants:

- clathrin
- Clathrin
- clathrin protein

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "caveolin" → "Caveolin coats _caveolae_ — a different type of membrane invagination. The coated pits used in classical receptor-mediated endocytosis are coated with a different protein."
- "actin" → "Actin is involved in vesicle scission and movement, but it does not form the coat of the pit itself."
- "dynamin" → "Dynamin is the GTPase that pinches off the vesicle from the membrane — but it is not the _coat_ protein of the pit."

Explanation: Receptor-mediated endocytosis begins at _clathrin-coated pits_ — invaginations of the plasma membrane lined on their cytoplasmic face by clathrin, a protein that assembles into a triskelion lattice resembling a soccer-ball geometry [Berne ch.1]. The clathrin coat helps shape the membrane into a curved pit and selects cargo. Adaptor proteins (adaptins) link the clathrin lattice to the cytoplasmic tails of cargo receptors. Dynamin, a GTPase, then pinches off the coated vesicle from the membrane. Once internalised, the clathrin coat dissociates, the vesicle delivers its cargo to early endosomes, and the receptor is typically recycled to the surface.

Hints:

1. The protein assembles into a triskelion-shaped basket-like lattice.
2. It is _not_ caveolin (which coats caveolae) and _not_ dynamin (which scissions the vesicle).
3. The name begins with "C-L" and is eight letters.

QUESTION 14
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Bacteria coated with antibody are recognised by macrophage Fc receptors and engulfed by phagocytosis. The most common antibody class involved in this opsonisation is \_\_\_ .

Canonical answer: IgG

Accepted variants:

- IgG
- immunoglobulin G
- IgG antibody

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "IgM" → "IgM activates complement very effectively but binds Fc receptors poorly. The major opsonising antibody for direct macrophage Fc-receptor recognition is a different class."
- "IgA" → "IgA dominates mucosal immunity but is not the major opsonising antibody class for systemic phagocytosis."
- "IgE" → "IgE binds mast cell and basophil Fc receptors and triggers allergic responses — it is not the antibody class involved in opsonising bacteria for macrophage phagocytosis."

Explanation: Opsonisation refers to the coating of an antigen — typically a bacterium — by host molecules that mark it for phagocytic uptake [Berne ch.1]. The two most important opsonins are _IgG_ antibodies and _complement protein C3b_. Macrophages and neutrophils have Fc receptors (specifically FcγR) that bind the Fc portion of IgG, leaving the antigen-binding region free to recognise the bacterial surface. Bacteria opsonised with IgG bind tightly to phagocytes and are engulfed efficiently; non-opsonised bacteria are engulfed much more slowly. The clinical relevance: encapsulated bacteria (such as _S. pneumoniae_, _H. influenzae_, _N. meningitidis_) resist opsonisation because their capsules conceal opsonin-binding sites — this is why splenectomised patients (lacking efficient opsonisation in the spleen) are vulnerable to overwhelming infection by these organisms.

Hints:

1. The antibody class has the highest serum concentration and the longest half-life.
2. The Fc receptor on macrophages that binds it is called FcγR.
3. The Greek letter γ in the receptor name is the clue to the antibody class.

Stem: Glucose typically begins to appear in the urine when plasma glucose exceeds approximately \_\_\_ mg/dL — the renal glucose threshold.

Canonical answer: 180 mg/dL

Accepted variants:

- 180
- 180 mg/dL
- ~180 mg/dL
- 180-200 mg/dL
- about 180

Tolerance: ±20 mg/dL

Yellow conditions:

- Numeric value matches but unit missing → "Right value — include the units (mg/dL)."
- "90" or "100" → "That is the _normal fasting_ plasma glucose. The threshold for glycosuria is roughly twice the normal fasting value."
- Value 300 or higher → "Too high — glycosuria appears at a lower threshold than that."

Explanation: The renal glucose threshold is the plasma glucose concentration above which glucose appears in the urine — typically around 180–200 mg/dL [Costanzo ch.1]. Below this threshold, the filtered glucose load is fully reabsorbed in the proximal tubule by SGLT carriers (SGLT2 reabsorbs ~90% in the early proximal tubule; SGLT1 reabsorbs the remainder). Above this threshold, the filtered load exceeds the carriers' capacity (Tm); the excess is excreted in the urine. The threshold underlies the classical clinical sign of uncontrolled diabetes — glycosuria. SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) are antidiabetic drugs that pharmacologically lower the renal glucose threshold by inhibiting reabsorption, intentionally promoting glycosuria as a glucose-lowering mechanism.

Stem: Glucose enters the enterocyte against its gradient via the apical Na⁺-glucose symporter SGLT1, accumulates in the cell, and then exits across the basolateral membrane by _facilitated diffusion_ through a member of the \_\_\_ family of glucose uniporters.

Canonical answer: GLUT (specifically GLUT2 in enterocytes)

Accepted variants:

- GLUT
- GLUT family
- GLUT2
- glucose transporter
- SLC2

Tolerance: not applicable

Yellow conditions:

- "SGLT" or "SGLT2" → "SGLTs are _symporters_ on the _apical_ membrane that move glucose into the cell against its gradient. The _basolateral_ exit pathway uses a different family of uniporters."
- "ABC" → "ABC transporters use ATP and transport a different set of substrates. Glucose's basolateral exit is via passive facilitated diffusion."

Explanation: Intestinal (and renal proximal tubular) glucose absorption is a two-step process [Berne ch.1; Costanzo ch.1]. _Apical step:_ glucose moves from the lumen into the enterocyte against its concentration gradient via SGLT1 (a Na⁺-glucose symporter; secondary active transport, with the Na⁺ gradient maintained by basolateral Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase). _Basolateral step:_ glucose, now at high intracellular concentration, leaves the cell down its concentration gradient via GLUT2, a uniporter (facilitated diffusion). The GLUT family (encoded by SLC2 genes) includes GLUT1 (most cells, especially RBCs and brain), GLUT2 (liver, β cells, intestine, kidney), GLUT3 (neurons), GLUT4 (insulin-regulated, in muscle and adipose tissue), and others. GLUT4 is the insulin-regulated transporter whose translocation defect underlies type 2 diabetes mellitus.

Hints:

2. Members include GLUT1, GLUT2, GLUT3, GLUT4.
