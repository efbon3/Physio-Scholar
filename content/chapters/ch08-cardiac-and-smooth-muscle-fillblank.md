## Pass 1 — Values (numerical anchors)

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The cardiac ventricular action potential lasts about \_\_\_ ms — far longer than the skeletal-muscle action potential.

Canonical answer: ~200 ms (150–300 ms)

Accepted variants:

- 200 ms
- ~200 ms
- 150–300 ms
- 200
- 250 ms

Tolerance: ±100 ms (100–300 ms graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "5" → "Too short — that is the _skeletal_ muscle AP duration; cardiac ventricular APs last ~200 ms because of the long Ca²⁺-driven plateau."
- "1000" → "Too long — cardiac ventricular APs are ~200 ms; that figure approaches uterine smooth muscle APs in labour."

Explanation: The plateau (Phase 2) accounts for most of this duration [Berne & Levy ch.13]. A long AP produces a long absolute refractory period, preventing tetanus and ensuring reliable diastolic filling — a critical design feature for a pump that must contract rhythmically without fusing into sustained contraction. Sustained Ca²⁺ entry through L-type channels during the plateau also amplifies the trigger Ca²⁺ for CICR, supporting graded contractility. Class III antiarrhythmics (amiodarone, dofetilide) prolong the AP further by blocking IKr; congenital long-QT mutations of IKs/IKr also prolong it.

Hints:

1. Far longer than the skeletal muscle AP.
2. The plateau makes up most of the duration.
3. Around 200 ms.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX) on the cardiac sarcolemma exchanges \_\_\_ Na⁺ ions for 1 Ca²⁺ ion.

Canonical answer: 3

Accepted variants:

- 3
- three

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "1" → "Too few — NCX exchanges 3 Na⁺ for 1 Ca²⁺, generating one net inward charge per forward cycle (electrogenic)."
- "2" → "That stoichiometry would be electroneutral; NCX is electrogenic, exchanging 3 Na⁺ : 1 Ca²⁺."

Explanation: 3 Na⁺ : 1 Ca²⁺ — electrogenic, generating one net inward charge per forward cycle [Berne & Levy ch.13; Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. NCX uses the inward Na⁺ electrochemical gradient (set by the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) to extrude Ca²⁺ — the dominant sarcolemmal Ca²⁺ removal route per beat. NCX direction depends on driving forces: at rest it extrudes Ca²⁺ (forward mode); when intracellular Na⁺ rises (e.g., digoxin-induced Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase inhibition) or the membrane depolarises, it can reverse, importing Ca²⁺ — the basis of digoxin's positive inotropy and of ischaemic Ca²⁺ overload.

Hints:

1. The exchanger is electrogenic — net charge moves per cycle.
2. The ratio uses the Na⁺ gradient to drive Ca²⁺ extrusion.
3. Three Na⁺ in for each Ca²⁺ out.

Stem: SERCA in cardiac muscle is regulated by an inhibitory protein called \_\_\_; PKA phosphorylation of this protein relieves SERCA inhibition and accelerates relaxation.

Canonical answer: phospholamban (PLB / PLN)

Accepted variants:

- phospholamban
- PLB
- PLN

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "calsequestrin" → "Calsequestrin is the _luminal_ SR Ca²⁺ buffer; the _PKA-regulated SERCA inhibitor_ is phospholamban."
- "calmodulin" → "Calmodulin is a Ca²⁺ sensor for many enzymes; the specific SERCA-inhibitory regulator phosphorylated by PKA is phospholamban."

Explanation: Unphosphorylated phospholamban (PLB) inhibits SERCA2a; PKA-mediated phosphorylation (e.g., during β-adrenergic stimulation) lifts the inhibition [Berne & Levy ch.13]. The result is faster Ca²⁺ reuptake → faster relaxation (positive lusitropy) — one of four PKA effects of sympathetic stimulation (the others: Cav1.2 phosphorylation increasing Ca²⁺ entry, troponin I phosphorylation reducing myofilament Ca²⁺ sensitivity, and HCN/If phosphorylation accelerating pacemaker activity). Loss-of-function PLB or rare gain-of-function variants cause familial dilated cardiomyopathy. SERCA2a gene therapy (CUPID trials) targeted this axis but produced disappointing clinical results.

Hints:

1. A small (~52 amino acid) integral membrane protein in the SR.
2. Inhibits SERCA2a until phosphorylated.
3. Phosphorylated by PKA during β-adrenergic stimulation.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Smooth muscle uses approximately \_\_\_ as much ATP as skeletal muscle to maintain the same isometric force.

Canonical answer: 1/10 to 1/300 (~1% to 10%)

Accepted variants:

- 1/10 to 1/300
- 10–100 fold less
- ~1%
- ~10%
- one-tenth to one three-hundredth

Tolerance: order of magnitude (1–10%)

Yellow conditions:

- "the same" → "Smooth muscle uses _much less_ ATP per unit force than skeletal muscle — the latch state is the energetic basis."
- "10× more" → "The ratio is _inverted_ — smooth muscle is 10–300× _more_ economical, not less."

Explanation: The "latch state" — slow cross-bridge cycling, prolonged cross-bridge attachment with low ATPase rate — gives smooth muscle exceptional energy economy, essential for sustained vascular and sphincter tone [Guyton & Hall ch.8]. After initial Ca²⁺/MLCK-driven phosphorylation and contraction, dephosphorylated cross-bridges remain attached but cycle very slowly (proposed by Murphy and Hai). Vascular smooth muscle holds blood-pressure-relevant tone for hours without exhausting ATP; uterine smooth muscle in labour can also sustain prolonged contraction at low ATP cost. The contrast with skeletal muscle (high force, high ATP cost, rapid fatigue at high effort) is one of the defining design contrasts in muscle biology.

Hints:

1. Smooth muscle is far more energy-economical for sustained tone.
2. The mechanism is the latch state.
3. One to two orders of magnitude less ATP than skeletal.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Slow waves in gastrointestinal smooth muscle in the stomach are generated at approximately \_\_\_ cycles per minute.

Canonical answer: 3 per minute

Accepted variants:

- 3
- 3/min
- 3 cpm
- ~3
- three per minute

Tolerance: ±1 (2–4 cpm graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "12" → "That is the _duodenal_ slow-wave frequency; gastric is ~3 cpm."
- "60" → "That is closer to a heart rate; gastric slow-wave frequency is ~3 cpm."

Explanation: Gastric: ~3 slow waves/min. Duodenum: ~12. Ileum: ~8. Large intestine: 6–12 [Guyton & Hall ch.8; Ganong]. Slow-wave rate is determined by interstitial cells of Cajal (ICC), c-Kit-positive pacemaker cells embedded in the gut wall. Slow waves are not action potentials per se but oscillating depolarisations that determine _when_ APs and contractions can occur (when superimposed APs reach threshold during the depolarised phase). Loss of ICC contributes to slow-transit constipation, gastroparesis, and chronic intestinal pseudo-obstruction; ICC are also the origin cells of GI stromal tumours (GISTs).

Hints:

1. The gastric pacemaker rate is set by interstitial cells of Cajal.
2. Slower than duodenal (12), ileum (8), or colon (6–12).
3. Around 3 per minute.

Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The resting membrane potential of cardiac ventricular muscle (and atrial myocytes) is approximately \_\_\_ mV.

Canonical answer: −80 to −90 mV

Accepted variants:

- −80 mV
- −90 mV
- −85 mV
- −80 to −90 mV
- ~−85

Tolerance: ±10 mV (−70 to −95 mV graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "−60" → "That is the _SA/AV nodal_ resting potential — pacemaker cells lack IK1 and rest at less negative potentials. Working ventricular myocytes rest at −80 to −90 mV."
- "0" → "That is closer to a depolarised cell; resting ventricular myocytes are strongly negative inside (−80 to −90 mV)."

Explanation: Set primarily by the inward rectifier K⁺ current (IK1) [Berne & Levy ch.13]. SA-node and AV-node cells lack IK1 and are less negative (~−60 mV) — allowing them to be spontaneously depolarising pacemakers via the funny current (If). The deeply negative resting potential of working myocardium ensures that spontaneous firing does not occur outside the conduction system; pathological loss of IK1 (or gain of inward leak) can produce ectopic automaticity and arrhythmias. Hyperkalaemia depolarises the resting potential (less negative) and eventually inactivates Na⁺ channels, producing wide QRS, bradycardia, and arrest.

Hints:

1. Working ventricular myocardium rests strongly negative.
2. IK1 sets the resting potential.
3. Around −85 mV.

Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Sarcomere length in cardiac muscle is constrained by stiff cardiac titin and ECM to a working range of approximately \_\_\_ µm.

Canonical answer: 1.9–2.2 µm

Accepted variants:

- 1.9–2.2 µm
- ~2.0 µm
- 2.0–2.2 µm
- ~2 µm

Tolerance: ±0.3 µm

Yellow conditions:

- "3.6" → "That is the upper limit of skeletal-muscle stretch (descending limb endpoint); cardiac sarcomeres are constrained to 1.9–2.2 µm."
- "1" → "Too short — cardiac sarcomeres operate near optimal length, ~1.9–2.2 µm."

Explanation: Cardiac muscle operates almost entirely on the ascending limb of the length–tension curve — the basis of monotonic Frank–Starling response within physiological volumes [Berne & Levy ch.13]. Skeletal muscle, by contrast, can be stretched well onto the descending limb. Cardiac titin's stiffer N2B isoform plus extracellular matrix collagen prevent over-stretch — protective because excessive sarcomere stretch would impair force generation. The Frank–Starling mechanism therefore manifests as an ever-increasing force with increasing preload, never as a paradoxical decline (which would occur on the descending limb).

Hints:

1. The cardiac working range is narrower than skeletal.
2. Stiff cardiac titin (N2B) and ECM collagen impose the limit.
3. Around 2 µm.

Stem: Approximately \_\_\_% of contractile Ca²⁺ in a typical ventricular twitch comes from the SR (the rest entering across the sarcolemma via L-type Ca²⁺ channels).

Canonical answer: ~75% (varies 70–90% by species and load)

Accepted variants:

- 75%
- ~75
- 70–90%
- about three-quarters

Tolerance: ±15% (60–90% graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "100" → "Cardiac contractile Ca²⁺ comes from _both_ SR and sarcolemma (~75/25); only _skeletal_ muscle is essentially 100% SR."

Hints:

Canonical answer: −50 to −60 mV

Accepted variants:

- −50 mV
- −60 mV

Explanation: Lower [K⁺] selectivity and active electrogenic transport contribute; the less-negative resting potential is closer to the activation threshold of L-type Ca²⁺ channels, allowing graded responses to small depolarisations and tonic Ca²⁺ entry [Guyton & Hall ch.8]. - 10⁻⁷ M

Stem: The minimal interval between cardiac action potentials at the maximum physiological heart rate is set by the absolute refractory period, which lasts approximately \_\_\_ ms.

Canonical answer: ~250 ms (200–300 ms)

Accepted variants:

- 250 ms
- ~250 ms
- 200–300 ms
- 240 ms

Stem: The cell–cell junction in cardiac muscle that contains both mechanical anchors (fascia adherens, desmosomes) and electrical connections (gap junctions) is the \_\_\_.

Hints:

## Summary

This 46-question fill-in-the-blank set covers cardiac and smooth muscle across three passes. Pass 1 (Q1–17) anchors numerical values: cardiac AP duration and refractoriness (~200 ms / ~250 ms), NCX stoichiometry (3:1), phospholamban and SERCA, smooth-muscle ATP economy (1/10–1/300), gastric slow waves (3/min), cardiac and smooth-muscle resting potentials (−85 vs −55 mV), Cx43 in intercalated discs, cardiac sarcomere working range (1.9–2.2 µm), Ca²⁺ source partitioning (75% SR / 25% sarcolemma), smooth-muscle contraction onset (a few seconds), cytosolic Ca²⁺ at rest (0.1 µM), cardiac fatty acid oxidation (60–70%), mitochondrial volume (~30%), dyad gap (~12 nm), and smooth-muscle cell dimensions. Pass 2 (Q18–32) defines named structures and concepts: intercalated disc, dyad, RyR2, the funny current (If), Frank–Starling law, positive inotropy, Bowditch (Treppe) effect, MLCK and MLCP, the latch state, dense bodies, single-unit (visceral) smooth muscle, interstitial cells of Cajal, stress-relaxation, and CPVT. Pass 3 (Q33–46) develops relations: PKA phosphorylation of Cav1.2 producing inotropy, GIRK/IK,ACh activation in vagal slowing, digoxin's inhibition of Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, Ca²⁺ channel blockers acting on Cav1.2, PDE5 inhibitors raising cGMP, NO activation of soluble guanylyl cyclase, Rho-kinase phosphorylation of MYPT1 producing Ca²⁺ sensitisation, the cardiac plateau as Ca²⁺ vs K⁺ balance, NCX1 for sarcolemmal Ca²⁺ extrusion, L-type channels for smooth-muscle Ca²⁺ entry, ETA for endothelin-1, MLCK in α1 vasoconstriction, torsade de pointes from drug-induced QT prolongation, and Gs for β2-mediated bronchial relaxation.
