## Pass 1 — Values (numerical anchors)

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The average resting length of a skeletal-muscle sarcomere is approximately \_\_\_ µm.

Canonical answer: ~2 µm (about 2.0 µm)

Accepted variants:

- 2 µm
- 2.0 µm
- ~2 µm
- 2
- 2.0–2.2 µm

Tolerance: ±0.3 µm (1.7–2.3 µm graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "1" → "Too short — 1 µm is approaching the lower end of the ascending limb where opposite thin filaments overlap; resting sarcomere length is ~2 µm."
- "3.5" → "Too long — 3.5 µm is near the upper end of the descending limb with minimal overlap; resting sarcomere length is ~2 µm."

Explanation: Sarcomeres average ~2 µm at rest [Berne & Levy ch.12]. The optimal length for active force generation is ~2.0–2.2 µm — the position of greatest thick–thin filament overlap, where every myosin head can engage an actin site. Above this length, force falls because of reduced overlap (descending limb of length–tension curve); below, force falls because opposite thin filaments interfere and thick filaments collide with Z lines (ascending limb). The plateau of optimal force corresponds to a narrow range around the resting length.

Hints:

1. The number is on the micrometre scale, not nanometre.
2. The same length corresponds to the peak of the length–tension curve.
3. Around 2.

Stem: Each tropomyosin dimer covers \_\_\_ actin monomers along the thin filament.

Canonical answer: 7

Accepted variants:

- 7
- seven

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "1" → "Too few — each tropomyosin dimer is long enough to span seven actin monomers along F-actin."
- "14" → "Too many — that is two consecutive tropomyosin dimers; each individual dimer covers seven."

Explanation: Sequential tropomyosin dimers are arranged head-to-tail along F-actin; each spans seven monomers and carries one bound troponin complex (TnT-TnI-TnC) [Berne & Levy ch.12]. At low cytosolic Ca²⁺, tropomyosin lies in the steric-block position over the myosin-binding sites on actin; Ca²⁺ binding to TnC shifts tropomyosin into the groove and exposes the binding sites — the steric blocking model of thin-filament regulation.

Hints:

1. A single repeating unit covers a small integer number of actin monomers.
2. The number is the same as the count of troponin complexes per tropomyosin (one per seven actins).
3. Seven.

Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: A skeletal-muscle action potential lasts only about \_\_\_ ms.

Canonical answer: 2–5 ms (~3 ms)

Accepted variants:

- 2–5 ms
- 3 ms
- ~3 ms
- 2 ms
- 5 ms

Tolerance: ±2 ms

Yellow conditions:

- "0.5" → "Too short — 0.5 ms is closer to a synaptic delay; skeletal-muscle APs last 2–5 ms."
- "200" → "Too long — that is closer to the cardiac AP; skeletal-muscle APs are brief (2–5 ms) so that tetanic summation is possible."

Explanation: Brevity is essential — a long AP would impose a long absolute refractory period and prevent tetanic summation, as is the case in cardiac muscle (~200 ms) [Berne & Levy ch.12]. Skeletal muscle uses a brief Na⁺-dependent AP without a sustained Ca²⁺-mediated plateau, allowing repeated stimulation at high frequency to produce unfused or fused tetanus, with force several-fold greater than a single twitch. Cardiac muscle's long AP is the design feature that prevents tetanic contraction (which would be incompatible with rhythmic pumping).

Hints:

1. Single digits of milliseconds.
2. Brief enough to allow tetanic summation.
3. Distinct from the cardiac AP, which is ~50–100× longer.

Stem: SERCA transports \_\_\_ Ca²⁺ ions into the SR for each ATP molecule it hydrolyses.

Canonical answer: 2

Accepted variants:

- 2
- two

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "1" → "Too few — SERCA's stoichiometry is 2 Ca²⁺ per 1 ATP, with 2–3 H⁺ countertransported."
- "4" → "Too many — that is closer to the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase per cycle (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in); SERCA pumps 2 Ca²⁺ per ATP."

Explanation: SERCA's stoichiometry is 2 Ca²⁺ pumped from cytosol into the SR per 1 ATP hydrolysed [Berne & Levy ch.12]. This active reuptake terminates the cytosolic Ca²⁺ transient and produces relaxation. SERCA1 dominates in fast-twitch muscle, SERCA2a in slow-twitch and cardiac (where it is regulated by phospholamban). Loss of SERCA1 function (Brody disease) impairs relaxation. SERCA inhibition by thapsigargin is a classical experimental tool. PMCA and Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchange contribute much less than SERCA to total Ca²⁺ removal in skeletal muscle.

Hints:

1. The classical "P-type ATPase" with 2 ions per ATP.
2. Same number as the K⁺ component of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase per cycle.
3. Two.

Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Aerobic oxidation of one mole of glucose-1-phosphate (i.e., from glycogen) generates approximately \_\_\_ mol of ATP in skeletal muscle.

Canonical answer: ~37 ATP

Accepted variants:

- 37
- ~37
- 36–38
- 37 mol ATP

Tolerance: ±2 (35–39 graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "2" → "Too few — that is the _anaerobic_ glycolysis yield from blood glucose; aerobic oxidation of glycogen-derived glucose-1-phosphate yields ~37 ATP."
- "100" → "Too many — single-glucose oxidation in muscle yields ~36–38 ATP, not 100."

Explanation: Glycogen-derived glucose-1-phosphate enters glycolysis without consuming ATP at the hexokinase step (because phosphoglucomutase converts G1P to G6P with no ATP cost), yielding ~37 ATP [Berne & Levy ch.12]. Free blood glucose costs 1 ATP at hexokinase, giving ~36. The newer textbook accounting (with realistic mitochondrial proton leak and shuttle inefficiencies) is ~30–32 ATP per glucose; the classical figure of 36–38 is what most physiology texts still teach. Anaerobically, the yields collapse to 3 (glycogen) and 2 (glucose) per glucose unit — 10-fold less efficient.

Hints:

1. Aerobic — uses the full ETC and oxidative phosphorylation.
2. Glycogen-derived input avoids the first ATP-consuming step.
3. ~37 ATP.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Anaerobic glycolysis from blood glucose yields only about \_\_\_ mol ATP per mole of glucose.

Canonical answer: 2

Accepted variants:

- 2
- two
- 2 mol
- 2 ATP

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "36" → "That is the _aerobic_ yield; anaerobic glycolysis from glucose stops at lactate and yields only 2 ATP."
- "3" → "That is the anaerobic yield from _glycogen_-derived G1P (no ATP spent at hexokinase); from _blood glucose_, the yield is 2."

Explanation: Anaerobically, glucose → 2 lactate + 2 ATP (or 3 ATP from glycogen-derived G1P) [Berne & Levy ch.12]. The much lower yield is why anaerobic capacity is limited and lactate accumulates rapidly in heavy exercise. Lactate is exported to the liver via the Cori cycle for gluconeogenesis (consuming 6 ATP per glucose regenerated — net energetic cost), or oxidised by other tissues (heart, brain, type I fibres). The lactate threshold during exercise marks the work rate above which aerobic oxidation cannot keep pace with glycolytic flux.

Hints:

1. Net ATP from substrate-level phosphorylation in glycolysis only.
2. About 18-fold less than aerobic oxidation.
3. Two.

Stem: The phosphocreatine reservoir in active skeletal muscle is sufficient to sustain maximal contraction for only about \_\_\_ seconds before glycolytic and oxidative ATP regeneration must take over.

Canonical answer: 5–10 seconds

Accepted variants:

- 5–10 seconds
- 10 s
- ~5 s
- a few seconds
- ~10 seconds

Tolerance: ±5 seconds

Yellow conditions:

- "1" → "Too short — PCr buffers ATP for several seconds, not less than one."
- "60" → "Too long — PCr is exhausted within ~5–10 seconds of maximal effort; glycolysis takes over from there."

Explanation: PCr provides the immediate ATP buffer via creatine kinase (PCr + ADP ↔ Cr + ATP) [Berne & Levy ch.12]. The store is ~5× resting ATP; depletion is rapid in maximal exertion. PCr is replenished post-exercise by oxidative phosphorylation (the reason recovery O₂ consumption — "EPOC" — remains elevated for minutes). Creatine supplementation in athletes raises PCr stores by ~20% and improves brief high-intensity performance. Serum creatine kinase (CK-MM in muscle, CK-MB in heart, CK-BB in brain) rises with muscle injury — the basis of clinical CK testing for rhabdomyolysis and (historically) MI.

Hints:

1. The PCr buffer is small but immediate.
2. Sufficient for a sprint, not a marathon.
3. ~5–10 seconds.

Stem: The gap between the T tubule and the SR terminal cisternae at a triad measures approximately \_\_\_ nm, bridged by RyR1 "feet."

Canonical answer: ~15 nm

Accepted variants:

- 15 nm
- ~15 nm
- 15
- 10–15 nm

Tolerance: ±5 nm

Yellow conditions:

- "100" → "Too wide — the triad gap is ~15 nm, narrow enough for direct DHPR-RyR1 mechanical coupling."
- "1" → "Too narrow — that is closer to the gap junction inter-membrane distance; the triad gap is ~15 nm."

Explanation: This narrow gap allows direct mechanical coupling between DHPR (in the T tubule) and RyR1 (in the SR) [Berne & Levy ch.12]. The four-fold symmetry of RyR1 corresponds to a tetrad of DHPRs in the T tubule. In skeletal muscle, the DHPR voltage sensor mechanically gates RyR1 without needing Ca²⁺ entry — distinguishing skeletal ECC from cardiac (where DHPR Ca²⁺ entry triggers RyR2 via Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release). The triad arrangement (one T tubule flanked by two SR cisternae) provides the structural basis for synchronous Ca²⁺ release across the entire fibre.

Hints:

1. Tens of nanometres — narrow enough for direct protein–protein contact.
2. RyR1 "feet" span this gap.
3. Around 15 nm.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The myosin power stroke moves the thin filament approximately \_\_\_ nm per cross-bridge cycle.

Canonical answer: ~10 nm

Accepted variants:

- 10 nm
- ~10
- 5–10 nm
- 10

Tolerance: ±5 nm (5–15 nm graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "1" → "Too short — that approaches the diameter of an actin monomer; the myosin lever-arm power stroke is ~10 nm per cycle."
- "100" → "Too long — single cross-bridge step is ~10 nm; longer movements come from many cycles in succession."

Explanation: Each ATP-hydrolysing cycle generates ~10 nm of filament displacement per cross-bridge — the step size that, multiplied across millions of cross-bridges firing asynchronously, produces smooth shortening [Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. The lever-arm theory of myosin movement (Spudich, Goldman, and others) explains how a 10 nm step arises from a small (~5°) rotation of the myosin head amplified by the rigid lever arm (the regulatory and essential light chain region). The stoichiometry is one ATP per power stroke, so the energetic efficiency of muscle contraction can be calculated from these atomic-scale steps to whole-organ work output.

Hints:

1. Tens of nanometres per cycle.
2. Each step costs one ATP.
3. ~10 nm.

Canonical answer: ~200 kDa

Accepted variants:

- 200 kDa
- ~200
- 200

Yellow conditions:

- "20" → "That is the size of a myosin _light_ chain; the heavy chain is ~200 kDa."

Explanation: MyHC is ~200 kDa; together with two essential and two regulatory light chains (~20 kDa each), one myosin II hexamer totals ~480 kDa [Berne & Levy ch.12]. Stem: The protein titin spans the half-sarcomere from the Z line to the \_\_\_ line.

Canonical answer: M line

Accepted variants:

- M line
- M-line
- M

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "Z" → "Z is the _origin_ of titin; the question asks about the _terminus_ in the half-sarcomere — the M line."
- "A band" → "Titin extends through the A band but its terminus is at the _M line_ in the centre of the sarcomere."

Explanation: Titin is the largest known protein (~3.7 MDa); its elastic PEVK region in the I band is the source of passive tension at long sarcomere lengths and helps re-centre the thick filament between Z lines [Berne & Levy ch.12]. Each half-sarcomere has one molecule of titin running from the Z line to the M line, anchoring the thick filament and providing the molecular basis for the passive length–tension curve. Mutations in titin cause several inherited cardiomyopathies and skeletal myopathies; the gene (TTN) is so large that sequencing-based diagnosis was historically limited until next-generation methods.

Hints:

1. The structure runs the full length of the half-sarcomere.
2. The terminus is at the centre of the A band.
3. M.

Stem: The maximum sarcomere length beyond which active force generation drops to zero (no thick–thin overlap) is approximately \_\_\_ µm.

Canonical answer: 3.6 µm

Accepted variants:

- 3.6 µm
- 3.6
- ~3.6 µm
- ~3.5–3.7 µm

Tolerance: ±0.3 µm

Yellow conditions:

- "2" → "That is the resting/optimal length, not the upper limit; beyond ~3.6 µm there is no overlap and active force is zero."
- "10" → "Too long — sarcomeres do not normally stretch beyond ~3.6 µm even passively; active force ends there."

Explanation: Beyond ~3.6 µm, no thick–thin overlap remains, so no cross-bridges can form and active force is zero (passive tension from titin still increases sharply) [Berne & Levy ch.12]. The descending limb of the length–tension curve reflects diminishing overlap above 2.2 µm. Below 2.0 µm (ascending limb), opposing thin filaments interfere and thick filaments collide with the Z line. Whole-muscle length–tension curves combine these sarcomere-level mechanics with passive elastic tissue (epimysium, perimysium, endomysium, tendon) to give the characteristic length–tension relationship of intact muscle.

Hints:

1. Above the optimal sarcomere length, active force falls because of less overlap.
2. At some critical length there is _no_ overlap left.
3. ~3.6 µm.

Stem: A typical adult human skeletal muscle is composed of bundles of muscle cells called \_\_\_, with diameters of 10–80 µm.

Canonical answer: muscle fibres (myocytes)

Accepted variants:

- muscle fibres
- muscle fibers
- myocytes
- skeletal muscle cells

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "myofibrils" → "Myofibrils are the _intracellular_ contractile bundles within each fibre (~1 µm diameter); the _cells_ themselves are muscle fibres (10–80 µm)."
- "fascicles" → "Fascicles are _bundles of fibres_ surrounded by perimysium; the individual cells are muscle fibres."

Explanation: Each fibre is a multinucleate syncytium formed by myoblast fusion [Berne & Levy ch.12; Costanzo]. Within each fibre, hundreds to thousands of myofibrils run in parallel, each ~1 µm wide. Connective-tissue layers organise the muscle hierarchically: endomysium around each fibre, perimysium around fascicles (bundles of fibres), and epimysium around the whole muscle. This nested organisation transmits force from sarcomeres through tendons to bone. Satellite cells lying beneath the basal lamina are quiescent muscle stem cells that mediate post-injury regeneration.

Hints:

1. The cells are large enough to be visible to the naked eye in a dissecting microscope.
2. Diameters are tens of micrometres, hundreds of micrometres long.
3. The proper anatomical name is the muscle fibre.

Stem: Type I (slow oxidative) muscle fibres are characterised by \_\_\_ myosin ATPase activity relative to Type II fibres.

Canonical answer: low

Accepted variants:

- low
- slower
- reduced
- lower

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "high" → "Type II fibres have _high_ myosin ATPase; Type I fibres have _low_ — explaining their slower shortening velocity but greater economy."
- "absent" → "Type I fibres still have measurable myosin ATPase, just lower than Type II; the difference is the rate, not absence."

Explanation: Slow MyHC has low ATPase rate → slower cross-bridge cycling → slower shortening velocity but greater economy of force per ATP [Berne & Levy ch.12]. Combined with high oxidative capacity (mitochondria, myoglobin, capillary density), this gives Type I fibres their fatigue resistance. Type IIa fibres are intermediate (fast oxidative-glycolytic), and Type IIx (humans; IIb in rodents) are fast glycolytic with high ATPase, low oxidative capacity, rapid fatigue. Postural antigravity muscles (soleus, deep paraspinals) are Type I-rich; phasic limb muscles for sprinting/jumping are Type II-rich. Aerobic training shifts fibres toward Type IIa (the most plastic intermediate phenotype).

Hints:

1. Slower shortening reflects slower ATP turnover.
2. Low ATPase pairs with high economy.
3. Low.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The maximum shortening velocity (V_max) of a skeletal-muscle fibre is observed when the external load is \_\_\_.

Canonical answer: zero

Accepted variants:

- zero
- 0
- minimal
- nil

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "maximal" → "At maximal load, velocity is _zero_ (isometric contraction at P0); V*max is observed at \_zero* load."
- "intermediate" → "V*max is the \_y-intercept* of the force–velocity curve (zero load); intermediate loads give intermediate velocities."

Explanation: As load increases, velocity falls hyperbolically (Hill's equation) [Berne & Levy ch.12]. At V_max — the y-intercept of the force–velocity curve — load is zero. At the x-intercept, velocity is zero and the force equals maximum isometric tension P0. The shape of the force–velocity curve is determined principally by myosin ATPase rate; Type II fibres have a higher V_max than Type I. Power output (force × velocity) is maximal at intermediate loads (~30% of P0). The curve also shifts with fatigue (lower P0 and V_max) and with training adaptations.

Hints:

1. The fastest a muscle can shorten is when nothing opposes it.
2. The y-intercept of the force–velocity curve.
3. Zero load.

Stem: The principal cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ buffer that helps speed Ca²⁺ removal during relaxation in fast skeletal-muscle fibres is \_\_\_.

Canonical answer: parvalbumin

Accepted variants:

- parvalbumin
- parvalbumins
- PV

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "calmodulin" → "Calmodulin is a Ca²⁺ _signalling_ protein for downstream pathways; the cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ _buffer_ in fast skeletal muscle is parvalbumin."
- "calsequestrin" → "Calsequestrin is the _SR luminal_ Ca²⁺ buffer; the _cytoplasmic_ buffer in fast skeletal muscle is parvalbumin."

Explanation: Parvalbumin (a small, high-mobility Ca²⁺-binding protein) is abundant in fast-twitch skeletal muscle, helping accelerate cytosolic Ca²⁺ buffering and speeding twitch relaxation [Boron & Boulpaep ch.9]. By lowering free [Ca²⁺] quickly after release, it allows a fast fibre to relax in time for the next contraction. Cardiac muscle has little parvalbumin and relies more heavily on SERCA2a + phospholamban regulation for Ca²⁺ removal. The contrasting buffering strategies match the contractile demands: cardiac wants fast SERCA-driven uptake (regulated by β-adrenergic phosphorylation of phospholamban) and is intolerant of tetanic summation; skeletal fast fibres need a fast cytosolic buffer to speed relaxation in repetitive contractions.

Hints:

1. A small high-mobility Ca²⁺-binding protein in the cytosol.
2. Especially abundant in fast-twitch fibres.
3. Distinct from the SR luminal buffer (calsequestrin).

Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I

Stem: The repeating contractile unit between two Z lines is the \_\_\_.

Canonical answer: sarcomere

Accepted variants:

- sarcomere

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "myofibril" → "A myofibril is a _bundle of sarcomeres_ in series; the unit between two Z lines is one sarcomere."
- "muscle fibre" → "A muscle fibre is the whole multinucleate cell; the _contractile unit_ between two Z lines is the sarcomere."

Explanation: With its A band, I bands, H zone, and M line, the sarcomere is the basic anatomical and functional unit of striated muscle [Berne & Levy ch.12; Costanzo]. The Z line itself is where adjacent sarcomeres meet and titin and α-actinin anchor thin filaments. The regular sarcomere repeat gives striated muscle its characteristic banded appearance under light microscopy.

Hints:

1. The repeating unit visible by light microscopy as one cycle of the striations.
2. Bounded by Z lines.
3. The fundamental contractile unit.

Stem: The model that explains muscle shortening by the sliding of thin filaments past thick filaments without change in their individual lengths is called the \_\_\_ theory.

Canonical answer: sliding filament

Accepted variants:

- sliding filament
- sliding-filament

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "rotating filament" → "Filaments do not rotate around each other; they _slide_ past each other while their individual lengths remain constant."
- "shortening filament" → "The filaments themselves do _not_ shorten; they slide past each other — a key prediction that distinguishes this theory from earlier models."

Explanation: Proposed independently by Huxley & Niedergerke and Huxley & Hanson in 1954 [Costanzo — Skeletal Muscle]. The key prediction: the A band stays constant in width (equal to the thick filament length, which does not change) while the H and I bands shorten as thin filaments slide inward past thick. This was confirmed by light microscopy and electron microscopy of contracting sarcomeres at different lengths. The theory was later mechanistically grounded in the cross-bridge cycle (Andrew Huxley's 1957 model and subsequent biophysical work). The sliding filament model remains the foundation of all subsequent muscle physiology.

Hints:

1. Filaments slide past each other; their lengths do not change.
2. The A band stays constant; H and I shorten.
3. Sliding filament.

Stem: A single α-motor neuron together with all the muscle fibres it innervates is called a \_\_\_.

Canonical answer: motor unit

Accepted variants:

- motor unit
- motor units

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

Stem: As external load increases on a contracting muscle, the velocity of shortening \_\_\_.

Canonical answer: decreases

Accepted variants:

- decreases
- falls
- declines
- slows

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "increases" → "Force–velocity is _inverse_: higher load means _slower_ shortening; at maximum load (P0), velocity reaches zero (isometric)."
- "stays the same" → "Velocity is _not_ independent of load; it falls hyperbolically as load increases — the Hill equation."

Explanation: The hyperbolic force–velocity relation: at zero load, velocity = V_max; at maximum load (P0), velocity = 0 (isometric) [Berne & Levy ch.12]. The relationship was characterised mathematically by A.V. Hill (Nobel 1922) and is determined by cross-bridge cycling kinetics, particularly myosin ATPase rate. Power output (force × velocity) is maximal at intermediate loads (~30% of P0) — useful for designing exercise that maximises mechanical power output. The shape of the curve also depends on fibre type: Type II fibres have higher V_max than Type I.

Hints:

2. Originally described by A.V. Hill.
3. As load goes up, velocity goes down.

Stem: The whole-muscle force at sarcomere lengths shorter than ~2 µm declines because the thin filaments from opposite Z lines begin to \_\_\_ each other.

Canonical answer: overlap (collide)

Accepted variants:

- overlap
- collide with
- interfere with
- overlap with

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "stretch" → "At short sarcomere lengths, thin filaments do not stretch — they overlap with each other from opposite Z lines, and thick filaments collide with the Z line."
- "depolarise" → "Force decline at short lengths is _mechanical_ (filament overlap), not electrical."

Explanation: At ~2 µm, optimal overlap is achieved [Berne & Levy ch.12]. At shorter lengths, opposing thin filaments overlap and interfere with cross-bridge formation, and at very short lengths the thick filament collides with the Z line. The ascending limb of the length–tension curve reflects this, with steeper force decline at very short lengths. The descending limb (>~2.2 µm) reflects loss of thick–thin overlap. Together they give the inverted-U length–tension relationship, with the plateau corresponding to the resting length range and explaining why the body is configured to operate muscles near their resting length.

Hints:

1. Below ~2 µm, the geometric problem is too much filament in too little space.
2. Thin filaments from opposite ends meet in the middle.
3. Overlap.

Stem: Resistance training increases muscle size by \_\_\_ — increasing contractile protein content per fibre — rather than by hyperplasia.

Canonical answer: hypertrophy

Accepted variants:

- hypertrophy
- muscle hypertrophy
- hypertrophy of fibres

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "hyperplasia" → "Hyperplasia (more _fibres_) is minimal in adult mammals; training-induced size gain is via _hypertrophy_ of existing fibres."
- "atrophy" → "Atrophy is the _opposite_ — loss of fibre size (disuse, denervation, glucocorticoids); training drives hypertrophy."

Explanation: Mechanotransduction, IGF-1/Akt/mTORC1 signalling, and satellite-cell-mediated myonuclear addition drive hypertrophy [Berne & Levy ch.12]. Adult mammalian muscle adds little or no new fibres; instead, existing fibres add sarcomeres in parallel (or in series, depending on stretch context) and increase contractile protein content. Atrophy from disuse, denervation, glucocorticoids, or cachexia operates through opposing pathways (myostatin, FoxO transcription factors, ubiquitin–proteasome ligases MuRF1 and atrogin-1). Anti-myostatin therapies have been trialled for muscular dystrophy and sarcopenia (limited clinical success so far). Anabolic hormones (testosterone, IGF-1) and amino acid supplementation augment training-induced hypertrophy.

Hints:

1. The cells get bigger, but their number doesn't change.
2. Driven by mTORC1 signalling and satellite cell incorporation.
3. Hypertrophy.

Stem: In sustained intense exercise, muscle fatigue is accompanied by accumulation of inorganic phosphate (P_i), which directly slows the cross-bridge cycle by impairing the \_\_\_ step.

Canonical answer: power stroke (P_i release)

Accepted variants:

- power stroke
- P_i release
- phosphate release
- the P_i-release step

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "ATP binding" → "Elevated P*i does not directly affect ATP binding; it shifts the equilibrium \_away* from the post-power-stroke state by reducing the favourability of the P_i-release step."
- "Ca²⁺ binding to TnC" → "H⁺ accumulation reduces _TnC Ca²⁺ sensitivity_ during fatigue, but _P_i_ accumulation specifically impairs the _power-stroke_ step."

Explanation: Elevated P_i shifts the cross-bridge equilibrium back toward the pre-power-stroke state, reducing force per active cross-bridge [Berne & Levy ch.12 — Fatigue]. H⁺ accumulation simultaneously reduces myofibrillar Ca²⁺ sensitivity. PCr/glycogen depletion and reduced SR Ca²⁺ release (secondary to ATP depletion and altered RyR1 function) also contribute to fatigue. ATP itself is rarely depleted to rigor levels under physiological exercise — the multiple compensatory mechanisms protect against catastrophic energy failure. Central fatigue (reduced motor neuron recruitment from the CNS) adds another layer; both peripheral and central mechanisms operate during prolonged exercise. The classical view that lactate causes fatigue is now revised — lactate per se is metabolically useful (a fuel for type I fibres and for the Cori cycle) and the more direct contributors to fatigue are P_i, H⁺, and altered Ca²⁺ handling.

Hints:

1. P*i is a \_product* of the power-stroke step.
2. By Le Chatelier's principle, accumulating product slows the forward reaction.
3. The power stroke (P_i-release step).

## Summary

This 46-question fill-in-the-blank set covers skeletal muscle physiology across three passes. Pass 1 (Q1–17) anchors numerical values: sarcomere geometry (resting length 2 µm, descending limb endpoint 3.6 µm), AP duration (~3 ms), tropomyosin coverage (7 actins), SERCA stoichiometry (2 Ca²⁺/ATP), aerobic and anaerobic ATP yields, PCr buffer duration (~5–10 s), triad gap (~15 nm), power stroke (~10 nm), MyHC mass (~200 kDa), titin span (Z to M), maximum overlap length (~3.6 µm), muscle fibre diameters (10–80 µm), Type I myosin ATPase (low), force–velocity (V_max at zero load), parvalbumin in fast fibres, and calsequestrin Ca²⁺ binding (~40–50 sites). Pass 2 (Q18–32) defines named entities: sarcomere, sliding filament theory, motor unit, DHPR/Cav1.1, RyR1, troponin C (Ca²⁺-binding subunit), triad, isometric contraction, twitch, dystrophin, Henneman size principle, rigor mortis, rapsyn (with agrin/MuSK upstream), phosphocreatine, and malignant hyperthermia. Pass 3 (Q33–46) develops relations: T-tubule depolarisation triggers DHPR-RyR1 mechanical gating without Ca²⁺ entry; ATP binding detaches myosin; ATP hydrolysis re-cocks the head into the high-energy state; P_i release triggers the power stroke; SERCA terminates contraction; Type I fibres are oxidative; force–velocity is inverse and hyperbolic; short-sarcomere force decline reflects thin-filament overlap; dantrolene blocks RyR1; succinylcholine causes hyperkalaemia in denervation/burns through extrajunctional AChR upregulation; postural muscles are Type I-dominant; phospholamban regulates SERCA2a; resistance training drives hypertrophy not hyperplasia; and exercise fatigue impairs the power-stroke step via P_i accumulation.
