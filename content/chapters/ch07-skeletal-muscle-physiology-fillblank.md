# Chapter 7 — Skeletal Muscle Physiology — Fill-in-the-Blank Questions

---

QUESTION 1

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The average resting length of a skeletal-muscle sarcomere is approximately **\_\_** μm.

Canonical answer: 2 (about 2.0)

Explanation: Sarcomeres average ~2 μm at rest; the optimal length for active force is ~2.0–2.2 μm — the position of greatest thick-thin overlap (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 2

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Each tropomyosin dimer covers **\_\_** actin monomers along the thin filament.

Canonical answer: 7

Explanation: Sequential tropomyosin dimers are arranged head-to-tail along F-actin; each spans seven monomers. One troponin complex is bound per tropomyosin (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 3

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A skeletal-muscle action potential lasts only about **\_\_** ms.

Canonical answer: 2–5 (~3)

Explanation: Brevity is essential: a long AP would impose a long absolute refractory period and prevent tetanic summation, as is the case in cardiac muscle (~200 ms) (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 4

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: SERCA transports **\_\_** Ca²⁺ ions into the SR for each ATP molecule it hydrolyses.

Canonical answer: 2

Explanation: SERCA's stoichiometry is 2 Ca²⁺ per 1 ATP. This active reuptake terminates the cytosolic Ca²⁺ transient and produces relaxation (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 5

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Aerobic oxidation of one mole of glucose-1-phosphate (i.e., from glycogen) generates approximately **\_\_** mol of ATP in skeletal muscle.

Canonical answer: 37

Explanation: Glycogen-derived glucose-1-phosphate enters glycolysis without consuming ATP at the hexokinase step, yielding ~37 ATP. Free blood glucose costs 1 ATP at hexokinase, giving ~36. Anaerobically, the yields collapse to 3 (glycogen) and 2 (glucose) per glucose unit (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Carbohydrates).

---

QUESTION 6

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Anaerobic glycolysis from blood glucose yields only about **\_\_** mol ATP per mole of glucose.

Canonical answer: 2

Explanation: Anaerobically: 2 ATP from glucose, 3 ATP from glycogen-derived glucose-1-phosphate, plus 2 lactate per glucose. The much lower yield is why anaerobic capacity is limited and lactate accumulates in heavy exercise (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 7

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The phosphocreatine reservoir in active skeletal muscle is sufficient to sustain maximal contraction for only about **\_\_** seconds before glycolytic and oxidative ATP regeneration must take over.

Canonical answer: 5–10

Explanation: PCr provides the immediate ATP buffer via creatine kinase. The store is ~5× resting ATP; depletion is rapid in maximal exertion. PCr is replenished post-exercise by oxidative phosphorylation (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 8

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The slack between the T tubule and the SR terminal cisternae at a triad measures approximately **\_\_** nm, bridged by RyR1 "feet."

Canonical answer: 15

Explanation: This narrow gap allows direct mechanical coupling between DHPR (in the T tubule) and RyR1 (in the SR). The four-fold symmetry of RyR1 corresponds to a tetrad of DHPRs in the T tubule (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 9

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The myosin power stroke moves the thin filament approximately **\_\_** nm per cross-bridge cycle.

Canonical answer: 10

Explanation: Each ATP-hydrolysing cycle generates ~10 nm of filament displacement per cross-bridge — the step size that, multiplied across millions of cross-bridges firing asynchronously, produces smooth shortening (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

---

QUESTION 10

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The myosin heavy chain has an approximate molecular mass of **\_\_** kDa.

Canonical answer: 200

Explanation: The MyHC is ~200 kDa; together with two essential and two regulatory light chains (~20 kDa each), one myosin II hexamer totals ~480 kDa. Tail interactions assemble ~300 myosin molecules into one bipolar thick filament (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 11

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The protein titin spans the half-sarcomere from Z line to **\_\_** line.

Canonical answer: M

Explanation: Titin is the largest known protein; its elastic PEVK region in the I band is the source of passive tension at long sarcomere lengths and helps re-centre the thick filament between Z lines (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 12

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The maximum sarcomere length beyond which active force generation drops to zero (no thick-thin overlap) is approximately **\_\_** μm.

Canonical answer: 3.6

Explanation: Beyond ~3.6 μm, no thick-thin overlap remains, so no cross-bridges can form and active force is zero (passive tension from titin still increases). The descending limb of the length-tension curve reflects diminishing overlap above 2.2 μm (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 13

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A typical adult human skeletal muscle is composed of bundles of muscle cells called **\_\_**, with diameters of 10–80 μm.

Canonical answer: muscle fibres (myocytes)

Explanation: Each fibre is a multinucleate syncytium formed by myoblast fusion. Within each fibre, hundreds to thousands of myofibrils run in parallel, each ~1 μm wide (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Costanzo, 7e).

---

QUESTION 14

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Type I (slow oxidative) muscle fibres are characterised by **\_\_** myosin ATPase activity relative to Type II fibres.

Canonical answer: low

Explanation: Slow MyHC has low ATPase rate → slower cross-bridge cycling → slower shortening velocity but greater economy. Combined with high oxidative capacity (mitochondria, myoglobin, capillary density), this gives Type I fibres their fatigue resistance (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 15

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The maximum shortening velocity (Vmax) of a skeletal-muscle fibre is observed when the external load is **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: zero

Explanation: As load increases, velocity falls hyperbolically (Hill's equation). At Vmax — the y-intercept of the force-velocity curve — load is zero. At the x-intercept, velocity is zero and the force equals maximum isometric tension P0 (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 16

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The principal cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ buffer that helps speed Ca²⁺ removal during relaxation in fast skeletal-muscle fibres is **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: parvalbumin

Explanation: Parvalbumin (a small Ca²⁺-binding protein) is abundant in fast-twitch skeletal muscle, helping accelerate Ca²⁺ buffering and speeding twitch relaxation. Cardiac muscle has little parvalbumin and relies more heavily on SERCA2a + phospholamban regulation (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

---

QUESTION 17

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The muscle Ca²⁺-binding protein in the SR lumen has a low-affinity, high-capacity binding profile, with ~**\_\_** Ca²⁺-binding sites per protein.

Canonical answer: ~40–50 (~50)

Explanation: Calsequestrin can bind ~40–50 Ca²⁺ ions with low affinity, allowing concentration of luminal [Ca²⁺] far above the cytosol while leaving Ca²⁺ available for rapid release through RyR1 (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

---

QUESTION 18

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The repeating contractile unit between two Z lines is the **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: sarcomere

Explanation: With its A band, I bands, H zone, and M line, the sarcomere is the basic anatomical and functional unit of striated muscle. Its components shift predictably during contraction following the sliding filament logic (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Costanzo, 7e).

---

QUESTION 19

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The model that explains muscle shortening by the sliding of thin filaments past thick filaments without change in their individual lengths is called the **\_\_** theory.

Canonical answer: sliding filament

Explanation: Proposed independently by Huxley & Niedergerke and Huxley & Hanson in 1954. Key prediction: A band stays constant in width while H and I bands shorten (Costanzo, 7e — Skeletal Muscle).

---

QUESTION 20

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A single α-motor neuron together with all the muscle fibres it innervates is called a **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: motor unit

Explanation: All fibres in one motor unit are of the same fibre type. Motor-unit size varies enormously — small (10–20 fibres) for fine control (extraocular, intrinsic hand), large (hundreds-thousands) for bulk power (gastrocnemius, gluteus) (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 21

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The L-type voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channel in the T-tubule membrane that acts as the voltage sensor for skeletal-muscle ECC is the **\_\_** receptor.

Canonical answer: dihydropyridine (DHPR; Cav1.1)

Explanation: The skeletal isoform Cav1.1 (CACNA1S gene) does not need to conduct Ca²⁺ for skeletal ECC; its voltage-driven conformational change is sufficient to gate RyR1 mechanically (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

---

QUESTION 22

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The SR Ca²⁺-release channel that is mechanically gated by the DHPR in skeletal muscle is the **\_\_** receptor.

Canonical answer: ryanodine (RyR1)

Explanation: RyR1 is the skeletal isoform; RyR2 is cardiac. Both are homotetrameric channels with very high single-channel conductance — among the largest ion channels known (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 23

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The Ca²⁺-binding subunit of the troponin complex that initiates contraction by triggering the conformational shift of tropomyosin is troponin **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: C

Explanation: Troponin T binds tropomyosin, troponin I inhibits actin-myosin interaction at low [Ca²⁺], troponin C binds Ca²⁺ and removes the inhibition. Troponin I and T are the cardiac biomarkers used clinically (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Costanzo, 7e).

---

QUESTION 24

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The arrangement at the A-I junction of skeletal muscle in which one T tubule is flanked by two SR terminal cisternae is termed a **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: triad

Explanation: Cardiac muscle has dyads (one T tubule with one cisterna), positioned at the Z line. The triad is the mechanical interface for skeletal-muscle ECC (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 25

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The contraction in which muscle length is held constant while force is measured is termed **\_\_** contraction.

Canonical answer: isometric

Explanation: Isometric: fixed length, force varies. Isotonic: fixed force, length varies. Most real-life movements are auxotonic — both length and force change (Costanzo, 7e — Skeletal Muscle).

---

QUESTION 26

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A single brief muscle action potential produces a **\_\_** — a rising and falling force transient lasting tens to hundreds of milliseconds.

Canonical answer: twitch

Explanation: A twitch is the mechanical response to one stimulus. Repeated stimulation faster than the rate of Ca²⁺ reuptake produces unfused or fused tetanus, with force several-fold greater than a single twitch (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 27

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The cytoskeletal protein that links sub-sarcolemmal F-actin to the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex and the basal lamina is called **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: dystrophin

Explanation: Loss-of-function mutations in dystrophin cause Duchenne muscular dystrophy; partial-function mutations cause Becker MD. The DGC includes α/β-dystroglycan, sarcoglycans, and syntrophin (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 28

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The principle that small/slow motor units are recruited before larger/faster ones as voluntary force increases is called Henneman's **\_\_** principle.

Canonical answer: size

Explanation: Smaller motoneurons have higher input resistance and lower threshold, so they fire first at any given synaptic drive. The size principle ensures that fatigue-resistant Type I fibres handle the most-used low-force tasks (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 29

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The state in which a muscle remains tightly contracted with no ATP available to detach cross-bridges (e.g., post-mortem) is called **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: rigor mortis

Explanation: Without ATP, myosin heads cannot detach from actin. Coupled with SR Ca²⁺ leak (also ATP-dependent to oppose), this produces sustained binding. Rigor breaks later through proteolytic decomposition (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12; Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9).

---

QUESTION 30

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The peripheral muscle protein that participates in nicotinic AChR clustering at the motor end-plate during NMJ formation is **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: rapsyn (with agrin and MuSK upstream)

Explanation: Motor-neuron-derived agrin activates the postsynaptic LRP4-MuSK receptor complex, which directs rapsyn to cluster nicotinic ACh receptors at the end-plate. Antibodies against MuSK cause a serological subset of myasthenia gravis (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Neuromuscular Junction; Ganong, 26e).

---

QUESTION 31

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The intracellular high-energy phosphate reservoir that rapidly regenerates ATP via creatine kinase is **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: phosphocreatine (creatine phosphate)

Explanation: PCr + ADP ↔ Cr + ATP, catalysed by creatine kinase. CK has cytosolic and mitochondrial isoforms, with CK-MM dominating in skeletal muscle. Serum CK rises with muscle injury (rhabdomyolysis, MI for CK-MB, etc.) (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 32

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The condition in which mutations in RyR1 (or, less commonly, the DHPR α₁s subunit CACNA1S) cause life-threatening hypermetabolism upon exposure to volatile anaesthetics or succinylcholine is **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: malignant hyperthermia

Explanation: Treatment is immediate withdrawal of trigger, dantrolene (a RyR1 channel blocker) and aggressive supportive care. Pre-anaesthetic family history screening and the in-vitro contracture test identify susceptibles (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 33

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The conformational change in DHPR that opens RyR1 is triggered by **\_\_**, not by Ca²⁺ entry.

Canonical answer: depolarisation of the T-tubule membrane (voltage)

Explanation: Skeletal ECC requires no Ca²⁺ influx through DHPR — depolarisation alone is sufficient. Cardiac ECC, by contrast, requires Ca²⁺ entry through DHPR to trigger Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release through RyR2 (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 34

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: ATP binding to myosin (in the rigor state) causes **\_\_** of the myosin head from actin.

Canonical answer: detachment (release)

Explanation: The first step of the cross-bridge cycle is ATP binding → detachment. This is why rigor is broken by adding ATP, and why rigor mortis is the consequence of post-mortem ATP depletion (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 35

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Hydrolysis of bound ATP to ADP·Pi on the detached myosin head re-cocks the head into the **\_\_**-energy configuration.

Canonical answer: high

Explanation: Hydrolysis is rapid; the products remain bound. The cocked head with ADP·Pi can now rebind a new actin site. Pi release on rebinding is the trigger for the power stroke (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 36

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The release of inorganic phosphate (Pi) from the actin-myosin·ADP·Pi complex triggers the **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: power stroke

Explanation: Pi release leads to ~10 nm of relative sliding of thin past thick. ADP release follows, returning the head to the rigor configuration ready for a new ATP. Without ATP, the cycle stalls in rigor (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 37

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Termination of contraction depends on cytosolic Ca²⁺ falling, which is achieved primarily by **\_\_**-mediated reuptake into the SR.

Canonical answer: SERCA (Ca²⁺-ATPase)

Explanation: SERCA1 dominates in fast-twitch muscle, SERCA2a in slow-twitch and cardiac (regulated by phospholamban). Loss of SERCA1 function (Brody disease) impairs relaxation. PMCA and Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchange contribute minimally to total Ca²⁺ removal (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

QUESTION 38

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Type I muscle fibres derive most of their ATP from **\_\_** metabolism, while Type IIb (or human IIx) fibres rely heavily on anaerobic glycolysis.

Canonical answer: oxidative (aerobic)

Explanation: Type I = slow oxidative, fatigue-resistant. Type IIa = fast oxidative-glycolytic, intermediate. Type IIx/IIb = fast glycolytic, rapid fatigue. Type I fibres are red (high myoglobin); Type IIb/IIx are pale ("white meat") (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 39

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: As external load increases on a contracting muscle, the velocity of shortening **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: decreases

Explanation: The hyperbolic force-velocity relation: at zero load, velocity = Vmax; at maximum load (P0), velocity = 0 (isometric). The relationship is determined by cross-bridge cycling kinetics, particularly myosin ATPase rate (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 40

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The whole-muscle force at sarcomere lengths shorter than ~2 μm declines because the thin filaments from opposite Z lines begin to **\_\_** each other.

Canonical answer: overlap (collide)

Explanation: At ~2 μm, optimal overlap is achieved. At shorter lengths, opposing thin filaments overlap and interfere, and at very short lengths the thick filament collides with the Z line. The ascending limb of the length-tension curve reflects this (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 41

Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: Dantrolene blocks **\_\_** in skeletal muscle, treating malignant hyperthermia.

Canonical answer: RyR1 (the ryanodine receptor type 1)

Explanation: Dantrolene reduces SR Ca²⁺ release, breaking the runaway hypermetabolic cycle. Its skeletal-muscle selectivity is favourable: cardiac RyR2 is not significantly blocked (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

QUESTION 42

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Succinylcholine, a depolarising NMJ blocker, can produce massive K⁺ release and dangerous hyperkalaemia in patients with denervation, burns, or prolonged immobilisation because of widespread **\_\_** across the sarcolemma.

Canonical answer: extrajunctional nicotinic acetylcholine receptor expression (denervation supersensitivity)

Explanation: Without trophic nerve input, embryonic γ-subunit-containing AChRs spread along the entire fibre. Succinylcholine depolarises this enlarged receptor pool, generating large K⁺ efflux and life-threatening hyperkalaemia. Caution is essential beyond ~24 hours after the precipitating event (Boron & Boulpaep, 2e — Ch.9; Ganong, 26e).

---

QUESTION 43

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The Achilles-soleus complex, which works against gravity for postural support throughout the day, is dominated by **\_\_** fibres.

Canonical answer: Type I (slow oxidative)

Explanation: Postural antigravity muscles (soleus, deep paraspinals) need fatigue-resistant tonic activity — perfectly matched to Type I fibres' oxidative profile. Phasic limb muscles for sprinting/jumping are Type II-rich (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 44

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: The protein whose phosphorylation by PKA disinhibits SERCA2a in slow skeletal muscle (and cardiac muscle) is **\_\_**.

Canonical answer: phospholamban (PLB)

Explanation: Unphosphorylated PLB inhibits SERCA2a; PKA-mediated phosphorylation (e.g., on β-adrenergic stimulation) relieves the inhibition, accelerating Ca²⁺ uptake and relaxation. In slow skeletal muscle this is one of several adaptations linking adrenergic drive to faster Ca²⁺ handling (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 45

Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Resistance training increases muscle size by **\_\_** — increasing contractile protein content per fibre — rather than by hyperplasia.

Canonical answer: hypertrophy

Explanation: Mechanotransduction, IGF-1/Akt/mTORC1 signalling, and satellite-cell-mediated myonuclear addition drive hypertrophy. Adult mammalian muscle adds little or no new fibres. Atrophy from disuse, denervation, glucocorticoids, or cachexia operates through opposing pathways (myostatin, FoxO, MuRF1, atrogin-1) (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12).

---

QUESTION 46

Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: In sustained intense exercise, muscle fatigue is accompanied by accumulation of inorganic phosphate (Pi), which directly slows the cross-bridge cycle by impairing the **\_\_** step.

Canonical answer: power stroke (Pi release)

Explanation: Elevated Pi shifts the cross-bridge equilibrium back toward the pre-power-stroke state, reducing force per active cross-bridge. H⁺ accumulation simultaneously reduces myofibrillar Ca²⁺ sensitivity. PCr/glycogen depletion and reduced SR Ca²⁺ release also contribute. ATP itself is rarely depleted to rigor levels under physiological exercise (Berne & Levy, 7e, Ch.12 — Fatigue).
