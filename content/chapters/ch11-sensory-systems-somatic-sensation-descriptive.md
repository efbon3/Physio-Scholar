Stem: Describe how a sensory receptor transduces a physical stimulus into a neural signal. How is stimulus intensity, modality, location, and duration encoded?

Model answer: Sensory transduction is the conversion of physical stimulus energy (mechanical, thermal, chemical, electromagnetic) into electrical signals the nervous system can process. The general scheme:

1. Adequate stimulus — Each receptor type is most sensitive to a specific stimulus energy (its "adequate stimulus"): mechanoreceptors → deformation; thermoreceptors → temperature; nociceptors → tissue damage; photoreceptors → light; chemoreceptors → chemicals.

2. Transducer molecule — A specific protein converts the stimulus into membrane permeability changes:

- Mechanoreceptors: stretch-gated cation channels (Piezo1, Piezo2, TRP channels) open when membrane is deformed.
- Thermoreceptors: TRP channels open at characteristic temperatures (TRPV1 >43°C; TRPM8 <25°C; TRPA1 <17°C).
- Nociceptors: TRPV1, TRPA1, ASIC, P2X3 — respond to noxious heat, cold, acid, ATP.
- Photoreceptors: rhodopsin/cone opsins → G-protein cascade → cGMP fall → CNG channel closure.
- Chemoreceptors: GPCRs (taste/smell) or direct ligand-gated channels.

3. Receptor potential (generator potential) — The transducer permeability change produces a graded depolarisation. Stimulus-amplitude-dependent: larger stimulus → larger depolarisation. Analogous to an EPSP. May involve secondary messengers in some receptors (e.g., olfaction's cAMP cascade).

4. Action potential generation — At the spike-initiation zone (often the first node of Ranvier in cutaneous afferents, or the axon hillock in some receptors), the receptor potential opens voltage-gated Na⁺ channels. If threshold (~−50 mV) is reached, an action potential propagates centrally.

5. Frequency-coded signal — Stronger stimuli produce larger receptor potentials → more frequent action potentials. The "code" the brain uses is firing frequency, not AP amplitude (since APs are all-or-none).

Encoding the four stimulus dimensions:

(a) Modality (what type) — Encoded by labeled-line principle: which axon carries the spikes determines what the brain perceives. Aδ axon → "sharp pain" no matter the actual stimulus; touch axon → "touch" no matter the stimulus. Phantom limb pain demonstrates this — pain is perceived in the absent limb because the central pathways are intact.

Stem: The "double pain" phenomenon — sharp pain followed by dull throbbing pain — illustrates the dual-fibre nociceptive system. Describe the anatomy, neurochemistry, and functional logic of Aδ vs C fibre nociception.

Model answer: When you stub your toe, you experience two distinct sensations: an immediate sharp pain (often described as "ouch — sharp!") followed seconds later by a dull, throbbing, lasting pain (often described as "a deep ache"). This "double pain" phenomenon is the perceptual signature of the dual-fibre nociceptive system.

Aδ fibres — "fast pain":

Structure:

- Small myelinated axons, 1–6 μm diameter.
- Conduction velocity 5–30 m/s.
- Receptive endings: free nerve endings in skin, joints, viscera.

Receptors:

- Most are mechanonociceptors — high-threshold mechanoreceptors that respond to noxious mechanical stimuli (sharp pinprick, severe pinch).
- Some are mechano-thermo-nociceptors (Type I A-mechano-heat).
- Express TRPV1 (some), TRPA1 (some), Piezo2, Nav1.7 (essential for pain), Nav1.8.

Pain quality:

- Sharp, pricking, well-localised, brief.
- Examples: pinprick, sharp injury, first wave after stubbing toe.

Function:

- Protective signal — "withdraw NOW" — drives rapid motor reflexes (flexor withdrawal).
- Well-localised because of small receptive fields and somatotopic representation.

Central pathway:

- Synapse in dorsal horn lamina I (and some lamina V).
- Glutamate is the principal transmitter.
- Decussates in cord, ascends as lateral spinothalamic.
- Terminates in lateral thalamic nuclei (VPL) → S1 (sensory-discriminative).

Structure:

- Small unmyelinated axons, 0.4–1.2 μm diameter.

Stem: Explain peripheral and central sensitisation. How do they differ, what produces each, and what are the clinical consequences for chronic pain conditions?

Model answer: Sensitisation — the increased responsiveness of the pain system after injury — is the fundamental mechanism by which acute pain becomes chronic pain. It operates at two levels: peripheral (at the nociceptor terminal) and central (in the dorsal horn and beyond).

Peripheral sensitisation:

Definition: Lowered threshold and increased responsiveness of nociceptors at the site of injury.

Mechanism — the "inflammatory soup":

Tissue injury releases multiple mediators:

- Bradykinin → B2 receptors → PLC → PKC activation.
- Prostaglandins (PGE2 from COX-2) → EP receptors → cAMP → PKA activation.
- Histamine → H1 receptors.
- Serotonin → multiple 5-HT receptors.
- H⁺ (low pH from ischaemia/inflammation) → ASIC channels and TRPV1.
- ATP (from dying cells) → P2X3 receptors.
- K⁺ (from damaged cells) → direct depolarisation.
- Cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α) → longer-term sensitisation.
- Nerve growth factor (NGF) → TrkA on nociceptors → upregulates Na⁺ channels, TRPV1, neuropeptides.

These mediators converge on the nociceptor terminal:

- Phosphorylate and modulate transducer channels (TRPV1) → lower thresholds.
- Phosphorylate Na⁺ channels (Nav1.7, Nav1.8) → easier excitation.
- Increase neuropeptide synthesis and release.

Clinical phenomenon: Primary hyperalgesia at the site of injury.

- Burned skin: increased pain to heat (lowered TRPV1 threshold).
- Bruised knee: increased pain to pressure (lowered mechanical thresholds).
- Inflamed joint: increased pain to movement.

Therapeutic targets:

- NSAIDs — Block COX → reduce PGE2.
- Anti-NGF antibodies (tanezumab) — Block NGF signalling.
- Local anaesthetics — Block Na⁺ channels.
- Capsaicin patches — TRPV1 desensitisation.
- Topical corticosteroids — Reduce cytokine production.

Central sensitisation:

Definition: Increased responsiveness of dorsal horn neurons (and higher central neurons) to nociceptive input.

Mechanism:

1. NMDA receptor activation:

- Repeated or intense C-fibre input releases glutamate + substance P + CGRP.

- This is functionally similar to LTP in hippocampus — pain-LTP.

Stem: Describe the gate-control theory of pain (Melzack and Wall, 1965). How does it explain why rubbing a sore area, vibration, and TENS reduce pain?

Model answer: The gate-control theory of pain, proposed by Ronald Melzack and Patrick Wall in 1965, was a landmark conceptual advance — establishing that pain transmission is _gated_ at the dorsal horn rather than passively transmitted from periphery to brain. The theory explained known phenomena (rubbing reduces pain) and predicted new ones (TENS, central modulation), guiding decades of research and therapy.

The original model:

In the dorsal horn, three classes of cells interact:

1. Projection neurons (P) — Output neurons. Send signals to the brain via spinothalamic tract. Pain perception requires P firing.
2. Inhibitory interneurons (SG — substantia gelatinosa) — Lamina II GABAergic and glycinergic neurons that inhibit P.
3. Two types of afferent input:
   - Small fibres (Aδ, C) — Excite P; inhibit SG (closing inhibition).
   - Large fibres (Aβ, touch) — Excite P; excite SG (opening inhibition).

The "gate":

When the SG inhibitory interneurons are active, they reduce the activation of P → less pain transmission ("gate closed").

When the SG interneurons are inhibited, P is fully driven by afferent input → more pain transmission ("gate open").

The balance is determined by large-fibre vs small-fibre input:

- Pure small-fibre activity (e.g., severe nociceptive stimulus) — SG inhibited; gate open; pain.
- Concurrent large-fibre activity (e.g., rubbing the area) — SG excited; gate closed; pain reduced.

Predictions and clinical applications:

1. Rubbing a sore area reduces pain.

- Aβ touch input activates SG → closes gate → reduced pain transmission.
- Universal observation: shake your hit thumb, rub a bumped shin.

2. Vibration reduces pain.

- Pacinian-mediated activation of Aβ → closes gate.
- Hand-held vibrator placed on injury reduces acute pain.

3. TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation):

- Skin electrodes deliver low-intensity electrical pulses → preferentially activate large Aβ afferents (which have lower threshold to electrical stimulation).
- Closes gate → reduces pain.
- Used for chronic pain (low back pain, fibromyalgia, neuropathic pain), labour pain, post-surgical pain.

4. Acupuncture (partly):

- May activate Aβ and Aδ afferents → modulate gate function.
- Also activates descending modulatory pathways (release of endogenous opioids, serotonin) — a more central mechanism.

Stem: Describe the descending pain modulation pathway and its key components: PAG, RVM, dorsal horn. How do endogenous opioids work in this system?

Model answer: The brain can suppress its own pain transmission. The descending pain modulation pathway is the anatomical substrate for this suppression — and the molecular target of opioid analgesics. 1. Periaqueductal grey (PAG) — midbrain command centre:

- Surrounds the cerebral aqueduct in the midbrain.
- Receives input from:
  - Cortex (especially anterior cingulate, prefrontal) — cognitive/emotional modulation.
  - Hypothalamus — autonomic and stress signalling.
  - Amygdala — emotional responses (fear, anxiety).
  - Ascending pain pathways (spinomesencephalic) — feedback from current nociception.
- Projects mainly to the rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM).

Stem: Explain visceral pain and referred pain. Why does heart attack pain go to the left arm and jaw, and how does the convergence-projection theory explain this?

Model answer: Visceral pain is the pain arising from internal organs. Its features differ profoundly from somatic (skin/musculoskeletal) pain — and the difference is rooted in the architecture of visceral innervation.

Features of visceral pain:

1. Quality — dull, aching, cramping, gnawing

- Distinctly different from sharp/pricking somatic pain.
- Often described as deep, "in the gut," "boring," "wrenching."

2. Poorly localised

- Patients struggle to point to where it hurts.
- Diffuse, often midline, periumbilical, or epigastric.

3. Often referred to a somatic dermatome

- The classic feature explained below.

4. Autonomic features

- Nausea, vomiting, sweating, pallor, BP changes, sometimes diarrhoea or urinary urgency.

5. Strong affective component

- Often described as "sickening," "deep dread," "feels wrong."
- Different cortical processing emphasises the emotional dimension.

6. Accompanied by reflex muscle guarding

- Abdominal wall rigidity in peritonitis.
- The body's protective response.

Why is visceral pain poorly localised?

Three reasons:

Wilder Penfield's discovery (1930s):

During neurosurgery for epilepsy, Penfield electrically stimulated different parts of the postcentral gyrus in awake patients (no general anaesthesia for cortex; the cortex itself has no pain receptors). Patients reported tingling, touch, or pressure sensations referred to specific body parts. By systematic stimulation, Penfield mapped the body representation in S1.

The body is represented in S1 with a characteristic distorted topography:

- Lateral/lower S1 (closer to lateral fissure): face, lips, tongue, jaw.
- More medial: hands (with fingers laid out), then arms, trunk.
- Most medial/superior: legs, feet, genitalia (extending into the medial wall in the paracentral lobule).

- Hands and face are vastly over-represented.
- Trunk and proximal limbs are tiny.
- Genitalia have a discrete substantial representation.
- Feet small relative to actual body proportion.

1. Receptor density — Fingertips have ~135 receptors/cm²; back has ~10. Cortex must process all the input from each receptor → more cortex for high-density areas.

2. Behavioural importance — Hands manipulate; face produces speech and expression; lips/tongue distinguish foods. These behaviours require fine discrimination → large cortical representation.

3. Two-point discrimination capability — Inversely correlates with cortical magnification: 2 mm at fingertip (large cortex) vs 50 mm on back (small cortex).

Stem: Common misconception: "Pain is a signal that travels from the injured tissue to the brain." Critique this view using modern pain neuroscience.

Model answer: The "transmission" model of pain — that injury produces a signal that travels to the brain where it is perceived as pain — is a 19th-century conception that modern neuroscience has largely overturned. The corrected understanding: pain is a constructed perception, not a faithful transmission.

Why the transmission model is incomplete:

1. Phantom limb pain demonstrates that pain can occur without any peripheral input. The limb is gone; there's no tissue to send a signal. Yet the brain perceives pain in the absent body part. The "signal" cannot be coming from the limb because there is no limb.

2. Stress-induced analgesia — Combat injuries, athletic injuries, and emergency situations are sometimes initially painless. The injury is severe, the peripheral signals are presumably present, but the brain suppresses pain perception because survival behaviour requires sustained function. The transmission model cannot explain why the same peripheral signal sometimes produces pain and sometimes doesn't.

3. Placebo analgesia — Belief that one is receiving an effective treatment can substantially reduce pain. Naloxone partially reverses placebo analgesia, demonstrating that endogenous opioid pathways are activated by belief alone. The peripheral signal is unchanged, but pain perception is altered.

4. Phantom pain in spinal cord injury — Patients with complete cord transection can experience pain "below" the level of injury, where no peripheral signal can possibly ascend.

5. Functional brain imaging — fMRI of pain reveals activation across a network: S1 (sensory-discriminative), S2, insula (interoception), anterior cingulate (affective), prefrontal (cognitive evaluation). The "pain matrix" extends across multiple cortical areas, not a single "pain centre." Different aspects of pain experience map onto different areas.
