## Pass 1 — Modes of cell communication and receptor classes

QUESTION 1
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Cell-to-cell communication occurs through several distinct modes that differ in distance and speed of signalling. Which option correctly pairs the mode with its description?

Correct answer: _Paracrine_ signalling — a molecule is released by one cell type and acts locally on a neighbouring cell of a different type, with the molecule typically degraded or taken up rapidly so it does not reach distant cells.

Distractors:

- "Autocrine signalling — a hormone is released into the bloodstream and reaches distant target cells via the circulation." — Reveals misconception: student conflates autocrine with endocrine. Correction: autocrine signalling is when a cell secretes a molecule that acts back on itself (or on cells of the same type); endocrine uses the bloodstream.
- "Endocrine signalling — a neurotransmitter is released across a narrow synaptic cleft to a single nearby target cell." — Reveals misconception: student conflates endocrine with synaptic. Correction: endocrine uses the bloodstream and reaches widely distributed targets; synaptic involves direct nerve-to-target contact.
- "Synaptic signalling — a hormone is secreted into the blood and acts on cells expressing the cognate receptor." — Reveals misconception: student again conflates synaptic with endocrine.

Explanation: Five basic modes of cell communication are recognised [Berne ch.3]. _Contact-dependent_ uses membrane-bound signalling molecules between adjacent cells. _Paracrine_ releases molecules that act locally on different cell types; the molecules are degraded or absorbed quickly to limit spread. _Autocrine_ is when the secreting cell expresses the receptor itself, so it acts back on the same cell or cells of the same type. _Synaptic_ is fast, point-to-point delivery of neurotransmitters across a narrow synaptic cleft. _Endocrine_ uses the bloodstream — slowest (seconds to minutes) but reaches all cells expressing the cognate receptor. Cells also communicate via gap junctions, which allow direct cytoplasmic continuity for small molecules and electrical coupling.

Hints:

1. Five modes; they differ in distance and selectivity.
2. The mode in question acts _locally_ but on cells of a _different_ type.
3. The cell secreting the signal does not act on itself in this mode.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Membrane receptors are typically classified into four major structural classes. Which option correctly identifies them?

Correct answer: Ligand-gated ion channels, G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), enzyme-linked receptors, and nuclear (intracellular) receptors.

Distractors:

- "Voltage-gated channels, ligand-gated channels, ion pumps, and carriers." — Reveals misconception: student lists transporter classes, not receptor classes.
- "α receptors, β receptors, γ receptors, and δ receptors." — Reveals misconception: student lists adrenergic receptor subtypes.
- "Tyrosine kinase receptors, serine kinase receptors, threonine kinase receptors, and histidine kinase receptors." — Reveals misconception: student lists subclasses of enzyme-linked receptors only.

Explanation: The four major receptor classes are [Berne ch.3]: (1) _Ligand-gated ion channels_ (e.g., nicotinic ACh receptor, NMDA glutamate receptor, GABA-A) — fast, direct synaptic signalling. (2) _GPCRs_ — couple to heterotrimeric G proteins to regulate effector enzymes (adenylyl cyclase, phospholipases) and ion channels; the largest single receptor class in humans. (3) _Enzyme-linked receptors_ — either possess intrinsic enzymatic activity (e.g., receptor tyrosine kinases like the insulin receptor, receptor guanylyl cyclase for ANP) or are tightly associated with kinases (e.g., cytokine receptors that recruit JAK kinases). (4) _Nuclear receptors_ — intracellular receptors for lipophilic ligands (steroid hormones, thyroid hormone, retinoids, vitamin D) that regulate gene transcription directly.

Hints:

1. Three classes are membrane-bound; one is intracellular.
2. The membrane-bound classes differ by what happens when ligand binds — ion channel opens, G protein is activated, or an enzyme activates.
3. The intracellular class binds lipophilic ligands.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) share a characteristic structural feature. They span the plasma membrane with how many α-helical domains?

Correct answer: Seven.

Distractors:

- "One." — Reveals misconception: student thinks of receptors generically as single-pass. Correction: many enzyme-linked receptors (e.g., RTKs) are single-pass, but GPCRs are seven-pass.
- "Four." — Reveals misconception: student conflates GPCRs with voltage-gated channels (the Na⁺ channel α subunit has 4 domains × 6 α-helices each).
- "Twelve." — Reveals misconception: student conflates GPCRs with solute carriers (some carriers have 12 transmembrane segments).

Explanation: GPCRs span the membrane seven times with α-helical transmembrane domains [Berne ch.3]. The N-terminus is extracellular and the C-terminus intracellular. The ligand-binding site is on the extracellular surface (for large ligands) or within the transmembrane region (for small ligands). The intracellular loops, especially the third intracellular loop and the C-terminus, bind to the heterotrimeric G protein. Because of this seven-pass architecture, GPCRs are sometimes called "seven-transmembrane receptors" or "serpentine receptors". Nearly half of all non-antibiotic prescription drugs target GPCRs, which makes them the single most clinically important receptor class.

Hints:

1. The number of TM domains is also reflected in an alternative name for these receptors ("seven-transmembrane").
2. The number is more than four but less than ten.
3. The number is one less than the number of subunits in some homotetrameric ion channels.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A heterotrimeric G protein is composed of three subunits — α, β, and γ. In humans, how many distinct α subunit isoforms exist that can combine with β and γ subunits to produce diverse signalling specificities?

Correct answer: 16 α subunits (which combine with 5 β and 11 γ subunit isoforms).

Distractors:

- "3 — one for each major class (Gs, Gi, Gq)." — Reveals misconception: student treats Gs, Gi, Gq as the only families. Correction: there are multiple α isoforms within each family, with 16 total α subunits.
- "1 — there is a single universal α subunit." — Reveals misconception: student denies the diversity.
- "More than 100 — every receptor has its own dedicated α." — Reveals misconception: student overestimates the diversity.

Explanation: Heterotrimeric G proteins contain 16 α subunits, 5 β subunits, and 11 γ subunits, which can combine into hundreds of different combinations [Berne ch.3]. The α subunits are functionally grouped into four major families: αs (stimulatory — activates adenylyl cyclase), αi/o (inhibitory — inhibits adenylyl cyclase), αq (activates phospholipase C-β), and α12/13 (regulates Rho GTPases). The combination of α isoform with specific β/γ pairs determines which receptors couple to which effectors. The βγ dimer also has independent signalling roles — it can activate ion channels and kinases. Heterotrimeric G proteins couple to over 1000 different receptors, mediating the cellular response to an enormous range of signalling molecules.

Hints:

1. The number is more than five but less than twenty.
2. It is the same magnitude as the number of common amino acids.
3. The number is the count of _α_ subunit isoforms specifically.

QUESTION 5
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Endocrine, synaptic, and gene-expression-mediated responses operate on different timescales. Which ordering is correct (fastest to slowest)?

Correct answer: Synaptic (milliseconds) → endocrine effects on existing proteins (seconds to minutes) → endocrine effects requiring new gene expression (hours to days).

Distractors:

- "Endocrine → synaptic → gene expression." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the relative speed of synaptic vs endocrine.
- "Gene expression → synaptic → endocrine." — Reveals misconception: student treats gene expression as fastest.
- "All three operate on the same timescale (seconds)." — Reveals misconception: student denies the time-scale spectrum.

Explanation: Signalling timescales differ by orders of magnitude [Berne ch.3]. Synaptic signalling is the fastest — neurotransmitter release at the synapse, receptor binding, and postsynaptic ion channel opening occur in milliseconds. Endocrine signalling on pre-existing target proteins takes seconds to minutes — the hormone must reach target cells via blood, bind receptors, and activate intracellular signalling. Endocrine effects requiring new protein synthesis (e.g., aldosterone-driven changes in renal sodium transport, steroid hormone-driven primary and secondary response gene transcription) take hours to days, because transcription, translation, and protein folding are all relatively slow processes. Recognising this temporal hierarchy explains why the same hormone can have rapid effects (via membrane signalling) and slow effects (via nuclear receptors).

Hints:

1. Three timescales: ms, seconds, hours.
2. Which mode delivers signals across the shortest distance?
3. The slowest mode involves making new proteins.

QUESTION 6
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: A patient is treated with cortisol (a glucocorticoid). The therapeutic effects appear over hours rather than seconds because:

Correct answer: Cortisol acts through nuclear receptors that regulate gene transcription, requiring new mRNA and protein synthesis to produce its effects.

Distractors:

- "Cortisol must be metabolised by the liver before it can bind its receptor." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the slow onset to metabolism. Correction: cortisol binds its receptor directly; the slow onset is from gene transcription, not metabolism.
- "Cortisol acts on GPCRs, which require multiple amplification steps." — Reveals misconception: student treats cortisol as a peptide hormone signalling via GPCRs. Correction: cortisol is a steroid that crosses the membrane and binds intracellular receptors.
- "Cortisol activates ligand-gated ion channels in the target cell membrane." — Reveals misconception: student misclassifies the receptor type.

Explanation: Cortisol is a steroid hormone — small, hydrophobic, and able to diffuse across the plasma membrane. It binds intracellular glucocorticoid receptors (in the cytoplasm), which then translocate to the nucleus and bind glucocorticoid response elements on DNA, regulating transcription of target genes [Berne ch.3]. The transcribed mRNA must be translated into protein, and the new proteins must reach their site of action — all of which takes hours. Steroid hormones characteristically have _primary response_ genes (transcription within ~30 minutes) followed by _secondary response_ genes (hours to days). Some non-genomic effects of steroids occur faster (via membrane-bound steroid receptors), but the dominant clinical effects of glucocorticoids unfold over hours to days. This explains why cortisol is not the right drug for an acute crisis requiring response within seconds (e.g., anaphylactic shock, where adrenaline — acting via GPCRs — is the first-line agent).

Hints:

1. The receptor for cortisol is not in the plasma membrane.
2. The receptor regulates gene transcription, which has its own timescale.
3. New proteins take time to be synthesised and reach their site of action.

QUESTION 7
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Acetylcholine binds two structurally distinct receptor classes, producing very different cellular responses. Which pairing is correct?

Correct answer: The nicotinic ACh receptor is a _ligand-gated ion channel_ (fast, direct depolarisation); the muscarinic ACh receptor is a _GPCR_ (slower, signal-cascade mediated effects).

Distractors:

- "Both nicotinic and muscarinic receptors are GPCRs that activate the same second-messenger pathway." — Reveals misconception: student denies the structural difference.
- "Nicotinic is a GPCR; muscarinic is a ligand-gated channel." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the classification.
- "Nicotinic and muscarinic receptors have identical structures but different binding sites." — Reveals misconception: student treats the two classes as the same protein with conformational variants.

Explanation: ACh has two receptor classes that look completely different at the molecular level [Berne ch.3]. The _nicotinic_ receptor is a pentameric ligand-gated cation channel (Na⁺, K⁺, some Ca²⁺); ligand binding opens the channel in milliseconds, producing rapid depolarisation. It is found at the neuromuscular junction (where it is the target of myasthenia-gravis antibodies) and in autonomic ganglia. The _muscarinic_ receptor is a GPCR (with subtypes M1–M5) that couples to αq (M1, M3, M5; activates PLC) or αi (M2, M4; inhibits adenylyl cyclase). Muscarinic effects are slower and indirect — for example, M2 in the heart slows heart rate by activating αi and βγ-mediated K⁺ channels. The pharmacological terminology reflects the different agonists that selectively activate each: nicotine (from tobacco) for the nicotinic; muscarine (from _Amanita muscaria_ mushroom) for the muscarinic.

Hints:

1. The two receptors share a ligand but not a structure.
2. One is a fast ion channel; the other uses an intracellular signalling cascade.
3. The names come from natural compounds that selectively activate each.

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Steroid hormones (e.g., aldosterone, cortisol, testosterone) and thyroid hormones share which fundamental signalling mechanism?

Correct answer: They cross the plasma membrane and bind intracellular receptors that ultimately regulate gene transcription.

Distractors:

- "They bind GPCRs at the plasma membrane and activate adenylyl cyclase." — Reveals misconception: student treats steroid and thyroid hormones as peptide-like in their signalling.
- "They are charged molecules that cannot cross the bilayer; they enter the cell via specific transporters." — Reveals misconception: student denies the lipophilic nature of these hormones. Correction: steroid and thyroid hormones are lipophilic and diffuse freely across the membrane (carrier proteins in blood may help solubility, but the hormones themselves cross the bilayer easily).
- "They open ligand-gated ion channels at the plasma membrane." — Reveals misconception: student applies a different receptor class.

Explanation: Steroid hormones, thyroid hormones, retinoids, and vitamin D share a common signalling architecture [Berne ch.3]. All four are small, lipophilic, and diffuse across the plasma membrane (despite being transported in blood bound to carrier proteins for solubility). Inside the cell, they bind to nuclear receptors — either in the cytoplasm (with subsequent translocation to the nucleus, as for cortisol and aldosterone) or already in the nucleus (as for thyroid hormone receptors). The hormone-receptor complex binds to specific DNA response elements, displacing inhibitory proteins, recruiting coactivators, and activating or repressing transcription of target genes. Because the response requires new protein synthesis, it operates over hours to days — a key clinical distinction from peptide hormone signalling (which acts within seconds to minutes via plasma-membrane receptors). The shared architecture explains why steroid drugs (e.g., dexamethasone), thyroid replacement, and vitamin D all have characteristically slow onset of action.

Hints:

1. The molecules are small and lipid-soluble.
2. They do not stop at the plasma membrane.
3. The ultimate target is the genome.

## Pass 2 — GPCRs and second messenger pathways

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: When a ligand binds a GPCR coupled to a _Gs_ α subunit (αs), the immediate molecular event is:

Correct answer: GTP replaces GDP on the αs subunit; αs dissociates from βγ and activates adenylyl cyclase, which converts ATP to cAMP.

Distractors:

- "GDP replaces GTP on αs, inactivating the protein and reducing cAMP." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the activation/inactivation.
- "αs directly activates phospholipase C, generating IP₃ and DAG." — Reveals misconception: student conflates αs with αq pathway. Correction: αq activates PLC; αs activates adenylyl cyclase.
- "αs activates a tyrosine kinase, which phosphorylates downstream proteins." — Reveals misconception: student conflates the GPCR pathway with RTK pathways.

Explanation: GPCR activation follows a stereotyped sequence [Berne ch.3]. (1) Ligand binds the GPCR's extracellular surface; the receptor undergoes a conformational change. (2) The activated receptor catalyses GDP-to-GTP exchange on the α subunit of the heterotrimeric G protein. (3) GTP-bound α dissociates from βγ. (4) Free α (and βγ) interact with downstream effectors. For Gs, activated αs binds and activates _adenylyl cyclase_, which converts ATP to _cAMP_. cAMP activates _protein kinase A (PKA)_, which phosphorylates serine/threonine residues on downstream effectors (and on the transcription factor CREB to regulate gene expression). The signal is terminated when α hydrolyses GTP to GDP (intrinsic GTPase activity), allowing α to reassociate with βγ and the cycle to reset. This stimulatory cascade is the canonical GPCR pathway and is the target of β1-adrenergic receptors (heart rate increase), V2 receptors (ADH-mediated water reabsorption), and many others.

Hints:

1. αs _stimulates_ adenylyl cyclase.
2. The activation step is GDP → GTP exchange (the "on" state).
3. The downstream second messenger is cyclic AMP.

QUESTION 10
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Compare the downstream effects of αs versus αi after ligand binding to their respective GPCRs:

Correct answer: αs activates adenylyl cyclase and _increases_ cAMP; αi inhibits adenylyl cyclase and _decreases_ cAMP.

Distractors:

- "Both αs and αi activate adenylyl cyclase but with different strengths." — Reveals misconception: student denies the inhibitory direction of αi.
- "αs activates phospholipase C; αi activates adenylyl cyclase." — Reveals misconception: student conflates αs with αq.
- "αs and αi work on the same pathway in series; αs activates first, then αi turns it off." — Reveals misconception: student treats αi as a feedback step within the αs pathway. Correction: αs and αi are independent pathways, typically activated by different receptors.

Explanation: αs and αi are functionally opposite members of the heterotrimeric G protein α family [Berne ch.3]. αs (stimulatory) activates adenylyl cyclase, raising cAMP; αi (inhibitory) inhibits adenylyl cyclase, lowering cAMP. Both pathways are independently activated by their own GPCRs — for example, β-adrenergic receptors couple to αs (raising cAMP, increasing heart rate, relaxing bronchial smooth muscle), while M2 muscarinic receptors couple to αi (lowering cAMP, slowing heart rate). The two pathways converge on the same effector enzyme (adenylyl cyclase) and produce opposite effects on cAMP and PKA. Many cells express both αs- and αi-coupled receptors, and the integrated cellular response depends on the balance of receptor occupancy and α subunit activity. This convergent architecture is what allows GPCR signalling to be both diverse and finely regulated.

Hints:

1. The names give the direction: αs = stimulatory, αi = inhibitory.
2. The shared effector is adenylyl cyclase.
3. The signal molecule that goes up or down is cAMP.

QUESTION 11
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Activation of αq (Gq) by a ligand-bound GPCR triggers which downstream pathway?

Correct answer: Activation of phospholipase C, which cleaves PIP₂ into IP₃ (releases Ca²⁺ from ER) and DAG (activates protein kinase C).

Distractors:

- "Activation of adenylyl cyclase, increasing cAMP and activating PKA." — Reveals misconception: student conflates αq with αs.
- "Direct opening of voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in the plasma membrane." — Reveals misconception: student bypasses the second-messenger step.
- "Inhibition of voltage-gated K⁺ channels via direct binding." — Reveals misconception: student conflates αq with βγ-mediated channel modulation.

Explanation: αq couples GPCR activation to phospholipase C (PLC-β) [Berne ch.3]. PLC cleaves _phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP₂)_ — a minor phospholipid in the inner leaflet — into two second messengers: _inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP₃)_ and _diacylglycerol (DAG)_. IP₃ diffuses to the endoplasmic reticulum and binds IP₃ receptors (ligand-gated Ca²⁺ channels), releasing stored Ca²⁺ into the cytosol. The Ca²⁺ rise activates Ca²⁺-binding proteins (especially calmodulin) and Ca²⁺-dependent enzymes. DAG remains in the membrane and activates protein kinase C (PKC), which phosphorylates downstream targets. Major αq-coupled receptors include α1-adrenergic (vascular smooth muscle contraction), M1/M3/M5 muscarinic (smooth muscle contraction, glandular secretion), and angiotensin II type 1 (AT1) receptors. The αq pathway thus generates _two_ second messengers from one substrate, providing parallel pathways for regulating Ca²⁺ release and protein phosphorylation.

Hints:

1. The "q" in αq stands for nothing simple, but the downstream enzyme is well-known.
2. Two second messengers come from a single phospholipid substrate.
3. One messenger releases Ca²⁺; the other activates a serine/threonine kinase.

QUESTION 12
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: _Cholera toxin_ causes severe secretory diarrhoea by acting on a specific GPCR signalling component. Its molecular mechanism is:

Correct answer: ADP-ribosylation of the αs subunit, which inhibits its GTPase activity, locking αs in the GTP-bound active state and chronically activating adenylyl cyclase.

Distractors:

- "Direct inhibition of cAMP phosphodiesterase, preventing cAMP breakdown." — Reveals misconception: student attributes the high cAMP to PDE inhibition. Correction: cholera toxin acts upstream — at αs.
- "ADP-ribosylation of αi, removing its inhibition of adenylyl cyclase." — Reveals misconception: student confuses cholera toxin with pertussis toxin. Correction: pertussis toxin ribosylates αi; cholera toxin ribosylates αs.
- "Direct activation of CFTR chloride channels by binding to CFTR." — Reveals misconception: student treats cholera toxin as a direct CFTR opener. Correction: cholera toxin acts on αs; the elevated cAMP/PKA then activates CFTR indirectly.

Explanation: Cholera toxin (from _Vibrio cholerae_) catalyses _ADP-ribosylation of the αs subunit_ of the heterotrimeric Gs protein [Berne ch.3]. The ADP-ribose modification inhibits αs's intrinsic GTPase activity — αs cannot hydrolyse GTP to GDP, so it remains in its activated GTP-bound state indefinitely. The chronically active αs continuously activates adenylyl cyclase, raising cellular cAMP to levels far above normal. In intestinal enterocytes, the high cAMP activates PKA, which phosphorylates and activates the CFTR chloride channel on the apical membrane, driving massive chloride (and therefore water) secretion into the gut lumen. The result is the watery, life-threatening diarrhoea characteristic of cholera. _Pertussis toxin_ (from _Bordetella pertussis_) acts by an analogous mechanism but on αi — ADP-ribosylation inactivates αi, removing its inhibition of adenylyl cyclase and similarly raising cAMP (though by a different mechanism). Both bacterial toxins illustrate how GPCR signalling can be hijacked at the G protein level.

Hints:

1. The toxin doesn't act directly on adenylyl cyclase or CFTR.
2. It acts on a G protein α subunit by a covalent modification.
3. The modification locks the α subunit in its active conformation by inhibiting its GTPase.

QUESTION 13
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Activated PKA (cAMP-dependent protein kinase) has a major direct effect in the nucleus. Specifically, the catalytic subunit:

Correct answer: Translocates to the nucleus and phosphorylates the transcription factor CREB, which then recruits coactivators and stimulates transcription of cAMP response element (CRE)-containing genes.

Distractors:

- "Inhibits all transcription by phosphorylating RNA polymerase II." — Reveals misconception: student treats PKA as a global transcription inhibitor. Correction: PKA effects on transcription are gene-specific via CREB, not global.
- "Cleaves DNA at specific sites to activate gene expression." — Reveals misconception: student treats PKA as an endonuclease.
- "Binds DNA directly as a transcription factor." — Reveals misconception: student treats PKA as a DNA-binding protein. Correction: PKA is a kinase; CREB is the DNA-binding transcription factor that PKA phosphorylates.

Explanation: PKA has both cytoplasmic and nuclear effects [Berne ch.3]. After activation by cAMP, the catalytic subunits of PKA dissociate from regulatory subunits and can translocate into the nucleus. There, they phosphorylate the cAMP response element-binding (CREB) protein on a specific serine residue (Ser133). Phosphorylated CREB recruits the coactivator CBP (CREB-binding protein), which has histone acetyltransferase activity, opens chromatin, and stimulates RNA polymerase II at genes containing cAMP response elements (CRE) in their promoters. This pathway is the molecular basis for many slower, longer-term effects of cAMP signalling — including hormone-responsive gene expression, neuronal long-term potentiation (memory consolidation), and metabolic adaptation. Cytoplasmic PKA effects (rapid phosphorylation of metabolic enzymes, ion channels, contractile proteins) provide the parallel fast component of cAMP signalling.

Hints:

1. PKA enters the nucleus and activates a specific transcription factor.
2. The transcription factor's name relates to "cAMP response element."
3. The phosphorylation event is on a specific serine residue.

QUESTION 14
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: _Sildenafil_ (Viagra) treats erectile dysfunction by enhancing the effect of nitric oxide. Its precise molecular target is:

Correct answer: Phosphodiesterase type 5 (PDE5) — sildenafil inhibits PDE5, preventing cGMP breakdown and prolonging NO-induced vasodilation.

Distractors:

- "Soluble guanylyl cyclase — sildenafil activates it directly to produce more cGMP." — Reveals misconception: student treats sildenafil as a guanylyl cyclase activator. Correction: sildenafil inhibits cGMP breakdown, not synthesis.
- "Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in vascular smooth muscle." — Reveals misconception: student conflates sildenafil with calcium channel blockers.
- "α1-adrenergic receptors, blocking sympathetic vasoconstriction." — Reveals misconception: student conflates sildenafil with α-blockers.

Explanation: Nitric oxide (NO) is generated locally in vascular endothelium by NO synthase and diffuses across the cell membrane to its target [Berne ch.3]. NO binds and activates _soluble guanylyl cyclase_, which converts GTP to cGMP. cGMP activates cGMP-dependent protein kinase (PKG), which dephosphorylates myosin light chains in vascular smooth muscle, producing vasodilation. The action of cGMP is terminated by phosphodiesterases — specifically _PDE5_ in vascular smooth muscle of the corpus cavernosum and pulmonary vasculature. Sildenafil, tadalafil, and vardenafil are PDE5 inhibitors — they prevent cGMP breakdown, prolonging the vasodilatory effect of NO. In the corpus cavernosum, this enhances erection (used clinically for erectile dysfunction). In the pulmonary vasculature, it reduces pulmonary arterial pressure (used for pulmonary hypertension). Nitroglycerin, by contrast, is a _NO donor_ — it generates NO directly and activates the same cGMP pathway. Co-administration of nitrates and PDE5 inhibitors is contraindicated because the combined effect can produce dangerous hypotension.

Hints:

1. The drug doesn't make NO or cGMP directly.
2. It prevents the breakdown of a second messenger.
3. The enzyme it inhibits has subtype number 5.

QUESTION 15
Type: misconception-targeted
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A first-year medical student says: "Once a hormone binds its GPCR, the signal continues until the hormone unbinds." Which correction is most accurate?

Correct answer: GPCR signalling is terminated by _desensitisation_ — receptor phosphorylation by GPCR kinases (GRKs) recruits β-arrestins, which inactivate the receptor and promote endocytosis, even while the hormone remains bound.

Distractors:

- "The student is correct — receptor signalling lasts as long as the hormone is bound." — Reveals misconception: student denies the existence of receptor regulation.
- "Signalling terminates only when the hormone is degraded by enzymes in the bloodstream." — Reveals misconception: student attributes termination solely to ligand metabolism. Correction: even with hormone present, receptors desensitise.
- "The cell rapidly synthesises new receptors that displace the bound hormone." — Reveals misconception: student invents a non-existent mechanism.

Explanation: GPCR signalling has a built-in termination architecture independent of ligand availability [Berne ch.3]. After a GPCR is activated, _GPCR kinases (GRKs)_ phosphorylate the receptor's intracellular C-terminus. Phosphorylated GPCRs recruit _β-arrestins_, proteins that bind the receptor, sterically uncouple it from G proteins (terminating signalling), and target the receptor for clathrin-mediated endocytosis. The internalised receptors are either dephosphorylated and recycled (resensitisation) or trafficked to lysosomes for degradation (downregulation). This process is called _homologous desensitisation_ — only receptors actively bound to ligand are desensitised, leaving other receptors unaffected. _Heterologous desensitisation_ is when one ligand activates kinases (e.g., PKA, PKC) that phosphorylate other receptors, reducing their responsiveness even without their own ligand. β-blockers used in heart failure are thought to work partly by reversing chronic β-receptor desensitisation in failing myocardium, restoring adrenergic responsiveness over weeks of therapy.

Hints:

1. Cells have an active mechanism to turn off receptor signalling.
2. A specific protein binds phosphorylated receptors and blocks G protein coupling.
3. The mechanism explains why opioid tolerance develops with chronic use.

QUESTION 16
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A patient is treated with a long-acting opioid for chronic pain. After several weeks, the same dose produces less analgesia. Which mechanism best explains this _tachyphylaxis_ (drug tolerance)?

Correct answer: Receptor desensitisation — repeated opioid receptor activation triggers GRK-mediated phosphorylation, β-arrestin recruitment, and receptor internalisation, reducing the number of cell-surface receptors available for binding.

Distractors:

- "Hepatic enzyme induction increases opioid metabolism, reducing the effective dose." — Reveals misconception: student attributes tolerance solely to pharmacokinetic factors. Correction: while metabolism may contribute, receptor-level desensitisation is a major mechanism for opioids.
- "The opioid receptor is mutated by chronic use, reducing its affinity for the drug." — Reveals misconception: student invokes mutation. Correction: receptors aren't mutated by drug use; they undergo reversible regulatory changes.
- "Opioids competitively inhibit their own breakdown over time." — Reveals misconception: student invents a non-existent mechanism.

Explanation: _Tachyphylaxis_ (or tolerance) refers to the diminished response to a drug with repeated administration [Berne ch.3]. For opioids and many other GPCR agonists, the dominant mechanism is _homologous desensitisation_: repeated activation of opioid receptors triggers GRK-mediated phosphorylation, β-arrestin recruitment, receptor internalisation, and a reduction in plasma membrane receptor density. The same dose of opioid then activates fewer receptors, producing less analgesia. Restoring the response requires either dose escalation (more drug to compensate for fewer receptors) or "drug holiday" (stopping the drug to allow receptors to resurface and the system to resensitise). The same mechanism underlies tolerance to β-agonists, GABA-A modulators (benzodiazepines), and many other clinically important drugs. Recognising tolerance as a _receptor regulation_ phenomenon (not a metabolic one) explains why dose escalation works and why cross-tolerance occurs between drugs that share a receptor.

Hints:

1. The mechanism of tolerance is at the receptor level.
2. It involves the same pathway as physiological GPCR desensitisation (GRK + β-arrestin).
3. The number of cell-surface receptors decreases with chronic exposure.

QUESTION 17

Stem: The insulin receptor is a member of the receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK) family. Its quaternary structure consists of:

Correct answer: An α₂β₂ tetramer — two extracellular α subunits (insulin binding) and two transmembrane β subunits (containing intracellular tyrosine kinase domains), held together by disulfide bonds.

Distractors:

- "A single transmembrane polypeptide that dimerises only after ligand binding." — Reveals misconception: student treats the insulin receptor like the EGF receptor (which exists as monomers and dimerises on ligand binding). Correction: the insulin receptor is a constitutive α₂β₂ tetramer.
- "A heterotrimeric complex of α, β, and γ subunits coupled to G proteins." — Reveals misconception: student conflates the insulin receptor with GPCRs.
- "A pentameric ligand-gated ion channel similar to the nicotinic ACh receptor." — Reveals misconception: student picks a different receptor architecture entirely.

Explanation: Unlike most other receptor tyrosine kinases (EGF, NGF, PDGF receptors, which are single-pass receptors that dimerise only after ligand binding), the insulin receptor exists as a _constitutively assembled α₂β₂ tetramer_ [Berne ch.3]. The two α subunits are entirely extracellular and bind insulin. The two β subunits cross the membrane once each and contain intracellular tyrosine kinase domains. α and β are linked by disulfide bonds. When insulin binds the α subunits, a conformational change is transmitted through the disulfide bridges to the β subunits, activating their tyrosine kinase activity. The β subunits _autophosphorylate_ on tyrosine residues in their kinase domains; the phosphotyrosines then recruit downstream signalling proteins (especially insulin receptor substrate-1, IRS-1) by SH2-domain interactions. IRS-1 then activates the PI3K-Akt pathway (membrane translocation of GLUT4 in skeletal muscle and adipose tissue), MAPK pathway (gene expression and cell growth), and other branches. The insulin receptor's tetrameric architecture is conserved across the IGF-1 receptor family.

Hints:

1. Most RTKs are single-pass and dimerise on ligand binding. Insulin receptor is different.
2. It has two α and two β subunits.
3. The α subunits are extracellular; the β subunits cross the membrane.

QUESTION 18
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Cytokine receptors (e.g., for interleukin-6, erythropoietin, growth hormone) signal through which mechanism?

Correct answer: They lack intrinsic kinase activity but are constitutively associated with cytoplasmic JAK kinases; ligand binding causes receptor dimerisation, JAK activation, and tyrosine phosphorylation of STAT transcription factors that translocate to the nucleus to regulate gene expression.

Distractors:

- "They are GPCRs that activate adenylyl cyclase via αs." — Reveals misconception: student assigns cytokine receptors to the wrong class.
- "They are intrinsic tyrosine kinase receptors like the insulin receptor, with kinase domains in their own intracellular tails." — Reveals misconception: student conflates cytokine receptors with RTKs. Correction: cytokine receptors lack intrinsic kinase activity — they require associated JAK kinases.
- "They are ligand-gated ion channels that depolarise the cell on cytokine binding." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong receptor class.

Explanation: Cytokine receptors are _tyrosine kinase-associated_ receptors — they lack intrinsic enzymatic activity but are constitutively bound to cytoplasmic _Janus kinases (JAKs)_ [Berne ch.3]. Ligand binding causes receptor dimerisation (or higher-order oligomerisation), bringing two JAKs into proximity. The JAKs _trans-phosphorylate_ each other (and the receptor's intracellular tail), creating phosphotyrosine docking sites. _Signal Transducer and Activator of Transcription (STAT)_ proteins bind these phosphotyrosines via SH2 domains, are themselves phosphorylated by JAKs, dimerise, and translocate to the nucleus where they bind DNA and regulate gene expression. The pathway is named _JAK-STAT_. It is the principal signalling route for many cytokines (interferons, interleukins, growth hormone, erythropoietin, leptin) and is targeted clinically: JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, ruxolitinib) treat rheumatoid arthritis, myelofibrosis, and some forms of inflammatory bowel disease by blocking JAK-STAT signalling downstream of inflammatory cytokines.

Hints:

1. The receptor itself has no enzyme activity — it recruits a separate enzyme.
2. The recruited enzyme phosphorylates a transcription factor that goes directly to the nucleus.
3. The pathway has a specific name made of two acronyms.

Stem: Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) is released from cardiac atria in response to volume overload. Its receptor on renal collecting duct cells signals through:

Correct answer: A receptor guanylyl cyclase — the receptor itself is an enzyme that converts GTP to cGMP, which activates cGMP-dependent protein kinase (PKG).

Distractors:

- "A GPCR coupled to αs, increasing cAMP and PKA activity." — Reveals misconception: student treats ANP like other peptide hormones (cAMP-mediated). Correction: ANP receptor has its own intrinsic guanylyl cyclase activity.
- "A nuclear receptor that regulates gene transcription." — Reveals misconception: student treats ANP as steroid-like. Correction: ANP is a peptide; its receptor is membrane-bound.
- "A ligand-gated Na⁺ channel that depolarises the cell on ANP binding." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong receptor class.

Explanation: ANP signals through _receptor guanylyl cyclase_ — a transmembrane protein with an extracellular ANP-binding domain and an intracellular catalytic domain that converts GTP to cGMP [Berne ch.3]. ANP binding causes receptor dimerisation and activation of guanylyl cyclase activity; cGMP rises and activates _PKG_ (cGMP-dependent protein kinase), which phosphorylates target proteins. In renal collecting duct cells, ANP-PKG signalling reduces sodium and water reabsorption, promoting natriuresis and diuresis. ANP also vasodilates blood vessels (via cGMP/PKG-mediated relaxation of vascular smooth muscle) and inhibits aldosterone secretion. The result: lower blood volume and pressure — a homeostatic response to volume overload. Note the parallel with NO/soluble guanylyl cyclase signalling (Q14): NO acts on a _soluble_ (cytoplasmic) guanylyl cyclase; ANP acts on a _receptor_ (membrane-bound) guanylyl cyclase. Both produce cGMP and activate PKG, but the upstream architecture differs.

Hints:

1. The receptor is itself an enzyme — not a GPCR.
2. The enzyme generates a cyclic nucleotide.
3. The cyclic nucleotide is similar to cAMP but with a different purine base.

QUESTION 20
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Nitric oxide (NO) and ANP both increase cGMP, but through different molecular mechanisms. The key difference is:

Correct answer: NO activates _soluble_ (cytoplasmic) guanylyl cyclase by binding directly to its haem group; ANP activates _receptor_ (membrane-bound) guanylyl cyclase by binding to its extracellular domain.

Distractors:

- "NO acts via a GPCR; ANP acts via a tyrosine kinase receptor." — Reveals misconception: student picks wrong receptor classes for both.
- "NO and ANP activate the same enzyme; the difference is purely pharmacokinetic." — Reveals misconception: student denies the architectural difference.
- "NO activates adenylyl cyclase; ANP activates guanylyl cyclase." — Reveals misconception: student confuses cyclic nucleotides. Correction: both NO and ANP raise cGMP, not cAMP.

Explanation: cGMP can be generated by two distinct guanylyl cyclase isoforms [Berne ch.3]. _Soluble guanylyl cyclase_ (sGC) is a cytoplasmic enzyme containing a haem prosthetic group; NO diffuses across the cell membrane (it is a small uncharged gas), binds the haem iron, and allosterically activates sGC, raising cGMP. NO is generated by NO synthase from L-arginine in endothelial cells, neurons, and macrophages; it is short-lived (seconds) and acts very locally. _Receptor guanylyl cyclase_ is a transmembrane protein where the extracellular domain binds ligand (ANP, BNP, or guanylin) and the intracellular domain has cyclase activity. Both produce cGMP and activate PKG, converging on similar downstream effects (vasodilation, smooth muscle relaxation, natriuresis). Recognising the two distinct cGMP pathways explains why drugs targeting them have different uses: nitrates (NO donors) for angina; sildenafil (PDE5 inhibitor that prolongs cGMP from both sources) for erectile dysfunction and pulmonary hypertension.

Hints:

1. cGMP can be made by enzymes in two different cellular locations.
2. NO is a gas — it doesn't bind a transmembrane receptor.
3. ANP is a peptide — it cannot cross the plasma membrane.

QUESTION 21

Stem: Calcium acts as a major second messenger. After Ca²⁺ enters the cytosol from the extracellular fluid (through Ca²⁺ channels) or from intracellular stores (through IP₃ receptor channels in the ER), its principal mediator protein is:

Correct answer: Calmodulin (CaM) — a small Ca²⁺-binding protein that, once Ca²⁺-loaded, binds and regulates many downstream effectors.

Distractors:

- "Calcium itself directly phosphorylates target proteins on serine/threonine residues." — Reveals misconception: student treats Ca²⁺ as having intrinsic kinase activity. Correction: Ca²⁺ acts via binding proteins like calmodulin, not by direct enzymatic action.
- "PKA — Ca²⁺ activates PKA, which phosphorylates downstream targets." — Reveals misconception: student conflates Ca²⁺ pathway with cAMP/PKA pathway.
- "Adenylyl cyclase — Ca²⁺ raises cAMP, which mediates downstream effects." — Reveals misconception: student conflates Ca²⁺ and cAMP pathways.

Explanation: Calmodulin (CaM) is a small (~17 kDa) cytoplasmic protein with four EF-hand Ca²⁺-binding sites [Berne ch.3]. When intracellular Ca²⁺ rises (from extracellular entry or ER release via IP₃ receptors or ryanodine receptors), CaM binds Ca²⁺, undergoes a conformational change, and then binds and activates a wide range of effectors. Examples include: Ca²⁺/calmodulin-dependent protein kinases (CaMKs) — which phosphorylate many targets, including ion channels, receptors, and CREB (linking Ca²⁺ signalling to gene expression); myosin light chain kinase (MLCK) — which phosphorylates myosin light chains in smooth muscle to drive contraction; calcineurin (Ca²⁺/CaM-dependent phosphatase) — which dephosphorylates the transcription factor NFAT, allowing it to enter the nucleus and drive immune-cell gene expression (the target of cyclosporine and tacrolimus in transplant immunosuppression); and several Ca²⁺ phosphodiesterases that degrade cAMP, providing crosstalk between Ca²⁺ and cAMP pathways. CaM is therefore the universal Ca²⁺-effector protein — the molecular "translator" between the Ca²⁺ signal and downstream cellular responses.

Hints:

1. Ca²⁺ is too small to act directly as a kinase or transcription factor.
2. It binds a protein that then mediates its effects.
3. The protein is named after the ions it binds.

QUESTION 22
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Cyclosporine and tacrolimus are immunosuppressants used after solid-organ transplant. They share which signalling target?

Correct answer: They inhibit _calcineurin_, a Ca²⁺/calmodulin-dependent phosphatase, blocking dephosphorylation of NFAT and preventing its translocation to the nucleus where it drives IL-2 and other cytokine gene expression in activated T cells.

Distractors:

- "They inhibit JAK kinases, blocking cytokine receptor signalling." — Reveals misconception: student conflates calcineurin inhibitors with JAK inhibitors.
- "They block T cell receptors directly, preventing antigen recognition." — Reveals misconception: student treats them as receptor blockers.
- "They inhibit adenylyl cyclase in T cells, lowering cAMP." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong pathway.

Explanation: T cell activation by antigen produces a cytosolic Ca²⁺ rise (via IP₃-mediated ER release through TCR/CD3 → Lck → ZAP70 → PLCγ → IP₃ → Ca²⁺), which activates Ca²⁺/calmodulin and ultimately _calcineurin_ — a Ca²⁺/CaM-dependent serine/threonine phosphatase [Berne ch.3]. Calcineurin dephosphorylates the transcription factor NFAT (Nuclear Factor of Activated T cells), unmasking its nuclear localisation signal and allowing NFAT to enter the nucleus. In the nucleus, NFAT drives transcription of IL-2 and other cytokine genes essential for T cell proliferation. Cyclosporine and tacrolimus both inhibit calcineurin (cyclosporine via cyclophilin; tacrolimus via FK506-binding protein) — preventing NFAT dephosphorylation, blocking IL-2 production, and suppressing T cell activation. This is the molecular basis of _calcineurin inhibitors_ in transplant medicine. Their selectivity for activated T cells (which depend on this pathway) gives them therapeutic value despite the broader presence of calcineurin in many tissues. Side effects (nephrotoxicity, hypertension) reflect calcineurin's roles in non-immune tissues.

Hints:

1. The immunosuppressants target a Ca²⁺/calmodulin-dependent enzyme.
2. The enzyme is a phosphatase (not a kinase).
3. Its substrate is a transcription factor that drives IL-2 expression.

QUESTION 23
Type: misconception-targeted
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A student claims: "Second messengers like cAMP, IP₃, and Ca²⁺ are different molecules but all do the same thing — they amplify the signal." Which correction is most accurate?

Correct answer: Each second messenger activates a _distinct_ set of downstream effectors and produces _different_ cellular responses; they are not interchangeable, and their distinct chemistries determine which target proteins they regulate.

Distractors:

- "The student is correct — second messengers are functionally interchangeable." — Reveals misconception: student denies the specificity of each pathway.
- "All second messengers activate PKA, just at different rates." — Reveals misconception: student treats PKA as the universal effector.
- "Second messengers don't amplify signals — they only relay information." — Reveals misconception: student denies amplification.

Explanation: Second messengers do amplify signals, but they are _not_ interchangeable [Berne ch.3]. Each has its own targets: _cAMP_ activates PKA → phosphorylates serine/threonine residues on a specific set of proteins (and CREB for transcription). _cGMP_ activates PKG → distinct targets including vascular smooth muscle myosin (via myosin light chain phosphatase), targeting vasodilation. _IP₃_ activates the IP₃ receptor on ER → releases Ca²⁺. _DAG_ activates PKC → phosphorylates a different set of targets than PKA. _Ca²⁺_ binds calmodulin → activates CaM kinases, calcineurin, MLCK. _NO_ directly activates soluble guanylyl cyclase (and modifies proteins by S-nitrosylation). Each messenger has its own degradation/inactivation system (PDEs for cyclic nucleotides; Ca²⁺ pumps for Ca²⁺) that terminates its signal. The cell uses different second messengers to deliver functionally specific messages — a single ligand can produce different effects in different cell types depending on which receptor and which second messenger is engaged. Recognising the specificity of each messenger is essential for understanding pharmacology and physiology.

Hints:

1. Different second messengers have different target proteins.
2. cAMP and cGMP look similar but activate different kinases.
3. Specificity at the molecular level explains tissue-specific effects.

QUESTION 24
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A patient's atherosclerotic coronary artery is partially occluded; coronary blood flow is reduced. Nitroglycerin sublingual relieves the angina rapidly. The molecular mechanism involves:

Correct answer: Nitroglycerin is metabolised to release NO; NO diffuses into vascular smooth muscle, activates soluble guanylyl cyclase to produce cGMP, which activates PKG and dephosphorylates myosin light chains, relaxing coronary vessels and improving perfusion.

Distractors:

- "Nitroglycerin directly blocks Ca²⁺ channels, preventing vascular smooth muscle contraction." — Reveals misconception: student conflates nitroglycerin with calcium channel blockers.
- "Nitroglycerin is a β-adrenergic agonist that dilates coronary vessels via αs-cAMP signalling." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong receptor pathway.
- "Nitroglycerin activates GPCRs that release intracellular Ca²⁺, contracting coronary smooth muscle." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the direction (the question asks about vasodilation, not contraction).

Explanation: Nitroglycerin (glyceryl trinitrate) is a _NO donor_ — it is metabolised in the body to release NO [Berne ch.3]. NO diffuses across cell membranes, enters vascular smooth muscle cells, and binds the haem group of _soluble guanylyl cyclase_, activating it. The resulting cGMP rise activates _PKG_, which phosphorylates targets including myosin light chain phosphatase. Activation of phosphatase increases dephosphorylation of myosin light chains, reducing actin-myosin cross-bridge cycling and producing smooth muscle relaxation. The vasodilation in coronary arteries improves myocardial perfusion; the venodilation reduces preload and myocardial oxygen demand. Both effects relieve angina. The same NO-cGMP pathway underlies the body's endogenous regulation of vascular tone (via endothelium-derived NO from NO synthase). PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil) prolong cGMP from this pathway — explaining why nitrates and PDE5 inhibitors must not be co-administered (combined cGMP elevation produces dangerous hypotension). Recognising nitroglycerin as an NO donor is the conceptual bridge between physiological NO signalling and clinical vasodilator pharmacology.

Hints:

1. Nitroglycerin doesn't act on a receptor directly.
2. It releases a small gas molecule that crosses cell membranes.
3. The downstream second messenger is cGMP.

Stem: Many cancer therapies target signal transduction pathways. _Trastuzumab_ (Herceptin) is used in HER2-positive breast cancer; _imatinib_ (Gleevec) is used in chronic myeloid leukaemia. What signalling principle explains why these drugs work?

Correct answer: Both target tyrosine kinase signalling — trastuzumab is a monoclonal antibody that blocks the HER2 receptor (a member of the EGF receptor family, an RTK); imatinib inhibits BCR-ABL, a constitutively active fusion tyrosine kinase. In each case, blocking aberrant tyrosine kinase signalling halts cancer cell proliferation.

Distractors:

- "Both drugs block GPCRs that drive cancer cell growth." — Reveals misconception: student picks wrong receptor class.
- "Both drugs inhibit DNA replication directly, like classical chemotherapy." — Reveals misconception: student treats targeted therapy like cytotoxic chemotherapy.
- "Both drugs are anti-VEGF agents that block tumour angiogenesis." — Reveals misconception: student conflates these targeted therapies with antiangiogenic agents.

Explanation: Many cancers depend on aberrant tyrosine kinase signalling for proliferation and survival, making this pathway a major target for "targeted therapy" [Berne ch.3]. _Trastuzumab_ (Herceptin) is a humanised monoclonal antibody that binds HER2 (ErbB2), an EGF receptor family member that is overexpressed in ~20–25% of breast cancers. By binding HER2's extracellular domain, trastuzumab prevents dimerisation and activation, blocking downstream MAPK and PI3K-Akt signalling, and also recruiting immune-mediated cytotoxicity. _Imatinib_ (Gleevec) is a small-molecule kinase inhibitor that binds the ATP-binding pocket of BCR-ABL, the constitutively active fusion tyrosine kinase produced by the Philadelphia chromosome translocation t(9;22) in chronic myeloid leukaemia. By blocking BCR-ABL kinase activity, imatinib halts the abnormal proliferation that defines CML. The success of these targeted therapies — and the broader class of tyrosine kinase inhibitors (erlotinib, gefitinib, sunitinib, sorafenib) and monoclonal antibodies (cetuximab, bevacizumab, panitumumab) — reflects how thoroughly receptor tyrosine kinase signalling is integrated into cancer biology. The same architectural understanding that drives basic cell physiology drives modern oncology drug development.

Hints:

1. Both drugs target the same family of signalling enzymes.
2. The enzymes phosphorylate tyrosine residues.
3. In cancer, the kinases are aberrantly active — drugs block them.

QUESTION 26
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Insulin resistance in type 2 diabetes mellitus reflects impaired signal transduction at the insulin receptor. Decompose the molecular pathway downstream of insulin binding and identify where the failure most commonly occurs.

Correct answer: Insulin → α subunit binding → β subunit autophosphorylation → IRS-1 phosphorylation → PI3K activation → Akt activation → GLUT4 vesicle translocation to plasma membrane (in muscle and adipose tissue). In insulin resistance, the failure typically occurs at the IRS-1/PI3K step — chronic inflammation and lipid signalling impair IRS-1 phosphorylation and PI3K activation.

Distractors:

- "Insulin → GPCR binding → αs activation → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA → GLUT4 translocation. Failure is at the cAMP step." — Reveals misconception: student treats the insulin receptor as a GPCR.
- "Insulin → ligand-gated ion channel opening → membrane depolarisation → Ca²⁺ entry → glucose uptake. Failure is at the channel." — Reveals misconception: student treats the insulin receptor as a ligand-gated channel.
- "Insulin → nuclear receptor binding → gene transcription → GLUT4 synthesis. Failure is at the gene level." — Reveals misconception: student treats insulin as a steroid-like hormone.

Explanation: The insulin signalling cascade is well characterised [Berne ch.3]. Insulin binds the α subunits of the insulin receptor (an RTK with α₂β₂ architecture). The β subunits autophosphorylate on tyrosine residues. Phosphorylated β subunits then phosphorylate _insulin receptor substrate-1 (IRS-1)_ on tyrosines. Tyrosine-phosphorylated IRS-1 recruits _phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K)_ via SH2 domains. PI3K phosphorylates membrane phospholipids (PIP₂ → PIP₃), which recruits and activates _Akt (also called PKB)_. Akt phosphorylates AS160, which releases GLUT4-containing vesicles from intracellular sequestration; the vesicles fuse with the plasma membrane, increasing glucose uptake into skeletal muscle and adipose tissue. In type 2 diabetes, the dominant failure is at the _IRS-1 / PI3K_ step. Chronic inflammation (TNF-α, IL-6 from adipose tissue) and lipid signalling (DAG, ceramide) activate serine kinases (JNK, IKK, PKC) that _phosphorylate IRS-1 on serine residues_, blocking its tyrosine phosphorylation. With IRS-1 effectively inactivated, PI3K signalling is reduced, GLUT4 translocation is impaired, and glucose uptake is compromised. The MAPK arm of insulin signalling (cell growth, gene expression) is often relatively preserved — explaining why type 2 diabetes is associated with selective insulin resistance and why pharmacological strategies (metformin, thiazolidinediones, GLP-1 agonists) target multiple points in this and parallel pathways.

Hints:

1. Insulin receptor is an RTK — the cascade is α₂β₂ → IRS-1 → PI3K → Akt → GLUT4.
2. The block in insulin resistance is upstream of GLUT4 translocation but downstream of receptor.
3. Inflammatory serine kinases interfere with IRS-1 tyrosine phosphorylation.
