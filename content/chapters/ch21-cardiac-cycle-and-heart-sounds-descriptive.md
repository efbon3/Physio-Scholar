QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Trace the events of one cardiac cycle from atrial systole through diastole.

Model answer: Cycle (~ 0.8 s at 75 bpm): (1) Atrial systole - P wave; atrial contraction adds ~ 20% to ventricular filling; (2) Isovolumetric contraction - QRS; ventricular pressure rises rapidly; AV valves close (S1: M1+T1); aortic/pulmonary valves still closed; no volume change; (3) Rapid ejection - aortic/pulmonary valves open when ventricular > arterial pressure; ~ 70% of stroke volume; (4) Reduced ejection - T wave begins; flow slows; (5) Isovolumetric relaxation - aortic valve closes (S2: A2+P2); ventricular pressure drops; AV valves still closed; (6) Rapid filling - AV valves open; ~ 70% of filling; possible S3 (normal in young, abnormal in adults - HF); (7) Diastasis - slow filling; (8) Atrial systole - cycle repeats. ECG-pressure-volume coupling: PV loop. Wiggers diagram visualises pressure, volume, ECG, sounds together [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Hints:

1. 8 phases over ~0.8 s at 75 bpm.
2. S1 = AV closure; S2 = aortic/pulmonary closure.
3. Atrial kick adds ~20% filling.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the heart sounds - S1, S2, S3, S4 - and common murmurs.

Model answer: S1: AV valve closure (M1 then T1; loud in mitral stenosis); S2: aortic/pulmonary closure (A2 then P2; physiological splitting on inspiration; widely split in RBBB, ASD; paradoxical in LBBB; loud A2 in HTN); S3: rapid filling - normal in young/athletes, abnormal in adults (HF, MR, TR); 'Kentucky'; S4: atrial kick into stiff ventricle - HTN, AS, HCM, ischaemia; 'Tennessee'. Murmurs - timing (systolic vs diastolic), location, radiation, intensity (1-6), pitch, dynamic manoeuvres. Systolic: AS (ESM crescendo-decrescendo, radiates carotids), MR (pansystolic, radiates axilla), MVP (mid-late systolic click + murmur), VSD (pansystolic, harsh, lower sternal border), HOCM (LV outflow ESM, ↑ Valsalva, ↓ squat - opposite of AS). Diastolic: AR (early decrescendo blowing, sitting forward, expiration), MS (mid-late diastolic rumble + opening snap, lateral position, expiration), TR (pansystolic, ↑ inspiration - Carvallo). Continuous: PDA (machinery murmur). Innocent murmurs in children/pregnancy [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

1. S1 AV; S2 semilunar; S3 normal young/HF adults; S4 stiff ventricle.
2. Murmur by timing, location, radiation, manoeuvres.
3. AS vs HOCM: opposite Valsalva response.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe the pressure-volume loop and how it's modified in disease.

Model answer: PV loop: x-axis volume, y-axis pressure. Trace one cycle: (1) Mitral closes at LVEDV (begin isovolumetric contraction); (2) Pressure rises at constant volume; (3) Aortic opens (begin ejection); (4) Pressure rises then falls; volume falls to LVESV; (5) Aortic closes (begin isovolumetric relaxation); (6) Pressure falls at constant volume; (7) Mitral opens (begin filling); (8) Volume rises along EDPVR. Stroke volume = LVEDV - LVESV. Area within loop = stroke work. Slope of ESPVR = end-systolic pressure-volume relationship = contractility. EDPVR = end-diastolic pressure-volume relationship = compliance (steeper = stiffer). Modifications: ↑ Preload (right shift, larger SV - Frank-Starling); ↑ Afterload (taller loop, smaller SV); ↑ Contractility (loop shifts left, larger SV, steeper ESPVR slope); HFrEF (depressed ESPVR slope, dilated, low EF); HFpEF (steep EDPVR, smaller volumes, normal EF); AS (high pressure, normal-low volume); MR (large volume, low pressure); AR (large volume + variable pressure depending on chronicity) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe Frank-Starling law and its physiological significance.

Model answer: _Frank-Starling_: stroke volume increases with end-diastolic volume up to a plateau (then declines in pathology). Mechanism: greater myocyte stretch → optimal actin-myosin overlap + increased Ca²⁺ sensitivity of troponin C (length-dependent activation). Allows: matching of CO between L and R ventricles (acute mismatch quickly corrects); response to changes in venous return; integration with autonomic and humoral inputs. Curve shifts: ↑ contractility (sympathetic, drugs) shifts up; ↓ contractility (HFrEF, drugs) shifts down. Clinical: preload optimisation in HF, septic shock; ICU monitoring of fluid responsiveness (passive leg raise, stroke volume variation). Pathological loss of Frank-Starling - severe HF, cardiogenic shock - the 'descending limb' [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Hints:

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe cardiac output - determinants, measurement, and regulation.

Model answer: CO = SV × HR (typically 5 L/min at rest, ~ 25 L/min vigorous exercise). SV determinants: preload, afterload, contractility. HR controlled by autonomic balance. Measurement: thermodilution (PA catheter - gold standard but invasive, less used now), Fick principle (O2 consumption / arterio-venous O2 difference), echocardiography (LVOT VTI), bioimpedance, oesophageal Doppler, pulse contour analysis. CI (cardiac index) = CO/BSA. Regulation: short-term - autonomic (β1 ↑ HR + contractility), Frank-Starling (preload-driven); medium-term - RAAS, ADH, baroreflex; long-term - hypertrophic remodelling, RAAS-mediated. Disease: low CO - HF, hypovolaemia, obstruction; high CO - sepsis, anaemia, hyperthyroidism, AV fistula, beriberi, Paget. CO measurement guides ICU management of shock and HF. Modern minimally invasive monitoring increasingly replaces PA catheter [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe cardiac mechanics in pregnancy and exercise.

Model answer: _Pregnancy_: CO ↑ 30-50% (peaks 2nd trimester); HR ↑ 10-20%; SV ↑ 30%; plasma volume ↑ 50%, RBC mass ↑ 30% (dilutional anaemia); SVR ↓ 30% (progesterone, NO, prostaglandins); diastolic BP often ↓ early-mid then up; supine hypotensive syndrome (aortocaval compression - lateral position); hypercoagulable (DVT/PE risk). Cardiomegaly + S3 + ESM physiological. Pre-eclampsia, peripartum CM, gestational HTN/DM common complications. ACE-I/ARB/DOAC contraindicated. _Exercise_: dynamic (running, cycling) vs static (weight). Dynamic: HR ↑ (vagal withdrawal initial then sympathetic), SV ↑ (Frank-Starling + contractility), CO ↑ to 25 L/min; SVR ↓ (vasodilation working muscle); SBP ↑ modest, DBP stable/down, PP widens; venous return ↑ via muscle/respiratory pump. Static: SVR ↑ (compression), MAP ↑ markedly, modest CO change. Limited by O2 delivery (HFrEF, anaemia) or extraction (mitochondrial). Heart rate recovery is prognostic marker. Cardiac rehabilitation improves outcomes. Athlete heart vs HCM distinction can be challenging [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Hints:

1. Pregnancy: ↑ CO 30-50%, ↑ plasma vol, ↓ SVR.
2. Exercise: dynamic CO ↑ 5x with SVR ↓ in muscle.
3. Athlete heart vs HCM.

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe cardiac PV loops in valvular heart disease.

Model answer: _Aortic stenosis (AS)_: ↑ afterload → tall loop, normal-small volumes; concentric hypertrophy → steep EDPVR; LV failure late. _Aortic regurgitation (AR)_: chronic - large LVEDV (eccentric hypertrophy) + ↑ SV + low diastolic pressure; loop wider; eventually LV dysfunction. Acute AR (endocarditis, dissection) - small loop with very high LVEDP - emergency surgery. _Mitral stenosis (MS)_: ↑ LA pressure; LV underfilled (small EDV, normal contractility unless RV failure); pulmonary HTN; AF complication. _Mitral regurgitation (MR)_: chronic - large LV (volume overload), low afterload (low aortic pressure), large SV but reduced forward CO; LA dilation → AF. Acute MR (papillary muscle rupture, chord rupture, endocarditis) - sudden pulmonary oedema, no compensation - emergency. _Tricuspid regurgitation (TR)_: RV volume overload; raised JVP; hepatic congestion; TR usually secondary (functional from RV/LV dilatation, pulmonary HTN). _Pulmonary stenosis (PS)_: rare adult; congenital; RV pressure overload; treat balloon valvuloplasty [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Hints:

1. AS: ↑ afterload, concentric hypertrophy.
2. AR/MR: volume overload, eccentric hypertrophy.
3. Acute valve lesions = emergency.

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 60-year-old with severe sudden chest pain, dyspnoea, S3, JVP raised, basal crackles. ECG sinus tach with non-specific changes. Decompose acute heart failure with cardiogenic shock.

Model answer: _Cardiogenic shock_ - inadequate perfusion from cardiac dysfunction. Most common cause: acute MI with extensive LV damage (~ 50%); also acute valve lesions (papillary muscle rupture, chord rupture, endocarditis), VSD post-MI, free wall rupture/tamponade, fulminant myocarditis, tachyarrhythmia, severe aortic dissection involving coronaries, large PE. Clinical: hypotension (SBP < 90 sustained or vasopressors), end-organ hypoperfusion (oliguria, altered mental state, lactic acidosis, cool clammy skin), pulmonary congestion (cardiogenic pulmonary oedema). Echo: assess EF, RV function, valves, pericardium, mechanical complications. Investigations: ECG, troponin, BNP, ABG, lactate, CXR, echo, coronary angiography. Management: (1) Reperfusion if MI - urgent PCI (mortality benefit SHOCK trial); (2) Vasopressor (noradrenaline first-line per SOAP-II); (3) Inotrope (dobutamine, milrinone) if needed; (4) Mechanical support - IABP (limited evidence post-IABP-SHOCK II), Impella (LV unloading, controversial mortality benefit - DanGer Shock 2024 positive in selected), VA-ECMO (selected centres); (5) Treat mechanical cause - emergency surgery (papillary muscle rupture, VSD, free wall); (6) Supportive (NIV, intubation, fluids cautiously, treat arrhythmia). Mortality high (~ 40-50%). Cardiogenic shock teams with structured protocols improve outcomes [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Stem: A 50-year-old with new-onset diastolic murmur and worsening dyspnoea. Decompose acute aortic regurgitation.

QUESTION 13
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Discuss the relationship between the systolic and diastolic dysfunction in heart failure.

Model answer: HFrEF (EF ≤ 40%) primarily systolic; HFpEF (EF ≥ 50%) primarily diastolic; HFmrEF (41-49%) intermediate. Both have neurohormonal activation (RAAS, sympathetic, ADH) → fluid retention, vasoconstriction, remodelling. _Systolic dysfunction_: contractile failure → ↓ SV → ↓ CO → compensation by Frank-Starling (initially helpful), neurohormonal (helpful then harmful), ventricular remodelling (eccentric hypertrophy → dilated chambers, fibrosis, wall thinning); EF reduced. _Diastolic dysfunction_: stiff ventricle → impaired relaxation → ↑ filling pressures → pulmonary congestion + poor exercise tolerance; LVH common (concentric); EF preserved but stroke volume may be reduced; usually multiple comorbidities (HTN, DM, obesity, age, AF, sleep apnoea). Most HFrEF also have diastolic dysfunction. HFpEF mechanisms: titin phosphorylation, AGEs, fibrosis, microvascular dysfunction, systemic inflammation. Treatment paradigms differ: HFrEF - GDMT 4 pillars (ACE-I/ARNI + β-blocker + MRA + SGLT2i); HFpEF - SGLT2i (DELIVER, EMPEROR-Preserved), comorbidity management, GLP-1 agonist for obesity (STEP-HFpEF), ARNI (selected). Both - lifestyle, diuretics for symptoms, exercise rehab. Modern HF care recognises overlap and treats both [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

QUESTION 14
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Discuss device therapy in heart failure - CRT, ICD, LVAD, transplant.

Model answer: _CRT (cardiac resynchronisation therapy)_: biventricular pacing (RV + LV via coronary sinus); resynchronises wide QRS LBBB hearts → improved EF, symptoms, mortality. Indications: EF ≤ 35% + LBBB + QRS ≥ 130 + NYHA II-IV (Class I); narrower benefit in non-LBBB, narrower QRS. CRT-D combines defibrillation function. ~ 30% non-responders. _ICD (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator)_: detects and treats VT/VF. Primary prevention: EF ≤ 35% NYHA II-III (post-MI ≥ 40 days; NICM after ≥ 3 months GDMT). Secondary prevention: SCA survivors. Complications: lead-related (infection, fracture), inappropriate shocks (psychological burden), pneumothorax. Subcutaneous ICD (no transvenous lead) for selected (no pacing/ATP need). _LVAD (left ventricular assist device)_: continuous-flow centrifugal pump (HeartMate 3). Bridge to transplant or destination therapy (refused/ineligible for transplant). Improves survival, function. Complications: pump thrombosis, GI bleeding (acquired vWD from shear, AVMs), driveline infection, stroke, RV failure. Anticoagulation needed. _Transplant_: gold standard for end-stage HF in selected (age, comorbidities, support). Median survival ~ 12 years post-transplant. Limitations: donor scarcity, immunosuppression complications (infection, malignancy, CKD, rejection), CAV (allograft vasculopathy). Living with HF requires multi-disciplinary care; modern devices and pharmacology have transformed prognosis [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Stem: Synthesise this chapter: how do cardiac mechanics integrate to produce blood flow under varying conditions?

Model answer: Cardiac mechanics integrate cellular ECC, tissue mechanics, and chamber dynamics: (1) _Cellular_ - CICR couples electrical to mechanical; β-adrenergic modulation via PKA; (2) _Tissue_ - syncytium via gap junctions; long plateau prevents tetanus; (3) _Chamber_ - PV loop framework; preload (Frank-Starling), afterload (impedance), contractility (ESPVR slope), compliance (EDPVR slope); (4) _Cycle_ - 8 phases timed by valves and pressures; sounds, pressures, volumes coordinated; (5) _Systemic integration_ - autonomic, RAAS, baroreflex, exercise pressor reflex match cardiac output to demand. Disease patterns: HFrEF (systolic failure - eccentric remodelling, neurohormonal cascade); HFpEF (diastolic failure - stiff LV, comorbidities); valve disease (pressure or volume overload); cardiomyopathies (genetic, infiltrative, ischaemic); arrhythmias (electrical pathology with mechanical consequences). Therapeutic interventions: drugs (ACE-I/ARNI/MRA/β-blocker/SGLT2i, diuretics, vasodilators, inotropes), devices (CRT/ICD/LVAD), procedures (PCI, valve repair/replacement, transplant), lifestyle. Modern CV medicine integrates all - and continues to evolve (SGLT2i, tafamidis, CAR-T for amyloidosis emerging). The cardiac mechanical system illustrates how cellular biochemistry scales up to organ-level function and clinical phenotypes [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].
