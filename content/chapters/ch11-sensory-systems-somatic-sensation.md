## Pass 1 — Sensory receptors, transduction, and adaptation

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The five basic types of sensory receptor classified by adequate stimulus modality are:

Correct answer: Mechanoreceptors (mechanical deformation), thermoreceptors (temperature), nociceptors (tissue damage), electromagnetic receptors (light), and chemoreceptors (taste, smell, blood gases, osmolality) — Müller's "law of specific nerve energies" predicts that the modality of perception is determined by the receptor type stimulated, not by the nature of the stimulus.

Distractors:

- "Visual, auditory, gustatory, olfactory, vestibular only." — Reveals misconception: student lists the special senses as if they exhausted sensory classification. Correction: the special senses are a subset; the principled global classification uses adequate stimulus modality and includes the somatic mechanoreceptor, thermoreceptor, and nociceptor classes.
- "Cutaneous, visceral, special, deep, superficial only." — Reveals misconception: student gives an anatomical/locational classification rather than a stimulus-based one. Correction: cutaneous/visceral/deep are useful descriptors of _location_, but sensory receptors are classified by what they detect, not by where they sit.
- "Cold, warm, pain, touch, vibration only." — Reveals misconception: student conflates conscious sensations with receptor classes. Correction: cold/warm/touch/vibration are perceptual qualities; the underlying receptors are mechanoreceptors and thermoreceptors, plus nociceptors and chemoreceptors and electromagnetic receptors.

Explanation: The five-class scheme is fundamental [Guyton & Hall ch.47–48; Costanzo ch.3]. Each receptor type is highly sensitive to its own modality (the law of specific nerve energies — Müller); the same nerve, however stimulated (mechanically, electrically, chemically), produces the sensation associated with its modality of origin (pressing on the eye produces "phosphenes" because optic afferents signal "light" regardless of the mechanical stimulus). Within these classes, sub-types exist: cutaneous mechanoreceptors (Meissner, Pacinian, Merkel, Ruffini), proprioceptive mechanoreceptors (muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs), thermoreceptors (cold, warm), nociceptors (mechanical, thermal, polymodal, silent), and chemoreceptors of taste, smell, and the carotid/aortic bodies.

Hints:

1. The classification axis is "what kind of energy or chemistry does the receptor detect?".
2. Five classes cover mechanical, thermal, damage, electromagnetic, and chemical stimuli.
3. Vision sits in the electromagnetic class.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The receptor potential (generator potential) at a sensory ending is best described as:

Correct answer: A graded, local depolarisation produced by stimulus-induced opening of receptor cation channels, with amplitude proportional to stimulus intensity (often logarithmically) — if it reaches threshold at the first node of Ranvier or at a trigger zone, it generates trains of action potentials whose frequency encodes intensity.

Distractors:

- "An all-or-none action potential at the receptor terminal." — Reveals misconception: student conflates a graded analogue signal with the digital action potential. Correction: the receptor potential is graded and local, not all-or-none; action potentials are produced _by_ it once threshold is reached.
- "A constant voltage independent of stimulus." — Reveals misconception: student denies stimulus encoding at the receptor. Correction: receptor potential amplitude scales with stimulus strength — that is precisely how the analogue stimulus is converted into a meaningful electrical signal.
- "A hyperpolarising response to all stimuli." — Reveals misconception: student picks the photoreceptor pattern as universal. Correction: only photoreceptors hyperpolarise to their adequate stimulus; somatic mechanoreceptors and thermoreceptors depolarise to theirs.

Explanation: Sensory transduction converts the stimulus into an electrical signal — the receptor (or generator) potential [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15; Berne & Levy ch.7]. It is graded (analogue), localised to the receptor terminal, and proportional to stimulus magnitude. Threshold is reached at the first node of Ranvier in afferent fibres; once crossed, action potentials of constant amplitude are fired, with frequency encoding stimulus intensity. The relationship between stimulus and receptor potential commonly follows a logarithmic or power-law function (Weber–Fechner / Stevens' power law), giving sensory systems a wide dynamic range. Photoreceptors are the famous exception, hyperpolarising to light because cGMP-gated channels close.

Hints:

1. The receptor end signals magnitude with a graded change, not an action potential.
2. The downstream axon converts this analogue signal into action-potential frequency.
3. The relationship between stimulus and percept is often logarithmic.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Sensory adaptation refers to:

Correct answer: The progressive decrease in firing frequency (or in receptor-potential amplitude) during sustained constant-intensity stimulation, allowing sensory systems to detect _changes_ in stimuli rather than steady backgrounds.

Distractors:

- "The increase in receptor sensitivity over time." — Reveals misconception: student inverts the direction of adaptation. Correction: adaptation generally _decreases_ responsiveness during sustained stimulation; sensitisation (the opposite phenomenon) occurs in inflammation and pain pathways.
- "The change in receptor type with age." — Reveals misconception: student picks a developmental change rather than a millisecond-to-second sensory phenomenon. Correction: adaptation is a moment-to-moment functional change, not an anatomical age-related change.
- "Movement of receptors to a new location." — Reveals misconception: student literalises the word "adaptation". Correction: receptors do not migrate; adaptation is functional decrement, not anatomical relocation.

Explanation: With sustained constant stimulation, most receptors progressively reduce their firing rate [Guyton & Hall ch.47; Boron & Boulpaep ch.15]. Mechanisms include receptor-potential decay through accommodation by accessory structures (e.g., fluid displacement in the lamellated capsule of a Pacinian corpuscle), Na⁺ channel inactivation, Ca²⁺-activated K⁺ currents reducing excitability, and synaptic depression downstream. Functionally, adaptation lets the sensory system detect _change_ — the smell of perfume fades after entering a room; clothes are not constantly perceived; a steady pressure on the skin disappears from awareness within seconds. Without adaptation the brain would be flooded by background information.

Hints:

1. Most receptors fire less when a stimulus does not change.
2. The behavioural consequence is that we notice change more than steady states.
3. The mechanism may live in the accessory capsule, the membrane, or downstream synapses.

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Phasic (rapidly adapting) cutaneous mechanoreceptors that fire mainly at stimulus onset and offset, and that mediate detection of vibration and motion, are exemplified by:

Correct answer: Pacinian corpuscles (high-frequency vibration ~100–400 Hz, deep pressure changes) and Meissner corpuscles (low-frequency vibration ~30–50 Hz, flutter, slip detection during object manipulation).

Distractors:

- "Pain receptors and joint capsule receptors." — Reveals misconception: student treats nociceptors and joint receptors as the prototypical phasic class. Correction: nociceptors are predominantly slowly adapting; the canonical phasic cutaneous receptors are Pacinian and Meissner corpuscles.
- "Muscle spindle group Ia fibres only." — Reveals misconception: student picks a proprioceptive afferent rather than cutaneous mechanoreceptors. Correction: Ia primary endings have a dynamic component but they are _muscle_ afferents; the cutaneous phasic receptors named in the stem are Pacinian and Meissner.
- "Merkel discs." — Reveals misconception: student picks a slowly adapting receptor as if it were phasic. Correction: Merkel discs are slowly adapting (SA1) — they encode sustained pressure, edges, and texture, not transient changes.

Explanation: Phasic (rapidly adapting) receptors signal stimulus _change_ — onset, offset, motion, vibration — while tonic (slowly adapting) receptors track _sustained_ magnitude [Berne & Levy ch.7–8; Guyton & Hall ch.48]. Pacinian corpuscles (deep dermis and subcutaneous tissue) and Meissner corpuscles (dermal papillae of glabrous skin) are the canonical phasic types. Tonic receptors include Merkel discs (SA1; sustained pressure, edges, texture), Ruffini endings (SA2; skin stretch and joint position), muscle spindle static endings, and nociceptors. The phasic/tonic distinction has clinical relevance: vibration sense is selectively lost in dorsal-column lesions and large-fibre neuropathies, and is tested at the bedside with a 128 Hz tuning fork to interrogate the Pacinian/Aβ pathway.

Hints:

1. Phasic receptors signal change; tonic receptors signal magnitude.
2. The two best-known phasic cutaneous receptors live at very different depths in the skin.
3. Vibration sense at the fingertip with a tuning fork tests one of them.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The cutaneous mechanoreceptor that lies deep in the dermis and subcutaneous tissue, has an onion-like layered (lamellated) capsule, and detects rapid vibration (~100–400 Hz) is the:

Correct answer: Pacinian corpuscle — the largest mechanoreceptor (~1 mm), with concentric lamellar capsules around a single afferent nerve ending; the capsule mechanically high-pass filters the stimulus, transmitting only fast pressure changes to the membrane.

Distractors:

- "Meissner corpuscle." — Reveals misconception: student picks the wrong rapidly adapting receptor. Correction: Meissner corpuscles sit in dermal papillae and tune to lower-frequency flutter (~30–50 Hz); the deep, lamellated, high-frequency vibration receptor is the Pacinian corpuscle.
- "Merkel disc." — Reveals misconception: student picks a slowly adapting tonic receptor. Correction: Merkel discs encode sustained pressure and edges, not high-frequency vibration; they are not lamellated.
- "Ruffini ending." — Reveals misconception: student picks the dermal stretch receptor. Correction: Ruffini endings are slowly adapting and respond to skin stretch and joint position, not to high-frequency vibration.

Explanation: The Pacinian corpuscle is the largest mechanoreceptor (~1 mm) and has an onion-like lamellar capsule around a single afferent ending [Berne & Levy ch.8; Guyton & Hall ch.48]. The capsule slides on its axis when sustained pressure is applied, dissipating low-frequency stimuli and transmitting only fast pressure changes — a structural high-pass filter. Innervation is by large myelinated Aβ fibres (~12 µm, ~70 m/s). Removing the lamellar capsule experimentally turns the bare nerve ending into a tonic receptor — proving that the capsule itself is responsible for adaptation. The Pacinian mediates vibration sense (especially high-frequency, e.g., a 256 Hz tuning fork) and detects accelerations during object manipulation.

Hints:

1. The receptor lives deep, not in the dermal papilla.
2. Its accessory capsule is lamellated like an onion.
3. The capsule's mechanical filtering is the reason adaptation is so rapid.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): A
Priority (M / S / G): G

Stem: The cutaneous mechanoreceptor most densely concentrated in the fingertips and lips, mediating fine flutter and slip detection at low frequencies (~30–50 Hz), is the:

Correct answer: Meissner corpuscle — encapsulated rapidly adapting (RA1) receptors in the dermal papillae of glabrous skin, critical for fine tactile discrimination and for the slip-detection signals that adjust grip force during object manipulation.

Distractors:

- "Pacinian corpuscle." — Reveals misconception: student picks the deep high-frequency receptor for a low-frequency, superficial role. Correction: Pacinian corpuscles tune to ~100–400 Hz and live deep; the receptor in the dermal papilla mediating fine flutter is the Meissner corpuscle.
- "Merkel disc." — Reveals misconception: student picks a slowly adapting tonic receptor. Correction: Merkel discs encode sustained pressure and edges; flutter and slip detection require a rapidly adapting receptor — the Meissner.
- "Ruffini ending." — Reveals misconception: student picks the deeper dermal stretch receptor. Correction: Ruffini endings are slowly adapting and signal skin stretch and joint position, not low-frequency flutter at the fingertip.

Explanation: Meissner corpuscles are encapsulated mechanoreceptors in dermal papillae of glabrous (hairless) skin, most numerous in fingertips, lips, soles, and palms [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15; Berne & Levy ch.8]. They are rapidly adapting (RA1 type) and best at low-frequency flutter. Critical for fine tactile discrimination during object manipulation — they detect micro-slips that signal grip-force adjustment. Density is ~25/mm² in the index fingertip, falling to <5/mm² on the palm and arm. Their loss with ageing partly explains the decline in tactile discrimination.

Hints:

1. The receptor lies superficially in the dermal papilla, not deep in the dermis.
2. It is most densely packed where we manipulate the world — fingertips, lips, soles.
3. Its job is to detect the brief flutters of micro-slip on a held object.

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The slowly adapting cutaneous mechanoreceptor that encodes sustained pressure, edges, and surface texture, with high spatial resolution suited for two-point discrimination, is the:

Correct answer: Merkel disc (Merkel cell–neurite complex) — slowly adapting (SA1) receptors in the basal epidermis with small receptive fields (~2–5 mm), giving the highest spatial resolution among cutaneous mechanoreceptors and dominating two-point discrimination at the fingertip.

Distractors:

- "Pacinian corpuscle." — Reveals misconception: student picks a deep rapidly adapting receptor for a fine spatial-discrimination role. Correction: Pacinian corpuscles have very large receptive fields and signal vibration; the high-resolution, slowly adapting receptor is the Merkel disc.
- "Meissner corpuscle." — Reveals misconception: student picks a rapidly adapting receptor for a sustained-pressure role. Correction: Meissner corpuscles signal flutter and slip detection, not sustained pressure or texture.
- "Ruffini ending." — Reveals misconception: student picks a deeper dermal stretch receptor. Correction: Ruffini endings are slowly adapting (SA2) but have larger receptive fields; their job is skin stretch and joint position, not fine spatial discrimination.

Explanation: Merkel cells in the basal epidermis form synaptic-like contacts with sensory afferent terminals, producing slowly adapting (SA1) responses [Guyton & Hall ch.48; Berne & Levy ch.8]. Their small receptive fields (~2–5 mm) — the smallest among cutaneous mechanoreceptors — give the highest spatial resolution and underpin braille reading, texture discrimination, edge detection, and sustained-pressure perception. Two-point discrimination is dominated by Merkel-disc spatial resolution: ~2 mm at the fingertip versus ~50 mm on the back — the basis of the cortical magnification factor seen in the somatosensory homunculus.

Hints:

1. The receptor is in the basal epidermis and "talks" to its afferent through Merkel cells.
2. It is slowly adapting and encodes magnitude, not change.
3. It dominates the fingertip's astonishingly small two-point threshold.

Stem: The slowly adapting mechanoreceptor in the dermis, with fusiform endings parallel to skin lines, sensitive to skin stretch and to joint position, is the:

Correct answer: Ruffini ending (SA2) — deep dermal and joint-capsule receptors with larger receptive fields than Merkel discs, signalling skin stretch and contributing to kinaesthetic (joint-position) sense.

Distractors:

- "Pacinian corpuscle." — Reveals misconception: student picks a deep rapidly adapting receptor. Correction: Pacinian corpuscles signal high-frequency vibration, not steady stretch or joint position.
- "Meissner corpuscle." — Reveals misconception: student picks a superficial rapidly adapting receptor. Correction: Meissner corpuscles sit in dermal papillae and signal flutter, not skin stretch.
- "Merkel disc." — Reveals misconception: student picks the small-receptive-field SA1 receptor. Correction: Merkel discs are slowly adapting too, but they have small receptive fields and encode pressure and texture; Ruffini endings signal stretch and joint position over larger receptive fields.

Explanation: Ruffini endings (SA2 type) lie deep in the dermis and in joint capsules [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15]. They have larger receptive fields than Merkel discs and respond to skin stretch — informing the brain about hand shape, finger position, and joint angle when combined with proprioceptive input from spindles and Golgi tendon organs. They contribute to the kinaesthetic sense ("position sense"). Less prominently studied than the other three cutaneous mechanoreceptor classes, they are nonetheless functionally important for shaping hand posture during manipulation and for joint-position sense.

Hints:

1. The receptor is slowly adapting, but unlike Merkel discs it sits deeper and has larger receptive fields.
2. The stimulus that activates it best is skin stretch.
3. It contributes to sensing where joints are without looking.

QUESTION 9
Type: application
Bloom's level: analyse
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Two-point discrimination — the minimum separation at which two simultaneous touches are perceived as distinct — is dominated by:

Correct answer: The combined density of Merkel and Meissner units in the skin region, the size of their receptive fields, and the cortical magnification of that body part in the primary somatosensory cortex; thresholds are ~2 mm at the fingertip but ~50 mm on the back.

Distractors:

- "Pacinian corpuscle density alone." — Reveals misconception: student picks a deep, large-receptive-field vibration receptor for a spatial discrimination task. Correction: Pacinian corpuscles have large receptive fields and contribute little to fine spatial resolution; superficial Merkel and Meissner units dominate.
- "Skin thickness only." — Reveals misconception: student picks a passive structural property. Correction: skin thickness is at most a minor influence; the dominant determinants are receptor density, receptive-field size, and cortical magnification.
- "Pain receptor density." — Reveals misconception: student conflates nociceptive and discriminative touch pathways. Correction: pain receptors are not used for two-point discrimination; the test interrogates the DCML / Aβ pathway through Merkel and Meissner afferents.

Explanation: Two-point discrimination thresholds vary across the body — ~2 mm at the fingertip, ~5 mm at the lips, ~10 mm on the palm, ~30–40 mm on the arm and forearm, and ~50–70 mm on the back [Guyton & Hall ch.48; Berne & Levy ch.8]. Three factors determine these thresholds: mechanoreceptor density (Merkel and Meissner units, both with small receptive fields), the size of the receptive fields themselves, and cortical magnification — the disproportionately large area of S1 cortex devoted to fingertips, lips, and tongue (the Penfield homunculus). Loss of two-point discrimination with normal pain and temperature sensation suggests dorsal column–medial lemniscus pathway disease.

Hints:

1. Two-point discrimination depends on whether two touches activate _different_ receptor populations.
2. Receptors with smaller receptive fields can resolve closer points.
3. Cortical area per square centimetre of skin tracks discrimination ability.

QUESTION 10
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The muscle spindle is a sensory organ that detects:

Correct answer: Muscle length and the rate of length change — primary endings (group Ia afferents) signal both static length and dynamic stretch, while secondary endings (group II) signal mainly static length; gamma motor neurons innervate the polar contractile ends of intrafusal fibres and let the CNS set spindle sensitivity independently of muscle length.

Distractors:

- "Muscle force (tension)." — Reveals misconception: student conflates spindles with Golgi tendon organs. Correction: tension is the variable signalled by Golgi tendon organs (Ib afferents); spindles signal length.
- "Muscle temperature." — Reveals misconception: student imagines a thermal role for spindles. Correction: spindles do not transduce temperature; thermal sensation comes from cutaneous thermoreceptors with TRP-channel sensors.
- "Tendon stress." — Reveals misconception: student again conflates spindles with GTOs. Correction: tendon stress maps onto the GTO output; spindles, being in parallel with extrafusal fibres, sense length, not series-tension.

Explanation: Muscle spindles are encapsulated structures of intrafusal muscle fibres (nuclear bag and nuclear chain types) lying in parallel with extrafusal muscle [Costanzo ch.3; Berne & Levy ch.8; Guyton & Hall ch.55]. When extrafusal muscle stretches, the spindle stretches too. Group Ia afferents (large, fast Aα) wrap the central regions ("annulospiral endings") and respond to both static and dynamic length; group II afferents (smaller Aβ, "flower-spray endings") respond mainly to static length. Gamma motor neurons innervate the polar (contractile) ends of intrafusal fibres, and alpha–gamma coactivation keeps spindle sensitivity matched to changing muscle length during voluntary movement. Spindles drive the **monosynaptic stretch reflex** — Ia afferent → α motor neuron of the same muscle → contraction — exemplified by the patellar reflex.

Hints:

1. The variable detected lives along the long axis of the muscle.
2. The two afferent groups (Ia and II) divide labour between dynamic and static length.
3. The CNS adjusts spindle sensitivity through γ motor neurons.

QUESTION 11
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: The Golgi tendon organ is a proprioceptor that detects:

Correct answer: Muscle tension — signalled by group Ib afferents responding to active tension developed across the tendon when the muscle contracts; in series with the muscle fibres at the musculotendinous junction.

Distractors:

- "Muscle length." — Reveals misconception: student conflates GTOs with muscle spindles. Correction: length is signalled by spindles (Ia and II); GTOs signal tension via Ib afferents.
- "Joint angle." — Reveals misconception: student picks a kinaesthetic variable. Correction: joint angle is signalled by joint receptors and Ruffini endings combined with spindle and cutaneous input; GTOs measure tendon tension.
- "Skin stretch." — Reveals misconception: student attributes a Ruffini-like role to GTOs. Correction: skin stretch is sensed by Ruffini endings; GTOs are within the tendon at the musculotendinous junction.

Explanation: GTOs lie at musculotendinous junctions in _series_ with muscle fibres [Costanzo ch.3; Berne & Levy ch.8]. Active muscle contraction stretches the tendon collagen interlocking with the GTO afferent terminal, generating Ib firing proportional to tension. The Ib afferent makes inhibitory connections (via spinal interneurons) on the parent α motor neuron — the **inverse stretch reflex** (autogenic inhibition). When tension becomes excessive, this reflex relaxes the muscle, protecting against tendon avulsion. The "clasp-knife" phenomenon in spasticity engages this circuit. GTOs are tonic sensors — their firing tracks tension throughout sustained contraction.

Hints:

1. The receptor sits at the musculotendinous junction, in series with the muscle fibres.
2. The reflex it drives is _inhibitory_, opposite in sign to the stretch reflex.
3. Its protective function is to prevent excessive tension on the tendon.

Stem: Conscious proprioception (joint position and movement sense) reaches the cerebral cortex via the:

Correct answer: Dorsal column–medial lemniscus (DCML) pathway, which carries fine touch, vibration, two-point discrimination, and conscious proprioception; unconscious proprioception used by the cerebellum ascends separately in the spinocerebellar tracts.

Distractors:

- "Anterolateral system." — Reveals misconception: student conflates fine and crude pathways. Correction: the anterolateral (spinothalamic) system carries pain, temperature, and crude touch — not conscious proprioception.
- "Spinocerebellar tracts." — Reveals misconception: student picks the unconscious proprioceptive pathway. Correction: spinocerebellar tracts deliver proprioceptive input to the cerebellum but never reach consciousness; conscious sensing uses the DCML.
- "Reticular formation." — Reveals misconception: student picks an arousal/modulation network as a primary sensory pathway. Correction: the reticular formation receives spinoreticular input and contributes to arousal and pain modulation, but conscious proprioception is a DCML function.

Explanation: The DCML pathway carries conscious proprioception, fine touch, vibration, and two-point discrimination to the cortex [Guyton & Hall ch.48; Costanzo ch.3]. _Unconscious_ proprioception (used by the cerebellum to coordinate movement, never reaching consciousness) ascends in the spinocerebellar tracts. The dual-pathway design separates conscious awareness of body position from the cerebellum's moment-to-moment use of position information — a beautiful example of physiological division of labour. Loss of DCML produces sensory ataxia with a positive Romberg sign because the patient can no longer tell where the limbs are without visual confirmation.

Hints:

1. Conscious sensation must reach the thalamus and cortex.
2. The cerebellum gets its own proprioceptive feed; it does not need to ask consciousness.
3. The conscious feed is the same one that carries vibration and fine touch.

Stem: The conduction velocity of the largest myelinated Aα fibres carrying proprioceptive information from muscle spindle Ia primaries and from Golgi tendon organ Ib afferents is approximately:

Hints:

1. Aα is the largest, most heavily myelinated peripheral axon class.
2. Its job is to drive reflex circuits that protect posture in real time.
3. Its conduction velocity is at the top end of the peripheral range.

QUESTION 14
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Stimulus intensity is encoded in afferent fibre firing through:

Correct answer: Frequency of action potentials in single afferent fibres (rate coding) and recruitment of additional fibres (population coding) — together giving the sensory system a wide dynamic range, often described psychophysically by the Stevens power law.

Distractors:

- "Action potential amplitude." — Reveals misconception: student forgets that action potentials are all-or-none. Correction: action-potential amplitude is fixed; intensity must be encoded by frequency or by population recruitment, not by amplitude.
- "Conduction velocity changes." — Reveals misconception: student treats velocity as adjustable to encode intensity. Correction: conduction velocity is set by axon diameter and myelination and does not scale with stimulus intensity in an individual axon.
- "Number of neurotransmitters released." — Reveals misconception: student picks a synaptic mechanism for the encoding step. Correction: the encoding occurs at the afferent before any synapse — by firing rate and by recruitment of additional afferents.

Explanation: Action potentials are all-or-none, so amplitude cannot encode intensity [Boron & Boulpaep ch.15; Berne & Levy ch.7]. Two coding strategies are used. **Rate coding** — a single afferent fires faster as stimulus intensity rises, often following a power law (rate ∝ intensity^k); this provides analogue intensity within one fibre. **Population coding** — stronger stimuli activate more receptors and afferents (which have varying thresholds); this provides a "population vote" on stimulus magnitude. The combination gives sensory systems a wide dynamic range — over six orders of magnitude for some modalities. The Stevens power law captures this psychophysically, with different exponents for different modalities (~3.5 for electric shock — small physical changes feel large; ~0.6 for brightness — large changes are needed to feel different).

Hints:

1. Action potentials are digital — amplitude is fixed.
2. One fibre encodes intensity by _how often_ it fires.
3. Stronger stimuli also recruit _more_ fibres.

Stem: The dorsal column–medial lemniscus pathway carries which set of modalities?

Correct answer: Fine (discriminative) touch, vibration, conscious proprioception, two-point discrimination, weight discrimination, and stereognosis — large-fibre, fast, well-localised; loss produces sensory ataxia with a positive Romberg sign and inability to identify objects by feel.

Correct answer: Receive convergent input from Aβ, Aδ, and C fibres and from visceral and somatic afferents, encode stimulus intensity by graded firing across a wide range, and are key cells in central sensitisation, referred pain, and gate-control modulation of pain transmission.

Explanation: Thermoreceptors are bare nerve endings with distinct molecular sensors [Berne & Levy ch.8; Boron & Boulpaep ch.15–16; Ganong]. Clinically, capsaicin patches (8% Qutenza) activate and then desensitise C fibres for postherpetic neuralgia and painful diabetic neuropathy; topical menthol activates TRPM8 for counter-stimulation; TRPV1 antagonists were trialled but caused hyperthermia (TRPV1 contributes to body temperature regulation), narrowing therapeutic windows.

Hints:

1. Thermoreceptors are morphologically simple — like nociceptors, they are bare endings.
2. The molecular sophistication lives in TRP channels.
3. Different TRP channels detect cold, warm, hot, and noxious cold.

1. Placebo analgesia is not psychological in opposition to biological — it is biological.
2. The descending modulation system mediates much of the effect.
