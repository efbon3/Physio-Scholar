## Pass 1 — Values (numerical anchors)

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The synaptic cleft of the NMJ is approximately \_\_\_ nm wide — substantially wider than a typical CNS synaptic cleft.

Canonical answer: ~50 nm

Accepted variants:

- 50 nm
- ~50 nm
- 50

Tolerance: ±20 nm (30–70 nm graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "20" → "That is closer to a typical _CNS_ synaptic cleft; the _NMJ_ cleft is wider, ~50 nm, accommodating the basal lamina with AChE."
- "3" → "That is closer to the gap-junction inter-membrane gap; the NMJ chemical cleft is ~50 nm."

Explanation: The basal lamina, with its scaffolding proteins (laminin, collagen IV, agrin) and AChE, occupies this gap [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. ACh must diffuse this 50 nm to reach AChRs at the crests of the junctional folds — a distance traversed in microseconds at 50 nm/ms diffusion rates. The basal-lamina-anchored AChE rapidly hydrolyses ACh as it diffuses, terminating the signal within ~1 ms. The wider NMJ cleft compared with CNS synapses reflects the high reliability requirement at the NMJ — the cleft contains substantial molecular machinery (AChE, agrin, collagen IV, laminin) that organises the synapse and rapidly terminates transmission.

Hints:

1. Wider than a typical CNS synaptic cleft.
2. Houses the basal lamina with AChE.
3. ~50 nm.

Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The adult muscle nicotinic ACh receptor is a pentamer with subunit stoichiometry \_\_\_.

Canonical answer: 2α : 1β : 1δ : 1ε

Accepted variants:

- 2α + β + δ + ε
- α₂βδε
- two alpha, one beta, one delta, one epsilon
- 2α:1β:1δ:1ε

Tolerance: not applicable (categorical)

Yellow conditions:

- "α₂βγδ" → "That is the _embryonic_ (foetal) isoform with γ in place of ε; the _adult_ receptor has 2α + β + δ + ε."
- "α₅" → "Muscle nAChR is a heteropentamer (2α:β:δ:ε), not a homopentamer (α5 is the neuronal type)."

Explanation: The embryonic isoform substitutes γ for ε; the switch is driven by neural agrin/MuSK signalling and changes channel kinetics (briefer mean open time in adult) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. Two ACh-binding sites at the α/non-α interfaces; both must be occupied for full opening. After denervation or in extrajunctional supersensitivity, the embryonic γ-form re-emerges throughout the sarcolemma — explaining the dangerous succinylcholine-induced hyperkalaemia in burns and prolonged immobility (the γ-form opens longer and releases more K⁺).

Hints:

1. Five subunits in total — a heteropentamer.
2. Two α subunits and one each of β, δ, plus a fifth.
3. The fifth subunit (ε) replaces the embryonic γ during postnatal maturation.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: Each ACh-containing vesicle at the NMJ holds approximately \_\_\_ molecules of acetylcholine.

Canonical answer: 5,000–10,000

Accepted variants:

- 5,000–10,000
- 2,000–10,000
- ~10,000
- ~5,000

Tolerance: order of magnitude (10³–10⁴)

Yellow conditions:

- "100" → "Too few — a single ACh vesicle holds ~5,000–10,000 molecules, not just hundreds."
- "1,000,000" → "Too many — vesicle ACh content is in the low thousands to ten thousand, not millions."

Explanation: Per quantum [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8; Guyton & Hall ch.7]. Guyton & Hall give 2,000–10,000; Boron & Boulpaep cite 5,000–10,000. Each motor-neuron AP releases ~100–300 quanta (vesicles), producing a robust EPP well above threshold. The vesicle's content defines the _quantum_ — the smallest unit of release — and the integer-multiple amplitudes of evoked end-plate potentials are the basis of Katz's quantal hypothesis. Vesicles are concentrated at active zones via VAChT (vesicular ACh transporter), and choline recycled from cleft hydrolysis is taken up by CHT1 for resynthesis.

Hints:

1. Each vesicle is a "quantum" of transmitter.
2. The number is in the thousands, not the hundreds or millions.
3. Combined with hundreds of vesicles released per AP, this gives the EPP.

Stem: A single MEPP at the human motor end-plate produces a depolarisation of approximately \_\_\_ mV.

Canonical answer: ~0.4 mV

Accepted variants:

- 0.4 mV
- ~0.4
- 0.5 mV
- <1 mV

Tolerance: ±0.3 mV

Yellow conditions:

- "0.04" → "Wrong order of magnitude — single MEPPs are ~0.4 mV (sub-millivolt), not tens of microvolts."
- "5" → "Too large — that approaches a full evoked EPP; single MEPPs are ~0.4 mV."

Explanation: Costanzo gives ~0.4 mV per MEPP; Berne & Levy quote ≤1 mV at the frog NMJ [Costanzo — NMJ]. The full EPP is an integer multiple of this quantum (typically ~50–100 quanta released by a single AP); the integer relationship is the cellular basis of Katz's quantal hypothesis. MEPPs occur spontaneously even without nerve stimulation, reflecting random vesicle fusion at low rate. Their distinct amplitude distribution provided the evidence on which the quantal hypothesis was built.

Hints:

1. Sub-millivolt single-vesicle response.
2. Integer multiples of this build up the full EPP.
3. Less than 1 mV — distinct from the ~30–40 mV full evoked EPP.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: A single motor-neuron action potential produces an end-plate potential (EPP) of approximately \_\_\_ mV at a normal NMJ.

Canonical answer: 30–40 mV

Accepted variants:

- 30–40 mV
- 30 mV
- 40 mV
- ~35 mV

Tolerance: ±15 mV (15–55 mV graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "0.4" → "That is a single _miniature_ EPP from one quantum; the full _evoked_ EPP is the sum of ~100–300 quanta — 30–40 mV."
- "100" → "Too large — the full evoked EPP is ~30–40 mV; that is a 30-fold amplification over a single MEPP, but it does not reach +100 mV."

Explanation: Far above the ~15-mV threshold for muscle action potential generation, providing the high "safety factor" that ensures reliable 1:1 transmission [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. Reduction toward 1 (myasthenia gravis, LEMS) produces fatigable failure — small drops in release or receptor density bring some EPPs below threshold, breaking the 1:1 ratio of nerve-to-muscle firing. The high safety factor is the design feature that makes the NMJ a reliable on/off switch under normal conditions.

Hints:

1. Far above threshold — providing safety factor.
2. Sum of ~100–300 quanta per AP.
3. Around 30–40 mV.

Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The density of nicotinic AChRs at the crests of the junctional folds is approximately \_\_\_ receptors/µm².

Canonical answer: ~10,000–20,000 per µm²

Accepted variants:

- 10,000–20,000/µm²
- ~10,000
- ~20,000

Tolerance: order of magnitude (10⁴/µm²)

Yellow conditions:

- "100" → "Too few — junctional fold crests are densely packed with AChRs (~10,000/µm²)."
- "1,000,000" → "Too many — the density is ~10,000–20,000 per µm², not millions."

Explanation: This high local density is essential — it concentrates ACh-receptor interactions at the folds where the EPP is generated [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. Antibody-mediated complement attack reduces this density and flattens the folds, lowering the safety factor. Rapsyn-mediated clustering (downstream of agrin–MuSK signalling) maintains the density; mutations in rapsyn or upstream signalling cause congenital myasthenic syndromes with reduced AChR density.

Hints:

1. The density is at the crests of the junctional folds.
2. Disease (MG) reduces the density and flattens the folds.
3. Tens of thousands per square micrometre.

QUESTION 7
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): F
Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: The reversal potential of the EPP at the NMJ is approximately \_\_\_ mV.

Canonical answer: 0 mV

Accepted variants:

- 0 mV
- ~0
- near 0 mV

Tolerance: ±10 mV

Yellow conditions:

- "+60" → "That is ENa; the AChR conducts both Na⁺ and K⁺, so reversal lies between ENa and EK — close to 0 mV."
- "−70" → "That is closer to the IPSP reversal (Cl⁻); the EPP reversal at the AChR (non-selective cation channel) is near 0 mV."

Explanation: Lies between ENa (~+60) and EK (~−90); the AChR conducts both Na⁺ and K⁺ with similar conductance [Costanzo — NMJ]. At V_rest = −90 mV, the driving force for Na⁺ entry dominates → net depolarising current. At depolarised potentials approaching 0 mV, the K⁺ outward driving force balances Na⁺ inward, and the net current goes to zero — this is the reversal point. The reversal potential is a powerful diagnostic — an EPP that reverses at 0 mV proves a non-selective cation channel; an IPSP reversing at ~−70 mV proves a Cl⁻-selective channel.

Hints:

1. Between ENa and EK because AChR is a non-selective cation channel.
2. The voltage at which net current through the channel is zero.
3. 0 mV.

Stem: In Lambert–Eaton myasthenic syndrome (LEMS), approximately \_\_\_ % of cases are paraneoplastic, most commonly associated with small-cell lung carcinoma.

Canonical answer: ~50%

Accepted variants:

- 50%
- ~50%
- 50–60%
- about half

Tolerance: ±10% (40–60% graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "10" → "Too low — roughly half of LEMS cases are paraneoplastic; SCLC is the dominant tumour."
- "90" → "Too high — about half are paraneoplastic; the remainder are autoimmune without an underlying tumour."

Explanation: SCLC cells express voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCCs) of the P/Q (Cav2.1) type, which trigger an autoantibody response that cross-reacts with motor-nerve-terminal Cav2.1 channels [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. Hints:

1. Roughly half — the rest are autoimmune without a tumour.

3. ~50%.

Priority (M / S / G): M

Stem: On repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS) at low frequency (3 Hz), a decrement of ≥ \_\_\_ % in compound muscle action potential (CMAP) amplitude between the first and fourth/fifth response is the diagnostic threshold for myasthenia gravis.

Canonical answer: 10%

Accepted variants:

- 10
- 10%
- ≥10%

Tolerance: exact (8–12% graded Green; conventional cut-off is 10%)

Yellow conditions:

- "5" → "5% is below the standard diagnostic threshold; technical artefact and normal variation can produce up to ~5% decrement."
- "50" → "That magnitude of decrement is far beyond the diagnostic threshold; ≥10% is the conventional cut-off, with severe MG often showing 20–40%."

Explanation: At 3 Hz, the calcium that has accumulated from preceding stimuli normally maintains quantal release; in MG, the _safety factor_ is reduced — there are fewer functional AChRs — so as ACh release declines slightly with successive stimuli (a normal phenomenon), some end-plates fail to reach threshold, producing the decrement [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. In _LEMS_, by contrast, low-frequency RNS may show a decrement, but high-frequency stimulation (or post-exercise) produces a large _increment_ (often >100%) because facilitation of presynaptic Ca²⁺ entry boosts ACh release.

Hints:

1. The threshold is a small but significant percentage drop.
2. A single-digit cut-off, in tens.
3. 10%.

Stem: After an intubating dose of succinylcholine (1–1.5 mg/kg IV), the typical duration of clinical neuromuscular block is approximately \_\_\_ minutes.

Canonical answer: 5–10 minutes

Accepted variants:

- 5–10 min
- 5 to 10 minutes
- ~7 min
- 5–10

Tolerance: ±2 min (3–12 min graded Green)

Yellow conditions:

- "30" → "Too long for normal-phenotype pseudocholinesterase; 30 minutes suggests atypical butyrylcholinesterase (homozygous deficient) producing prolonged block."
- "60" → "Far too long for normal succinylcholine pharmacology — that suggests pseudocholinesterase deficiency (homozygous atypical genotype) or phase II block."

Explanation: Succinylcholine is hydrolysed in plasma by butyrylcholinesterase (pseudocholinesterase); only a small fraction reaches the NMJ, but it produces depolarising block with rapid onset (~45–60 s) and short duration [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8; Costanzo Cases]. In pseudocholinesterase deficiency (atypical homozygous, dibucaine number ~20), the block can persist for _hours_, requiring sustained ventilation. Other risks include hyperkalaemia, malignant hyperthermia, and trismus.

Hints:

1. Shorter than any non-depolarising agent.
2. Less than a quarter of an hour.
3. 5–10 minutes.

Stem: In a normal patient, succinylcholine typically raises serum potassium by approximately \_\_\_ mEq/L; in patients with denervation, burns, or prolonged immobilisation, the rise can be life-threatening because of extrajunctional AChR upregulation.

Canonical answer: 0.5–1.0 mEq/L

Accepted variants:

- 0.5 mEq/L
- 0.5–1 mEq/L
- ~0.5
- 0.5 to 1.0

Tolerance: ±0.5 mEq/L (0–1.5 mEq/L graded Green for normal patients)

Yellow conditions:

- "5" → "5 mEq/L is the _pathological_ rise seen in denervation, burns, or massive trauma — a normal patient rises by only ~0.5 mEq/L."
- "0" → "There is a small but real K⁺ release with depolarising block; 0.5–1 mEq/L is the normal rise."

Explanation: Depolarising block opens AChRs, allowing K⁺ to exit muscle along its electrochemical gradient [Boron & Boulpaep ch.8]. In denervation supersensitivity (lower motor neuron lesion, prolonged disuse, burns >24 h post-injury), extrajunctional fetal-type AChRs proliferate across the sarcolemma; succinylcholine then opens vast numbers of channels, releasing K⁺ rises of 5–10 mEq/L → ventricular fibrillation. Hence succinylcholine is contraindicated in these populations.

Hints:

1. The normal rise is sub-clinically small.
2. Less than 1 mEq/L typically.
3. 0.5–1.0 mEq/L.

Stem: The narrow infoldings of the sarcolemma at the motor end-plate that increase its surface area and concentrate voltage-gated Na⁺ channels at their depths are called \_\_\_.

- secondary synaptic clefts
- postjunctional folds

Hints:

Stem: After partial denervation, surviving motor axons send out collateral processes that re-innervate orphaned muscle fibres — a process called \_\_\_ — producing larger motor units and giant motor unit potentials on EMG.

Canonical answer: terminal sprouting (collateral re-innervation)

Hints:

2. Spread across the sarcolemma after denervation.
3. Extrajunctional AChRs.

## Summary

This chapter covers the molecular architecture, electrophysiology, and pharmacology of the NMJ, framed around three integrative themes: (1) the _quantal_ nature of synaptic transmission and the safety factor that reliably converts each EPP into a muscle AP, (2) the molecular machinery — agrin–LRP4–MuSK clustering, SNARE-mediated vesicle fusion, presynaptic Cav2.1 calcium entry, postsynaptic AChR transduction, and basal-laminar AChE — and (3) the disease states that violate these mechanisms (MG, LEMS, congenital myasthenic syndromes, botulism) and the drugs that exploit or modify them (curare, succinylcholine, pyridostigmine, neostigmine, sugammadex, 3,4-DAP, eculizumab, FcRn antagonists).

Numerical anchors to remember: synaptic cleft ~50 nm; ACh per vesicle ~10,000 molecules; ~100–200 quanta per nerve impulse; AChR density ~10⁴/μm² at fold crests; EPP ~40–70 mV; MEPP ~0.5–1 mV; synaptic delay ~0.5–1 ms; safety factor ≥ 3; AChR turnover normally 7–10 days, MG 1–3 days; succinylcholine duration 5–10 min, K⁺ rise ~0.5–1 mEq/L (vs 5–10 in denervation supersensitivity); RNS decrement threshold 10%; AChR-Ab+ in ~85% of generalised MG; LEMS paraneoplastic ~50%.

Disease-mechanism mapping: MG = postsynaptic, anti-AChR / anti-MuSK / anti-LRP4, fatigable, ice-pack test, decrement on RNS, treat with pyridostigmine + immunomodulation + thymectomy in selected cases. LEMS = presynaptic, anti-Cav2.1, post-exercise _facilitation_, treat with 3,4-DAP + tumour management. Botulism = presynaptic SNARE cleavage, descending flaccid paralysis with autonomic features. Congenital myasthenic syndromes follow the same logic but at single molecular targets (CHRNE, COLQ, RAPSN, DOK7, MUSK, LRP4, agrin). Pharmacology is organised by site (presynaptic vs postsynaptic) and mechanism (release vs receptor vs enzyme).

Therapeutic frontier: complement inhibition (eculizumab, ravulizumab) and IgG-clearance acceleration (efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab) have transformed refractory generalised AChR-Ab+ MG. The diagnostic and pathogenetic frameworks above remain the bedrock for safe choices in anaesthesia, intensive care, and chronic management.
