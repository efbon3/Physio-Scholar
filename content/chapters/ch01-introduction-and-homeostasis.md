## Pass 1 — The internal environment and what physiology controls

QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Which physiologist coined the term "homeostasis" to describe the maintenance of nearly constant conditions in the internal environment?

Correct answer: Walter Cannon (in 1926).

Distractors:

- "Claude Bernard" — Reveals misconception: student conflates Bernard's _milieu intérieur_ concept (introduced in the 19th century) with the term homeostasis itself. Correction: Bernard introduced the concept of an internal environment (_milieu intérieur_), but Cannon coined the word "homeostasis" later, in 1926 [Guyton ch.1].
- "Andreas Vesalius" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception (Vesalius is associated with anatomy rather than physiology).
- "William Harvey" — Reveals misconception: student associates any historical physiology figure with the central concept of homeostasis. Correction: Harvey discovered the systemic circulation in the 17th century; he had no role in framing homeostasis.

Explanation: Walter Cannon, an American physiologist, introduced the term "homeostasis" in 1926 to describe the maintenance of nearly constant conditions in the body's internal environment [Guyton ch.1]. The concept itself — that cells require a constant fluid environment for life — was first articulated by Claude Bernard as the _milieu intérieur_. Cannon's contribution was naming and formalising the regulatory framework that keeps that internal environment within narrow limits [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1].

Hints:

1. The term was introduced in the 20th century, after the underlying concept of an "internal environment" had already been articulated.
2. The physiologist worked in the United States in the early 1900s.
3. He coined the term in a paper published in 1926.

QUESTION 2
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The 19th-century French physiologist Claude Bernard introduced which foundational concept?

Correct answer: The _milieu intérieur_ — the internal environment of the body in which cells live.

Distractors:

- "The term 'homeostasis'" — Reveals misconception: student attributes the word to Bernard rather than Cannon. Correction: Bernard described the concept; Cannon named it 80 years later.
- "Negative feedback control" — Reveals misconception: student assumes anyone associated with the internal environment also formalised feedback control. Correction: feedback as an engineering principle was articulated in cybernetics, not by Bernard; Bernard described the constancy of the internal environment, not the regulatory architecture itself.
- "The cardiac function curve" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific misconception.

Explanation: Claude Bernard (1813–1878) recognised that the cells of the body do not contact the external world directly but instead live in an internal fluid environment — the extracellular fluid — whose composition is held within narrow limits [Guyton ch.1]. He called this internal environment the _milieu intérieur_. This concept is the philosophical foundation on which all of regulatory physiology rests: homeostatic mechanisms exist precisely to defend the constancy of the _milieu intérieur_ [Sherwood ch.1; Boron ch.1].

Hints:

1. The concept describes where cells live, not how they are regulated.
2. The term is two French words.
3. It refers to the extracellular fluid as a stable internal world.

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: remember
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The adult human body is approximately what percentage water?

Correct answer: 50–70%.

Distractors:

- "20–30%" — Reveals misconception: student dramatically underestimates body water, perhaps confusing it with blood volume (~7%). Correction: water makes up roughly half to two-thirds of body weight; blood volume is only about 7%.
- "30–40%" — Reveals misconception: same confusion, slightly less extreme.
- "85–95%" — Reveals misconception: student is thinking of the water content of an individual cell (~70–85%) rather than of the whole body.

Explanation: About 50% to 70% of the adult human body is fluid, mostly water containing dissolved ions and other solutes [Guyton ch.1; Costanzo p.1; Ganong ch.1]. The exact value depends on body composition — adipose tissue contains little water, so total body water correlates inversely with body fat. A lean young man is closer to 60% water; a person with greater adipose tissue (including most adult women, on average) is closer to 50% [Costanzo p.1; Guyton ch.1]. Ganong notes the complementary breakdown: in an average young adult male, about 18% of body weight is protein and related substances, 7% is mineral, and 15% is fat — leaving the rest (≈60%) as water [Ganong ch.1].

Hints:

1. The body is mostly water — but how much is "mostly"?
2. The percentage is well over half but below three-quarters.
3. Guyton gives a range rather than a single number because adipose tissue affects it.

QUESTION 4
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: How is total body water distributed between intracellular and extracellular compartments?

Correct answer: About two-thirds is intracellular fluid and one-third is extracellular fluid.

Distractors:

- "Half intracellular, half extracellular" — Reveals misconception: student assumes equal distribution because cells and the surrounding fluid feel symmetrical. Correction: cellular volume dominates; the ECF is only one-third of total body water [Costanzo p.1].
- "About one-third intracellular and two-thirds extracellular" — Reveals misconception: student has the proportions inverted. Correction: cells are crowded together throughout the body, so intracellular fluid is the larger compartment.
- "Almost all intracellular, with extracellular fluid less than 10%" — Plausible but does not reveal a specific repeatable misconception.

Explanation: Total body water is split between two major compartments: intracellular fluid (ICF), which is about two-thirds of total body water, and extracellular fluid (ECF), which is about one-third [Costanzo p.1; Ganong ch.1]. The ECF is itself divided into plasma (the smaller subcompartment, contained within blood vessels) and interstitial fluid (the larger subcompartment, bathing the cells). The capillary wall separates plasma from interstitial fluid, and because it is virtually impermeable to plasma proteins, the interstitial fluid is essentially an ultrafiltrate of plasma [Costanzo p.1; Ganong ch.1].

Hints:

1. One compartment is roughly twice the size of the other.
2. The cells take up more total volume than the spaces around them.
3. The fraction matches the fraction of body weight that is the ICF.

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: The extracellular fluid (ECF) is itself divided into two subcompartments. Which subcompartment is the larger of the two, and what separates them?

Correct answer: Interstitial fluid is the larger subcompartment; the capillary wall separates plasma from interstitial fluid.

Distractors:

- "Plasma is the larger subcompartment; the capillary wall separates them" — Reveals misconception: student over-weights the conspicuousness of blood and assumes plasma is the bulk of the ECF. Correction: plasma is the smaller of the two; interstitial fluid bathes every cell in the body and is far larger in total volume [Costanzo p.1].
- "Plasma is the larger subcompartment; the cell membrane separates them" — Reveals misconception: student conflates the cell membrane (separates ICF from ECF) with the capillary wall (separates plasma from interstitial fluid).
- "Interstitial fluid is the larger subcompartment; the cell membrane separates them" — Reveals misconception: same confusion of membrane vs capillary wall.

Explanation: The ECF is divided into plasma (within the blood vessels) and interstitial fluid (between cells). Plasma is the smaller subcompartment; interstitial fluid is the larger one. The capillary wall separates these two ECF compartments, while the cell membrane separates ECF from ICF [Costanzo p.1; Guyton ch.1]. Because the capillary wall is virtually impermeable to plasma proteins, interstitial fluid is essentially an ultrafiltrate of plasma — almost the same composition, but with negligible protein.

Hints:

1. Two membranes are involved in body fluid compartmentalisation. Each separates a different pair of compartments.
2. Plasma is contained within the blood vessels; interstitial fluid bathes the cells.
3. The fluid within blood vessels is the smaller of the two ECF subcompartments.

QUESTION 6
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Which set of ions and substances is characteristic of _extracellular_ (rather than intracellular) fluid?

Correct answer: High sodium, high chloride, high bicarbonate, with nutrients (glucose, amino acids, fatty acids) and dissolved oxygen.

Distractors:

- "High potassium, high magnesium, high phosphate, high protein" — Reveals misconception: student has the ICF/ECF ionic distinction inverted; this is the _intracellular_ signature. Correction: ECF is sodium-rich and potassium-poor; ICF is the opposite.
- "Equal sodium and potassium, with proteins as the dominant solute" — Reveals misconception: student has not internalised the asymmetric ionic distribution maintained by Na+/K+-ATPase.
- "High calcium and high hydrogen ion, with low sodium" — Reveals misconception: student confuses ECF composition with the contents of secretory vesicles or the gastric lumen.

Explanation: The ECF contains large amounts of sodium, chloride, and bicarbonate ions, plus the nutrients (oxygen, glucose, fatty acids, amino acids) cells need, and carbon dioxide and metabolic waste products awaiting excretion [Guyton ch.1; Ganong ch.1]. The ICF, by contrast, contains large amounts of potassium, magnesium, and phosphate ions instead of sodium and chloride [Guyton ch.1; Ganong ch.1]. This compositional asymmetry is maintained by transport proteins in the cell membrane — notably the Na+/K+-ATPase — and is fundamental to electrical excitability, secondary active transport, and cell volume regulation [Costanzo p.1].

Hints:

1. The ECF is the fluid that bathes cells from the outside.
2. Sodium is the dominant cation in only one of the two compartments.
3. Think about which ion the Na+/K+-ATPase pumps _out_ of the cell.

QUESTION 7
Type: prediction
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: Blood circulates through the entire body about once per minute when a person is at rest. By approximately how many times can this circulation rate increase during strenuous exercise?

Correct answer: Up to about six times per minute.

Distractors:

- "About twice per minute" — Reveals misconception: student underestimates the cardiovascular reserve, perhaps assuming exercise can only modestly increase circulation. Correction: cardiac output can increase 4–6 fold during strenuous exercise, with the entire circulation traversed up to six times per minute [Guyton ch.1].
- "About 20 times per minute" — Reveals misconception: student overestimates the reserve, confusing the fold-increase in cardiac output (~5×) with a much larger number.
- "It does not change appreciably" — Reveals misconception: student does not appreciate that cardiac output is highly responsive to metabolic demand. Correction: the circulation matches oxygen demand and rises substantially with exercise.

Explanation: At rest, the entire blood volume traverses the circulation about once per minute; in someone exercising strenuously, this rate can reach about six times per minute [Guyton ch.1]. This reflects the manifold rise in cardiac output during exercise (driven by increased heart rate and stroke volume), which delivers more oxygen to working muscle. The capacity to scale circulation to demand is one of the most striking examples of how regulatory mechanisms preserve homeostasis under varying conditions.

Hints:

1. Cardiac output can rise four- to sixfold during strenuous exercise.
2. The "transit number" follows the same fold change as cardiac output.
3. The answer is between five and seven times per minute.

QUESTION 8
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A medical student notices that capillaries are present throughout almost every tissue of the body. What is the approximate maximum distance that any cell lies from a capillary, and why does this matter?

Correct answer: Few cells are more than about 50 µm from a capillary; this short distance ensures that diffusion of oxygen, nutrients, and wastes between blood and tissue cells occurs within seconds.

Distractors:

- "About 5 µm; this allows diffusion within milliseconds" — Reveals misconception: student underestimates the distance and overestimates the speed required. Correction: 5 µm is the size of a small cell, not the distance to the nearest capillary.
- "About 500 µm; diffusion takes minutes" — Reveals misconception: student underestimates capillary density, assuming tissue is a sparser network. Correction: capillary density is high enough that no cell is more than ~50 µm away.
- "About 5 mm; cells rely mainly on bulk flow rather than diffusion" — Reveals misconception: student assumes that bulk flow, not diffusion, supplies cells. Correction: at the cellular scale, diffusion across short distances is fast and adequate; bulk flow brings the blood close, then diffusion takes over.

Explanation: Few cells in the human body are more than about 50 µm from a capillary [Guyton ch.1]. This short distance is crucial because diffusion is the mechanism that delivers oxygen and nutrients across the final stretch from capillary to cell, and diffusion rate falls steeply with distance. At 50 µm, almost any small molecule equilibrates within seconds. If cells lay further away, diffusion would be too slow to meet metabolic demand, and the dense capillary network is therefore an architectural prerequisite for high metabolic activity.

Hints:

1. The distance is measured in micrometres, not millimetres.
2. It is several times the diameter of a typical cell, but well below a millimetre.
3. The number is two digits when expressed in micrometres.

## Pass 2 — Feedback architecture: how homeostatic control works

QUESTION 9
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A homeostatic control system contains three essential components. Which of the following correctly names them?

Correct answer: A receptor (sensor), a control centre (integrator), and an effector.

Distractors:

- "A stimulus, a response, and a recovery phase" — Reveals misconception: student confuses the _components_ of a control system with the _temporal stages_ of a reflex response. Correction: the question asks about the architecture, not the timeline.
- "A nerve, a hormone, and a muscle" — Reveals misconception: student assumes a control system always uses these specific structures. Correction: receptor, integrator, and effector can each be a wide variety of structures — receptors may be specialised cells or molecular sensors, integrators may be neural or endocrine, and effectors may be muscle, glands, or other cells.
- "An afferent limb, a synapse, and an efferent limb" — Reveals misconception: student is thinking of a reflex arc rather than the more general homeostatic control system; the synapse is not itself a control component.

Explanation: Every homeostatic control system has three functional parts: a _receptor_ that detects deviation from normal in the regulated variable, a _control centre_ (integrator) that compares the receptor signal against a set point and decides on the response, and an _effector_ that acts on the variable to oppose the deviation [Sherwood ch.1; Guyton ch.1]. For arterial pressure regulation, for instance, the receptors are baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch, the integrator is the medulla, and the effectors are the heart and blood vessels [Guyton ch.1].

Hints:

1. There is one component for sensing, one for processing, and one for acting.
2. The middle component compares the sensed value against a target.
3. The third component is whatever finally changes the regulated variable.

QUESTION 10
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Which of the following best describes a _negative feedback_ system in physiology?

Correct answer: A change in the regulated variable triggers a response that opposes the original change, returning the variable toward its set point.

Distractors:

- "A change in the regulated variable triggers a response that amplifies the original change, driving the variable further from its set point" — Reveals misconception: student has confused negative with positive feedback. Correction: amplification is positive feedback; opposition is negative feedback.
- "The regulated variable is held perfectly constant at its set point" — Reveals misconception: student assumes negative feedback achieves perfect control. Correction: negative feedback minimises but cannot eliminate variation; some residual error always persists, captured numerically by the gain of the system.
- "The system responds only when the variable falls below the set point, not when it rises above" — Reveals misconception: student assumes negative feedback acts in only one direction. Correction: negative feedback opposes any deviation from the set point — both rises and falls.

Explanation: Negative feedback is the central architectural principle of homeostatic control. When a regulated variable deviates from its set point, the deviation is detected and the system produces a response _opposite_ to the deviation, returning the variable toward the set point [Guyton ch.1; Sherwood ch.1; Ganong ch.1]. The opposition is what makes the feedback "negative." Because the response acts in the opposite direction of the disturbance, the system stabilises rather than oscillates wildly. Most homeostatic mechanisms in the body — temperature regulation, arterial pressure control, blood glucose homeostasis, acid–base balance — operate by negative feedback [Guyton ch.1; Ganong ch.1].

Hints:

1. The word "negative" describes the direction of the response, not the sign of the variable.
2. The response acts to undo the change, not to extend it.
3. If body temperature rises, what does the body do?

QUESTION 11
Type: calculation
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A blood transfusion is given to a patient whose baroreceptor reflex is non-functional, and arterial pressure rises from 100 to 175 mm Hg. When the same transfusion is given to a patient with an intact baroreceptor reflex, pressure rises only to 125 mm Hg. The gain of the baroreceptor pressure-control system in this scenario is approximately:

Correct answer: −2.

Distractors:

- "−1" — Reveals misconception: student has computed gain as the ratio of the corrected to the uncorrected pressure rise, rather than as correction divided by error. Correction: gain = correction / error = −50 / +25 = −2 [Guyton ch.1].
- "−10" — Reveals misconception: student has reversed numerator and denominator or misread the magnitudes.
- "−0.5" — Reveals misconception: student has the ratio inverted.

Explanation: Gain is defined as correction divided by remaining error: Gain = correction / error [Guyton ch.1]. In the worked example, the system "corrected" the pressure rise from a hypothetical 175 mm Hg back down to 125 mm Hg — a correction of −50 mm Hg. The error that remains (the deviation from the original 100 mm Hg setpoint that the system did not correct) is +25 mm Hg. Therefore gain = −50 / +25 = −2. The negative sign indicates that the correction is in the _opposite direction_ of the disturbance, which is exactly what defines negative feedback. By comparison, the temperature control system has a much higher gain (about −33), reflecting tighter regulation of core temperature than of arterial pressure.

Hints:

1. Gain is one quantity divided by another. The numerator describes how much the system corrected; the denominator describes how much error remained.
2. Correction is the magnitude (with sign) by which the system pulled the variable back toward the set point.
3. Compute correction (−50) divided by error (+25); pay attention to the sign.

QUESTION 12
Type: comparison
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: The body temperature control system has a gain of about −33, whereas the baroreceptor system controlling arterial pressure has a gain of about −2. What does this difference imply?

Correct answer: Body temperature is regulated much more tightly than arterial pressure — a given perturbation produces a much smaller residual error in core temperature than in arterial pressure.

Distractors:

- "Body temperature responds more slowly than arterial pressure" — Reveals misconception: student conflates _gain_ (steady-state effectiveness) with _speed_ (time constant). Correction: gain is about how completely the system corrects, not how fast.
- "The temperature control system uses positive feedback while the baroreceptor system uses negative feedback" — Reveals misconception: student misreads the negative sign on the gain as indicating positive feedback for one and negative for the other. Correction: a negative sign in gain indicates negative feedback in _both_ systems; the magnitude tells you tightness of control.
- "Arterial pressure has no homeostatic control" — Reveals misconception: a low gain is mistaken for absent control. Correction: a gain of −2 is real negative feedback; it is just less tight than temperature control.

Explanation: Higher absolute gain means a larger correction relative to the residual error — i.e., tighter regulation. Core body temperature is regulated to within fractions of a degree because metabolic enzymes are exquisitely temperature-sensitive, so the system has evolved very high gain (about −33) [Guyton ch.1]. Arterial pressure tolerates wider variation (a healthy person's systolic pressure varies by 20–30 mm Hg with posture and activity), so the baroreceptor system has lower gain (about −2). Both are negative feedback systems — the negative sign in the gain reflects this — but they differ in how tightly they correct deviations [Guyton ch.1].

Hints:

1. Gain magnitude tells you how complete the correction is, not how quick.
2. A higher absolute value of gain means smaller residual error after the system has acted.
3. Which variable would be more dangerous to let drift — core temperature or pressure?

QUESTION 13
Type: misconception-targeted
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Which of the following is the _best_ example of physiologically useful positive feedback?

Correct answer: The opening of voltage-gated sodium channels during the upstroke of a nerve action potential.

Distractors:

- "The carotid baroreceptor reflex" — Reveals misconception: student identifies any reflex involving an integrator with positive feedback. Correction: the baroreceptor reflex is canonical _negative_ feedback — it opposes deviations in arterial pressure.
- "Renal handling of glucose by the proximal tubule" — Reveals misconception: student misinterprets saturation kinetics (Tm) as positive feedback. Correction: saturation is not feedback at all — it is the carrier system reaching capacity.
- "The carbon dioxide-driven increase in pulmonary ventilation" — Reveals misconception: student identifies any system where increased input causes increased output as positive feedback. Correction: high CO₂ drives more ventilation, which _reduces_ CO₂ — that is negative feedback (the response opposes the deviation).

Explanation: Positive feedback occurs when a deviation in a variable triggers a response that _amplifies_ the deviation. In the nerve action potential, a small depolarisation opens voltage-gated sodium channels, sodium entry causes more depolarisation, which opens more channels — a self-reinforcing loop that explosively raises the membrane potential [Guyton ch.1]. Other physiologically useful examples of positive feedback include cervical stretch causing stronger uterine contractions during childbirth, and the clotting cascade, in which activated clotting factors generate more activated factors until the vessel is sealed [Guyton ch.1]. Each of these positive-feedback loops sits inside an overall negative-feedback context (e.g. clotting maintains blood volume; uterine contractions terminate when the baby is delivered).

Hints:

1. Look for a process in which the response _increases_ the original stimulus rather than reducing it.
2. The process must be self-amplifying once started.
3. The example involves an explosive change in membrane potential.

QUESTION 14
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A meal arrives in the duodenum and triggers insulin secretion _before_ blood glucose has appreciably risen. Which control mechanism best describes this anticipatory release?

Correct answer: Feed-forward control — the body anticipates a future change in the regulated variable and acts in advance.

Distractors:

- "Negative feedback" — Reveals misconception: student categorises every regulated response as negative feedback. Correction: in negative feedback, the response is triggered by a deviation that has already occurred; here, insulin rises _before_ glucose rises.
- "Positive feedback" — Reveals misconception: student associates an "early" response with self-amplification. Correction: nothing in the example is self-amplifying; insulin is being released to anticipate, not to amplify.
- "Adaptive control" — Reveals misconception: student confuses adaptive control (refining a feed-forward signal across repeated trials) with feed-forward itself. Correction: adaptive control modifies the next feed-forward signal based on errors from the last; it is delayed negative feedback layered on top of feed-forward.

Explanation: Feed-forward control acts in _anticipation_ of a change rather than in response to one [Sherwood ch.1; Guyton ch.1]. Incretin hormones released by the gut when food is detected (e.g. GIP, GLP-1) stimulate insulin secretion before the glucose load reaches the bloodstream, blunting the post-prandial rise. Feed-forward complements negative feedback (which corrects glucose excursions that do occur) by reducing the magnitude of those excursions in the first place. Guyton notes that the brain layers _adaptive control_ on top of feed-forward — when a feed-forward command produces a small error, the next feed-forward command is adjusted to reduce that error, making adaptive control a form of "delayed negative feedback" [Guyton ch.1].

Hints:

1. The release occurs before the deviation it would correct.
2. The system is acting in anticipation, not in response.
3. The term begins with "feed".

QUESTION 15
Type: prediction
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A pregnant woman in late labour experiences progressively stronger uterine contractions that culminate in delivery, after which the contractions stop. Which feature of this process is most characteristic?

Correct answer: It is a positive-feedback loop that contains its own termination event — delivery removes the stretch stimulus, ending the cycle.

Distractors:

- "It is a runaway positive-feedback loop with no built-in termination" — Reveals misconception: student assumes all positive feedback is dangerous and uncontrolled. Correction: physiologically useful positive-feedback loops always have a built-in stop; in childbirth, expulsion of the baby removes the cervical stretch that drives the cycle [Guyton ch.1].
- "It is a negative-feedback loop because the contractions eventually stop" — Reveals misconception: student equates "self-limiting" with "negative feedback." Correction: cessation of a positive-feedback loop because the stimulus is removed is not the same as negative feedback opposing the deviation.
- "It is a feed-forward mechanism — the body anticipates labour" — Reveals misconception: student confuses anticipation with self-amplification. Correction: each contraction strengthens the next; this is amplification, not anticipation.

Explanation: Stretching of the cervix by the descending fetal head sends signals to the uterus that increase contraction strength, which pushes the head harder against the cervix, which stretches it further — a classical positive-feedback loop [Guyton ch.1]. The loop continues until the baby is delivered, at which point the stretch stimulus is removed and the cycle terminates. Useful positive feedback in physiology always operates this way: it sits inside a larger system and has a built-in termination (e.g. clotting stops when the vessel is sealed; the action potential terminates when sodium channels inactivate). When the stop fails to fire — as in heatstroke, where the temperature control centre itself becomes impaired by hyperthermia — positive feedback becomes a vicious cycle.

Hints:

1. Each contraction makes the next one stronger.
2. The cycle must end somehow — what removes the stimulus?
3. The terminating event is delivery itself.

QUESTION 16
Type: misconception-targeted
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: Which statement about useful positive feedback in physiology is _correct_?

Correct answer: Useful positive feedback always sits inside an overall negative-feedback process and has a defined termination event.

Distractors:

- "Useful positive feedback continues indefinitely until exhaustion of substrate" — Reveals misconception: student assumes positive feedback always runs to depletion. Correction: physiologically useful positive feedback always has a built-in stop (sealing the vessel, delivering the baby, sodium channel inactivation).
- "Positive feedback is always pathological" — Reveals misconception: student associates self-amplification with disease. Correction: positive feedback is essential for several normal processes (clotting, parturition, action potential).
- "Positive feedback is faster than negative feedback by definition" — Reveals misconception: student confuses architecture with kinetics. Correction: speed is determined by the components of the loop, not by whether it is positive or negative.

Explanation: Guyton makes the point explicitly: "in each case in which positive feedback is useful, the positive feedback is part of an overall negative feedback process" [Guyton ch.1]. Clotting, for instance, is a positive-feedback amplification embedded within the larger negative-feedback control of blood volume — the clot forms rapidly to seal the leak, but the larger system (blood volume) is kept stable by negative feedback. Similarly, the action potential is a positive-feedback event embedded within negative-feedback nervous control. Useful positive feedback is not unbounded; it is bounded by a termination condition that fires reliably.

Hints:

1. Positive feedback is not the opposite of "good"; it is the opposite of negative feedback in architecture.
2. Look at how each useful example terminates — there is always a stop condition.
3. The stop condition is what protects the body from the runaway cycle.

## Pass 3 — Failure modes, vicious cycles, and clinical context

QUESTION 17
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): f
Priority (M / S / G): m

Stem: A trauma patient bleeds 2 L of blood. Cardiac output falls, coronary perfusion drops, the heart weakens further, cardiac output falls more, and so on. Which architectural feature describes this lethal sequence?

Correct answer: A positive-feedback vicious cycle that has overwhelmed the body's negative-feedback compensations.

Distractors:

- "A negative-feedback overshoot — the system corrects too aggressively" — Reveals misconception: student assumes any feedback failure must be a negative-feedback error. Correction: in this example, each cycle reinforces the previous one; this is positive feedback, not over-correction.
- "Adaptive control failure — the brain has failed to refine its feed-forward signal" — Reveals misconception: student maps the spiral onto a feed-forward concept that does not apply here. Correction: cardiac decompensation in haemorrhage is not a feed-forward problem; it is a positive-feedback collapse of the negative-feedback systems that normally stabilise circulation.
- "Homeostatic compensation — the body is restoring normal arterial pressure" — Reveals misconception: student misreads spiraling decompensation as compensation. Correction: a stable compensated state is _not_ what is happening; pressure and cardiac output are continuing to fall.

Explanation: Guyton describes precisely this scenario: a 2 L haemorrhage drops cardiac output, which reduces coronary blood flow, which weakens the heart further, which drops cardiac output further — a vicious positive-feedback cycle that ends in death unless interrupted [Guyton ch.1]. With a smaller bleed (1 L), the body's negative-feedback mechanisms (baroreflex, RAAS, vasoconstriction) successfully compensate. The clinical lesson is that haemorrhage has a "tipping point" beyond which compensation fails and positive feedback takes over — recognising and interrupting this transition (with fluid resuscitation, blood products, or surgical haemostasis) is the basis of trauma resuscitation.

Hints:

1. Each step in the sequence makes the next worse.
2. The body's normal stabilising mechanisms have been overrun.
3. The architectural term is the same as the architecture of the action-potential upstroke, but here the consequence is harmful rather than useful.

QUESTION 18
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: A patient with severe heatstroke has a core temperature of 42°C. The cooling mechanisms of the hypothalamus are themselves becoming impaired by the high temperature, so the body cannot dissipate heat. This is best classified as:

Correct answer: A pathological positive-feedback loop — an abnormal vicious cycle in which the temperature control centre's failure further raises the temperature.

Distractors:

- "Useful positive feedback that protects against further heat loss" — Reveals misconception: student treats all positive feedback as serving an adaptive purpose. Correction: this is precisely the pathological case — the loop is destructive, not protective.
- "Negative feedback that has merely been overwhelmed" — Reveals misconception: student labels any failed control system as negative feedback that "didn't work." Correction: heatstroke is not just failed cooling — it is an _active_ positive-feedback loop where rising temperature actively impairs the cooler.
- "Adaptive control failure" — Reveals misconception: student attributes failure to a feed-forward / learning mechanism that is not at issue here.

Explanation: In heatstroke, the temperature regulating centre in the hypothalamus is itself temperature-sensitive [Sherwood ch.1]. Once core temperature rises high enough, the centre begins to malfunction, which reduces sweat output and other cooling responses, which lets temperature climb further, which damages the centre more. This is a positive-feedback vicious cycle of exactly the same architecture as Guyton's haemorrhage example. Survival requires interrupting the loop with rapid external cooling (cold IV fluids, ice baths, evaporative cooling) before irreversible cellular damage from temperatures above ~42°C destroys neural tissue. Guyton notes that an increase in body temperature of only 11°F (about 7°C) above normal can lead to a vicious cycle of cellular metabolism that destroys cells [Guyton ch.1].

Hints:

1. The thing that should fix the problem has been damaged by the problem.
2. Each cycle makes the next worse, the same architecture as runaway haemorrhage.
3. The cooler itself is broken.

QUESTION 20
Type: prediction
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): i
Priority (M / S / G): s

Stem: When the plasma calcium ion concentration falls to about _half_ its normal value, what is the most likely consequence?

Correct answer: Tetanic contraction of muscles throughout the body, due to spontaneous generation of nerve impulses in peripheral nerves.

Distractors:

- "Severe cardiac muscle depression" — Reveals misconception: student conflates the consequences of low calcium with those of high potassium. Correction: it is high (not low) potassium — about twice normal — that depresses cardiac muscle [Guyton ch.1].
- "Mental irritability and convulsions" — Reveals misconception: student conflates low calcium with low glucose. Correction: low glucose (below ~half normal) causes mental irritability and possibly convulsions; low calcium causes tetany.
- "Paralysis from inability of nerves to carry signals" — Reveals misconception: student conflates low calcium with low potassium. Correction: it is low (about one-third normal) potassium that causes nerve conduction failure [Guyton ch.1].

Explanation: Calcium has a stabilising effect on the resting membrane potential of nerves: at normal extracellular calcium, neurons are appropriately excitable; when extracellular calcium drops, neurons become hyperexcitable and fire spontaneously, producing tetany [Guyton ch.1]. Clinical signs include carpopedal spasm, Chvostek's and Trousseau's signs, and laryngospasm. The physiological mechanism is that calcium normally binds the outer face of voltage-gated sodium channels and raises their threshold; lowering calcium lowers the threshold, increasing the likelihood of spontaneous depolarisation.

Hints:

1. Calcium has a stabilising effect on nerve excitability.
2. Lowering calcium lowers the threshold for nerve firing — neurons fire spontaneously.
3. The clinical sign is carpopedal spasm in the hands and feet.

QUESTION 21
Type: clinical application
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A diabetic patient on insulin therapy is found to have a plasma glucose of about 35 mg/dL — roughly half the normal fasting value. What symptoms are most likely?

Correct answer: Mental irritability and possibly convulsions.

Distractors:

- "Tetanic muscle contractions" — Reveals misconception: student conflates hypoglycaemia symptoms with hypocalcaemia. Correction: tetany is characteristic of low calcium, not low glucose.
- "Cardiac muscle depression with arrhythmia" — Reveals misconception: student attributes severe hypoglycaemia to a primarily cardiac syndrome. Correction: the brain — which depends almost exclusively on glucose — manifests dysfunction first.
- "Profound muscle paralysis" — Reveals misconception: student conflates hypoglycaemia with severe hypokalaemia. Correction: paralysis is a hypokalaemia symptom; hypoglycaemia produces predominantly neuropsychiatric features.

Explanation: The brain depends almost exclusively on glucose for energy and has minimal capacity to use alternative substrates rapidly [Guyton ch.1]. When plasma glucose falls below about half its normal value, neuronal function deteriorates — initially producing mental irritability, confusion, and tremor, and in severe hypoglycaemia, seizures and loss of consciousness [Guyton ch.1]. This is why insulin therapy carries a risk of acute neurological emergencies: the brain has limited tolerance for low glucose. Rapid recognition and oral or IV glucose is the standard intervention.

Hints:

1. Which organ is most dependent on glucose for function?
2. The brain manifests dysfunction before the heart or muscles do.
3. The picture is neuropsychiatric, not muscular.

QUESTION 22
Type: analysis
Bloom's level: analyze
Difficulty (F / I / A): a
Priority (M / S / G): g

Stem: A patient with longstanding renal disease develops sustained hypertension. The raised pressure is initially helpful — it returns sodium and water excretion to balanced levels — but harmful in the long term because it damages the kidneys, worsening the original problem. Which concept does this best illustrate?

Correct answer: A homeostatic compensation that becomes a long-term trade-off and contributes to additional pathology.

Distractors:

- "A failed compensation that has no useful effect" — Reveals misconception: student treats compensation as binary — either it works or it does not. Correction: in this case the compensation does succeed in restoring sodium balance, but at a cost.
- "An intentional, designed mechanism for raising blood pressure" — Reveals misconception: student treats the body's compensatory choices as teleological designs. Correction: the rise in pressure is a side-effect of restoring sodium balance, not a designed goal.
- "A primary disease without compensatory component" — Reveals misconception: student assumes hypertension here is independent of renal physiology. Correction: in this scenario, the hypertension _is_ the compensation — it forms a feedback loop with the failing kidneys.

Explanation: Guyton makes the point that homeostatic compensations can themselves become pathological in the long term: "homeostatic compensations that ensue after injury, disease, or major environmental challenges to the body may represent trade-offs that are necessary to maintain vital body functions but, in the long term, contribute to additional abnormalities of body function" [Guyton ch.1]. In renal disease, raising arterial pressure restores pressure natriuresis and re-balances sodium intake and output — necessary to survive — but the high pressure then damages the kidneys and other organs, including the kidney itself, which can drive a slow vicious cycle of progressive renal failure and worsening hypertension [Guyton ch.1]. This is the core concept of pathophysiology: disease states are often a mixture of the original lesion and the compensatory responses to it, and recognising both is essential for treatment.

Hints:

1. The body's response is helpful in the short term but harmful in the long term.
2. The compensation maintains balance of one variable while damaging the organ doing the compensating.
3. The concept is central to how Guyton defines pathophysiology.

- _Distractor design._ For most questions I used distractors that are _real misconceptions held by first-year students_ (confusing ICF with ECF composition, attributing homeostasis to Bernard rather than Cannon, calling baroreflex positive feedback because it is a reflex). - _Aspects covered._ Internal environment composition (Q3–Q6); receptors and sensors (Q9, Q11); control architecture (Q9, Q10, Q12); effectors (implicit in Q9, Q12, Q15); failure modes (Q17–Q22); clinical context (Q8, Q14, Q17, Q18, Q21, Q22).
