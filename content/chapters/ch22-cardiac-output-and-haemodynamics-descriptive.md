QUESTION 1
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Discuss Poiseuille's law and its clinical implications.

Model answer: Q = ΔP × π × r⁴ / (8 × η × L). Resistance R = 8ηL/πr⁴. Implications: (1) flow proportional to ΔP/R; (2) very sensitive to radius (4th power - 16x change for halving radius); (3) viscosity matters (polycythaemia ↑, anaemia ↓); (4) length matters (vessels in series sum). Clinical: arterioles principal site of resistance (highest R, smallest radius); vasodilators ↑ Q dramatically; hypertension reflects increased SVR (mostly arteriolar). Stenosis → marked Q reduction (severe coronary > 70% area). Anaemia → ↓ viscosity and ↑ CO. Polycythaemia → ↑ viscosity, sluggish flow, thrombosis. Catheter size matters: large for resuscitation. Laminar vs turbulent flow (Reynolds number) - turbulence in stenosis produces bruits/murmurs [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Hints:

2. Arterioles main resistance site.
3. Anaemia ↓ viscosity ↑ CO; polycythaemia opposite.

Stem: Describe regulation of vascular tone — local, neural, humoral mechanisms.

Model answer: Local: (1) myogenic (Bayliss - stretch contracts smooth muscle); (2) metabolic (CO2, H+, K+, adenosine, lactate, low O2 → vasodilation in active tissue); (3) endothelial (NO via shear stress, kinins, ACh, histamine; PGI2; EDHF; opposite - ET-1, TXA2). Drugs: NO donors (nitrates), calcium channel blockers (DHP for vasodilation), α1 blockers (BPH), endothelin antagonists (PAH). Endothelial dysfunction underlies atherosclerosis, HTN, DM [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

QUESTION 3
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe Starling forces and capillary fluid exchange.

Model answer: Net filtration = K_f × [(P_c - P_i) - σ(π_c - π_i)]. P_c (hydrostatic capillary) and π_i (interstitial oncotic) push out; P_i (interstitial hydrostatic) and π_c (plasma oncotic, mainly albumin) push in. Starling forces vary along capillary - filtration arteriolar end (P_c high), reabsorption venular end. Net 2-4 L/day filters out, returned by lymphatics. Disequilibrium → oedema: (1) ↑ P_c (HF, venous obstruction); (2) ↓ π_c (nephrotic, malnutrition, liver failure); (3) ↑ permeability (inflammation, sepsis, burns, anaphylaxis); (4) lymphatic obstruction (filariasis, post-mastectomy, malignancy). Treatment: diuretics, albumin replacement, treat underlying. Pulmonary oedema specifically - cardiogenic (high P_c) vs non-cardiogenic (ARDS - high permeability) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Elaborative explanation: A complete answer integrates the named structures, mechanisms, and clinical correlates with quantitative anchors.

Hints:

1. Starling: hydrostatic (out) + oncotic (in).
2. Albumin (π_c) most important plasma oncotic.
3. Imbalance → oedema.

QUESTION 4
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe systemic vascular resistance, MAP, and BP regulation.

Model answer: MAP ≈ DBP + (PP/3) ≈ CO × SVR. SVR = (MAP - RAP) / CO; ~ 1100 dynes·s/cm⁵. PP = SBP - DBP; reflects SV and arterial compliance (widens with age, AS, atherosclerosis - stiff arteries). MAP regulated short-term by baroreflex; medium by RAAS; long by renal pressure-natriuresis. BP measured: cuff sphygmomanometry, ABP monitoring, intra-arterial. Hypertension: > 130/80 (ACC/AHA) or > 140/90 (most others). Causes: primary (95%) - genetic + environmental; secondary - renal (renovascular, parenchymal), endocrine (Conn, phaeo, Cushing, hyperthyroid), drugs (NSAIDs, OCP, sympathomimetics), sleep apnoea, coarctation. Treatment: lifestyle + ACE-I/ARB, CCB, thiazide, β-blocker, MRA; specific for cause. CV risk reduction primary goal. Hypotension: hypovolaemic, distributive (sepsis), cardiogenic, obstructive shock [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Hints:

1. MAP = CO × SVR; PP reflects SV + compliance.
2. Baroreflex short, RAAS medium, kidney long term BP control.
3. HTN ≥ 140/90 (or 130/80 ACC/AHA).

QUESTION 5
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe cardiac output measurement methods.

Model answer: (1) _Thermodilution_ via PA catheter (Swan-Ganz): cold saline injected RA, temp measured PA; reciprocal of area under curve = CO. Less used now (invasive, no mortality benefit). (2) _Fick principle_: CO = O2 consumption / (CaO2 - CvO2). Gold standard. (3) _Echocardiography_: SV from LVOT VTI × LVOT area; CO = SV × HR; non-invasive, bedside. (4) _Bioimpedance_: thoracic electrical impedance changes with cardiac volume. (5) _Pulse contour analysis_ (LiDCO, PiCCO, FloTrac): arterial line waveform → SV; less invasive. (6) _Oesophageal Doppler_: monitors descending aortic flow; ICU/intra-op. CI (cardiac index) = CO/BSA, normal ~ 2.5-4 L/min/m². Modern preference for less invasive methods. Clinical: ICU shock differentiation, fluid responsiveness assessment (passive leg raise, SVV, PPV). Modern HF and ICU care relies on flow measurement [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Elaborative explanation: A complete answer integrates the named structures, mechanisms, and clinical correlates with quantitative anchors.

Hints:

1. Thermodilution (PAC), Fick (gold), echo, bioimpedance, pulse contour.
2. CI = CO/BSA, normal 2.5-4.
3. Less invasive methods preferred.

QUESTION 6
Type: recall
Bloom's level: understand
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Describe how heart rate, stroke volume, and contractility interact.

Model answer: CO = HR × SV. HR controlled by autonomic balance (β1 ↑, M2 ↓); intrinsic SA rate ~ 100. SV = LVEDV - LVESV; depends on preload (Frank-Starling), afterload (impedance), contractility (ESPVR slope). Contractility independent of preload/afterload - augmented by β1 (cAMP-PKA), thyroxine, glucagon, digoxin, calcium; reduced in HF, ischaemia, hypoxia, acidosis, β-blockers. EF = SV/LVEDV (normal ≥ 55%; HFrEF ≤ 40%). Tachycardia at high rates (> 180) reduces SV (less filling time) → CO falls (the 'optimal HR' concept). Bradyarrhythmias - SV may increase by Frank-Starling but CO often falls if HR too low. β-blockers reduce HR → improve myocardial O2 supply/demand balance (especially angina, MI, HF) but may compromise CO in some. Digoxin (cardiac glycoside): mild positive inotropy + vagal tone enhancement → reduced HR in AF [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Elaborative explanation: A complete answer integrates the named structures, mechanisms, and clinical correlates with quantitative anchors.

Hints:

1. CO = HR × SV; SV = preload + afterload + contractility.
2. Contractility independent of load.
3. Tachycardia at extremes reduces SV.

Self-grading checklist:
A Green answer should include all of the following:

- Mentions β-blocker effects.

A Red answer treats topic superficially.

QUESTION 7
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Discuss the cardiovascular response to standing - postural BP regulation.

Model answer: On standing, ~ 500 mL blood pools in lower limbs/splanchnic → ↓ venous return → ↓ SV → ↓ CO → ↓ MAP. Compensation: (1) Baroreflex (carotid + aortic) detects ↓ MAP → ↓ vagal + ↑ sympathetic; ↑ HR (~ 10-20), ↑ SV (Frank-Starling), ↑ SVR; restores MAP within ~ 30 s. (2) Skeletal muscle pump (calf contraction) augments venous return. (3) Respiratory pump. Failure - orthostatic hypotension (drop SBP ≥ 20 or DBP ≥ 10 within 3 min standing). Causes: (a) Volume depletion (dehydration, blood loss, diuretics); (b) Autonomic failure (PD, MSA, pure autonomic failure, DAN, SCI); (c) Drugs (antihypertensives, diuretics, antidepressants - TCAs, antipsychotics, dopaminergic, prazosin/α1 blockers); (d) Endocrine (Addison, hypothyroid). Symptoms: dizziness, syncope, falls (especially elderly). Investigation: lying/standing BP, tilt-table test if vasovagal suspected. Management: (1) Non-pharmacological - rise slowly, compression stockings, increased salt and fluid, avoid hot environments, eat small meals; (2) Pharmacological - midodrine (α1 agonist), fludrocortisone (volume expansion), pyridostigmine, droxidopa, octreotide. Postprandial hypotension common in elderly [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

Hints:

1. Standing → blood pooling → BP fall → baroreflex compensation.
2. Failure = orthostatic hypotension.
3. Lifestyle + midodrine + fludrocortisone.

Stem: A 70-year-old smoker with claudication and weak foot pulses. Decompose peripheral arterial disease.

Model answer: PAD: atherosclerotic narrowing; same RFs as CAD. Spectrum: asymptomatic, claudication (reproducible exercise-induced calf/buttock pain relieved by rest), critical limb ischaemia (rest pain, ulcers, gangrene), acute limb ischaemia (sudden 6Ps - pain, pallor, paraesthesia, paralysis, pulselessness, perishing cold). ABI: < 0.9 diagnostic; > 1.4 calcified non-compressible (DM/CKD); 0.5-0.9 claudication; < 0.5 critical. Investigation: ABI, duplex US, MRA/CTA, conventional angiography. Management: (1) RFs - smoking cessation #1, statin, BP, glucose, antiplatelet (aspirin or clopidogrel; rivaroxaban 2.5 BD + aspirin per COMPASS reduces MACE in stable PAD); (2) Supervised exercise programme; (3) Cilostazol for claudication; (4) Revascularisation (endovascular angioplasty/stent or surgical bypass) for limiting claudication, CLI, ALI; (5) Acute limb ischaemia = vascular surgery emergency. PAD = systemic - manage CV risk aggressively (worse than localised CAD) [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

QUESTION 9
Type: clinical
Bloom's level: apply
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 50-year-old with sudden severe headache. CT shows subarachnoid haemorrhage. Decompose SAH management.

Model answer: _SAH_ - bleeding into subarachnoid space; ~ 80% from ruptured saccular (berry) aneurysm; AVM, trauma. Risk: HTN, smoking, alcohol, family history, ADPKD. Clinical: thunderclap headache ('worst ever'), neck stiffness, photophobia, nausea/vomiting, syncope, focal deficits, seizures, raised ICP signs. Hunt-Hess and WFNS grading. Diagnosis: non-contrast CT (sensitive within 6h - 95%+); LP if CT negative + suspicion (xanthochromia after 12h); CTA/digital subtraction angiography for aneurysm. Complications: rebleeding (highest first 24h), vasospasm (peak 4-14 days; nimodipine prophylaxis), hydrocephalus (acute communicating - EVD), seizures, hyponatraemia (cerebral salt wasting or SIADH), cardiac (Takotsubo, arrhythmia, neurogenic pulmonary oedema). Management: (1) ICU; (2) Aneurysm secured 24-72h - clipping (open) or coiling (endovascular - ISAT favours coiling for selected); (3) Nimodipine for vasospasm; (4) BP control (intermediate target); (5) Manage hydrocephalus, ICP, seizures, electrolytes; (6) DVT prophylaxis after securing; (7) Long-term BP, lifestyle, family screening if multiple aneurysms or family history. Mortality ~ 30-50%; significant morbidity in survivors [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

QUESTION 11
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: A 50-year-old with palpitations and chest pain. ECG shows narrow complex tachycardia at 180 bpm. Decompose SVT management.

Model answer: _SVT_ - narrow QRS (< 120 ms) tachycardia. Differential: sinus tach (gradual onset/offset; warm-up/cool-down); AVNRT (most common - re-entry within AV node; sudden); AVRT/WPW (accessory pathway; delta wave inter-ictal); atrial tach (warm-up; non-paroxysmal); atrial flutter (sawtooth, ~ 300 atrial with 2:1, 3:1, 4:1 conduction → ventricular 150, 100, 75); AF (irregularly irregular, no P); junctional tachy. Acute management: (1) Vagal manoeuvres (Valsalva - modified - leg raise after bear-down most effective; carotid sinus massage if no contraindications; ice cold water face immersion); (2) Adenosine 6 mg IV rapid push then 12 mg if needed (causes brief AV block - reveals atrial activity in atrial flutter; terminates AVNRT and AVRT through AV node; warn patient of brief flushing/chest discomfort/dyspnoea); (3) IV β-blocker, non-DHP CCB (verapamil/diltiazem) or amiodarone; (4) DC cardioversion if unstable. Long-term: catheter ablation (curative for AVNRT/AVRT/atrial flutter; high success); β-blocker, CCB; flecainide for some. WPW with AF: avoid AV-blocking agents (digoxin, verapamil, β-blockers, adenosine) - can precipitate VF; use procainamide, flecainide, or DC cardioversion. Genetic in some [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

QUESTION 13
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Discuss venous return and the role of the venous system.

Model answer: Venous return = total systemic blood flow returning to RA. = CO at steady state. Determinants: (1) Mean systemic filling pressure (MSFP) - ~ 7 mm Hg; reflects total volume + venous tone; (2) Right atrial pressure (RAP) - normally 0; (3) Resistance to venous return; (4) Skeletal muscle pump (calf contraction in particular); (5) Respiratory pump (negative thoracic pressure during inspiration aids return); (6) Sympathetic venoconstriction (mobilises venous reservoir). Guyton's analysis: VR curve and CO curve intersect at operating point; cardiac output = venous return at steady state. Failure to maintain VR → low CO. Clinical: shock - mobilise venous return (fluids, leg elevation, pressors which venoconstrict); HF - chronic ↑ MSFP from fluid retention; tamponade/PE - obstructive shock (impaired RV/LV inflow). Veins hold ~ 70% of blood volume (capacitance); venoconstriction redistributes. Pregnancy - aortocaval compression supine → ↓ VR (lateral position). Standing - ↓ VR initially. CVP measurement - reflects RAP, useful trend marker [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].

QUESTION 14
Type: integration
Bloom's level: evaluate
Difficulty (F / I / A): I
Priority (M / S / G): S

Stem: Synthesise this chapter: how do haemodynamic forces produce blood flow and tissue perfusion?

Model answer: Cardiovascular system delivers O2/nutrients and removes waste through coordinated function: (1) Heart - pump (CO = HR × SV); (2) Arteries - high-pressure conduit; PP reflects compliance; (3) Arterioles - resistance vessels (Poiseuille - r⁴); main site of regulation; (4) Capillaries - exchange (Starling forces); (5) Venules/veins - capacitance reservoir; venous return; (6) Lymphatics - returns interstitial fluid + immune. Pressure gradient + resistance + viscosity + compliance govern flow. Auto-regulation maintains organ perfusion (cerebral, coronary, renal). Multi-time-scale BP regulation: baroreflex (seconds), RAAS/sympathetic (medium), kidney pressure-natriuresis (long). Disease patterns: HTN (↑ SVR), HF (↓ pump), atherosclerosis (vessel narrowing → MI, stroke, PAD), valvular (mechanical disruption), arrhythmias (electrical → mechanical), shock (4 categories - hypovolaemic, distributive, cardiogenic, obstructive). Therapy targets each: drugs (multiple classes), devices (pacemakers, ICDs, LVAD, valve interventions), procedures (PCI, surgery), lifestyle. Modern CV care has transformed life expectancy; CVD remains leading cause of death globally - need access [Boron & Boulpaep ch.22].
