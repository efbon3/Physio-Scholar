# Layer 1 — Core

**Cross-bridges cycle when myosin heads bind actin, swing, release, and
re-bind — powered by ATP hydrolysis and gated by Ca²⁺ binding to
troponin C.** Each cycle slides the thin filament along the thick
filament, shortening the sarcomere. Tension equals the number of
attached cross-bridges; velocity equals the rate at which they cycle.

## Clinical Hook

Rigor mortis: after death, ATP supply fails. Cross-bridges that have
attached can't release (release requires ATP binding to myosin), so
the muscle stiffens. Resolution comes from proteolytic breakdown of
the contractile apparatus over 24–48 h. The same biochemistry — ATP is
needed to _break_, not to make, the cross-bridge — explains why ATP
shortage in fatigue and ischaemia produces stiffness, not flaccidity.

# Questions

## Question 1

**Type:** mechanism
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** foundational
**Stem:** Outline the cross-bridge cycle in skeletal muscle, naming each step and its energetic role.
**Correct answer:** (1) Resting state: myosin head has ADP + Pi bound and is "cocked." (2) Cross-bridge attachment: head binds exposed actin site (after Ca²⁺ removes troponin block). (3) Power stroke: Pi releases, myosin head rotates ~45°, sliding actin past myosin; ADP is released. (4) Detachment: a new ATP binds myosin, reducing its affinity for actin → head releases. (5) Cocking: ATP hydrolysis to ADP + Pi re-cocks the head; cycle restarts.
**Elaborative explanation:** ATP is needed to _detach_ (step 4) — the energy of hydrolysis is stored in the cocked configuration (step 5) and released as mechanical work during the power stroke (step 3). Without ATP, heads cannot release — this is rigor.

### Hint Ladder

1. Five steps; ATP enters the cycle at one specific point.
2. The power stroke happens between Pi release and ADP release.
3. ATP binding causes detachment, not power stroke.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "ATP binding triggers the power stroke directly" → Misconception: ATP binding causes detachment; hydrolysis re-cocks for the next stroke.
- Wrong answer: "Ca²⁺ binds myosin to start the cycle" → Misconception: Ca²⁺ binds troponin C on the thin filament, not myosin.
- Wrong answer: "Each cross-bridge cycle hydrolyses 2 ATP" → Misconception: one ATP per cycle.

## Question 2

**Type:** mechanism
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** Describe how Ca²⁺ activates contraction at the molecular level, working through troponin and tropomyosin.
**Correct answer:** At rest, tropomyosin sits over the myosin-binding site on actin, blocking cross-bridge formation. When Ca²⁺ binds troponin C (subunit of the troponin trimer), troponin undergoes a conformational change that pulls tropomyosin off the binding site, exposing actin. Cross-bridges can now form.
**Elaborative explanation:** Three subunits of troponin: TnC binds Ca²⁺, TnI inhibits actin-myosin interaction, TnT anchors the complex to tropomyosin. Cardiac troponin I (cTnI) is the diagnostic marker for myocardial infarction; its release into plasma indicates myocyte death.

### Hint Ladder

1. Tropomyosin physically blocks something at rest.
2. Ca²⁺ binding shifts a regulatory protein.
3. The myosin binding sites are revealed.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Ca²⁺ binds tropomyosin directly" → Misconception: Ca²⁺ binds TnC, not tropomyosin.
- Wrong answer: "Troponin is on the thick filament" → Misconception: troponin is part of the thin filament.
- Wrong answer: "Without Ca²⁺, tropomyosin is in the off-position (binding sites exposed)" → Misconception: at rest, tropomyosin is in the _on_ position blocking sites.

## Question 3

**Type:** mechanism
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** Describe the length-tension relationship for skeletal muscle. Why is there an optimum sarcomere length?
**Correct answer:** Active tension is maximal at sarcomere length 2.0–2.2 μm — the optimum where every myosin head can reach an actin binding site. At shorter lengths (~1.6 μm), thin filaments overlap each other and interfere; at longer lengths (>2.2 μm), thick and thin filaments lose overlap and fewer cross-bridges can form.
**Elaborative explanation:** This relationship is the molecular basis of the Frank-Starling mechanism in cardiac muscle (preload determines sarcomere length, which determines force). In skeletal muscle, joint positioning across multiple sarcomeres usually keeps muscle near optimum, so the curve's plateau matters more clinically.

### Hint Ladder

1. Force depends on number of attached cross-bridges.
2. At extreme lengths, geometric overlap fails.
3. There's a sweet spot in between.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Maximum tension at maximum sarcomere length" → Misconception: ignoring overlap loss at long length.
- Wrong answer: "Tension is independent of length" → Misconception: missing the entire concept.
- Wrong answer: "Optimum is where filaments don't overlap at all" → Misconception: confusing maximum and minimum overlap.

## Question 4

**Type:** classification
**Bloom's level:** remember
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** Compare slow-oxidative (Type I), fast-oxidative-glycolytic (Type IIa), and fast-glycolytic (Type IIb/IIx) muscle fibres.
**Correct answer:** Type I (red, slow): myoglobin-rich, mitochondria-rich, oxidative metabolism, fatigue-resistant; postural muscles. Type IIa (intermediate): mixed oxidative and glycolytic; moderate fatigue resistance; sustained dynamic activity. Type IIb/IIx (white, fast): glycolysis-dominated, sparse mitochondria, fatigue-prone; powerful brief contractions (sprinting).
**Elaborative explanation:** Training shifts proportions slightly (endurance training favours Type I→IIa transition; resistance training increases Type IIa). Disease can selectively affect fibre types — denervation atrophy hits Type II first; corticosteroid myopathy preferentially affects Type II proximal muscles.

### Hint Ladder

1. Three fibre types; named for speed and metabolism.
2. Slow = oxidative = fatigue-resistant.
3. Fast = glycolytic = fatigues fast.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Type I fibres are stronger than Type II" → Misconception: Type II generates more peak force.
- Wrong answer: "All muscle fibres are the same in any given muscle" → Misconception: most muscles have all three types in varying proportions.
- Wrong answer: "Type I fibres rely on glycolysis" → Misconception: Type I is oxidative.

## Question 5

**Type:** application
**Bloom's level:** apply
**Priority:** should
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** A patient performs heavy weightlifting until exhaustion. Describe the metabolic transitions and the mechanism of fatigue.
**Correct answer:** Initially, ATP comes from creatine phosphate (~10 s buffer), then anaerobic glycolysis (~1–2 min) producing lactate, then oxidative phosphorylation. Fatigue mechanisms: ATP depletion (uncommon), lactate-driven acidosis (impairs Ca²⁺ release and cross-bridge cycling), Pi accumulation (binds myosin head and slows it), and central fatigue (motor cortex output). Glycogen depletion limits prolonged exercise.
**Elaborative explanation:** Pi accumulation is now thought to be the dominant proximate cause of fatigue at the cross-bridge level, more than acidosis. ATP rarely drops below ~70% of resting; the muscle would rigor if it did. Central fatigue is real and explains why mental effort and motivation prolong endurance.

### Hint Ladder

1. Energy sources have a hierarchy by duration.
2. Multiple molecular mechanisms compete to slow the muscle.
3. Both peripheral and central factors contribute.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Fatigue is purely from ATP depletion" → Misconception: ATP rarely falls enough to limit cycling.
- Wrong answer: "Lactate accumulation is the only mechanism of fatigue" → Misconception: oversimplifying; multiple metabolites contribute.
- Wrong answer: "Central fatigue isn't real" → Misconception: motor cortex output reduces during prolonged effort, demonstrably.

## Question 6

**Type:** mechanism
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** should
**Difficulty:** advanced
**Stem:** Explain how a single muscle fibre can produce graded contraction strength even though each AP triggers the same cross-bridge response (all-or-none).
**Correct answer:** A single fibre's response is modulated by frequency. A single AP produces a twitch; rapid sequential APs produce wave summation (each Ca²⁺ release adds before the previous is removed). At high enough frequency, twitches fuse into tetanus — sustained contraction at maximum tension. Whole-muscle gradation comes from (a) recruitment of more motor units, and (b) increased firing frequency.
**Elaborative explanation:** Henneman's size principle: small motor units are recruited first, large ones last. This produces graded force from minimal to maximal across the muscle. Tetanic frequency varies by muscle (eye muscles fuse at ~300 Hz, leg muscles at ~50 Hz).

### Hint Ladder

1. A single AP triggers a twitch; multiple APs may not be independent.
2. Ca²⁺ levels build with repeated firing.
3. Whole-muscle force has both unit-level and population-level controls.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Each AP gives a different size response" → Misconception: ignoring all-or-none at the AP level.
- Wrong answer: "Force is graded only by recruiting more units" → Misconception: frequency coding is also important.
- Wrong answer: "Tetanus is the same as wave summation" → Misconception: tetanus is the _fused_ state of summation, not the summation itself.

## Question 7

**Type:** integration
**Bloom's level:** analyze
**Priority:** good
**Difficulty:** advanced
**Stem:** A patient has Duchenne muscular dystrophy. Explain the molecular basis and predict the histological and clinical findings.
**Correct answer:** DMD is caused by mutations in the dystrophin gene (X-linked). Dystrophin links the actin cytoskeleton to the dystroglycan complex in the sarcolemma; without it, repeated contraction tears the sarcolemma, causing Ca²⁺ influx, fibre necrosis, and replacement by fat and connective tissue (pseudohypertrophy, especially of calves). Clinically: progressive proximal weakness from age 3–5, Gower's sign, calf pseudohypertrophy, elevated serum CK, eventual cardiomyopathy and respiratory failure.
**Elaborative explanation:** Becker MD is a milder allelic variant where dystrophin is partially functional. Other dystrophies target different links in the dystroglycan complex (sarcoglycanopathies, dystroglycanopathies). Treatment is increasingly genetic — exon-skipping antisense oligonucleotides for specific mutations.

### Hint Ladder

1. The disease destroys a structural protein, not a contractile one.
2. Membrane damage from each contraction accumulates over years.
3. Replacement tissue mimics enlargement.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "DMD is from a defective myosin protein" → Misconception: contractile protein vs structural; DMD is structural.
- Wrong answer: "DMD is autosomal" → Misconception: it's X-linked recessive.
- Wrong answer: "Calf pseudohypertrophy means strong calves" → Misconception: it's fibrofatty infiltration, not muscle gain.

# Facts

## Definitions

- **Sarcomere**: the contractile unit between two Z-discs; ~2.0–2.2 μm at rest.
- **Thick filament**: myosin polymer with cross-bridge heads; ~1.6 μm long.
- **Thin filament**: actin polymer with troponin and tropomyosin; ~1 μm long.
- **Cross-bridge**: myosin head bound to an actin site, capable of generating force.
- **Twitch**: contractile response to a single AP.
- **Tetanus**: fused contraction at high firing frequency; maximum tension.

## Functions

- Sliding filament theory describes the molecular basis of contraction.
- Ca²⁺ regulates cross-bridge formation via troponin C.
- ATP fuels detachment (not power stroke).

## Normal values

- **Resting sarcomere length**: 2.0–2.2 μm.
- **Power stroke distance per cycle**: ~10 nm.
- **Cross-bridge cycle rate (slow fibre)**: ~5 cycles/sec.
- **Cross-bridge cycle rate (fast fibre)**: ~50 cycles/sec.
- **Maximum tetanic force**: ~35 N/cm² of cross-section.

## Relations

- Force ∝ number of attached cross-bridges (∝ sarcomere length within range).
- Velocity ∝ cycle rate (independent of force at zero load).
- Force-velocity curve is hyperbolic (Hill equation).

## Classification

- **Type I**: slow-oxidative, red, fatigue-resistant.
- **Type IIa**: fast-oxidative-glycolytic, intermediate.
- **Type IIb/IIx**: fast-glycolytic, white, fatigue-prone.

# Values

- **Sarcomere length, optimum**: 2.0–2.2 μm.
- **Sarcomere length range with active tension**: 1.6–3.6 μm.
- **Maximum tetanic force**: ~35 N/cm² cross-sectional area.
- **ATP per cross-bridge cycle**: 1.
- **Twitch duration (Type I)**: ~100 ms.
- **Twitch duration (Type II)**: ~30 ms.
- **Tetanic frequency (skeletal)**: 30–80 Hz.
