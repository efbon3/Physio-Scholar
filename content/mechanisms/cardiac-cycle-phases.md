# Layer 1 — Core

**Pressure decides the valves; the valves decide the volumes.** One
heartbeat is a 0.8-second loop in which left ventricular pressure
crosses two thresholds twice — atrial pressure on the way up
(closing the mitral valve), aortic pressure on the way up (opening
the aortic valve), aortic pressure on the way down (closing the
aortic valve), and atrial pressure on the way down (opening the
mitral valve again). Every clinical heart sound, every wiggle on the
pressure-volume loop, every murmur, is one of those four crossings
behaving correctly or behaving wrong.

## Clinical Hook

The first heart sound (S1) is mitral and tricuspid closure at the
start of systole; the second (S2) is aortic and pulmonic closure at
the end. A split S2 widens on inspiration because the right
ventricle, momentarily preloaded by the negative thoracic pressure,
takes a few extra milliseconds to finish ejecting. That tiny
respiratory split is the bedside reminder that the cycle is not one
event but two ventricles running in parallel.

# Layer 2 — Working Explanation

A cardiac cycle has **four phases on the ventricular side**, named
by what the valves are doing:

| Phase                                    | Mitral | Aortic | What changes                            |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------ | ------ | --------------------------------------- |
| Isovolumetric contraction                | closed | closed | pressure rises, volume constant         |
| Ejection                                 | closed | open   | pressure peaks then falls; volume falls |
| Isovolumetric relaxation                 | closed | closed | pressure falls, volume constant         |
| Filling (with atrial systole at the end) | open   | closed | volume rises; pressure low              |

## Phase 1 — Isovolumetric Contraction

The mitral valve has just closed (S1). The aortic valve is still
shut because aortic diastolic pressure (≈ 80 mmHg) exceeds
ventricular pressure. With both valves closed, the ventricle is a
sealed chamber: it contracts, pressure climbs steeply, and volume
does not change. Duration: ~50 ms.

## Phase 2 — Ejection

When ventricular pressure crosses aortic pressure (~80 mmHg), the
aortic valve opens and ejection begins. Pressure continues to rise
to a peak (~120 mmHg) early in ejection and then falls as the
ventricle empties. Stroke volume (~70 mL) leaves over this phase,
which lasts ~250 ms. Two sub-phases are conventionally recognised:
**rapid ejection** (first third, ~70% of stroke volume) and
**reduced ejection** (last two-thirds, the remainder).

## Phase 3 — Isovolumetric Relaxation

When ventricular pressure falls below aortic pressure, the aortic
valve closes (S2). Mitral pressure has not yet risen above atrial
pressure, so the mitral valve is still shut. Both valves closed
again — now the ventricle relaxes and pressure falls fast, but
volume is fixed. Duration: ~80 ms.

## Phase 4 — Filling

When ventricular pressure drops below atrial pressure, the mitral
valve opens and blood flows in. Filling has its own sub-phases:

- **Rapid filling** (~110 ms) — most of stroke volume crosses the
  valve in a passive pressure-driven inflow.
- **Diastasis** (~190 ms) — atrial and ventricular pressures
  equalise; flow slows almost to zero.
- **Atrial systole** (~110 ms) — the P wave fires the atrial kick,
  topping up the last 15–25% of end-diastolic volume.

## Key Variables

- **End-diastolic volume (EDV)** — set by filling, ~120 mL at rest.
- **End-systolic volume (ESV)** — set by ejection, ~50 mL at rest.
- **Stroke volume (SV)** — EDV − ESV, ~70 mL.
- **Ejection fraction (EF)** — SV / EDV, normally 55–70%.

## Common Exam Framings

Most exam questions trace one disturbance through the cycle: an
extra valve lesion, an arrhythmia eliminating atrial systole, a
sudden change in afterload. The trick is to identify which valve
crossing is delayed or absent and to follow the consequence.

# Layer 3 — Deep Dive

## The Pressure-Volume Loop

Plot ventricular pressure on the y-axis against ventricular volume
on the x-axis and one heartbeat traces a counter-clockwise loop:

- **Bottom-right corner** — end-diastolic volume, ~120 mL,
  ventricular pressure ~10 mmHg.
- **Right side, rising** — isovolumetric contraction. Volume fixed,
  pressure climbs vertically until aortic valve opens at ~80 mmHg.
- **Top side, leftward** — ejection. Pressure peaks ~120 mmHg early,
  declines as volume falls.
- **Left side, falling** — isovolumetric relaxation. Volume fixed,
  pressure falls vertically until mitral valve opens.
- **Bottom side, rightward** — filling. Volume rises along the
  passive end-diastolic pressure-volume relationship.

Stroke work — the work done by the ventricle in one beat — is the
**area enclosed** by this loop. Anything that moves a corner
changes the work.

## Timing Across the Two Sides of the Heart

The right ventricle ejects at much lower pressures (peak ~25 mmHg)
because pulmonary vascular resistance is roughly one-tenth that of
the systemic circulation. Right ventricular ejection actually
starts slightly **earlier** and ends slightly **later** than left:

- Pulmonary valve opens before aortic valve (lower diastolic pressure
  to overcome).
- Pulmonary valve closes after aortic valve (longer ejection at
  lower resistance).

This is the physiological basis for the inspiratory split S2 (P2
delayed further on inspiration as right ventricular preload
transiently rises).

## Quantitative Estimates

At rest, heart rate ~75 bpm, cycle length ~800 ms:

- Systole (isovolumetric contraction + ejection): ~300 ms
- Diastole (relaxation + filling + atrial systole): ~500 ms

When heart rate doubles to 150 bpm, **diastole shortens
disproportionately** — systole drops to ~200 ms, but diastole
collapses to ~200 ms. Coronary perfusion (which happens almost
entirely in diastole on the left side) is the first casualty of
sustained tachycardia.

## Misconceptions

- "Atrial systole drives most of ventricular filling" — **no**, at
  rest atrial systole tops up only 15–25%; the bulk crosses during
  rapid filling. At high heart rates the contribution rises (because
  diastasis disappears), which is why losing atrial kick (atrial
  fibrillation) hurts more during exercise than at rest.
- "S1 is the aortic valve closing" — **no**, S1 is mitral and
  tricuspid closing at the start of systole. S2 is aortic and
  pulmonic closing at the end.
- "The ventricles relax actively" — **mostly, yes**: relaxation is
  ATP-dependent (calcium re-uptake into the SR), which is why
  diastolic dysfunction often appears before systolic failure in
  ischaemic disease. The intuition that diastole is "passive rest"
  is misleading.

# Layer 4 — Clinical Integration

## Vignette 1 — Atrial Fibrillation and Exercise Tolerance

A 65-year-old in chronic atrial fibrillation walks one flight of
stairs and stops, breathless. Resting ejection fraction on
echocardiogram is normal. The mechanism is the loss of atrial
systole: at rest, with long diastolic filling time, passive flow
fills the ventricle adequately and stroke volume is preserved. On
exertion, heart rate rises, diastolic filling time collapses, and
without an atrial kick the end-diastolic volume falls. Stroke
volume drops with it, and cardiac output cannot meet the rising
metabolic demand.

## Vignette 2 — Aortic Stenosis and the Pressure Loop

A 70-year-old with severe aortic stenosis has a pressure gradient
of 60 mmHg across the valve. Trace it on the pressure-volume loop:
the ventricle has to climb to ~180 mmHg before the aortic valve
opens. Isovolumetric contraction takes longer; ejection happens
against an unrelenting wall of resistance. The loop becomes tall
and narrow, stroke volume falls, and the area inside the loop —
stroke work — has actually risen because of the pressure axis. The
ventricle hypertrophies in response, which over years stiffens it,
shifts the diastolic curve up, and produces the syncope-on-exertion
that classically heralds severe disease.

## Vignette 3 — Mitral Regurgitation Reverses an Arrow

A 55-year-old has chronic mitral regurgitation. During systole, a
fraction of stroke volume goes backward through the incompetent
mitral valve into the atrium instead of forward through the aortic
valve. The pressure-volume loop loses its sharp top-left corner —
ejection no longer works against constant afterload because some of
the volume escapes into a low-pressure chamber. Effective stroke
volume to the body is reduced, but ventricular work is not, and the
left atrium dilates over years to absorb the regurgitant volume.

## Pathophysiology

- **Diastolic heart failure** — relaxation impaired (hypertensive
  heart disease, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy). Ejection fraction is
  preserved; the cycle's filling phase is what fails.
- **Cardiac tamponade** — pericardial fluid restricts diastolic
  filling. End-diastolic volume falls; stroke volume falls; pressure
  rises just enough to keep flow forward, until it can't.
- **Aortic regurgitation** — incompetent aortic valve allows
  backflow during diastole. End-diastolic volume rises chronically,
  which by Frank-Starling raises stroke volume — until ventricular
  remodelling exhausts the compensation.

# Questions

## Question 1

**Type:** recall
**Bloom's level:** remember
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** foundational
**Stem:** During isovolumetric contraction, which valves are open and which are closed?
**Correct answer:** All four atrioventricular and semilunar valves are closed. Specifically, the mitral and tricuspid valves have just closed (producing S1), and the aortic and pulmonic valves are still closed because ventricular pressure has not yet risen above arterial diastolic pressure.
**Elaborative explanation:** Isovolumetric contraction is defined by the simultaneously closed state of both inflow and outflow valves. The ventricle behaves as a sealed chamber whose volume cannot change while its pressure climbs. The phase ends when ventricular pressure exceeds aortic (or pulmonary) diastolic pressure, opening the semilunar valve and beginning ejection.

### Hint Ladder

1. The phase begins with S1 and ends when the aortic valve opens.
2. If volume cannot change, no blood is leaving or entering — what does that imply about every valve in the chamber?
3. Both inflow valves and both outflow valves are in the same state.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Mitral open, aortic closed" → Misconception: Conflating filling with isovolumetric contraction; the mitral valve closes at the START of systole, not partway through.
- Wrong answer: "Aortic open, mitral closed" → Misconception: Confusing isovolumetric contraction with ejection. Ejection begins only after pressure rises high enough to push the aortic valve open.

## Question 2

**Type:** prediction
**Bloom's level:** apply
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** A patient's heart rate rises from 75 to 150 beats per minute during exercise. Which phase of the cardiac cycle shortens the most in absolute terms, and what is the most clinically relevant downstream consequence?
**Correct answer:** Diastole (relaxation + filling + atrial systole) shortens the most — from roughly 500 ms to roughly 200 ms — while systole shortens less, from ~300 ms to ~200 ms. The most clinically relevant consequence is that left coronary artery perfusion, which occurs almost exclusively during diastole, falls disproportionately. Patients with fixed coronary stenoses become symptomatic at the heart rate where diastolic perfusion time becomes inadequate.
**Elaborative explanation:** Cycle length is inversely proportional to heart rate, but the cycle is not compressed proportionally — systolic duration is set by contraction kinetics and falls modestly, whereas diastolic duration is what gets squeezed when the cycle is short. The clinical implication is that beta-blockers' anti-anginal effect is partly mediated by lengthening diastole, not just by reducing oxygen demand.

### Hint Ladder

1. Cycle length at 150 bpm is half what it was at 75 bpm.
2. Of the two large compartments (systole and diastole), which has more "reserve" duration to lose?
3. Coronary perfusion has a temporal dependence that explains why this matters in ischaemic heart disease.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Both halves shorten equally" → Misconception: Treating heart rate as compressing the cycle proportionally; in reality systolic duration is relatively fixed and diastole bears the cost.
- Wrong answer: "Right coronary perfusion suffers most" → Misconception: Forgetting that the right ventricle is perfused throughout the cycle because RV pressure never approaches aortic pressure; left coronary flow is the diastole-dependent one.

## Question 3

**Type:** clinical reasoning
**Bloom's level:** analyze
**Priority:** should
**Difficulty:** advanced
**Stem:** A patient has a fixed-rate ventricular pacemaker but no atrial coordination. At rest the patient is comfortable; on mild exertion they fatigue much earlier than expected for their age. Explain the mechanism of exertional fatigue using the cardiac cycle.
**Correct answer:** Without atrial-ventricular coordination, the atrial systolic contribution to end-diastolic volume is lost. At rest, long diastolic filling time allows passive filling to bring EDV close to its normal value, so stroke volume is adequate. On exertion, heart rate rises (or, in a fixed-rate pacemaker, fails to rise enough), diastolic filling time shortens, and without an atrial kick to top up the ventricle, end-diastolic volume drops below the level needed to sustain stroke volume. Cardiac output cannot meet the rising metabolic demand, and the patient fatigues — this is the "pacemaker syndrome" pattern.
**Elaborative explanation:** Atrial systole contributes 15–25% of end-diastolic volume at rest, but the percentage rises sharply at high heart rates because diastasis disappears and only rapid filling and atrial systole remain. Loss of atrial kick is therefore much more costly during exertion than at rest. Modern pacemakers solve this by delivering AV-synchronous pacing (DDD mode) so the atria contract appropriately before the ventricles.

### Hint Ladder

1. Identify what the atrium contributes to ventricular filling and when that contribution matters most.
2. Diastolic filling has three components — which one is missing here, and what happens to the other two as heart rate rises?
3. Compare end-diastolic volume at rest vs. on exertion in this patient and trace the consequence to cardiac output.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "The pacemaker is set too low; the heart rate cannot meet demand" → Misconception: Identifying only the chronotropic incompetence but missing the additional mechanism of lost atrial kick.
- Wrong answer: "The ventricle does not fill adequately because diastole is too short" → Misconception: Half-right; diastole shortening matters but only because the patient also lacks atrial systole to compensate.

## Question 4

**Type:** prediction
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** should
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** Why does inspiration produce a wider split between A2 and P2 (the two components of the second heart sound)?
**Correct answer:** Inspiration lowers intrathoracic pressure, which transiently increases venous return to the right side of the heart. Right ventricular end-diastolic volume rises, prolonging right ventricular ejection by a few milliseconds. The pulmonic valve therefore closes a few milliseconds later than usual, while the aortic valve is largely unaffected (left ventricular preload, drawn from the lungs, transiently falls during inspiration because pulmonary vasculature expands). The audible result is a wider split between A2 (aortic) and P2 (pulmonic).
**Elaborative explanation:** This is a normal "physiological" split. A widely fixed split (no respiratory variation) suggests an atrial septal defect, where right and left atrial pressures equalise and respiratory effects on right-sided preload are masked. A paradoxical split (P2 before A2) suggests delayed left ventricular ejection from causes such as left bundle branch block or severe aortic stenosis.

### Hint Ladder

1. What does inspiration do to intrathoracic pressure and to venous return?
2. Right and left ventricular ejection times are not equal — which one changes more with respiration?
3. The valve that closes later is the one whose ventricle is still ejecting.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Inspiration speeds the heart, which separates the sounds" → Misconception: The split is not from respiratory sinus arrhythmia; it is from the differential effect of inspiration on right vs left ventricular preload.
- Wrong answer: "Aortic valve closes earlier on inspiration" → Misconception: A2 timing is largely unchanged; it is P2 that moves later, not A2 that moves earlier.

## Question 5

**Type:** prediction
**Bloom's level:** apply
**Priority:** should
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** A patient has severe aortic stenosis with a peak transvalvular gradient of 60 mmHg. Compared to a healthy heart, what happens to the duration of isovolumetric contraction and to the area of the pressure-volume loop?
**Correct answer:** Isovolumetric contraction lengthens — the ventricle must climb to roughly 140 mmHg (instead of 80 mmHg) before the aortic valve opens, and the pressure-rise rate is finite. The pressure-volume loop becomes taller (peak pressure rises) and somewhat narrower (stroke volume falls). The total area enclosed — stroke work — actually rises despite the falling stroke volume, because the pressure axis has expanded more than the volume axis has shrunk.
**Elaborative explanation:** Aortic stenosis is the classic pressure-overload lesion. The ventricle responds chronically with concentric hypertrophy (sarcomeres added in parallel, wall thickness increased, chamber size unchanged or reduced) to normalise wall stress per Laplace's law. Over years, the hypertrophied wall stiffens, shifting the diastolic pressure-volume curve upward and producing diastolic dysfunction on top of the existing pressure overload.

### Hint Ladder

1. The ventricle has to develop higher pressure before the aortic valve will open — what does that do to the duration of isovolumetric contraction?
2. Sketch the loop: the top-left corner of ejection is at a higher pressure; the right side (filling) is unchanged; the loop is taller.
3. Stroke work is the area inside the loop — which axis has changed more?

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Stroke work falls because stroke volume falls" → Misconception: Considering only the volume axis. Stroke work is pressure × volume; in pressure overload the pressure axis dominates.
- Wrong answer: "The loop is shorter and narrower" → Misconception: Confusing the loop's height (pressure) with its width (stroke volume); the loop is taller, not shorter.

## Question 6

**Type:** clinical reasoning
**Bloom's level:** analyze
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** Identify on the pressure-volume loop where each of the four heart sounds (S1, S2, and physiological S3, S4) would occur.
**Correct answer:** S1 occurs at the start of isovolumetric contraction — the bottom-right corner of the loop, where the mitral valve has just closed and pressure begins climbing vertically. S2 occurs at the start of isovolumetric relaxation — the top-left corner, where the aortic valve has just closed and pressure begins falling vertically. S3, when present, is heard during rapid filling — along the bottom edge of the loop as it moves rightward early in diastole. S4 is heard during atrial systole — the very last segment of the bottom edge, just before isovolumetric contraction begins.
**Elaborative explanation:** Mapping the heart sounds to the loop reinforces that sounds are events at valve crossings or chamber-wall events during specific cycle phases. S3 and S4 are vibrations of a filling ventricle: S3 from rapid filling into a compliant ventricle (normal in young adults; pathological in older patients indicating volume overload or systolic dysfunction), S4 from atrial contraction into a stiff ventricle (always pathological in adults — suggests diastolic dysfunction or hypertrophy).

### Hint Ladder

1. S1 and S2 mark the two valve closures that bookend systole.
2. S3 and S4 are filling sounds — somewhere along the bottom of the loop.
3. Order the four sounds in time across one cardiac cycle and you can place them on the loop in order.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "S3 occurs during atrial systole" → Misconception: Swapping S3 and S4. S3 is rapid filling; S4 is atrial systole.
- Wrong answer: "S2 occurs at the bottom of ejection" → Misconception: Putting the aortic valve closure at the wrong corner of the loop. It is at the top-left corner, where ejection ends and isovolumetric relaxation begins.

# Facts

## Definitions

- **Isovolumetric contraction**: phase of systole between mitral closure and aortic opening — both valves shut, ventricular pressure rises, volume unchanged.
- **Isovolumetric relaxation**: phase of diastole between aortic closure and mitral opening — both valves shut, pressure falls, volume unchanged.
- **Diastasis**: middle sub-phase of diastolic filling when atrial and ventricular pressures are nearly equal and inflow slows almost to zero.

## Normal values

- **End-diastolic volume (EDV)**: ~120 mL in a resting adult.
- **End-systolic volume (ESV)**: ~50 mL.
- **Stroke volume (SV)**: ~70 mL.
- **Ejection fraction (EF)**: 55–70%.
- **Cycle length at HR 75 bpm**: ~800 ms (systole ~300 ms, diastole ~500 ms).
- **Aortic diastolic pressure**: ~80 mmHg (the threshold isovolumetric contraction must climb past).
- **Peak left ventricular pressure**: ~120 mmHg.
- **Peak right ventricular pressure**: ~25 mmHg.

## Functions

- **S1 (first heart sound)**: marks closure of the mitral and tricuspid valves at the start of systole.
- **S2 (second heart sound)**: marks closure of the aortic and pulmonic valves at the end of systole.
- **Atrial systole**: tops up the last 15–25% of end-diastolic volume just before ventricular contraction.
- **Diastasis**: provides a "rest" interval during which ventricular and atrial pressures equalise; disappears at high heart rates.

## Relations

- **Stroke volume formula**: SV = EDV − ESV.
- **Ejection fraction formula**: EF = SV / EDV.
- **Cardiac output formula**: CO = HR × SV.
- **Diastolic filling time**: shortens disproportionately as heart rate rises (~500 ms at 75 bpm → ~200 ms at 150 bpm).

## Associations

- **Inspiratory split S2**: associated with delayed P2 due to transiently increased right ventricular preload.
- **Fixed split S2**: associated with atrial septal defect (right and left atrial pressures equalise).
- **S3**: associated with rapid filling into a compliant ventricle; physiological in young adults, pathological in older.
- **S4**: associated with atrial systole into a stiff ventricle; always pathological in adults.
- **Atrial fibrillation + exercise intolerance**: associated with loss of atrial kick and shortened diastolic filling time at high rates.

## Classifications

- **Phases of the cardiac cycle**: isovolumetric contraction, ejection, isovolumetric relaxation, filling.
- **Sub-phases of ejection**: rapid ejection (~70% of stroke volume), reduced ejection (remainder).
- **Sub-phases of filling**: rapid filling, diastasis, atrial systole.
- **Heart sounds**: S1 (mitral + tricuspid closure), S2 (aortic + pulmonic closure), S3 (rapid filling), S4 (atrial systole).

# Values

- **End-diastolic volume (EDV)**: 120 mL (range 100–160 mL, resting adult).
- **End-systolic volume (ESV)**: 50 mL (range 40–70 mL).
- **Stroke volume (SV)**: 70 mL (range 60–100 mL).
- **Ejection fraction (EF)**: 60% (normal 55–70%).
- **Heart rate (resting adult)**: 75 bpm (normal 60–100 bpm awake).
- **Cardiac cycle length at HR 75**: ~800 ms.
- **Systole duration at rest**: ~300 ms.
- **Diastole duration at rest**: ~500 ms.
- **Diastole at HR 150 bpm**: ~200 ms.
- **Aortic diastolic pressure**: ~80 mmHg.
- **Aortic systolic pressure (peak LV)**: ~120 mmHg.
- **Pulmonary peak (peak RV)**: ~25 mmHg.
- **Atrial systole contribution to EDV**: 15–25%.
- **Isovolumetric contraction duration**: ~50 ms.
- **Isovolumetric relaxation duration**: ~80 ms.
- **Rapid filling duration**: ~110 ms.
- **Diastasis duration at rest**: ~190 ms.
