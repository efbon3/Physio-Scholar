# Layer 1 — Core

**No troponin, no T-tubules, no sarcomeres — but the same fundamental
sliding filaments.** Smooth muscle uses Ca²⁺/calmodulin to activate
myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK), which phosphorylates myosin and
allows cross-bridge formation. Slower than skeletal, but extraordinarily
energy-efficient through the "latch state" — once cross-bridges have
attached, they can hold tension at low ATP cost.

## Clinical Hook

Pulmonary hypertension drugs (sildenafil, riociguat) work through the
NO/cGMP pathway, which dephosphorylates myosin light chains in
pulmonary vascular smooth muscle. This drops vascular resistance.
Erectile dysfunction drugs use the same pathway in cavernosal smooth
muscle. The molecular target — phosphodiesterase-5 — happens to be
expressed prominently in lung vasculature, smooth muscle of the penis,
and not many other places.

# Questions

## Question 1

**Type:** classification
**Bloom's level:** remember
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** foundational
**Stem:** Differentiate single-unit (visceral) and multi-unit smooth muscle by their innervation, electrical coupling, and clinical examples.
**Correct answer:** Single-unit (visceral): cells are electrically coupled by gap junctions; one AP spreads through the syncytium; sparse autonomic innervation; spontaneous activity is common (slow waves and pacemaker cells). Examples: GI tract, ureter, uterus, small blood vessels. Multi-unit: cells act independently; each fibre individually innervated; finer control; less spontaneous. Examples: iris, ciliary body, vas deferens, large blood vessels.
**Elaborative explanation:** Visceral muscle's electrical coupling allows coordinated contraction of large tissue volumes from a single signal — peristalsis is the consequence. Multi-unit's independence allows precise dose-response control — the iris can dilate finely.

### Hint Ladder

1. Single-unit vs multi-unit: think electrical coupling.
2. Where does the muscle need coordinated mass contraction?
3. Where does it need fine independent control?

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Single-unit smooth muscle has no autonomic innervation" → Misconception: it has sparse innervation, not absent.
- Wrong answer: "Multi-unit smooth muscle is found in the GI tract" → Misconception: GI is single-unit.
- Wrong answer: "Both types have identical contractile proteins" → Misconception: they share most proteins; the differentiation is in coupling and innervation.

## Question 2

**Type:** mechanism
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** Describe how Ca²⁺ activates contraction in smooth muscle.
**Correct answer:** Ca²⁺ enters the cytoplasm (from extracellular space via voltage-gated and receptor-operated channels, plus from SR via IP₃ and CICR) and binds calmodulin. Ca²⁺/calmodulin activates MLCK, which phosphorylates myosin light chains. Phosphorylated myosin can now interact with actin and cycle. Relaxation: Ca²⁺ falls, MLCK inactivates, myosin light chain phosphatase dephosphorylates myosin, cross-bridges detach.
**Elaborative explanation:** The "shift-the-myosin" regulation in smooth muscle differs from the "shift-the-actin-tropomyosin" regulation in striated muscle. Both are Ca²⁺-driven but the molecular switch is on different filaments.

### Hint Ladder

1. Smooth muscle has no troponin — what's the regulator instead?
2. The regulator works on the thick filament, not the thin filament.
3. Phosphorylation makes the difference.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Smooth muscle uses troponin like skeletal muscle" → Misconception: smooth muscle lacks troponin.
- Wrong answer: "Phosphorylation of actin allows myosin binding" → Misconception: it's myosin (light chains) that gets phosphorylated.
- Wrong answer: "Ca²⁺ binds myosin directly in smooth muscle" → Misconception: it binds calmodulin first.

## Question 3

**Type:** mechanism
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** should
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** What is the latch state in smooth muscle, and why is it metabolically advantageous?
**Correct answer:** Latch state: a low-energy condition in which cross-bridges remain attached to actin even after myosin light chains are dephosphorylated. The cycle rate is dramatically reduced (~10× slower), so ATP consumption per second drops, but force is maintained. Smooth muscle can hold tonic contraction for long periods at minimal cost.
**Elaborative explanation:** Vascular smooth muscle, ureter, urinary bladder, and the uterine cervix all use the latch mechanism for sustained tone. The molecular basis is incompletely understood but involves dephosphorylated myosin remaining attached to actin in a slowly cycling state.

### Hint Ladder

1. Smooth muscle holds force without burning ATP rapidly.
2. The cross-bridge cycle slows down.
3. The hallmark is sustained tension at low cost.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Latch state is a state of complete relaxation" → Misconception: it's sustained contraction at low energy cost.
- Wrong answer: "Latch state requires fresh ATP every cycle at the same rate as active cross-bridge cycling" → Misconception: latch is energetically efficient because cycling slows.
- Wrong answer: "Skeletal muscle uses the latch state for posture" → Misconception: latch is unique to smooth muscle.

## Question 4

**Type:** mechanism
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** must
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** What are slow waves in GI smooth muscle, and what's their relationship to action potentials and contractions?
**Correct answer:** Slow waves are spontaneous, rhythmic depolarisations (10–40 mV) generated by interstitial cells of Cajal (ICC, the GI pacemaker cells). They don't directly cause contraction but bring the membrane to threshold; if a stimulus (stretch, neural, hormonal) adds further depolarisation, the slow wave triggers a burst of APs and a contraction.
**Elaborative explanation:** Slow wave frequency varies along the gut: 3/min in stomach, 12/min in duodenum, 8/min in ileum, 6/min in colon — setting the maximum rhythm of segmenting contractions. Diabetic gastroparesis often involves loss of ICCs, dropping slow-wave activity.

### Hint Ladder

1. Slow waves come from a specialised pacemaker cell type.
2. They modulate excitability rather than directly cause contraction.
3. Frequency varies along the gut.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "Slow waves are just slow APs" → Misconception: they are sub-threshold depolarisations, not APs.
- Wrong answer: "Slow waves originate in smooth muscle cells themselves" → Misconception: ICCs are the pacemaker.
- Wrong answer: "Slow waves are uniform throughout the GI tract" → Misconception: frequency varies dramatically by region.

## Question 5

**Type:** application
**Bloom's level:** apply
**Priority:** should
**Difficulty:** standard
**Stem:** A patient has Hirschsprung disease (congenital aganglionic megacolon). Explain why a segment of bowel fails to relax, despite ICC pacemaker activity being intact.
**Correct answer:** Hirschsprung disease is congenital absence of enteric ganglia (especially inhibitory NO-releasing motor neurons) in a distal colon segment. Without inhibitory innervation, the smooth muscle remains tonically contracted (the "spastic" segment), preventing peristalsis and causing functional obstruction. The proximal bowel dilates as faecal matter accumulates above the spastic segment.
**Elaborative explanation:** Smooth muscle tone is a balance of intrinsic excitatory pacemaker activity and extrinsic inhibitory neural input. Loss of inhibition produces spasm, not loss of contraction. The diagnostic test is rectal biopsy showing absent ganglia. Treatment is surgical resection of the aganglionic segment.

### Hint Ladder

1. The bowel needs both contraction and relaxation for peristalsis.
2. Inhibitory neurons normally release a relaxing signal — what is it?
3. Without inhibition, the muscle stays tightened.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "The aganglionic segment is paralysed (flaccid)" → Misconception: it's spastic, not flaccid.
- Wrong answer: "Hirschsprung is from bacterial infection" → Misconception: it's a developmental neural defect.
- Wrong answer: "Smooth muscle requires excitatory innervation to contract" → Misconception: smooth muscle has spontaneous tone; absence of inhibition makes it more contracted.

## Question 6

**Type:** mechanism
**Bloom's level:** understand
**Priority:** should
**Difficulty:** advanced
**Stem:** How does NO (nitric oxide) cause vascular smooth muscle relaxation at the molecular level?
**Correct answer:** NO diffuses into smooth muscle cells and activates soluble guanylate cyclase → cGMP rises. cGMP activates protein kinase G (PKG), which phosphorylates targets that lower intracellular Ca²⁺ (closing Ca²⁺ channels, opening K⁺ channels, activating SERCA) and reduces Ca²⁺ sensitivity of myosin light chain phosphatase. Net result: dephosphorylated myosin, relaxation.
**Elaborative explanation:** PDE-5 inhibitors (sildenafil) block cGMP breakdown, prolonging vasodilation. This is exploited therapeutically in pulmonary hypertension and erectile dysfunction. The same pathway, with cAMP instead of cGMP, mediates β-adrenergic vasodilation in skeletal muscle vasculature.

### Hint Ladder

1. NO acts on a soluble enzyme inside the cell.
2. Cyclic nucleotide signaling is involved.
3. Multiple downstream effects all reduce active myosin.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "NO directly dephosphorylates myosin" → Misconception: NO works through a kinase cascade.
- Wrong answer: "NO causes vasoconstriction in pulmonary vessels" → Misconception: NO is a vasodilator everywhere.
- Wrong answer: "PDE-5 inhibitors block NO production" → Misconception: they block cGMP breakdown, prolonging the NO signal.

## Question 7

**Type:** integration
**Bloom's level:** analyze
**Priority:** good
**Difficulty:** advanced
**Stem:** A patient with phaeochromocytoma has hypertension. Explain mechanistically how circulating catecholamines act on smooth muscle to raise blood pressure.
**Correct answer:** Adrenaline and noradrenaline activate α₁-adrenergic receptors on vascular smooth muscle. α₁ couples via Gq → phospholipase C → IP₃ + DAG. IP₃ releases SR Ca²⁺; DAG activates PKC, which sensitises the contractile apparatus. Increased [Ca²⁺] triggers MLCK-mediated myosin phosphorylation; PKC reduces phosphatase activity. Net result: vasoconstriction, raised peripheral resistance, hypertension. The α₁-receptor antagonist phenoxybenzamine is used pre-operatively to prevent surgical hypertensive crisis.
**Elaborative explanation:** Phaeochromocytoma episodes can produce catecholamine surges of 10–100× normal, with massive vasoconstriction and reflex bradycardia. Surgical removal requires α-blockade first (to prevent peri-operative blood pressure spikes), then β-blockade if tachycardia is a concern; β-blockade alone is dangerous because unopposed α-stimulation can be catastrophic.

### Hint Ladder

1. Catecholamines act on which receptor for vasoconstriction?
2. The receptor couples to which G-protein and second messenger?
3. The end result is increased Ca²⁺ and contraction.

### Misconception Mappings

- Wrong answer: "α₁ receptors couple via cAMP" → Misconception: that's β-adrenergic; α₁ is Gq/IP₃.
- Wrong answer: "Phaeochromocytoma should be treated first with β-blockade" → Misconception: β-blockade alone unmasks α-effects and worsens hypertension.
- Wrong answer: "Vasoconstriction comes from K⁺ channel opening" → Misconception: K⁺ opening hyperpolarises and _relaxes_ smooth muscle.

# Facts

## Definitions

- **Single-unit smooth muscle**: gap-junction-coupled, syncytial; contracts as a sheet.
- **Multi-unit smooth muscle**: independent fibres, each innervated.
- **Latch state**: low-energy sustained contraction with slow cross-bridge cycling.
- **Slow wave**: spontaneous sub-threshold depolarisation generated by ICCs.
- **MLCK / MLCP**: myosin light chain kinase / phosphatase — the on/off switch for smooth muscle contraction.

## Functions

- Smooth muscle handles all involuntary contraction of viscera, vasculature, reproductive tract, and eye.
- Latch state is the energetic basis of vascular tone and sphincter function.

## Normal values

- **GI slow wave frequency**: stomach 3/min, duodenum 12/min, ileum 8/min, colon 6/min.
- **Smooth muscle resting potential**: −50 to −60 mV.
- **Twitch duration**: ~3–10 sec (vs ~30–100 ms in skeletal).
- **Velocity of contraction**: 0.1–0.3× of skeletal muscle.

## Relations

- Smooth muscle uses 100–500× less ATP per unit force per unit time than skeletal muscle.
- It generates similar maximum force (~30 N/cm²) despite no sarcomeres.

## Classification

- **By mechanical organisation**: tonic (always partially contracted) vs phasic (rapid contractions).
- **By electrical coupling**: single-unit vs multi-unit.

# Values

- **Resting potential**: −50 to −60 mV.
- **GI slow wave amplitude**: 10–40 mV.
- **Latch state ATP cost**: ~10× lower than active cycling.
- **Maximum tension**: 25–35 N/cm² (similar to skeletal).
- **Smooth-to-skeletal force-velocity ratio**: 1:5 to 1:10.
